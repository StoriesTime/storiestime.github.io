Prejudice in the Heart of the City


The city was bustling with life. Its heart was a maze of crowded streets, towering buildings, and busy commuters. For many, it was a symbol of opportunity—a place where dreams could be made, fortunes could be won, and lives could be rebuilt. But beneath the surface of this vibrant metropolis, there simmered a silent force, more powerful than any traffic jam or economic disparity. It was prejudice.

Jamil sat at the corner café, sipping his black coffee, his fingers tapping anxiously against the mug. He wasn’t sure why he always found himself drawn to this particular spot. Maybe it was the view—looking out over the street where people from all walks of life seemed to converge. Or maybe it was the anonymity the city offered, a feeling that, for once, no one was watching him.

Yet, no matter how many times he sat here, the same thing always gnawed at him. The looks. The whispers. The way people saw him. Jamil, a young man of Middle Eastern descent, had grown used to it. The city may have been big, but sometimes, it felt too small for someone like him.

It wasn’t just the subtle stares he noticed from passersby, or the hurried footsteps when someone walked past him too closely. It was the way people treated him when they didn’t think he was listening. The comments that slipped out in the midst of casual conversation. The way his presence was met with a hesitation, a careful avoidance.

He had learned to ignore it, for the most part. He had come to the city with nothing but a suitcase and a dream. Like many others, he had hoped that this place would be a haven, a place where his past—his heritage, his identity—wouldn’t matter. But over the years, Jamil had learned that even the anonymity of a big city couldn’t erase the prejudices people carried deep within.

“Another day in paradise,” he muttered to himself, staring at the sea of faces outside the café window. Some were lost in their phones, others in conversation. No one paid him any real attention, and that suited him just fine.

But then, as the clock ticked towards noon, the door to the café opened, and a young woman walked in. She was striking, with sharp features and fiery red hair that cascaded down her back like a wave of flame. She ordered a cappuccino, her voice soft but firm, her accent unmistakable. Jamil’s eyes followed her instinctively as she made her way to an empty table near the back.

The woman was an anomaly in the city—an outsider in a place that had grown so accustomed to its own image. But it wasn’t her appearance that caught his attention; it was the way she moved. She had an air of confidence, as if she was fully aware of her surroundings and yet completely unaffected by them.

As the woman settled into her seat, Jamil hesitated for a moment before gathering the courage to approach her. There was something about her that seemed so different from the others in the café. She didn’t glance at him with that familiar judgmental look, nor did she seem to carry the weight of expectation that often hung heavy in the air when he was around.

“Excuse me,” Jamil said, his voice hesitant. The woman looked up, her eyes locking onto his with an intensity that startled him.

“Can I help you?” she asked, her voice warm and unbothered.

Jamil swallowed his nerves. “I—I couldn’t help but notice you’re new here. You don’t look like you’re from around these parts.”

The woman raised an eyebrow, her lips curling into a small smile. “You’re right. I’m not. I’m from Ireland. Just moved here last week. New job, new life.” She leaned back in her chair, studying him for a moment. “And you? You look like you’ve been here a while.”

Jamil smiled wryly, shaking his head. “Feels like forever sometimes.” He hesitated again, feeling the familiar sting of self-consciousness creeping up. “So… how are you finding it?”

Her gaze softened. “It’s different. People here… they don’t really talk to each other, do they? Everyone’s in their own world. It’s kind of lonely, actually.”

He nodded. “Yeah, that’s what they say. It’s a city of strangers.”

The woman leaned forward slightly, her gaze intense now. “What’s it like, for you? Being here?”

Jamil’s heart skipped. The question wasn’t just casual. It was probing, and it caught him off guard. He knew what she was asking—how did he, as someone who didn’t fit the mold of the city’s typical inhabitant, feel about living in a place that wasn’t always kind to people like him?

The truth hung on his lips, but he swallowed it down, unwilling to bare himself too quickly to someone he didn’t even know. Instead, he shrugged and offered a half-hearted smile. “I manage. It’s not always easy, but I’m doing okay. You learn to adapt.”

“Adapt to what?” she asked, her curiosity piqued.

“People’s assumptions,” he said quietly. “The way they see you, before they even know you. It’s like… they have a script in their heads, and you have to play your part, even if you don’t fit.”

She blinked, her expression thoughtful. “I can understand that. I’ve felt that too. Being from somewhere else, I mean. People expect you to be a certain way because of where you come from.” She paused for a moment, as if searching for the right words. “It’s exhausting, isn’t it? Having to prove yourself constantly.”

Jamil let out a breath he hadn’t realized he was holding. “Yeah, it is.”

For a moment, there was a silence between them. The noise of the café, the chatter of other patrons, seemed to fade into the background. Jamil realized, with a sudden clarity, that he wasn’t alone in his feelings. This woman, a stranger, understood him in a way that few ever had.

“You know,” she said after a beat, “I’ve been here for a week, and already, I’ve had people make assumptions about me. Just because of my accent, my clothes, the way I carry myself. And sometimes, it’s hard to shake that feeling, like you’re always being judged.”

Jamil chuckled softly, a bitter edge to his laughter. “It’s a city built on diversity, but at the same time, people seem to want to keep their walls up. They think they know who you are before you even open your mouth.”

The woman nodded, her expression darkening for a moment. “Yeah. It’s like they see you as a threat. Or as something less than them.”

“Prejudice, I guess,” Jamil said, his voice quieter now. “It’s everywhere, even in a place like this. The heart of the city. You’d think that would mean something more… accepting.”

She stared at her coffee for a moment, her fingers lightly tracing the rim of the cup. “You’d think.”

They sat in silence for a while longer, neither of them quite sure what to say next. The connection between them was palpable, a shared understanding of something neither of them had been able to articulate before. In that moment, it felt like the weight of the world had lifted—if only for a brief while.

When the silence was finally broken, it was by the woman’s soft voice. “I think it’s the way people fear what’s different. They’re scared of anything that challenges their view of the world. It’s easier to stay in their bubble, where everything fits into neat little boxes.”

Jamil nodded slowly. “I think you’re right. But it’s also exhausting. You spend so much energy trying to conform, trying to make yourself fit, that you forget who you are. It’s hard not to lose yourself in all that.”

She smiled gently. “Yeah. I guess that’s the real challenge, isn’t it? Finding a way to stay true to yourself, even when the world around you is telling you to be something else.”

Jamil met her gaze, a spark of understanding passing between them. “Maybe that’s the key. Not fitting in, but finding a place where you don’t have to hide who you are.”

“Yeah,” she agreed, “maybe that’s the dream. To be seen for who you really are.”

For the first time in a long while, Jamil felt a sense of peace settle over him. He didn’t need to fit into the city’s mold, didn’t need to prove anything to anyone. The city might still have its prejudices, but he could choose to live outside of them.

And in that moment, as the woman stood to leave, he realized that perhaps the city wasn’t as cold and indifferent as it seemed. Maybe, just maybe, it was waiting for people like him—and her—to make it a little warmer, a little more welcoming.

With a final nod, she smiled at him. “Take care,” she said, before walking out the door.

Jamil watched her disappear into the crowd, feeling a sense of something new stirring inside him. Something hopeful.
