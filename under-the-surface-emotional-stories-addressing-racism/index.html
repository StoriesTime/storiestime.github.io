Under the Surface: Emotional Stories Addressing Racism


The city had a rhythm all its own—a chaotic mix of honking cars, distant sirens, chatter spilling out of cafés, and the quiet hum of countless lives intersecting in moments both fleeting and profound. To an outsider, it seemed vibrant, diverse, a tapestry of experiences and faces. But beneath the surface, seeped currents of pain and invisible wounds carried by many.

Jasmine adjusted her scarf as she walked through the busy plaza, her senses absorbing the cacophony. The sun felt unusually warm for late autumn, but the chill persisted beneath her skin, a cold she couldn’t shake no matter how many layers she wore.

At twenty-seven, Jasmine had become a community organizer, someone who bridged gaps and tried to create spaces where voices long ignored could be heard. But the weight of years of racial bias, subtle and overt, layered invisibly on her every encounter, made this work heavy.

This morning’s meeting had been tense. Jasmines’s heart still throbbed with the sting of condescension when a colleague questioned her statistics on racial disparities in local education. “Are you sure that’s accurate? Couldn’t it be just a coincidence?” he had asked, brows knitting together in doubt.

It was the sort of thing Jasmine had grown accustomed to hearing, a polite dismissal that ignored hard data because it challenged comfortable narratives. She had kept her smile tight, answering calmly and precisely, but inside, a storm brewed.

She wandered toward a nearby bench, where Mr. Thomas, an elderly man she often saw feeding pigeons, sat silently. His face was mapped with wrinkles, each a story of endurance and wisdom. She sat beside him, letting the silence between them be a balm.

“You look tired, Jasmine,” he said softly after a while.

She nodded. “Sometimes I wonder if I’m just pushing against a wall.”

Mr. Thomas reached into his coat and pulled out a faded photograph, handing it to her. It was black and white, showing a young African American boy standing proudly in front of a school, holding a book.

“That’s me,” he said, voice thick with memories. “Back when schools were segregated, when people like me weren’t allowed in many places. My parents fought for a better future, hoped that children like you would have it easier.”

Jasmine held the photo carefully, feeling the weight of his history settle over her like a mantle. “Sometimes I feel like we’re just reliving those battles.”

Mr. Thomas smiled gently. “The battles might shift but don’t let that make you believe they’re any less real. What matters is how you carry your truth. And that you carry it with others.”

Her phone buzzed—a message from her younger brother, Malik.

“Did you hear about the protest downtown? They’re organizing a march against police brutality next week.”

Jasmine’s chest tightened. It was the kind of news that punctuated the days now, each headline echoing with a familiar ache.

She typed back quickly: “Let’s go. Together.”

Later that day, Jasmine met Malik at a little coffee shop tucked between towering office buildings. Malik, with his easy smile and energy, was the spark she sometimes needed to remember hope.

“I hate that it feels like every other week we have to fight just to be seen as human,” he said, swirling milk into his coffee.

They talked about their experiences—Malik’s run-ins with suspicion on the streets, Jasmine’s battles in meeting rooms where her voice was too often softened or ignored. Each story was a thread weaving them closer, reminding them that beneath the cultural noise, the emotional undertow was shared.

Malik’s expression darkened. “Did I tell you about what happened at the grocery store? A clerk followed me around like I was going to steal something. Just because of the color of my skin.”

Jasmine clenched her fists. “I’m so sorry. That happens way too often.”

They spoke of resilience, of frustration, and of the invisible scars racism etched on their souls.

“It’s exhausting,” Malik said finally, “but if we don’t stand up, who will?”

Jasmine nodded. “Exactly. And we aren’t alone anymore.”

Over the next days, the city thrummed with anticipation and tension. Flyers appeared on lampposts and social media buzzed with calls for justice. Jasmine threw herself into organizing, making sure the voices of those who lived these struggles daily were heard loud and clear.

At the protest, crowds poured into the streets—diverse, determined, and vibrant. People chanted, sang, and shared stories. Signs raised high told tales of sorrow and hope.

Jasmine found herself face to face with officers who stood stoically, the tension almost tangible.

Suddenly, an older woman approached her, eyes wet with tears. “Thank you,” she whispered. “For speaking out. For not letting us disappear.”

Jasmine squeezed the woman’s hand, feeling a surge of solidarity. In that moment, beneath the sea of faces, beneath the surface, something shifted—a collective acknowledgement of pain, and the power of people coming together.

Weeks later, at a city council meeting, Jasmine stood before a panel to present a new community program designed to combat racial bias in schools and workplaces.

Her voice trembled at first, but then grew steady as she spoke not just of statistics, but of human stories—the small victories, the wounds, the unyielding hope.

When the meeting ended, council members nodded thoughtfully, and Jasmine felt the first true breath of change.

Back at the bench one afternoon, Jasmine met Mr. Thomas again. She showed him the council’s approval letter, tears glistening behind her glasses.

He smiled proudly. “You see? The surface changes not just by fighting but by planting seeds.”

Jasmine leaned forward. “I couldn’t do this without you.”

He chuckled, “And I couldn’t do it without you.”

The city continued to buzz outside, its layers of stories and struggles ever intertwining. Beneath the surface, real change was stirring—born from shared pain, resilience, and the courage to speak.

And whatever storms lay ahead, Jasmine was ready to face them, knowing she wasn’t alone. The emotional stories, once whispered in shadows, now roared in the light.
