Unseen Chains: Short Fiction Exploring Racism and Its Impact


Eli Martin woke to the soft hum of the city outside his window, a sound both familiar and intrusive. The early morning sun cast long shadows across his modest apartment in the heart of Washington, D.C., and as he stretched, a familiar heaviness settled in his chest. It wasn’t from lack of sleep or any physical ailment — it was the invisible burden that had clung to him ever since childhood. Today, like many days before it, the unseen chains of racism tugged insistently at his soul.

At 28, Eli was a software developer, one of the top programmers at his startup. Yet, his success did not shield him from the virulent undercurrents of prejudice embedded in everyday life. As he prepared his breakfast, his mind revisited the meeting from yesterday — a colleague subtly dismissing his ideas, a manager who overlooked him in favor of others with less experience, and the whispered conversations that abruptly stopped when he entered the room.

Eli sighed and grabbed his coat. The city awaited, indifferent to his inner struggles. He locked the door behind him and stepped into a world where the color of his skin was a silent sentence.

The subway was crowded, as usual. Eli positioned himself near the door, headphones in place, music drowning the cacophony of voices. Amidst the throng, his mind drifted back years ago to his childhood neighborhood in Southeast D.C., a place marked by neglect but rich in community spirit. It was the same neighborhood etched with stories of injustice, systemic barriers, and unfulfilled promises.

His mother, Loretta, had been a warrior in her own right. A schoolteacher with a fierce dedication to her students, she carried the weight of teaching in underfunded schools, battling stereotypes while nurturing hope. Eli remembered her late-night talks, the way she tried to prepare him for a world that didn’t always see him as a person but as a statistic.

“You’ll have to be twice as good just to be considered,” she once said, her eyes steady yet sorrowful. “But remember, your worth isn’t defined by how others see you.”

These words became Eli’s armor, even as the world chipped away at his confidence.

At the office, Eli sat behind his desk, headphones off, ready to immerse himself in code. His project involved developing an app designed to connect marginalized communities with resources like job training and mental health services. It was personal; he had seen firsthand how systemic racism made access to such support uneven, creating a vicious cycle.

Yet, as he presented a new feature to the team, the subtle tensions surfaced again. Marissa, his project manager, nodded politely but ignored his suggestions when speaking with upper management. Tom, a senior developer, made a joke about “those communities not really being tech-savvy,” a comment that hung heavy in the air but went unchallenged.

Eli clenched his fists but held his tongue. Speaking up could mark him as difficult, a label often unfairly applied to Black professionals advocating for themselves. The invisible chains tightened, constricting his voice.

That evening, Eli attended a community meeting in his old neighborhood. The room was filled with familiar faces: activists, educators, and residents united by a desire to disrupt the cycle of inequality. The topic was policing and racial profiling — a matter painfully close to home.

As the discussion unfolded, stories poured out: young men stopped without cause, families torn apart by arrests, dreams deferred by suspicion rather than support. Each narrative emphasized the same point — racism was not a relic of the past but a living, breathing force shaping lives daily.

A woman named Jasmine shared how her brother had been wrongfully accused, beaten, and branded a criminal despite innocence. Her voice cracked with pain, but her resolve was clear. The room listened, absorbing the harsh realities often ignored by mainstream society.

Eli felt a mix of anger and solidarity. These were the unseen chains — not of metal, but of assumptions, systemic bias, and inherited wounds. Breaking free required more than hope; it demanded collective action and courage.

Back home, Eli reflected on the day’s events. His mother’s words echoed anew with greater depth. Changing the world wasn’t just about personal success; it was about dismantling structures that perpetuate inequality. But how?

He opened his laptop and began typing a blog post, pouring out his experiences with microaggressions, bias in tech, and community struggles. He wrote about the invisible chains of racism that bind so many, his story a thread in a broader tapestry demanding recognition.

Publishing the post, Eli felt a flicker of empowerment. Awareness was the first step, but change required persistence. Tomorrow, he’d continue coding his app, continue advocating, and continue being unafraid to reveal the unseen chains — so that, one day, freedom might be more than a distant hope.

Unseen Chains is a story of resilience in the face of systemic racism — a reflection of lives shaped and constrained by invisible forces. It sheds light on how discrimination permeates even the most personal spaces, and how courage and community can forge paths toward justice. Through Eli’s journey, we witness the complex interplay between individual aspiration and the social barriers that obstruct. This narrative beckons a collective awakening, inviting readers to recognize the invisible chains and work together to break them.
