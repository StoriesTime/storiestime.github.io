The Trolley Problem: Ethics on the Edge of a Decision


Dr. Rachel Kim stared out the window of the train, her eyes fixed on the tracks ahead. She was on her way to a critical conference in philosophy, where she would be debating the ethics of utilitarianism against deontology. The debate was stormy, with philosophers on both sides passionately arguing for their stance. Yet, as she watched the tracks unfold before her, Rachel began to think of a familiar thought experiment that would challenge her very foundations.
The train, old and rickety, was carrying a cargo of passengers to a remote station. Rachel had requested to be on this train for its notorious "zig-zag route," where it traversed a treacherous mountainous terrain. As the sun set ahead, casting a golden glow on the landscape, the train hurtled down a winding route. Suddenly, a loud screech shook the carriage, the diesel engines sputtering as they caught. Rachel felt her heart sinking. The conductor's voice came over the intercom, pale and urgent.
"Ladies and gentlemen, we have experienced a mechanical failure. Our steering wheel is stuck, making it impossible for us to steer the vehicle safely through the next turn." He hesitated, his voice weighted. "I'm afraid we're in a situation, which will require a difficult decision. We can either take this track, which goes straight ahead. The stationary points near the train track near that bridge, there are about 10-15 invisible workers, asleep, in sheltered overhangs. Alternatively, we can change direction on this slippery track…the right hand overpass bridge, avoiding disaster, but killing at least 200 close by if our train's wheels drift here." Rachel could only listen, lost for words, the time seeming to stretch like rubber.
Instinctively, she knew she would prioritize human life. After all, this was her "plus addition," the shiny armor in the face of sheer chaos. There was only "life itself."
