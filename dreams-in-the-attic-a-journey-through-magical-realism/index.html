Dreams in the Attic: A Journey Through Magical Realism


The attic of the Ketterly house had long been a place of whispered stories and cobwebbed memories. It was a domain of dust and forgotten relics, where sunlight filtered through a cracked, circular window and painted the room with shifting patterns of gold. Three generations of Ketterlys had used the space as storage, piling battered suitcases, half-empty trunks, and antique furniture under its sloped ceiling. Yet, no one in the family spoke of the strange sensation that overcame them when they ventured too close to its solitary door at the top of the creaking stairs.

To twelve-year-old Eleanor Ketterly, the attic was forbidden territory. Her grandmother, stern and sharp-eyed, had warned her repeatedly. “Some doors,” Gran would say, “are best left unopened.” But Eleanor wasn’t easily deterred. She had read too many books of adventure and mystery to let a cryptic remark stop her. Besides, the attic practically called to her. It tugged at the edge of her thoughts during the day, and at night, her dreams felt infused with its peculiar essence—a sense of longing and an invitation she couldn’t quite comprehend.

One fateful afternoon, while Gran napped in her chair and the house lay still, Eleanor decided to sneak upstairs. Her heart raced as she tiptoed down the hallway, her bare feet silent against the worn floorboards. The attic door loomed at the end, its brass handle tarnished with age. Taking a deep breath, she turned the knob and stepped inside.

The air in the attic felt thick, yet strangely alive. Dust motes hung suspended in the beams of late-afternoon light, and an unexplainable warmth wrapped around her like the weight of an invisible hand. Eleanor’s gaze settled on an ornate wooden trunk at the back of the room, its surface inscribed with intricate carvings of crescent moons, stars, and swirling vines. It stood out against the neglected clutter surrounding it, as though untouched by time.

Something inside her whispered: Open it.

As her fingers brushed the trunk’s edge, the carvings shimmered faintly, and she felt a sudden jolt, like electricity traveling up her arm. The latch clicked open on its own. Eleanor knelt and slowly lifted the lid.

Inside was an assortment of peculiar objects: a pocket watch with no hands, a quill that seemed to pulse with a soft blue glow, and a tattered book that looked as though it had survived centuries of storms. But the object that captivated Eleanor was a small, silver key resting atop a velvet cushion. Its design mirrored the etchings on the trunk, and its surface felt unnaturally cold as she picked it up.

The attic changed in an instant.

The room tilted, and a sensation of falling—not physically, but through layers of reality—washed over her. She squeezed the key tightly as her surroundings dissolved into a kaleidoscope of colors and shapes. When her vision cleared, she found herself standing in a vast meadow, the grass impossibly green and dotted with flowers that seemed to hum softly, each emitting a unique shimmering light. Overhead, two suns bathed the landscape in hues of amber and rose, their radiance a strange comfort.

Eleanor swallowed hard, unsure whether to be awed or terrified. She glanced at the silver key in her hand, now glowing faintly, and understood at once that it was more than a simple trinket. This was the key to a door—or perhaps many doors—hidden in plain sight.

A voice startled her from her thoughts. “New to the Riverlands, are you?”

Eleanor spun around to see a man-like figure, though he seemed more shadow than substance. His features shifted subtly as he spoke, as if he were a reflection in rippling water. He wore a hat with a wide brim and carried a walking stick adorned with feathers and bells.

“I—I didn’t mean to come here,” Eleanor stammered, unsure of what she was admitting.

The figure tilted its head in amusement. “Few ever do. But if you’ve come through the Dreamer’s Door, then you’re part of its story now.”

“Dreamer’s Door?” she repeated, clutching the key tighter.

“It’s what lies between here and there,” the figure explained, gesturing in every direction at once. “This realm and the one you call ‘home.’ Most pass through in their sleep, none the wiser. But you,” he added, eyeing her key, “well, you’ve found a way to linger.”

“Is that… bad?” Eleanor asked hesitantly.

“No, child, not bad,” the figure replied with a faint chuckle. “It all depends on what you do while you’re here. And whether you choose to leave.”

Eleanor wasn’t sure what he meant by that, but she wasn’t given much time to think. The shadowy figure extended his walking stick, pointing toward the horizon. Strange, towering shapes moved in the distance, their forms too fluid to discern clearly.

“The Meadowfolk are coming,” he said. “They’ll have questions for you. A Dreamer hasn’t walked among them in many moons.”

“Dreamer?” Eleanor asked, her voice quivering. But the figure was already fading away, as though melting into the air itself.

Left alone, Eleanor turned her gaze to the horizon. Curiosity tugged at her again, the same pull that had drawn her to the attic in the first place. The Meadowfolk, whatever they were, would have answers—answers about this strange realm, the glowing key, and perhaps even how to return home. That pull of discovery outweighed her fear, and she began to walk toward the shapes in the distance.

As she ventured deeper into the meadow, the flowers seemed to respond to her presence, their hums forming melodic patterns that matched the rhythm of her steps. The grass, soft and springy under her feet, released a faint, calming fragrance. Yet, for all its beauty, the landscape held an undercurrent of tension, as though the world itself watched her with bated breath.

The Meadowfolk were unlike any beings Eleanor had seen—or imagined. Their forms shifted like weather patterns, part solid, part mist. Their voices overlapped as they approached, creating a symphony of whispers and murmurs.

“A Dreamer walks among us,” they sang, their tones melodic but edged with caution. “What does she seek?”

Eleanor swallowed her nerves. “I… I don’t know. I found this key,” she said, holding it up. “And it brought me here. I just want to understand.”

The Meadowfolk hesitated, their shifting forms intertwining as though in silent council. Then one stepped forward—or rather, emerged slightly more defined than the rest. Its “face,” if it could be called that, flickered with colors that matched the flowers around her.

“The Dreamer’s Door grants passage, but not understanding,” it said. “The key you hold is a shard of possibility, tethered to truths long forgotten. To wield it is to shape the story—but beware. Stories have lives of their own.”

Before Eleanor could respond, the Meadowfolk began to dissolve, their forms scattering like fog in the wind. The meadow shifted with them, and Eleanor felt herself being pulled again—not falling this time, but rising, the environment folding around her as though she were the center of some great, unseen design.

When she opened her eyes, she was back in the attic.

The trunk was gone. Everything in the room appeared as dusty and mundane as before, yet the silver key still rested in her palm, its surface cool and shimmering faintly. Eleanor’s heart raced as she glanced around, searching for some evidence of her journey—something to prove it wasn’t just her imagination. But only silence greeted her.

She lingered in the attic for a moment longer before retreating downstairs. Gran was awake now, her sharp eyes narrowing as she saw Eleanor come into the room.

“You were in the attic, weren’t you?” Gran asked, her tone unreadable.

“What is it?” Eleanor blurted. “What’s up there?”

Gran regarded her in silence, then sighed, the weight of many years softening her expression. “It’s not the attic you need to be wary of,” she said at last. “It’s the doors you open and the stories you step into.”

Eleanor’s fingers tightened around the key in her pocket. She didn’t know what lay ahead, but one thing was certain—her journey was far from over.
