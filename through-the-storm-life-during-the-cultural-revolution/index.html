Through the Storm: Life During the Cultural Revolution


Jinli’s earliest memories were crowded with shouting. She was only eight in 1966, but she knew the sound of revolution—heavy slogans painted in red across the school gates, furious voices echoing down corridors, the shuffle of feet before dawn as neighbors were pulled from their homes. In the little flat she shared with her father on Baoshun Road, Jinli learned to shrink herself smaller as the world outside grew louder and harder.

It began on a wet morning in June when father packed up his books, tied them in a blue cloth, and hid them behind the wardrobe. He looked tired, an unfamiliar stubble shadowing his jaw. Jinli watched as he pressed his fingers to a faded photograph of mother, lost the year before to illness.

She wanted to ask what was happening, why so many things—books, photographs, the heavy radio—were being tucked away, almost as though they might bite. But when father caught her watching, he only pressed a warning finger to his lips. For a long time, Jinli held her questions in silence.

At school, everything had changed overnight. Her teacher, Mrs. Tang, no longer stood at the front. Instead, a teenager wearing a red armband and an arrogant grin yelled quotations from the Chairman’s little book. Jinli despised how he treated her classmates, how he tore the old posters from the walls and demanded that even the smallest children shout revolutionary slogans.

Where there had been lessons on algebra and poems about riverbanks, now there was only a single story:

“Down with the Four Olds! Destroy old ideas, old culture, old customs, old habits! Long live the revolution!”

Jinli’s best friend, Min, used to draw horses and rivers in the backs of exercise books—until someone caught her. Now Min kept her head low, and barely spoke above a whisper. The chalkboard remained filled with angry scrawls, and laughter was a rare visitor in their classroom.

Fall of the Iron Gate

Autumn arrived, bringing the chill of rumors. Jinli’s father, who had worked as an accountant, was “struggled” in the factory yard—forced to stand with a cardboard placard, accused of bourgeois tendencies for correcting a younger comrade’s calculation. Jinli saw him cry for the first time as his co-workers jeered. She wanted to rush to him, to wrap her arms around his trembling body, but Min’s mother caught her arm.

“You must be careful, Jinli. Even the walls have ears.”

That night, father’s face was pale and bruised. He sat at the table, staring at nothing, turning the battered radio dial with shaking hands. The broadcasts seemed endless—praising the Party, denouncing traitors, demanding loyalty. Every night, Jinli dreamed of red banners and footsteps in the stairwell.

Winter closed in early, and so did hunger. Grain rations shrank, and sometimes supper was nothing more than dry buns and cabbage boiled with too much water. Despite the cold, the streets buzzed with Red Guards marching, burning stacks of books. Jinli found herself torn between fear and fascination. At school, she was pressed to join the Young Pioneers, but she barely knew how to tie the scarf.

One day, she wandered past the old temple—now half-destroyed, its stone lions beheaded. An old man swept the courtyard, humming a melody that Jinli’s own grandmother had once soothed her with. Jinli lingered at the gate.

“Do you remember the old days?” she asked.

He put down his broom and studied her. “Child, the old days are gone. All that’s left are memories, neat as dried leaves. Be careful not to step on them.”

Jinli wondered if she would remember this place in the years to come, whether any song from before could survive the storm outside.

The Black Bulletin Board

Jinli’s world shrank to the apartment, the alleyway, and the black bulletin board posted outside the Party office. Each day, new names appeared: “Class Enemy,” “Counter-Revolutionary,” “Rightist.” She saw her own uncle’s name listed once and ran all the way home, heart pounding.

It was only then that she realized her father was no longer at the table.

“He’s been sent,” whispered Min’s mother. “Re-education.”

Jinli’s skin burned with shame and confusion. She didn’t know what her father was supposed to learn, or where he had gone. He reappeared once, thin as a reed, to leave her a single note folded in a blue envelope:

“My dearest Jinli,
Keep your chin up. Say little. Stay safe.
Love,
Father”

She pressed the note between the pages of her only book—a volume of Lu Xun stories her mother had hidden. She read the brittle pages under her blanket long after sunset, her finger tracing the ink even after the words blurred with tears.

New Year, New Scars

The city dressed itself with paper flags, but only the wind seemed to celebrate. On New Year’s Eve, Min came over, her hair cut uneven. Her older brother had joined the Red Guards, leaving their family in a suspended state of unease.

That night, Jinli and Min wrapped themselves in heavy quilts and whispered stories beneath the flickering lamp. They made promises:

To meet again, no matter what.

To remember one another in whatever comes.

Just after midnight, raucous shouting battered the silence. Through the glass, Jinli saw the mob outside, dragging a neighbor—Mr. Liu—into the mud.

“He taught me arithmetic,” Min said quietly.

Jinli squeezed her hand. Their bond was one lasting thing, something unbroken as the world outside tilted and broke.

The Longest Spring

When the first plum blossoms opened, rumors of “sent-down youth” grew louder. Older children from Jinli’s school vanished, sent to the countryside “to learn from the peasants.” Many never returned.

Min’s brother wrote her once about rice paddies so endless he could barely remember Shanghai. He described mildewed sheets and endless labor, confessed to missing hot water and clean socks—but never mentioned regret. His letter finished with the official phrase:
“Wishing that you serve the people with a red heart.”

Jinli wondered whether, when her time came, she would have anything to write home about.

In those months, the city tried to remember itself. A few hidden tea shops reopened, cautiously. The old men gathered to play chess in the alley, their eyes wary, their jokes muted. Jinli learned to fetch water, patch trousers, cook a thin porridge.

Her father returned that summer, his hair bleached white, his hands forever trembling. He never spoke of the farm, or recalled his lectures at the Party Office. Instead, he tended the window-box flowers, coaxing them to bloom even as faded newspapers lined the floor.

Jinli brought him bowls of rice and listened. Sometimes, he told her about the stars—their ancient names, stories lost in the revolution’s noise. One night, beneath a blackout window, he pointed to the sky.

“Some things last,” he said quietly. “Even through storms.”

The Slow Return

The temperatures rose year by year, and eventually, the tides turned. The slogans on the walls faded, peeled by rain and time. New leaders whispered promises of change, and one by one, old classmates drifted back. Some returned physically, thin and strong from labor in fields; some only in stories, lost to the countryside.

Min came home in the final summer, carrying a basket of peaches and the scent of faraway rivers. Her hair was longer, her eyes sadder. She and Jinli sat in the window, legs dangling, sharing the fruit and memories sharp as the pit of a plum.

Across the alley, the bulletin board stood empty. The red paint lay blanched by the sun.

“I dreamed of this,” Min said, peeling the last peach. “A time when we could just…talk.”

Jinli thought about all she’d lost and all she’d learned. The world moved forward, unevenly, but like the window-box flowers her father coaxed from bare stems, something persistent grew in her.

Remnants

Decades later, Jinli still walked Baoshun Road. The city was filled with glass buildings and new slogans painted in neon lights. She often paused by the courtyard where the old temple stood—now rebuilt, stone lions watching once again.

Sometimes, she spotted a young woman in red armbands leading a tour, echoing slogans Jinli could recite in her sleep. But the violence was gone, replaced by stories. People remembered in hushed voices, careful now with their memories.

Her father’s hands had long since grown still, but his window-boxes flowered every spring. Min ran a tea shop; sometimes they sat together, two women born of storms, holding quiet testimony to a time when laughter and reading and hope itself had seemed like dangerous acts.

One day, Jinli showed her granddaughter the old note in blue paper.

“He loved me fiercely,” she whispered. “Through the storm, he taught me that kindness was a form of rebellion. That we survive not because history is kind, but because we carry each other through.”

The child nodded solemnly and, as dusk blurred the city’s new skyline, Jinli decided that this too—simple conversation, remembering—was its own kind of revolution.

As Jinli and Min faded into the rhythms of new times, their story lingered in the plum blossoms, in the rebuilt temple, and in the daily rituals of survival and gentleness they passed along. Through the chaos, through the silence, through the storm, life had persisted—not unscarred, but unbroken.
