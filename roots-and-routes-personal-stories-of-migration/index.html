Roots and Routes: Personal Stories of Migration


The sun was dipping low over the horizon as Amina stood on the porch of her childhood home in Lusaka, Zambia, her fingers tracing the grain of the weathered wooden railing. The air around her was thick with the scents of rain-soaked earth and blooming jacarandas, but her mind caught in the tangled memories of the past two decades. Migration had reshaped her life like a river carving new paths in the landscape—a constant movement between roots and routes.

Her story, like so many others, was stitched with complexities of departure and arrival, loss and gain, belonging and alienation.

The Ties That Bind: Leaving Home

Amina was born into a close-knit family in Lusaka. Her parents, teachers at a local primary school, instilled in her a love for learning and a fierce pride in her heritage. Yet, from a young age, she felt the pull of the world beyond—the stories her father shared of his travels across southern Africa, the vivid tales her mother told of a distant grandparent who had crossed continents during a time of turmoil.

When Amina won a scholarship to study environmental science at a university in Cape Town, it felt like a triumph. But with the suitcase packed, she also carried a silent ache for home. Saying goodbye was a knot of conflicting emotions—hope tangled with fear. Would she find ways to stay connected to Lusaka’s vibrant community? Could she forge new roots while honoring the ones she’d planted?

New Soil, New Challenges

Cape Town was mesmerizing and overwhelming. The Table Mountain watching silently over sprawling urban life, the diverse patches of township and suburbs, the shifting tides of language and culture—all of it was a world apart.

Amina quickly realized that migration wasn’t just a physical shift, but an internal journey. It was about redefining identity in a landscape that didn’t always recognize the full contours of hers. She wrestled with homesickness, the weight of expectations from family back home, and the challenge of making new friendships in environments shaped by histories she’d only begun to understand.

She found refuge in small communities—students and migrants from all over Africa, each weaving their stories into a shared fabric. Together, they navigated the thorny paths of belonging, reminding one another that roots could stretch further than soil alone.

The Echoes of Ancestors

Amina’s fascination with environmental science wasn’t accidental. It connected to her desire to understand how landscapes hold memories, how migration shapes ecosystems and human lives alike.

As she delved deeper into her studies, she uncovered stories of ancient migrations, forced and voluntary, that transformed southern Africa’s peoples and places. The routes of cattle herders, traders, and labor migrants left echoes in the languages, foods, and customs she grew up with. She realized her own journey was part of a much bigger narrative—one that stretched beyond borders, charting a collective story of resilience and adaptation.

A New Chapter Abroad

After graduation, Amina received an offer for a research position in Germany. The prospect was thrilling but daunting—another leap into the unknown. She agonized over leaving behind the community she’d come to cherish in Cape Town and the family in Lusaka who longed for her presence.

In Berlin, she navigated a different cultural landscape: cold winters, a language foreign to her tongue, and nuanced social dynamics. But the city’s cosmopolitan pulse brought new opportunities. She joined migrant networks, volunteered in cultural exchange programs, and gradually carved out a space where her African identity and her new European experiences could coalesce.

In moments of quiet, she journaled her reflections—how roots and routes weren’t binaries, but intertwined currents shaping who she was becoming.

The Return Home, Transformed

Years later, Amina returned to Lusaka for a visit—no longer a wide-eyed student, but a woman with stories etched into her being. She was greeted with hugs and tears, and a familiar landscape that somehow felt both the same and utterly changed.

In the marketplace, she noticed new faces and voices, many echoing her own journey. The city had become a hub of migration itself—people arriving from rural areas, neighboring countries, even further afield. They brought languages, cuisines, and dreams, weaving Lusaka into an ever-evolving tapestry.

Amina gave talks at her old school, sharing her experiences and encouraging young people to embrace the possibilities migration could offer, while staying grounded in their roots.

Intertwining Paths: Stories of Others

Her experiences were mirrored in the stories of friends and colleagues:

  • Samuel, originally from Malawi, had journeyed to Namibia to escape economic hardship. He spoke of the bittersweet nature of migration—how each route taken brought new skills and perspectives, but sometimes fractured families.

  • Fatima, a Somali refugee in South Africa, shared her story of fleeing conflict and rebuilding life in a context that was often hostile yet also full of unexpected kindness.

  • Diego and Maria, migrants from Latin America in Spain, recounted the challenges of navigating bureaucracy and cultural alienation, and the resilience that grew from building communities across continents.

Each narrative highlighted migration’s multifaceted impact—not just on destinations or origins, but on identity, memory, and belonging.

The Roots That Grow Along Routes

Amina’s journey illustrated how roots and routes were not competing forces, but deeply linked threads in the tapestry of migration.

  • Roots represented the cultural heritage, family bonds, and landscapes that anchor people emotionally and spiritually.

  • Routes encompassed the journeys, transitions, and transformations individuals undertake across time and space.

Together, they create fluid identities that enrich societies and expand notions of home.

The Ongoing Journey

Now, as Amina looked toward the future, she envisioned a world more connected yet mindful of diversity, where migration was appreciated as a fundamental human experience rather than a problem to be solved.

She continued her research, focusing on sustainable migration policies and community empowerment. Her life was a testament to the power of embracing both where we come from and where we choose to go.

In the quiet moments on that Lusaka porch, watching the jacarandas bloom, she understood migration as a story still unfolding—one she was proud to live and share.

Roots and routes intertwine, creating pathways of connection, adaptation, and hope. In personal stories like Amina’s, the human heart reveals its enduring capacity to navigate change without losing the essence of home.
