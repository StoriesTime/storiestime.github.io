What Would You Do? A Story of Ethical Dilemmas and the Consequences


For years, the scent of floor polish and old paper brought back the moment every time. Peter Crane, now forty-four, still avoided the east wing of the public library. He had lived a life of quiet, mostly measured decisions—until that evening when an ethical test had rearranged the straight lines of his world.

In those days, Peter had been a newly minted reference librarian, eager to impress but mostly convinced his job was invisible. He clocked in, shelved books, found citations for university students, and kept a close eye on the archive room, where sunlight never reached and every echo sounded like a secret.

On that particular December Thursday, the city had been bracing itself for a blizzard. The news anchor harped about road closures; families hurried home clutching groceries; and the library emptied steadily until, by dusk, it was nearly silent. Peter preferred working alone—less small talk, more time to perfect the Dewey Decimal System’s near-religious order.

He was about to shut down his station for the night when a woman in a red parka approached the desk. Her face was pinched with worry; her eyes kept flicking to the large snowflakes gathering outside.

“Excuse me,” she said. “Could you help me find these articles? They’re for my son. He has a project and I’m—well, I should have come sooner.” She paused, her breath visible. “We don’t have the internet at home.”

She handed over a crumpled list. Peter glanced at the clock—ten minutes before closing—and felt a surge of impatience. But he nodded. “Sure. Give me a few minutes.”

As he scanned the list, tracing the titles—ancient history, some obscure poetry, a scientific article—he realized most of it could be gathered quickly. He darted between stacks, collected books, and returned to the counter.

She thanked him, then hesitated. “I’m sorry… I know you’re closing, but is there any way to just… make copies?” Her voice faltered. “The bus won’t run later, and we really need this tonight.”

The copy machine had a protocol: no copies after hours, to avoid overtime abuse. Peter hesitated. The right thing, by the rules, was to decline. Still, the desperation in her face was familiar somehow—a reflection of every anxious parent, every last-minute scramble.

“I’ll do it,” he said, and within ten minutes, the pages were stacking up beside her, white with black ink and fresh possibilities.

She thanked him again, her eyes brighter, and vanished into the storm.

Peter thought little of this until the following Monday, when his supervisor, Mr. Gardiner, summoned him.

“Peter, I need to talk to you about Thursday.” Gardiner’s voice was a low rumble, never urgent but always foreboding. “There’s a problem with the machine logs. It looks like someone was here late.”

Peter considered lying. He envisioned himself shrugging, feigning ignorance. But the woman’s gratitude flickered in his mind, bright and real.

“It was me.” He spoke before he could stop himself. “A woman needed help for her son. I let her make the copies.”

Mr. Gardiner sighed. “You know we have rules for a reason.” He looked weary, as if he had gone through variations of this speech a thousand times.

“I know,” Peter said.

“We’ll have to write you up,” Gardiner replied. “I’d hate to, but fairness matters. If I give you a pass, I have to give everyone one.”

Peter nodded and left the office, a dull sense of shame settling over him.

Two

Time moved on, as it does. The clouds cleared, the semester changed, and one blizzard yielded to a wet, early spring.

The yellow notepad, marked up with copies discipline, lived in his personnel file—a footnote to an otherwise unremarkable tenure. But the moment had shifted something inside Peter. He began to see the cracks in every system: the way guidelines, meant to be impartial, sometimes bent towards injustice; the moments where strict adherence closed off help to those who needed it most.

One evening, Peter found himself shelving books near the Reading Room. Mr. Gardiner approached with a stack of returns and an envelope. Gardiner’s face, as always, gave nothing away.

“A letter came for you,” he said, handing it over.

Peter frowned. Mail, if it came, usually went to his apartment. He opened the envelope, fumbled out a crumpled sheet of lined paper.

“Dear Mr. Crane,” it began. “You probably don’t remember me. I came to the library on the night of the blizzard. You helped me copy articles for my son. He got an A on his project. He’s always struggled with school, but he was so proud this time. I just wanted to thank you.”

At the bottom, a shaky signature: “Elena Martinez (and Javier).”

Peter folded the note. For a moment, he allowed himself a small smile.

Ethical dilemmas, he reflected, didn’t always scream their arrival. Sometimes they tiptoed in, disguised as minor questions, inviting you to look at the world sideways.

Three

Years clicked by like clock hands. Peter grew older, more grey at the temples, and the city changed around him.

Libraries, once bustling, struggled to hold their place in a world enamored by screens and instant answers. Funding was always tight; policies became stricter in an effort to squeeze more help out of fewer resources.

By now, Peter was head of reference. He watched his younger colleagues, fresh-faced and earnest, wrestle their own moments of decision between pages and pixels.

On a wet November, a new test arrived.

A volunteer, fourteen-year-old Malik, was stacking books when Peter spotted him by the donation bin, frowning at a battered, leather-bound volume. When Peter approached, Malik looked up, guilt flickering across his face.

“I saw this in the bin,” Malik stammered. “I think it’s valuable. Can I take it home?”

Library policy stated donations belonged to the city—the rare, valuable or interesting went up for auction, raising much-needed funds. Peter’s first thought was automatic: rules are rules. Then he caught himself.

A cascade of memories: Elena’s desperate eyes; that fleeting letter; the cold, practical justice that gave no quarter to kindness.

He motioned Malik to sit, and together, they pulled up the book’s title and history. The volume—an old family Bible, inscribed with names and dates—held no commercial value, but clearly belonged to a family long gone from the city.

“What would you do?” Peter asked Malik quietly. “You want to keep it?”

Malik hesitated. “It… it’s not really mine. It just looked important.”

Peter nodded. “What if instead of following the letter of the policy, we used our heads? Let’s check if there’s a family with that name on record. Maybe they want it back.”

Malik grinned, relieved. For the next hour, the two worked together, tracking down a distant cousin in another state, who replied the next day—in tears, overjoyed to reclaim the heirloom.

Peter signed off on the shipment himself, with only a brief entry in the library’s donation log.

That evening, he mulled over the quiet consequences of each small act. Year after year, the consequences aggregated—a life measured out in cups of coffee, reference requests, and those few, vital moments when rules met reality.

Four

It was June, heavy with rain and perfume from green things growing wild along the city’s crumbling riverbanks. A local councilman arrived, all starched shirt and quick smiles, for the library’s “Friends of the Community” event.

Peter hated these functions—the awkward speeches, the forced laughter—but attended dutifully, program in hand.

After the usual formalities, the councilman took to the mic. He stood tall, confidence radiating from every syllable.

“Ladies and gentlemen. Tonight, we thank those who have not only kept knowledge alive, but have also demonstrated true empathy and wisdom in service.” He gestured at Peter. “Mr. Crane, would you join me?”

Peter, blindsided, walked to the stage amid restrained applause.

“We hear stories, sometimes, of everyday choices making all the difference,” the councilman began, reading from a note. “Of a child empowered by a late-night exception, of a family’s history returned, of compassion guiding the hand when rules might be too rigid to allow it.”

He handed Peter a small plaque, the inscription plain: “For Integrity and Kindness in Stewardship.”

Peter stood silently, the weight of a dozen quiet acts pressing in. The applause washed over him—friendly, but impersonal.

Afterward, drinks in hand, a local reporter approached. “Didn’t you get written up once?” she asked, half-grinning. “Seems odd, getting an award for not following protocol.”

Peter tilted his head, considering. “Sometimes, you have to break a rule to keep a promise,” he replied. “Or, at least, to keep your own sense of who you are.”

She scribbled this down, nodding.

Five

Decades later, Peter retired. The city’s life ebbed on—aggressive, hopeful, still messy at the edges. The library digitized its catalogue. Old policy manuals faded into outmoded files. New dilemmas replaced the old: digital privacy, access in a networked world, the ethics of algorithmic recommendations.

His daughter, visiting one afternoon, found him poring over a stack of old journals. She caught him, smiling, and said, “Dad, you’re such a stickler for the rules at home. But you sure bent them at work.”

Peter considered her. “I bent them when the rules got in the way of helping people. The point is to know when to make the exception. And to be willing to bear the consequences when you do.”

She rolled her eyes, but hugged him tightly.

When news of his death came, it was a modest affair—an item in the local paper, a gathering of old colleagues and students, a framed photo tucked behind the service desk. The young librarians remembered him not for any singular act, but for a legacy strung together from a thousand small, ethical choices: moments when the duty to integrity outbalanced duty to policy, when empathy tipped the scales.

And in the library quiet, under the hum of old lights and the shuffling of pages, the question lingered.

If given that choice—risk trouble to do what’s right for another—what would you do?

Epilogue

Elena’s son, Javier—now a middle-aged teacher himself—sometimes brought his own students to the library. On rainy afternoons, he’d point to the place by the circulation desk and say:

“That’s where I first realized someone outside my family could care enough to break the rules. It made all the difference in the world.”

He’d smile, watching the faces of the next generation, and hope that one day, when the moment came for them, they’d be willing to decide not only with their heads, but with their hearts as well.

Life’s ethical dilemmas rarely come labeled, and the consequences are rarely obvious at the time. Most are just books to be shelved, forms to be stamped, a quick copy made as the snow falls outside. But each one builds the story of who you become—a story written, not just in what you obey, but in what you can’t ignore.
