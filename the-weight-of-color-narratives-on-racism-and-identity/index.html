The Weight of Color: Narratives on Racism and Identity


Amara sat by the window, watching the world outside blur by. The city, always bustling, seemed to echo the rhythm of her own thoughts—hastily moving forward yet never quite settling. She had never quite fit into the neighborhoods that dotted her childhood landscape, though she had lived in them all her life. Her skin, dark and rich like the fertile earth, contrasted with the lighter tones of her classmates, neighbors, and even her teachers. It was a constant reminder that she belonged to a history and a culture that was not easily understood by the people around her.

As a child, Amara learned early on that the world often defined her by her skin, and not her thoughts, dreams, or achievements. Her mother, a gentle woman of grace and dignity, had taught her that the color of her skin was not something to be ashamed of, but it was hard to ignore the weight it carried.

At school, her classmates would often ask about her “heritage,” curiosity laced with an uncomfortable tinge of ignorance. They didn’t mean harm, but the questions always felt intrusive, as though her identity were a puzzle for them to solve. She would answer them patiently, yet always with the feeling that something was missing. How could she explain what it felt like to be both present and invisible at the same time?

Her best friend, Sarah, had always been the opposite. Sarah was pale, with bright red hair and freckles that dotted her fair skin. They had been inseparable since childhood, yet even their closeness couldn’t shield Amara from the world’s gaze. When they walked together, Amara could see the glances—the quick, subtle stares from strangers who weighed the difference between them.

“Does it ever bother you?” Sarah asked once, her brow furrowed with concern. “How people look at you differently?”

Amara had smiled then, a smile that didn’t quite reach her eyes. “I’ve gotten used to it,” she had said. But that wasn’t entirely true. She hadn’t gotten used to it. She had merely learned to live with it, to move through the world as though she were both a part of it and somehow apart from it.

Chapter 2: The Weight of Words

The first time Amara realized the weight of words was in high school. It was during a history class, one of those lessons that all students seem to remember for one reason or another. The teacher, Mr. Harris, was explaining the civil rights movement, and Amara could feel her heart rate quicken as he spoke. She had heard this story a thousand times before, but each retelling felt more personal. The stories of Rosa Parks, Martin Luther King Jr., Malcolm X—these were not just historical figures to her. They were part of her family’s legacy, part of the fabric of her identity. But as Mr. Harris spoke, he began to focus on the progress that had been made.

“We’ve come a long way,” he said with a certain satisfaction. “Look at how much things have changed. Today, it’s not about color anymore. People of all races are equal.”

The words hung in the air, almost like a trap. Amara’s stomach tightened. She knew better than to respond, but the quiet fury inside her was growing. Her hand shot up.

“Excuse me, Mr. Harris,” she began, her voice steady despite the rush of emotions inside her. “What do you mean by ‘we’ve come a long way’? Can you really say that when so many people still face discrimination based on the color of their skin?”

Mr. Harris blinked, caught off guard by her interruption. “I mean, we’ve made a lot of progress. The laws have changed, right?”

“But the laws don’t change people’s hearts,” Amara replied softly. “You can’t legislate kindness, or understanding. People still look at me differently. They still judge me by the color of my skin, not my abilities or my character. And that’s not just history. That’s today.”

Her classmates shifted uncomfortably, glancing at each other, unsure of how to respond. Sarah, sitting beside her, placed a hand on Amara’s arm, offering silent support. But Mr. Harris just nodded, a patronizing smile creeping onto his face. “Well, Amara, I think you might be overreacting. We’ve made great strides.”

Amara stood up, the chair scraping against the floor as her heart pounded. “It’s not about ‘strides,’ Mr. Harris. It’s about reality. And the reality is that the weight of color is still here, in every look, every word, every assumption. It’s not gone.”

And with that, she left the classroom, the door slamming behind her as her words hung in the silence.

Chapter 3: Identity in a Fragmented World

Amara’s world was a series of fragmented identities. She didn’t just see herself as one thing; she was a mosaic, composed of her African heritage, her family’s history, her education, her experiences, and her relationships. But the world didn’t see her as a whole. The world saw her as her skin, the weight of it pressing down on her every time she stepped out into public spaces. It was like wearing an invisible burden, one that no amount of success or achievement could erase.

At university, Amara found herself in a similar situation. She had been accepted into one of the most prestigious programs in the country, but she felt like a token—a reminder that the institution was “diverse.” In her lectures, professors often highlighted her achievements, but not without a certain tone of surprise. As if, somehow, her success was an anomaly.

“Amara, you’re really brilliant,” one of her professors, Dr. Jensen, had said. “It’s so refreshing to see someone from your background achieving so much.”

The words felt like a compliment, but also an acknowledgment of the strange rarity of her existence. It was as if her intelligence had to be explained, had to be qualified by the color of her skin. She had worked twice as hard to get here, not just for her grades, but to prove to herself and others that her presence in this space was legitimate, not a mistake.

Chapter 4: Reclaiming the Narrative

As Amara approached graduation, she realized something crucial: she had spent so much of her life navigating the world through the lens of others’ perceptions, but it was time to take control of her own narrative. No longer would she let others define her based on preconceived notions of race. She would define herself, based on the fullness of who she was.

The day she graduated, Amara stood tall. Her family was in the crowd, their faces radiant with pride. Her mother’s eyes sparkled with unshed tears as she embraced her daughter.

“You’ve done it,” her mother whispered. “You’ve made it.”

Amara smiled, but this time, there was no hesitation, no lingering doubt. She had made it on her own terms. She had embraced the weight of her color, carried it with grace, and now, she had transcended it.

She had learned that the weight of color was not a burden to carry, but a story to tell. And it was her story. Her identity was not defined by the narrow confines of what others saw, but by the richness of her experiences, her struggles, her victories, and her place in the world. And as she walked across the stage, she knew that her story, one of resilience and pride, was just beginning.
