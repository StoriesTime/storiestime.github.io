From Strangers to Soulmates


The number 17 bus arrived three minutes late that morning, tires hissing against the wet asphalt. Commuters huddled under a crooked shelter, sunlight leaking through the patchwork clouds. Jez placed her foot on the lowest step, glanced up at the driver, and offered the smallest nod before swiping her worn-out pass. Her mind thudded with thoughts of deadlines and emails, her tote bag heavy with unfinished work and half-lost hope.

A sudden flurry of motion behind her made Jez turn slightly. A man in a navy peacoat—tall, a little gaunt, his hair wind-flattened into disarray—stepped up, clutching a faded paperback and glancing around uncertainly. Their eyes met for one half-second and flicked away. She moved toward the middle, found an empty seat, and surrendered herself to the rhythm of the city.

He sat facing her in the row across, visibly uncomfortable, slowly rifling through his coat pockets for his headphones. His fingers brushed the book as if for comfort. Jez caught the title—Cities Invisible—and remembered how much she’d loved it back in university, scribbling in the margins late at night while everyone else partied. Another pang of nostalgia fluttered beneath her nerves.

Traffic lights flickered red, the engine rumbling beneath tired feet. The man’s gaze found the window, then drifted, unwittingly, to her. Jez tried not to stare, but she was already searching his expression: cautious, mildly disheveled, absorbed by something ageless. Her mother called her “incurably curious,” and she supposed it was true. She reached for her phone, but her thumb rested on the locked screen, unmoving.

They exchanged unwilling smiles, the kind you give when you notice you’re not quite alone. The bus lurched through Market Street, city life pressing against the glass like a half-forgotten song.

Jez always said if she ever wrote an autobiography, it would be called Minor Acts of Bravery. She looked up again, impelled by something she didn’t quite understand.

“That’s a good book,” she said softly, nodding toward his lap.

He blinked, surprised she’d spoken. “Yeah, it’s… It’s been keeping me alive lately.” There was a tremor beneath his words—not fear, but the cautious optimism of someone who’d long ago learned to expect disappointment.

For a few stops, their conversation drifted between literature and awkward silences. She found out his name was Elliot, he liked long trains and old maps, and he drank too much peppermint tea. His laugh—after a joke about how even coffee shops now expected customers to be programmers—skipped over the usual surface small talk and made Jez catch her breath.

They parted with a quick nod outside the Sanctuary Centre, where she worked in a third-floor cubicle overlooking a weed-choked alley. But that day didn’t end with waving goodbye. Jez thought of Elliot twice before lunch, four times after, and once right before sleep threatened to erase the entire world.

It became a ritual. Every Wednesday, the 17 bus, a shared smile, and depending on the week, an exchange of book recommendations or half-confessed stories. Jez treasured the liminal time, their conversations peppered with confessions that felt safer in motion than at rest.

Two weeks in, heavy rain battered the city, and Jez found herself sharing her umbrella with Elliot. “You’d think after all these years I’d invest in a decent raincoat,” he admitted, rain carving rivers down his peacoat.

“What’s the point?” she replied. “Getting drenched builds character.”

They laughed, pressed closer, and Jez noticed that her heart was beating somewhere around her throat.

The friendship unfurled quietly. Jez would text Elliot memes at midnight; he sent her photos of sidewalk chalk art and curry recipes. She told him about her mother’s obsession with houseplants, how conversations always revolved around leaf shape and the perfect watering schedule. He confessed his father sent postcards from every new city, but never remembered to address them. They traded vulnerabilities as if swapping favorite records—a mix-tape of flaws and wild hopes.

Spring arrived, gentle and green, the city exhaling after a long, dour winter. On a Wednesday morning, Jez found Elliot hunched over at the bus stop, face gray as gravel.

“Bad news?” she asked, concern needling past her usual reticence.

He glanced up, eyes rimmed red. “My job’s gone. Not… not just mine, the whole team. Restructuring. You know.”

She reached for his hand. The gesture was natural, almost inevitable. His fingers were cold; she squeezed, and neither let go until the bus doors hissed open.

Adversity often tests the seams of a fledgling friendship. But their bond, instead of fraying, tightened. Jez dropped by Elliot’s apartment with takeaway; he taught her to play chess on a battered board, pieces missing, substituted with coins. They hunted for jobs together, combed through CVs, and indulged in long, circuitous conversations about what made them feel alive.

What Jez discovered was that Elliot’s kindness was a quiet language. He remembered birthdays, fixed loose shelves, kept a list of friends’ favorite songs on his phone. When her bike chain snapped, he spent an hour fixing it on the curb, hands black with grease.

Jez, for her part, introduced Elliot to karaoke, stand-up comedy in musty basements, and the riotous joy of dancing without caring who watched. She laughed more in three months than she’d laughed in years.

One evening in late May, they found themselves sitting on the half-finished city amphitheater’s steps, watching the stars unscroll above them. Jez’s voice cracked the silence.

“Do you ever feel like—” she faltered, “like you’re not sure where you’re supposed to be?”

Elliot leaned back, hands behind his head. “All the time. But lately… I feel pretty close.”

She smiled, unsure what to say. Before she could answer, he reached out and tucked a stray hair behind her ear. The touch was careful, declarative—a small revolution. Jez closed her eyes, allowed an awkward, hopeful silence to bloom.

They didn’t so much fall in love as recognize, slowly, that something irreversible had already occurred. One night, in Jez’s apartment, surrounded by books and candles and three empty wine bottles, Elliot kissed her over a chessboard. His hand hovered at her cheek for a beat longer than necessary. “Best out of three?” he whispered, laughter and longing colliding in his eyes.

If love was a sequence, theirs was built from interrupted conversations and imperfect mornings. Jez burned toast; Elliot lost his keys. They fought about IKEA furniture, about whether the Beatles or the Rolling Stones were superior (Jez was a diehard for the Stones), about whether it was permissible to microwave day-old coffee.

Yet for every argument, there were hours curled beneath old blankets, secrets whispered behind cupped hands, and long walks through the city at dusk, their shadows growing together. Every Wednesday, Jez still rode the 17, even when she didn’t need to. Elliot sometimes walked just to meet her at the stop.

Six months passed, and everything changed and nothing did. Jez found a new job that she didn’t hate; Elliot started volunteering at the community library, rediscovering his passion for teaching. Their friends grew used to the way they finished each other’s sentences, laughed too loudly at the other’s jokes, and vanished from parties together to share a quiet drink at their favorite nook.

One autumn afternoon—the sky a riot of orange and violet—Jez met Elliot on the amphitheater steps, their old haunt. The city murmured below, wind tossing leaves around their ankles. Jez pulled from her bag a battered copy of Cities Invisible.

“I found my old edition,” she said, holding it out. The margins were cluttered with her handwriting, underlining certain phrases, exclamation marks, smiley faces, question marks clustered at particularly obtuse sentences.

Elliot leafed through, stopping at a note scrawled in bright blue.

“Find your person. Share your city.” He smiled, tracing the words.

Jez took a breath, her heart racing. “I was never really looking, but… I think I found both.”

He folded the book closed, leaned in, and rested his forehead against hers. “You did.”

Autumn bowed into winter. Their lives—once parallel, always just outside one another’s orbit—grew tangled in the best of ways. The city outside went about its business, indifferent to the small miracles unfolding on buses, in coffee shops, across sidewalks.

On Wednesdays, Jez and Elliot still caught the 17 when they could, content to watch the world blur by together—theirs now, made soft by the promise that they were no longer alone.

Endings are for stories. For Jez and Elliot, every day was another beginning, forged from chance, kindness, and the quiet power of letting yourself be known.
