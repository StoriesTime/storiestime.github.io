The Rise and Fall of Steve Jobs: A Short Fictional Account


Steve Jobs stared out his office window at midnight, a pale moon glinting on the glassy façade of Apple’s headquarters. In its reflection, he could almost spot the silhouette of the boy who once dropped out of Reed College, a kid making circuit boards in a Los Altos garage. The world now demanded icons, not boys.

Beginnings in the Garage

The world liked to remember Apple’s birth as mythic simplicity: Steve and his friend, Steve Wozniak, soldering motherboards atop a kitchen table. In truth, the floors were sticky with cola spills, and the metal smell clung to their hands for days. Risking everything, they built the first Apple computer—part vision, part rebellion—seeking to make technology not just useful, but beautiful.

The early years burned with intensity. Steve charmed investors with his relentless energy, recruited the wildest talent, and bucked the conventions of entrenched giants like IBM. The Apple II and Lisa computers hinted at revolution, but nothing prepared him, or the world, for the Macintosh.

A single white box, soft corners and a smile blinking from its grey screen, the Macintosh emerged as an anthem for counterculture—the computer for the rest of us. Steve’s famous keynote, where “1984 won’t be like 1984,” echoed into eternity. Suddenly, he was not just a young entrepreneur. He was a prophet.

Success, Hubris, and Exile

Prophets, though, rarely please boardrooms. Steve’s perfectionism became a double-edged sword. He obsessed over typefaces, spent hours selecting the shade of beige for the Macs, and drove his employees to exhaustion, sometimes to tears. For Steve, a product wasn’t finished until it sang, but shareholders craved profit, not poetry.

John Sculley, lured from Pepsi to run Apple, at first admired Steve’s vision. But as the vision turned fractious, the board grew anxious. In a corridor meeting, while rain streaked the glass, Sculley asked Steve, directly, “Do you even know how to run a company?”

By May 1985, the answer was clear. Steve’s badge no longer opened doors. The security guard, apologetic, collected his things in a cardboard box—some drawings, a Newton’s cradle, a spider plant with roots curling over the soil. He walked out under the heavy drizzle, not knowing where to go next.

The Lost Years and the Seed of an Empire

Exile did not mellow Steve. Instead, it sparked reinvention. He poured money into a new company, NeXT. The hardware, sculpted from matte black magnesium, exuded luxury; the software whispered of power. NeXT found few buyers, its price too high, but its code elegant enough to court the curiosity of computer scientists at MIT and beyond.

Concurrently, Steve invested in a small computer graphics company. In dim, humming offices, animators tinkered with renderings of lamps and toys. That company would become Pixar. In 1995, with the release of “Toy Story,” computers created magic for the silver screen, and Steve Jobs returned to the map as the unlikely patron of Hollywood’s reinvention.

Meanwhile, Apple floundered. The company entangled itself in expensive failures, lost at sea without the compass of Steve’s aesthetic. By 1996, Apple’s fate looked sealed—a relic of the past, overtaken by Microsoft’s global dominance.

Return of the Visionary

And so the circle turned. In a boardroom charged with nostalgia and resignation, Steve strode in again—older, thinner, but his eyes ablaze with the same, old intensity. Apple acquired NeXT, more for its software than its founder, yet Steve became the temporary CEO—a “iCEO,” he half-joked.

He gutted the company to save it: product lines chopped from dozens to a handful, projects scrapped, layers of middle management dissolved. Each product received his full attention, from concept to box. Like a conductor with an orchestra, he demanded harmony across disciplines—engineering, design, marketing.

The iMac, translucent and cheerful, hit the market like a comet. Gone was the beige box; in came Bondi blue and curves—a promise of the future with a smile. Steve’s knack for capturing desire didn’t stop there. The iPod spun music into one’s pocket, and soon the iPhone, an object of tech mythology, transformed communication.

Apple blossomed. For a time, Steve Jobs became the paragon of innovation: turtlenecked, focusing a global audience’s attention with a flick of the wrist, a “one more thing” dangling on his lips.

Shadows and the Beginning of the End

But no story runs uninterrupted. While Apple soared into unprecedented heights, Steve’s health faltered. Whispers of illness followed him; speculation shadowed every keynote. Still, he pressed on, launching the iPad—a blank slate promising new forms of work and play.

Behind the images of perfection, tension simmered. Apple’s secrecy became legend—employees signed NDA after NDA, whispers of midnight meetings, and harsh demands to match Jobs’ impossible standards. Some called him inspiring, others tyrannical. His reality distortion field could make people believe six impossible things before breakfast; sometimes, that meant expecting teams to bend physics or, failing that, to simply look like they could.

New competitors arose—Google and Samsung raced to catch up. The world that greeted Jobs’ second reign had changed; smartphones flooded the market, and innovation, once Apple’s sole domain, now belonged to many.

Legacy, Farewell, and What Remains

In August 2011, Steve stepped down as CEO, his voice growing softer but his presence unmistakable. A few months later, the world mourned his passing with a strange intimacy—candles outside Apple stores, post-it notes of thanks on glass doors, a million tweets all at once.

The company he left behind raced forward—some say with less audacity, others with newfound freedom. iPhones multiplied, services diversified, and Apple Park, the ring-shaped headquarters Jobs had obsessively designed, became a Silicon Valley landmark. Yet, the old stories lingered in every device: the care in a rounded corner, the click of a virtual button, the elegance of a hidden feature.

What made Steve Jobs unique was not always easy or kind—perfection was an obsession, not a comfort. His legacy was not built on infallibility but on the pursuit of something greater: to put a piece of poetry in the everyday, to turn technology into magic for the masses, and to see, even in the tangle of circuits and code, a reflection of the messy, idealistic boy from Los Altos.

In the end, his rise and fall mirrored the promise and peril of the age he helped shape: brilliant, flawed, and unforgettable. Every new product announcement, every glowing apple on a lid in a coffee shop, quietly echoes the myth of a man who imagined how the future might look, and—at least for a little while—bent the world towards that vision.
