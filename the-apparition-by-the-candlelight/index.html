The Apparition by the Candlelight


The storm raged outside, rattling the windows of the old house like a growling beast clawing at its prey. Thunder rolled so forcefully that it seemed to shake the foundation of the ancient structure. Shadows danced on the cracked plaster walls, stirred to life by the flickering of a single candle resting on a weathered wooden table. It was the only source of light in the room, casting warm but unreliable illumination across the sparse interior.

Sylvie shivered, pulling her woolen shawl tighter around her shoulders. She had spent the better part of the afternoon trying to reach this house—a remote, abandoned cottage deep in the heart of the forest. Her boots had sunk into the damp earth more times than she could count, and her soaked stockings clung unpleasantly to her legs. But she knew she had made the right decision in seeking refuge here. It was the safest place she could think of, even if its solitude unnerved her.

The door rattled in its frame as the wind lashed against it. Sylvie forced her gaze to remain steady, focusing on the steady pulses of the candlelight instead of the uncertainty pressed against the walls outside. What was it that old Marta had said about this particular house? Something about it being cursed—or maybe haunted? Sylvie didn’t believe in superstitions, but she couldn’t deny a certain uneasiness clinging to the space, as if the walls themselves breathed in quiet anticipation.

She brushed those thoughts aside and reached for her pack, pulling out a loaf of stale bread she had brought with her. As she tore a piece off to eat, she thought of her late husband, Etienne. He had passed on nearly three years ago, but this night, with its oppressive silence and predatory wind, conjured his memory as if it hung in the murky air itself. They had shared nights like this, nights when the power had gone out in their little cabin, and they had only a candle and each other to wile away the hours. The thought brought the sting of tears to her eyes.

“Etienne would have laughed at my fear,” Sylvie whispered, her voice trembling as she tried to sound braver than she felt. The sound of her own words startled her, bouncing off the empty walls with a hollowness that felt eerie in the small space.

As the shadows resumed their languid, flickering waltz across the room, a faint, unmistakable shift in the periphery of her vision froze Sylvie mid-bite. It was as if the light from the candle had suddenly reached for something—had been disturbed by some unseen movement. She turned her head sharply, scanning the room. Nothing. Just the faded flowered wallpaper peeling at the edges and the dusty furniture arranged around the edges of the space, covered in sheets to protect them from the long years of neglect.

Sylvie tried to shake off the uneasy feeling that crawled up her spine. “Wind,” she whispered to herself, though the word didn’t carry down to her chest. “It’s just the damn wind.”

But then it happened again. This time, there was no mistaking it. The candle’s flame swayed unnaturally—tilting in the direction of something invisible, as though caressed by fingers not of this world.

Her breath hitched as goosebumps peppered her arms. Slowly, Sylvie reached for the poker hanging from the fireplace nearby. It wasn’t much, but it made her feel slightly less defenseless. Her heart clattered in her chest like the shutters banging outside, loud enough that it seemed to fill the room.

“Who’s there?” Sylvie demanded, her voice uncertain yet defiant. She stood, her knees trembling, listening so hard that the blood rushing in her ears nearly drowned out her surroundings.

For a few moments, there was only the sound of the storm and the faint hiss of the candle. Then, as the flame stuttered once more, another presence seemed to seep into the room—a feeling heavier and far colder than her chilled skin had been before. The darkness beyond the faint glow of the candle seemed to thicken, growing suffocating in its intensity.

And then, just as Sylvie considered fleeing into the storm rather than confronting whatever lingered here, something emerged. At first, it seemed as though it were part of the shadows themselves, its edges blurry and insubstantial. But as the moments passed, the figure became clearer: pale, shimmering faintly in the golden light of the candle, and unmistakably human in form. It stood across the room, unmoving yet palpable.

Sylvie staggered back and gripped the poker so tightly her knuckles turned white. “Who… who are you?” she managed to choke out, her voice a rasp.

The figure did not respond immediately. It seemed to study her, its head tilting slightly as though assessing her reaction. Then, like a whisper carried on the wind, a voice—not loud but enough to chill her to her core—replied.

“You shouldn’t have come here.”

Sylvie blinked, unsure whether the voice had been real or conjured by her own imagination. The apparition stepped closer, its bare feet silent on the floorboards. Now she could make out more detail—features blurred, but reminiscent of someone she once knew. Her heart clenched as recognition struck her like a lightning bolt.

“Etienne?” she gasped, her voice breaking as she faltered under the weight of her emotions. The specter looked like him, or at least an echo of him: the same strong jawline, though it seemed etched in sadness; the eyes, a dim reflection of the warmth she had loved so deeply.

The spirit said nothing, merely standing there, its gaze fixed on her with that same, unsettling sorrow. Sylvie felt her breath quicken. She reached toward him instinctively, but her fingers hesitated in the air—somehow, she knew he wasn’t truly there.

“Why… how?!” Sylvie’s voice cracked into the silence. “What’s happening? Why have you—”

But the apparition raised a translucent hand, silencing her mid-sentence. His lips moved, though she heard no sound at first. Then came words so faint they felt like memories unspoken: “Leave this place, Sylvie. It is not safe.”

“I don’t understand,” she whispered back, her mind swirling with questions. But before she could plead for answers, the spirit began to fade, its outline dissolving like mist in the morning sun. Sylvie reached out as if to hold onto what remained of him, and then he was gone. The candle flickered violently in the sudden absence.

Sylvie stood frozen, the silence heavier than the storm outside. Something deeper than fear nestled inside her now—a certainty that the house, this old relic of forgotten lives, harbored secrets far darker than she could imagine. And if even Etienne, or whatever echo of him that had appeared, had warned her away, she knew this was no place to linger.

She didn’t bother to snuff out the candle or collect her belongings. Wrapping her damp shawl tightly around her, she turned toward the door. As she opened it, the storm’s biting wind greeted her with open arms. For once, she welcomed it. Stepping into the tempest, Sylvie didn’t look back.

Inside the house, the candle burned to the nub, its final flicker swallowing the last sliver of light. Shadows reclaimed the room entirely, waiting in silence for the next soul unwary enough to stumble into their grasp.
