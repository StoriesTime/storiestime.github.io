Breaking Bias: Powerful Short Stories on Racism


It was late afternoon when I first met Maya, though I didn’t know that she would forever change the way I saw the world. I had always considered myself open-minded, or at least, I thought I did. It was only when I was forced to confront the biases I didn’t even know I had that my worldview would begin to shift.

Maya stood at the front of the café, her figure slim and poised, yet there was a softness in her demeanor that made her approachable. She was a first-time customer at the bookstore café I worked at. I had seen her a few times before, mostly during the busiest hours, but our paths had never crossed.

“Hi, I’m Maya. I’m looking for something to read,” she said, with a warm smile that matched the tone in her voice.

I nodded and gestured toward the bookshelves behind me. “We have a wide selection,” I said, scanning the shelves for something I could recommend. “Anything in particular you’re interested in?”

Maya hesitated. “Maybe something about… overcoming adversity, or stories about resilience?”

Her response wasn’t unusual. Many people came to the café looking for stories that offered solace, stories that provided a sense of hope. I often thought of books as ways to escape the harshness of reality. But Maya’s words resonated with me in a way I couldn’t quite place.

I turned toward the shelves and picked out a few books, but as I did, I felt a slight discomfort in the air, as if I were missing something important. I knew I was only seeing the surface of things, but what lay underneath was a deeper reality, one that both Maya and I were part of but in vastly different ways.

“You’re new to town, right?” I asked as I handed her the books.

“Yes,” she said, nodding. “I moved here from a much smaller town. It’s been a big change, but I’m hoping it’ll be for the best.”

“Small towns can be cozy, but I imagine they can also be… insular?” I said, testing the waters.

She paused, her smile faltering for just a moment. “Insular, yes. You could say that.”

I didn’t think much of her response. I didn’t know her story yet. I didn’t realize that what she was alluding to was a reality I had never even considered.

Over the next few weeks, Maya became a regular at the café. We started talking more during her visits. At first, our conversations were mostly small talk, but as time passed, we began to share more personal stories. Maya told me about her experience growing up in a predominantly white town, where being a person of color meant constantly having to prove herself. Her story echoed a common one I had heard many times before, but it was the way she told it—the quiet strength in her voice—that made me realize how little I truly understood.

One afternoon, while we were chatting over coffee, Maya shared a particularly painful memory.

“I remember being told by one of my teachers,” she began, “that I wasn’t capable of the same things as the other students. Not because of my intelligence, but because of where I came from. I was born into poverty, you see, and there were expectations about what people like me could achieve.”

I leaned in, listening intently, but my mind raced. I had always prided myself on being a progressive thinker. I supported equal rights, I voted in favor of policies that promoted diversity, and I did my best to treat everyone equally. But hearing Maya’s story, I began to realize that I hadn’t done nearly enough to confront my own biases.

In that moment, I recognized that my understanding of racism and inequality had been somewhat theoretical. I understood the concepts on an intellectual level, but I had never truly felt them. I had been blind to the ways in which people’s skin color, heritage, or background shaped their experiences in the world. The privileges I took for granted—like being treated equally in professional settings or assuming that my background would never hinder my chances—were things Maya had to fight for every single day.

I started to think back to my own life, wondering if I had ever made assumptions about people based on their race, ethnicity, or appearance. I had a vague recollection of dismissing certain people before even getting to know them, perhaps because they looked different from me, spoke with an accent, or came from a different part of the world. I had done so unknowingly, but that didn’t make it any less damaging.

As weeks went by, Maya’s words stayed with me, growing louder, more insistent. I started paying closer attention to how I spoke to and about people of color. I realized that, without meaning to, I often treated them differently, either by overcompensating with politeness or by assuming they were somehow “other.” My efforts to be kind were often rooted in a place of ignorance, not true understanding.

One day, Maya invited me to join her at a community event in town. It was a small gathering at a local park, where people from different backgrounds came together to celebrate their culture and history. As we walked through the park, I couldn’t help but notice how the event was predominantly attended by people of color. There was a subtle tension in the air, a sense of unity that was tinged with the weight of generations of exclusion.

Maya introduced me to several people who, like her, had experienced the unspoken divide between races. One man, a local artist named Andre, told me about his experience growing up in a segregated neighborhood. He spoke candidly about how he had never been able to escape the stigma that came with being Black in a predominantly white city. He had to work twice as hard to gain recognition, and even then, his achievements were often dismissed as mere “exceptions” to the rule.

“That’s the thing about systemic racism,” Andre said, his voice steady. “It doesn’t just affect you on the surface. It seeps into every part of your life—the way you’re perceived, the opportunities you get, the way people treat you. It’s exhausting, to be honest.”

I stood there, struck by the raw honesty of his words. I had never considered the exhausting nature of racism, how it wasn’t just about one-off incidents or blatant acts of discrimination, but about the constant, invisible weight that people like Maya and Andre carried every day.

That evening, as I drove home, I couldn’t stop thinking about the things I had learned. I had always believed that racism was a thing of the past, that it had been mostly eradicated by civil rights movements and laws designed to promote equality. But the more I listened, the more I realized that racism was far from gone. It had merely evolved, hidden beneath the surface, manifesting in subtler ways. It wasn’t just about overt hatred or violence—it was about the quiet, everyday assumptions people made, the unconscious biases that shaped their actions and decisions.

I knew I had to confront my own biases if I truly wanted to change. But that wasn’t enough. I had to actively work toward breaking down the systems of inequality that perpetuated racism. I had to challenge myself to be more than just passively supportive of diversity; I needed to be an active participant in dismantling prejudice wherever I saw it, in my own life and beyond.

A few months later, I had the chance to speak at a local community event about the importance of breaking down biases. I spoke about Maya, about Andre, and about the lessons I had learned in those quiet moments of introspection. I spoke about the need for empathy and understanding, for recognizing the humanity in everyone, regardless of their race or background.

And as I spoke, I realized something profound: change doesn’t happen overnight. It’s not enough to acknowledge our biases; we must actively work to change them. It takes conscious effort, it takes courage, and it takes a willingness to listen, to learn, and to grow.

Maya’s influence had been subtle, but it had been life-changing. Through her, I began to understand the true meaning of breaking bias—not just as a personal endeavor, but as a collective one. We are all responsible for creating a world where everyone, regardless of their race, ethnicity, or background, can thrive without the weight of prejudice holding them back.

And as I walked away from that event, I felt a sense of hope. Change was possible, but it started with individuals who were willing to confront their own biases and take action. I knew that I, too, had a role to play in making the world a more just and equal place for all.
