Joan of Arc: Courage and Faith in a Time of War


The scent of damp earth hung in the cold morning air, mingling with the smell of freshly churned mud. Above the distant, looming hills of Lorraine, the sky had barely begun to lighten, casting a faint glow on the rough-hewn landscape. A young girl, cloaked in the colors of her homeland, stood tall against the winds that swept across the open fields. Her name was Jeanne, though history would soon come to know her as Joan of Arc.

At this moment, she was no more than a shepherd’s daughter, with dirt-streaked hands and the calluses of a life lived in the fields. But even the land she walked upon seemed to acknowledge her in ways no one could quite understand. The year was 1428, and France was on the precipice of disaster.

Joan’s village had known the harshness of war. The Hundred Years’ War, a brutal conflict between the Kingdom of France and the Kingdom of England, had raged for decades. The English were poised to claim the throne, with the young King Henry VI asserting his rights in a nation torn asunder by internal strife. The French crown, weak and fragmented, was slipping from the hands of its royal family. The country had been driven into despair, its people war-weary and uncertain of their future.

But Joan—though still only a girl of seventeen—believed differently. She believed that France was not yet lost. Her faith, unwavering and fierce, propelled her forward as she heard voices. Not voices in the traditional sense, but something more—divine messages, whispers in the wind, urging her toward a destiny she could scarcely comprehend.

One night, as she prayed before her family’s hearth, Joan’s life would change forever. She had been hearing the voices for months now, but this time, they were clearer, more insistent. She knelt by the fire, her eyes closed, when the voice of Saint Michael came to her. He told her to rise, to go to the Dauphin, Charles VII, and to help him reclaim his kingdom. She would lead France to victory, but only if she had the courage to act.

At first, Joan’s parents were hesitant. They knew the dangers of war and the cost of ambition. Yet, Joan was resolute. Her faith was as pure as the mountain streams she had crossed as a child, and her conviction grew stronger with every passing day. The voices continued, urging her to don armor, take up a sword, and carry out her divine mission.

By the spring of 1429, Joan set out from her small village to meet Charles VII in Chinon, a town under threat from the English. With nothing more than her faith and a burning desire to save her country, she was determined to present herself to the Dauphin, who was then in hiding from the encroaching English forces. Word of a peasant girl claiming divine guidance quickly spread, and many saw her as nothing more than a delusion, a girl in the throes of madness. Yet there was something about her—something unyielding in her eyes—that compelled people to listen.

In Chinon, Joan was finally granted an audience with Charles VII. The Dauphin, though skeptical, found himself moved by her presence. There was no mistaking her determination. Joan spoke with the confidence of someone who had received a divine mandate. Her faith was absolute. She claimed that Charles was the true king of France, and with the help of God, he could still be crowned.

Charles, unsure of how to respond, agreed to allow Joan to accompany his army. If nothing else, her presence might bolster the morale of his soldiers. But Joan had no intention of simply offering words of encouragement. She was a warrior, destined for battle, and in the months that followed, her actions would prove that her faith was not just a belief but a force of nature.

The first major battle Joan would lead was the Siege of Orléans, one of the key strongholds held by the English. For nearly a year, the city had been under siege. The English forces, confident in their superiority, believed it was only a matter of time before Orléans would fall. But Joan, armed with nothing but a sword and her unshakable faith, led a relief force to break the siege.

As Joan rode into the city, her white armor shining against the dark sky, the people of Orléans were overwhelmed with a sense of hope. It was as if the heavens themselves had sent her to deliver them from the oppression they had endured. The English forces, startled by the sudden appearance of this young, seemingly invincible warrior, were forced to retreat. Within days, the siege was broken.

Joan’s victory at Orléans sent shockwaves through the English ranks and inspired the French soldiers like nothing before. Her leadership and faith had proven that a single person, no matter how humble, could change the course of history. The victory also brought new hope to the French cause. Joan’s name became synonymous with bravery, and her courage gave rise to a new spirit of resistance.

The following months were a whirlwind of victories. Joan led her troops in battle after battle, her reputation growing with every successful campaign. But her faith was tested, not just by the brutality of war, but by the politics of the French court. Charles VII, once hesitant, was now confident in Joan’s abilities. But the more she succeeded, the more some of the French nobility began to fear her influence. They saw Joan not just as a warrior, but as a symbol of power that could challenge their own positions.

Joan’s rise did not go unnoticed by the English either. In 1430, as the French continued to make gains, Joan was captured during a military campaign in Compiègne. The English, desperate to neutralize the one person who had become the embodiment of French resistance, placed her on trial. The charges were numerous: witchcraft, heresy, and cross-dressing. But the trial was little more than a political maneuver to silence her.

For months, Joan was imprisoned, her fate hanging in the balance. Throughout her trial, she remained steadfast in her faith, denying the charges against her and maintaining that her mission had been divinely ordained. She refused to recant her beliefs, even under the threat of death. The English, frustrated by her unyielding courage, condemned her to the flames.

On May 30, 1431, Joan of Arc was burned at the stake in the marketplace of Rouen, her body consumed by fire. She was only nineteen years old. The flames that took her life could not extinguish the legacy she had created. In death, Joan became a martyr—a symbol of the unbreakable bond between faith and courage in the face of adversity.

Years later, her trial would be revisited, and the charges against her were overturned. Joan of Arc was declared innocent and canonized as a saint by the Catholic Church in 1920. But the impact of her life and death transcends the confines of history. She was a girl who dared to believe in something greater than herself, and through her unwavering faith, she changed the course of history.

Joan of Arc is remembered not only for her military victories but for the strength of her character, the purity of her faith, and her unrelenting courage in a time of war. Her story is a testament to the power of conviction and the belief that one person, no matter how small or insignificant they may seem, can change the world. In a time of darkness, Joan of Arc shone brightly—a beacon of hope for a nation torn apart by war. And though she may have been a martyr, her spirit continues to inspire all who face adversity with the courage to stand firm in their beliefs.
