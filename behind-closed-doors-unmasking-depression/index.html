Behind Closed Doors: Unmasking Depression


Most mornings, Anna shifted from sleep to waking as if she were wading through thick fog. Between the first cracks of sunlight and her feet finding the cold floor, there was a moment she often wished she could bottle—a place in her mind where she hovered, untouched by anxiety, not yet aware of the crushing weight that would settle quietly on her chest like a leaden shadow. She remembered, vaguely, what it had felt like to wake up excited for a new day, to slide out of bed humming snippets of songs and making coffee just for the joy of swirling cream in the mug.

Now, the days bled into each other. She dressed, her movements mechanical, blending with the pale walls and muted floors of her small apartment. Coffee was made and sipped out of necessity, not pleasure. The morning headlines flickered on her phone, irrelevant and distant.

But Anna was good at pretending. To her colleagues, she was efficient. Sometimes she even forced a bright laugh at meetings. Her emails were cheerful, punctuated with exclamation marks that she typed out of obligation. If anyone noticed her silence at lunch, or how her smile took a second too long to reach her eyes, they didn’t say.

After work, Anna’s world narrowed further. She watched old sitcoms, letting canned laughter fill the barren air. The invitations from friends—the ones that trickled in after her first few excuses—had slowed to a stop. On her bookshelf, self-help guides gathered dust next to novels she could no longer focus on. The phone rang less often now.

She had grown skilled at masking her struggle, turning her pain invisible behind closed doors. After all, wasn’t everyone a little tired these days? Who would have guessed that, on weekends, Anna sat on the edge of her tub, her hands clenched, listening to the hum of the bathroom fan like it might drown out the whirlpool of self-doubt, the thoughts that pressed in, telling her she was hollow, useless, broken?

She kept up the act. The mask grew heavier.

II.

Things hadn’t always been this way. Anna once shared a city apartment with her best friend Mariah, laughter echoing through the halls at midnight, pizza boxes stacked precariously in the kitchen, plans for concerts and trips scrawled on the calendar. Back then, sadness was a passing guest—sometimes visiting after a hard day, but never overstaying its welcome.

The unraveling had been subtle. At first, Anna thought she was just stretched thin from her new job in digital marketing, from distant parents whose voices grew clipped over the years, from feeling out of place at after-work drinks where jokes swirled around her like smoke.

Small things slipped: missed calls, unopened mail, takeout containers stacking up. She started sleeping more but never woke refreshed. The weight in her chest grew, shifting from a flutter of worry to a constant ache.

When Mariah finally moved cross-country, Anna hardly noticed the boxes being packed or the emptying of the apartment’s laughter. She was busy building walls no one else could see.

People started asking less. Anna deleted the social apps from her phone, one by one. A protective cocoon settled around her. In this shell, invisible if not invincible, she wondered if anyone would notice if she vanished.

III.

One gray Tuesday in November, Anna stopped by her manager Ben’s office with a report. As usual, she kept her eyes on the numbers, never on the man behind the desk.

He looked up. “Anna, wait.”

Her stomach lurched. She braced for mistakes in her report, for clipped criticism. Instead, Ben hesitated, then said quietly, “You doing okay? You seem a bit… distant lately.”

Anna’s throat tightened. Her rehearsed deflection stuttered: “Yeah, I’m fine. Just, uh—long week.”

He nodded. An awkward moment lingered, then Ben released her back to her routines.

She wondered, afterwards, why she hadn’t told him the truth. That some days, she drove home with her hands gripping the wheel as if it alone kept her tethered to the world. That her apartment felt like a cave. That her smiles, now reflexive, often came when she felt the urge to cry.

Admitting it out loud seemed like surrender. She’d read countless articles about the importance of “reaching out,” but in practice, it felt alien. She didn’t want to concern people. And she feared, perhaps irrationally, that if anyone truly understood the darkness behind her smile, they might disappear altogether.

IV.

That night, Anna lay in bed counting cracks on the ceiling, insomnia gnawing at her from the edges. She replayed Ben’s words, the quiet concern there.

Memory tumbled her back to a childhood summer, her mother reaching for her when Anna lost her grip on monkey bars and scraped her knee. The gentleness of someone noticing pain, the relief of hearing, “It’s okay”—that sense of being seen.

She missed that feeling.

A muted ping pulled her from the memory. Her phone lit up: Mariah’s number. The text was plain but pulsed with warmth.

Hey, I know it’s late, but I dreamed about our old apartment and woke up smiling. Miss you. How are you, really?

Anna’s fingers hovered on the keyboard. Something broke free.

Not that great, actually, she typed. Not sure if I’ve been for a while.

There was a pause. Anna considered deleting the message. Before she could, three dots appeared—Mariah was typing. Then:

Glad you told me. Want to talk about it? I’m here.

Relief, thin and tentative, threaded through her. For the first time in months, she felt the mask shift. She called Mariah. They spoke for hours, about everything and nothing—the years since college, the sadness that crept in, the loneliness Anna couldn’t shake.

When they hung up, Anna didn’t feel “fixed.” But the darkness seemed a little less absolute.

V.

The next morning, Anna did something that felt impossible before: she searched online for local counseling options. She waded through lists of therapists, overwhelmed by abbreviations and pictures of professionals in serene offices.

Questions filled her mind. Was she “bad enough” to need help? Did others struggle as she did?

A forum for people struggling with low mood caught her attention. She scrolled, reading thread after thread. The posts were raw and aching, yes—but threaded through them was something unexpected: hope. People talking about small breakthroughs, finding tools that chipped away at the weight. Some spoke of medication, others of meditation, journaling, reaching out.

She left her first anonymous post:

I’m good at pretending, but not at feeling. Does anyone else feel like a ghost in their own life?

Replies came quickly.

Yes, one wrote. Every day. But you’re not invisible here.

Another: Took me three therapists before I found someone who got it. Don’t give up.

Anna blinked. Something in her chest eased.

VI.

Therapy was nothing like she imagined. The first session, Anna sat on the edge of a gray chair in a softly lit room, staring at a mug of herbal tea she hadn’t asked for. Across from her, Dr. Singh waited, not speaking, simply present.

Anna expected to be handed solutions; instead, Dr. Singh asked questions about her routines, her childhood, the triggers for her numb, drifting days. It took time—weeks of talking, missing appointments, then coming back. But gradually, the conversations gave Anna a language for her experiences: “persistent sadness,” “anhedonia,” “self-stigma.”

They explored her fear of burdening others, her isolation, her longing for meaning. Dr. Singh suggested small exercises—journaling, breathing techniques, tiny social outings even if just saying hello to the barista.

The process was painfully slow. Setbacks happened: lost weekends, nights where sleep wouldn’t come, days when nothing Dr. Singh suggested seemed to make a difference. But there were moments, too, where Anna caught herself laughing—an unforced sound, like sunlight peeking through storm clouds.

She reached out to her parents. She attended a coworker’s birthday dinner, speaking little, but staying till the end. She ran into Ben in the hallway; when he asked if all was well, Anna allowed that some days were hard. Ben offered her flexible hours and recommended an Employee Assistance Program.

Anna looked in the mirror, searching for the mask. It was still there, but thinner now. She wasn’t cured, whatever that meant. But she was less alone in her struggle.

VII.

By spring, the world outside Anna’s windows blossomed in ways she’d forgotten possible. Sunlight crept through the glass, dust motes dancing over the same kitchen counter where she had once only noticed dirt. She kept to many of her old routines, but life now felt less like a series of boxes to check, more like days with possibility—a subtle difference, but one she clung to.

There was no dramatic transformation. Anna still battled low moods, still found chores exhausting, still doubted herself and missed social cues. Depression, she learned, was not a “phase” or a flaw, but a complex challenge that would ebb and flow.

But things had shifted: when the fog arrived, Anna could name it, talk through it. She returned Mariah’s calls. She joined an online writing group, something she hadn’t believed herself capable of months before. She brewed her coffee and noticed the swirl of cream again.

Most importantly, she stopped hiding from herself. She allowed that her struggle had silence and shadows—but also, now, a voice.

VIII.

On a warm June evening, Anna sat on her fire escape, city lights blinking around her. She sent a message to another poster on the forum where she’d once written, reaching out to someone who sounded trapped in the same lonely spaces she’d known.

You’re not invisible. I’ve felt it too. If you want to talk, I’m here.

As she pressed send, Anna understood that behind every closed door was a person with a story—sometimes hidden, sometimes whispered, yet always real. The journey didn’t end, but now it included hope, connection, and the quiet power of being seen.

She leaned back, looking up at the scattering of stars. For a long moment, Anna breathed deeply, feeling—for the first time in forever—something like peace.
