Hand Sanitizer and Heartbreaks


It was the smell of it that always got to Sarah. A sharp, clean scent, tinged with something so faintly artificial it almost burned her nose. She had never paid much attention to hand sanitizer until that night — the night everything changed.

The world had been in chaos for months. People were learning to live with things they had once only seen in apocalyptic movies. Streets that were once bustling with life were now eerily quiet. Faces once full of smiles were hidden behind masks. Hand sanitizer had become the modern-day talisman, carried in every bag, pocket, and car, the lifeblood of a sanitized, socially distanced world.

But for Sarah, that bottle of hand sanitizer meant so much more than just safety.

The First Meeting

She hadn’t intended to meet Liam that day. If she had been completely honest, she wasn’t looking for anything at all. The pandemic had made everything feel distant, unreal. Work from home, virtual hangouts, Zoom calls that drained more energy than they gave back. When her friends had insisted on an in-person, socially distanced gathering, she had reluctantly agreed, dragging herself to the park for a “safe” catch-up.

Liam was standing by the water fountain when Sarah arrived, a bottle of hand sanitizer in his hand, the small, clear plastic container catching the afternoon sun. He was tall, with dark, curly hair and a mischievous grin that seemed to promise trouble. But when she saw him, the first thing she noticed was the hand sanitizer.

“Hi,” he said, his voice warm, as if they had known each other for years. “Need some?”

Sarah blinked, still unsure how to even greet anyone these days. It had been a long time since she had met someone new in person.

“Uh, sure,” she said, holding out her hands, a little awkwardly.

Liam squirted a generous amount into her palms. The scent of alcohol and citrus filled the air between them. It should have been just a simple gesture, a momentary exchange, but it lingered, a strange intimacy in that fleeting touch of sanitizer.

“Are you okay?” he asked, his eyes searching hers with a kindness that was unusual, given the times. “I mean, with all this…?”

Sarah looked around, watching as people moved in the distance, all keeping their distance, walking in their own little bubbles. The isolation was getting to everyone. But she didn’t know how to explain that to Liam. So instead, she shrugged.

“I’m making it,” she said. “One day at a time, right?”

Liam chuckled, his eyes crinkling at the corners. “Yeah. But sometimes it feels like we’re all just pretending to be fine.”

The conversation continued, casually at first, with Sarah surprised by how easy it was to talk to him. The usual small talk about work, the weather, and then deeper things — about how the pandemic had shifted their perspectives, about relationships that had been strained by quarantine, about the quiet loss of connection.

It felt good. It felt like something normal.

The Slow Burn

Over the next few weeks, Sarah found herself thinking about Liam more and more. He sent her texts, sometimes a simple “Hey, how’s your day?” and other times more profound thoughts about the world and its sudden fragility. They’d chat about books, music, their favorite food (which they both agreed was pizza), and, of course, the virus and the new rules everyone was following. The topics kept shifting, but there was a thread that always connected them — the shared experience of living through a strange, terrifying time.

They met up again a few days later, this time in a different park, farther from the usual crowd. It was their second meeting, though it felt like more. When Sarah arrived, Liam was already there, with a small picnic laid out. A blanket, two water bottles, and a single sandwich in the middle.

“I thought you could use some fresh air,” Liam said, grinning. “It’s not much, but it’s ours.”

They sat on the grass, keeping a cautious distance between them, but the conversation flowed easily, like they had known each other for years. It was like a balm for the rawness that the world had inflicted on both of them.

“Can I ask you something?” Sarah said, suddenly serious.

“Sure,” Liam replied, raising an eyebrow.

“Do you think this is real? Like… this thing between us?”

Liam paused, staring at her as if weighing his words. “I think it could be,” he said softly. “But I also think we’re both scared. Scared of how fragile everything is, how everything could disappear in an instant.”

Sarah didn’t know what to say. She didn’t know how to say what she felt — the way her heart ached, not just from the isolation of the pandemic, but from the way something inside her was beginning to awaken. But it wasn’t just that. It was the slow, steady feeling of falling for someone when the world was upside down.

“Yeah,” she whispered. “I’m scared too.”

Liam smiled, the kind of smile that made her feel like the world was smaller, simpler, and for a brief moment, everything was okay.

And then it happened. They reached for the same bottle of hand sanitizer, both of them instinctively trying to clean their hands before touching anything. It was a silly, perfect moment — two people, both a little shy, reaching for something that had become as much a part of life as air itself. But as their fingers brushed over the cap, Sarah felt something deeper, more profound.

There was something there. A connection. A spark.

The First Heartbreak

A month later, everything changed.

Sarah had woken up to an early message from Liam, one that felt cold, distant. A simple text: “We need to talk.”

Her heart dropped into her stomach. She had spent the last few days replaying every conversation, every smile, every shared bottle of sanitizer, wondering if there had been a sign. She had known, deep down, that something was off. But she hadn’t expected this.

They met at their usual park, under the same tree where they had shared their first picnic. Liam was already there, his hands stuffed deep in his pockets, his face unreadable.

“Sarah,” he began, his voice tight. “I think it’s better if we don’t keep seeing each other. I can’t give you what you need right now. I’m sorry.”

The words hit Sarah like a punch to the gut. She stared at him, her mind scrambling for an explanation, a reason, anything to make sense of it. But there was nothing. Just the empty space between them, the weight of things unsaid, the feeling of the ground shifting under her feet.

She felt the sudden urge to reach for her hand sanitizer, to rub the cold gel into her palms as if it could clean away the raw ache inside. But she didn’t. She just stood there, frozen, her world collapsing one piece at a time.

“I don’t understand,” she whispered. “Was it something I did? I thought we…”

Liam looked away, his face etched with guilt. “No. It’s just that I can’t keep pretending. I can’t keep building something on shaky ground. The world’s already upside down. I don’t want to make it worse for you.”

There it was — the words she hadn’t wanted to hear. The fear that had always been there, unspoken but present, had been realized. He was scared too. But unlike her, he had decided to walk away.

And in that moment, Sarah realized something: the pandemic hadn’t just created physical distance between people. It had created emotional distances too. Everyone was so focused on staying safe that they had forgotten what it meant to truly connect, to be vulnerable, to give and receive love.

A Bottle of Hand Sanitizer

Sarah went home that day, numb, her mind clouded by the events of the morning. The empty house felt like a hollow echo of what it used to be. She sat on her bed and looked around. Her eyes fell on the bottle of hand sanitizer sitting on her nightstand. The same brand, the same scent. It had been there since the beginning of the pandemic, just another object that had come to define her days.

She picked it up, twisted the cap open, and squeezed a small amount into her hands. The cool liquid slid over her skin, and for a moment, it felt like she was washing away the remnants of the heartbreak.

But no amount of hand sanitizer could cleanse her heart.

She knew she would be okay eventually. In time, she would heal. But for now, the sharp scent of that sanitizer was all that remained — a reminder of a time when connection felt possible, even in the midst of a world turned upside down.

And maybe that was enough.
