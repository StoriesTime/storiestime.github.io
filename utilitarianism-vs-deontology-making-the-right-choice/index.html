Utilitarianism vs Deontology: Making the Right Choice


On a cloudy afternoon in the bustling city of Serenvale, a moral crisis unfolded quietly within the walls of a modest hospital. Dr. Evelyn Hart, a seasoned physician known for her unwavering commitment to her patients, faced a dilemma that pitted two powerful ethical frameworks against each other: utilitarianism and deontology.

The emergency room was overwhelmed. A devastating accident had brought in ten patients, each suffering from critical injuries. Among them was a young mother, Clara, who was pregnant and stable for the moment but required immediate surgery to save her unborn child. The hospital, however, had only one operating room ready and just enough medical staff to handle a single operation urgently.

Dr. Hart had to decide how to allocate limited resources. The choice wasn’t purely medical—it was profoundly ethical.

The Crisis Unfolds

Clara’s condition was precarious. The surgical team predicted that if Clara underwent the operation now, both she and her unborn child had a 70% chance of survival. However, this meant postponing treatment for the other nine patients, who were all in critical but varying conditions. If those nine patients were attended to promptly, six had a high chance of survival, while three were likely to succumb, regardless of intervention.

Dr. Hart’s mind raced through the ethical labyrinth. On one hand, utilitarianism—the philosophy that advocates for actions that maximize overall happiness or well-being—would suggest prioritizing the nine patients because more lives could be saved by treating the greatest number. On the other hand, deontology—a duty-based ethical approach—emphasizes following moral rules or duties, such as the doctor’s obligation to care for each patient equally without discrimination or calculation.

Utilitarian Perspective: The Greater Good

Utilitarianism, famously championed by philosophers like Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, would encourage Dr. Hart to maximize the total lives saved, even if it meant making painful sacrifices.

In this scenario, treating nine patients first could potentially save six lives, which numerically outweighs saving Clara and her unborn infant. The consequence-focused nature of utilitarianism sees this outcome as morally preferable because it maximizes overall survival and happiness.

Dr. Hart imagined the families of six healthy patients rejoicing, the relief felt by society knowing more lives were preserved, and the increase in community well-being as a result.

Yet, in this view, the individual rights and dignity of Clara and her unborn child become secondary considerations, sacrificed for the aggregate benefit. The decision, although rooted in compassion for the majority, risked reducing people to mere numbers in a statistical calculation.

Deontological Stance: Moral Duties and Rights

Conversely, deontology, particularly the Kantian ethics school, emphasizes acting in accordance with moral duties regardless of outcomes. From this standpoint, Dr. Hart’s primary responsibility is to honor her duty as a physician: to treat each patient with equal respect and care, irrespective of the numbers.

Deontological ethics stresses that some actions are intrinsically right or wrong. Prioritizing patients based on probable outcomes risks violating principles such as fairness, respect for persons, or treating individuals as ends rather than means.

If Dr. Hart followed deontology to the letter, she would have to care for Clara and her unborn child first because she cannot justify neglecting a patient who has been given her promise of care, especially when Clara’s surgery is expected to be life-saving.

However, this approach might result in losing the potential lives of several other patients, a heavy price to pay. Yet it preserves Dr. Hart’s commitment to moral duty and the inviolability of individual rights.

The Decision and Its Aftermath

As the clock ticked down to critical moments, Dr. Hart called a brief meeting with senior staff. They debated passionately, aware that any choice bore profound consequences.

Ultimately, Dr. Hart chose to operate on Clara first, aligned with deontological ethics—upholding her duty to care for the patient currently under her care, treating her as an end rather than as a means to save others.

The surgery was painstaking, and by the time it concluded, critical hours had passed for the other patients. The surgical team then turned to the other nine, triaging carefully. Some survived, others did not.

Days later, the hospital’s ethics committee reviewed the case. Some praised Dr. Hart for her steadfastness in honoring patient dignity, while others argued a more utilitarian approach might have saved more lives.

Reflections on Moral Complexity

Dr. Hart’s story illustrates how real-world decisions rarely fit neatly into ethical frameworks. Utilitarianism offers a pragmatic, outcome-focused lens, emphasizing the greatest good for the greatest number. It’s attractive for policymakers and emergency contexts, pushing decision-makers to consider consequences and optimize welfare.

Deontology champions principles and moral rules, providing clarity and boundaries that protect individuals from being sacrificed for abstract calculations. It appeals deeply to the human sense of justice and fairness.

Both perspectives have strengths and limitations. Utilitarianism’s challenge lies in potentially overshadowing individual rights, while deontology’s rigidity may sometimes lead to less globally beneficial outcomes.

For practitioners like Dr. Hart, the “right choice” can depend on context, values, and the nuances each situation reveals. Ethical decision-making is rarely about choosing a perfect solution; it’s about balancing conflicting imperatives, acknowledging trade-offs, and acting with integrity.

Beyond the Hospital: Broader Ethical Implications

This dilemma extends beyond medicine—into law, business, governance, and everyday life. When governments allocate resources during crises, when companies make decisions that affect stakeholders, or when individuals confront moral quandaries, the tension between utilitarianism and deontology remains.

For instance, during public health emergencies, policies might prioritize population-wide benefits (utilitarianism), sometimes at the cost of individual freedoms or rights (deontological concerns). In contrast, legal systems enforce duties and rights to protect citizens, even when such enforcement might not maximize overall welfare.

Understanding these ethical frameworks helps people navigate complex choices, fostering empathy and nuanced thinking rather than simple moral judgments.

The Human Element in Ethics

What Dr. Hart’s experience reminds us most powerfully is the role of human judgment, emotion, and responsibility in ethical decisions. Neither cold calculation nor rigid rule-following can wholly capture the richness of human life, fraught with ambiguity and urgency.

Utilitarianism and deontology provide essential tools for moral reasoning, but the “right choice” ultimately depends on compassion, courage, and the willingness to face moral uncertainty openly.

Final Thoughts

In the shadow of Serenvale’s hospital, the story of Dr. Evelyn Hart continues to inspire debates about ethics and morality. It reveals that making the right choice is less about choosing one ethical theory over another and more about understanding the values underlying each, appreciating their interplay, and applying them judiciously in the real world.

The struggle between utilitarianism and deontology is not about winners or losers; it reflects the vibrant, ongoing quest to live ethically in a complex, interconnected world.

If you found this story thought-provoking, embracing its philosophical tensions can deepen your understanding of ethics in everyday decisions, leading to a more reflective and compassionate approach to life’s toughest questions.
