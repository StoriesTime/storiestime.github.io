Shadows of Bias: A Story About Racism in America


In the heart of New Orleans, under the murmur of slow-moving streets and the quiet clinking of glasses in the jazz bars, there lived a young woman named Eliza Williams. She was a high school teacher, passionate about history and social justice, known in her community for speaking her mind and challenging the world around her. But, like many in her position, she found herself in an ongoing struggle with the ever-present weight of bias and racism that colored every corner of her life.

A Growing Discomfort

Eliza had grown up in a family that emphasized education above all else. Her parents, who had both attended historically black colleges, instilled in her the importance of striving for excellence, but always with an awareness of the systemic challenges that Black people faced in America. She had come to accept that no matter how educated, talented, or skilled she was, the color of her skin would always be a factor in how the world viewed her. It was a reality that haunted her even when she thought she had escaped it.

It was during her second year of teaching at a predominantly white public high school that Eliza first truly felt the sting of prejudice creeping into her daily life. She had been hired with the promise of making a difference in the lives of her students, but soon after she started teaching, Eliza began to realize that not everyone saw her as a beacon of inspiration. Her students’ attitudes toward her were mixed, and while many admired her, a subtle but persistent discomfort began to grow in the halls.

One afternoon, Eliza sat in the teacher’s lounge, nursing a cup of lukewarm coffee. A group of her colleagues—mostly white, middle-aged teachers—were gathered around a table, gossiping about the latest developments in the school. The conversation turned to the school’s curriculum and the diversity of the faculty.

“I just don’t know if we’re being too political now,” one of her colleagues, Mr. Peterson, remarked with a dismissive laugh. “History’s already been told. Why are we bringing in so much about Black history and the struggle for civil rights? It’s in the past. Why keep focusing on it?”

Eliza’s heart sank. She had heard comments like this before—small, veiled dismissals of Black culture and history. She had long been used to the idea that her race was a side note, something to be acknowledged in passing but not examined deeply. She took a breath, gathering her strength.

“Mr. Peterson,” she said, trying to remain calm, “If we don’t learn from the past, we’re doomed to repeat it. Understanding the struggles of marginalized groups is crucial to understanding where we are today. You can’t erase history, no matter how uncomfortable it may make us.”

The room fell silent for a moment. Some of the other teachers shifted uncomfortably, but no one spoke up. It was a familiar silence—a silence that Eliza had come to associate with ignorance, with the kind of deep-rooted prejudice that refused to acknowledge its own existence.

The Unseen Struggle

Eliza spent the following weeks grappling with this discomfort, both in the school and in her personal life. Her students, though some were bright and eager to learn, often seemed perplexed by her. In their eyes, she wasn’t just a teacher—she was an anomaly. Her presence in a classroom filled with mostly white students seemed to perplex them, and many of them acted as though they didn’t know how to respond to her.

One day, as Eliza was walking through the hallway between classes, she overheard a conversation between two of her students, Jake and Sarah. They were talking about a project Eliza had assigned, one that explored the history of race relations in America.

“She’s making us study all that Civil Rights stuff,” Jake said, his voice filled with disdain. “I don’t get why we have to focus on all this slavery and segregation stuff. It’s like she wants us to feel guilty for something we didn’t even do.”

“Yeah,” Sarah agreed. “I don’t get it. Why can’t we just move on? It’s like every year she makes us talk about the same thing. It’s like she wants to make us feel bad for being white.”

Eliza felt her breath catch in her chest. It wasn’t the first time she’d heard sentiments like this, but hearing it from her students, the ones she was trying to reach, hurt in a way that words could not explain. She had hoped that the lessons of history would encourage empathy, not resentment. But perhaps this was the inevitable result of a system built on systemic bias—this constant questioning of the value of Black lives, of Black experiences, and of Black contributions to the fabric of American society.

Confronting the Shadows

Eliza knew that she couldn’t stay silent. If she did, the biases would only grow stronger. So, one afternoon after school, she stayed late to prepare for the following day’s lesson on systemic racism and its impact on modern American society. It was going to be a difficult conversation, but it was necessary.

The next day, Eliza stood in front of her classroom, her hands steady but her heart racing. Her students were quiet, unsure of what to expect. She opened the lesson with a story—one that was both personal and historical.

“I want to tell you about a young boy named Tyrone. Tyrone was a bright student in a school much like this one, but his opportunities were limited. Tyrone was Black, and his family didn’t have the same resources as others. Because of this, he struggled to keep up with his classmates, even though he worked harder than anyone. His story isn’t unique. In fact, it’s shared by millions of people in this country.”

As she spoke, she watched the faces of her students. Some were uncomfortable, shifting in their seats. Others looked down, avoiding her gaze. Eliza knew that this discomfort was a sign of something important—an opportunity for growth. The silence in the room was a space in which learning could happen.

She continued, “Racism is not just something that happens in the past. It is something that is still alive today. It shapes the way people are treated, the opportunities they have, and the way they see the world. It is something we all need to recognize and address.”

The class was silent for a long time after she finished speaking. Eliza had given them more than a lesson on history; she had given them a glimpse into the lived reality of millions of Americans. Whether they chose to acknowledge it or not, racism was not just a thing of the past—it was present, in every classroom, every office, and every corner of American society.

Change Through Conversation

As the weeks passed, Eliza continued to challenge her students, not just through history lessons, but by encouraging open, honest conversations about race, privilege, and injustice. It wasn’t easy—there were moments of tension, moments when the class seemed to fracture into opposing sides. But through it all, Eliza stood firm. She knew that the shadows of bias could only be chased away with the light of understanding.

One day, after a particularly heated debate about affirmative action, one of her students, Rachel, approached her after class.

“I never really understood what you were talking about before,” Rachel admitted, her voice quieter than usual. “I always thought people just needed to work harder, but now I see how much of it is about what we’re given, what we’re allowed to have access to. I still don’t get it all, but I think I understand a little more.”

Eliza smiled, a small but genuine smile. It wasn’t a perfect solution, but it was a step forward. Every small shift in understanding, every new perspective, was a victory.

The Long Road Ahead

The road to combating racism and bias was long, and the shadows that loomed over American society were deep and unyielding. But Eliza knew that change was possible. It wouldn’t come easily or quickly, but it could come. And it would begin with conversations—difficult, uncomfortable, and often painful conversations. But these were the conversations that would eventually lead to a society where every voice, no matter the color of the speaker’s skin, was valued equally.

Eliza Williams understood this better than anyone. She knew that, in order to move forward, America had to confront its past—acknowledge its biases—and commit to a future where justice wasn’t just a word, but a reality for everyone.

The battle against racism was not one that could be won overnight, but Eliza had learned that the most powerful weapon in this fight was the truth. And she, along with countless others, would continue to wield it—one lesson, one conversation, and one student at a time.
