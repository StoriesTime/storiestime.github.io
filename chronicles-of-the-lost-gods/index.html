Chronicles of the Lost Gods


In the beginning, when the stars were still young, there were gods—celestial beings of immense power, whose existence was woven into the very fabric of the cosmos. These gods were not the abstract entities of ancient mythologies, but tangible forces, entities who shaped realms, oceans, mountains, and even the lives of mortals. They were revered by the people of the realms they protected, and their names were sung in the halls of kings and whispered in the temples of mystics.

However, as eons passed, something changed. The gods began to fade. The celestial light dimmed, their influence waned, and the once-thriving connection between them and the mortal realm slowly unraveled. The world turned in silence as the divine presence that had shaped it faded from memory. The Lost Gods, as they would come to be known, slipped into the forgotten corners of the cosmos, their stories relegated to mere myth.

But the gods did not vanish without a trace. They left behind clues, fragments of their power scattered across the world, waiting for those brave enough to seek them out. These artifacts, known as the Remnants, were all that remained of the divine beings who once ruled over the earth and sky.

The Last Quest of Veris

In the heart of the Hollowed Forest, nestled between the ancient oak trees whose roots stretched as deep as the secrets of the earth, there was a young woman named Veris. She was an outsider to the world of gods and immortals, a mortal with no claim to divinity or noble blood. Yet, Veris held within her heart an unquenchable thirst for knowledge—a desire to uncover the lost tales of the gods who had once ruled the heavens.

Her journey began when she found an old, tattered map—an artifact from a time long past—hidden beneath the floorboards of a forgotten library in the crumbling ruins of a city. The map, drawn in fading ink, was a guide to the Lost Gods, marking the locations of their temples, their resting places, and the Remnants that once contained their power.

Veris knew that finding the Remnants was no mere treasure hunt. Each artifact was said to hold a fragment of the god it represented, and those who sought them would not only uncover the truth of the Lost Gods but also gain unimaginable power. It was a temptation few could resist, but Veris was different. She sought not power but answers.

Her first stop was the Temple of Zoryn, the god of time. The temple stood in the heart of a vast desert, its golden spires half-buried in the sand. Zoryn had been the god who governed the flow of time, whose presence had once ensured that the fabric of existence remained stable. But after his disappearance, time itself had become erratic, unpredictable. Mortals spoke of time loops, where the past and future collided in a chaotic dance, but few dared to explore these phenomena.

Veris approached the temple cautiously, knowing that it would not be an easy task to claim the Remnant of Zoryn. As she stepped into the ruins, the air grew heavy, as if the very concept of time was being compressed around her. The walls of the temple were lined with intricate carvings, depictions of mortals and gods locked in a constant battle against the ticking of the clock.

At the heart of the temple, Veris found a pedestal, upon which rested a small, hourglass-shaped artifact. The Remnant of Zoryn was a delicate piece, its sand shimmering like stars trapped within. But as Veris reached for it, the ground trembled beneath her feet, and the walls of the temple began to shift, as if time itself was coming alive.

“Foolish mortal,” a voice boomed, echoing through the chamber. “You seek that which you do not understand. The power of the gods is not meant for those of your kind.”

Veris stood her ground, her heart steady despite the warning. “I seek the truth,” she said, her voice unwavering. “Not power, but the knowledge of what happened to you.”

The voice paused, as if considering her words. Finally, the ground stilled, and the artifact’s sand flowed gently within the hourglass. “If it is truth you seek,” the voice whispered, “then take what you have come for, but beware, for the path ahead will lead you to places where even gods fear to tread.”

With those final words, the hourglass in Veris’ hand began to glow, its sand swirling faster and faster, until it shattered into a thousand pieces. A vision filled her mind—an image of Zoryn, standing before a massive rift in the fabric of time, his once-bright eyes now dimmed with sorrow. His last act had been to seal away the power of time itself, a decision that would cost him his immortality.

And then, the vision faded. The Remnant of Zoryn had been claimed, but the question remained: What had truly happened to the gods?

The Ruin of Aeryn, the Goddess of Life

Veris’ journey took her across realms, through treacherous mountains, forgotten cities, and beneath the oceans’ deepest trenches. Each Remnant she uncovered told a different story—a tale of power, sacrifice, and eventual decay. But it was in the ruins of Aeryn’s Garden, deep within the Caverns of the Undying, that Veris uncovered the most heart-wrenching tale.

Aeryn had been the goddess of life, the one who breathed life into the earth and nurtured all living things. Her garden, said to be the most beautiful place in the universe, was once a paradise—a realm where the flowers bloomed eternally, and the rivers flowed with the nectar of life. But after the gods’ fall, the garden had withered. The once-vibrant colors of the plants had dulled, and the creatures that once roamed freely had disappeared.

Veris descended into the caverns, guided by the faint glow of the Remnant of Aeryn, which pulsed with an eerie light. As she made her way deeper into the heart of the garden, she found the remains of a massive tree, its roots stretching into the depths of the cavern. The tree’s bark was cracked and brittle, and its branches were devoid of life. This was the Tree of Aeryn, the source of all life in the universe, and its death had marked the downfall of the goddess herself.

Veris knelt before the tree, her hand resting on its withered trunk. “What happened to you, Aeryn?” she whispered.

A voice, soft and fragile, responded. “I was betrayed,” it said, the sound like the rustling of dying leaves. “By the very forces I sought to protect.”

The goddess’s story was one of heartbreak. Aeryn had been forced to choose between the preservation of life and the preservation of balance. In her attempts to protect her creations, she had ignored the laws of the cosmos, which demanded that life be cyclical—birth, death, and rebirth. Her defiance had shattered the natural order, causing the gods to intervene.

Aeryn had chosen to fight against the inevitable, to hold on to life even as it began to decay. But in doing so, she had doomed herself and her creations. The Remnant of Aeryn was the last piece of her essence, the final reminder of a goddess who had once given everything for the sake of life.

The End of the Lost Gods

As Veris traveled further, uncovering more Remnants, the picture of the Lost Gods grew clearer. It was not a tale of mere defeat or fading glory. The gods had fallen not because of weakness but because of their refusal to accept the inevitable. They had tried to fight against the very forces that governed their existence, and in doing so, they had caused their own undoing.

In the end, Veris understood the truth: the gods were not lost because they had disappeared, but because they had been unwilling to let go. Their struggle to maintain their power, to hold on to their divine rule, had ultimately destroyed them.

But the Remnants were not mere relics of the past. They were seeds, remnants of the divine that still held the power to shape the future. And Veris, who had sought knowledge, now understood her role in the grand tapestry of the cosmos. The gods were not gone; their power lived on in the hearts of those who dared to seek the truth.

The Lost Gods had not vanished. They had merely passed on the torch to those who would come after them.

Veris, now in possession of the last Remnant, stood at the edge of a new dawn. The gods’ stories were not finished. They had only just begun.
