Faces in the Mirror: Living with Identity Disorders


Eva stood before the bathroom mirror, staring at her reflection as if hoping the glass would offer some answers. Her eyes, though tired, were the same as they always had been. Brown. Wide. Reflecting a deep sense of confusion. Yet, no matter how long she looked, she couldn’t seem to recognize the person staring back at her. The face was hers, and yet it wasn’t. The features, the expression, the skin tone – all familiar. But the feeling? It was foreign. Distant.

Eva wasn’t always like this. At least, she didn’t think she was. Her life had been normal, or at least that’s how she remembered it. She had a job she enjoyed, a small group of friends who were always ready to listen, and a partner who loved her. Yet, in the past few months, something had changed. Something subtle at first, like a whisper in the back of her mind. It grew louder, more persistent, until it became a constant hum, unrelenting.

Who am I?

At first, it was just the mirror. Then, it started to spill into other areas of her life. She would be having a conversation with someone, and suddenly, a strange feeling of detachment would wash over her. Her voice would sound unfamiliar, as if it wasn’t her own. She would find herself acting in ways she couldn’t explain, saying things that didn’t sound like something she would say. It was as though she was watching her life from the outside, a silent observer in someone else’s story.

Eva tried to dismiss it, blaming stress from work or personal issues. But as time went on, it became harder to ignore. The uncertainty gnawed at her, and the feeling of disconnection grew stronger. It wasn’t just her reflection. It was as though she was wearing a mask that she couldn’t take off, a mask that constantly shifted, changing her appearance in ways she couldn’t control.

She started researching online, searching for answers. The more she read, the more she recognized symptoms she had never considered. Dissociative Identity Disorder. Depersonalization. Derealization. The words seemed to float before her eyes, like puzzle pieces she was afraid to put together. Was this what was happening to her? Had she developed some kind of disorder?

One evening, Eva found herself in a coffee shop with her friend Sarah. It had been a long time since they had caught up, and as Sarah spoke animatedly about her new job, Eva felt herself zoning out. Sarah’s words were distant, as if they were being spoken through a fog. The more Sarah talked, the more Eva’s mind began to wander.

It was then that Sarah’s voice snapped her back to reality.

“Eva, are you okay?” Sarah asked, concern creeping into her tone.

Eva blinked, realizing that her friend had been talking to her for several minutes. She had no idea what had been said. She forced a smile.

“Yeah, I’m fine. Just… a little tired.”

Sarah didn’t seem convinced but didn’t press the issue. They talked for a while longer, but Eva couldn’t shake the feeling of being lost in her own mind. Her thoughts were fractured, like pieces of a broken mirror, each reflecting a different version of herself. Each one seemed to be someone else entirely.

Later that night, as she lay in bed, Eva thought about the mirror again. She had always felt a strange connection to it. Not because she admired her appearance – she didn’t. But because it was the one place where she could see herself. Yet now, the mirror seemed to be mocking her. The reflection that stared back at her didn’t match the person she thought she was. It was as if the face in the mirror belonged to someone she had never met.

The next day, she scheduled an appointment with a therapist. It was a step she had avoided for months, telling herself that she just needed some rest. But deep down, she knew she couldn’t keep ignoring the growing rift inside her. Something wasn’t right, and she needed help.

Her therapist, Dr. Adler, was kind and patient. After a few sessions of talking about her life, her relationships, and the strange experiences she had been having, Dr. Adler suggested that Eva might be experiencing symptoms of a dissociative disorder.

“I think what you’re describing could be depersonalization,” Dr. Adler explained. “It’s when you feel disconnected from your own thoughts, feelings, or sense of identity. You might feel like you’re observing yourself from outside your body, or like you’re living in a dream. It can be disorienting and frightening.”

Eva felt a sense of relief. Finally, someone had put a name to what she was going through. But there was also a wave of fear. Depersonalization? Was she losing herself?

Dr. Adler continued, “Depersonalization is often a response to trauma, but it can also happen in people who are under extreme stress. It’s your brain’s way of coping with overwhelming emotions or situations. But it’s important to note that you’re not ‘losing’ yourself. You’re just having trouble connecting with the part of you that feels real.”

Eva nodded slowly, absorbing the information. She had never considered trauma as a factor in her life. She had always thought of herself as strong, capable of handling whatever came her way. But the more she thought about it, the more she realized that stress and anxiety had been a constant undercurrent in her life, especially over the past few years.

Her job had become increasingly demanding, with late nights and constant pressure to perform. Her personal life had been equally challenging, with her partner, Ben, becoming distant and withdrawn. Eva had always tried to be the strong one, the one who held everything together. But now, it seemed that the cracks had begun to show.

“You’re not alone in this, Eva,” Dr. Adler said gently. “Many people experience these kinds of symptoms, and with the right treatment, it’s possible to reconnect with yourself. It may take time, but you can heal.”

Eva left the session feeling both hopeful and apprehensive. For the first time in months, she felt like there was a way out of the maze of confusion she had been trapped in. But she also knew that healing would take time, and she wasn’t sure if she was ready for the journey ahead.

Over the next few weeks, Eva began to explore the depths of her identity. With Dr. Adler’s guidance, she started to uncover memories and emotions that had long been buried. She realized that her sense of self had always been fragile, shaped by the expectations of others and her own fears of inadequacy. The pressure to be perfect, to constantly prove her worth, had slowly chipped away at her sense of who she was. She had become so focused on meeting external demands that she had lost touch with the inner parts of herself.

It wasn’t easy. There were days when she felt like she was drowning in a sea of confusion, unable to find her way to the surface. But with each step, she began to reconnect with the person she had lost. She learned to accept the parts of herself that she had once rejected, to embrace the flaws and imperfections that made her human.

Slowly, she began to see herself in a new light. Not as the person she had once been, but as the person she was becoming. The journey was far from over, but for the first time in a long while, Eva felt like she was moving in the right direction.

One afternoon, as she sat in front of the mirror once more, she realized something. The reflection that stared back at her no longer felt like a stranger. It was still her face, but now it felt more like a map of her journey, a symbol of the strength she had found within herself. The faces she had seen in the mirror before were not gone, but they had become a part of her. She was no longer afraid of them. Instead, she saw them as pieces of a whole, each contributing to the person she was becoming.

Who am I? Eva smiled at her reflection, finally knowing the answer.

I am me.
