Inventive Pursuits: How Hertha Marks Ayrton Boldly Shattered Glass Ceilings


Nestled in the Victorian era, the cobblestone streets of London danced with the clopping of horses’ hooves and the laughter of children playing Tunbridge Wells. Amidst this period of swift change and industrial progress, a young woman named Hertha Marks dared to challenge the very glass ceilings that once seemed impenetrable. She would become known for her innovative pursuits and ground-breaking discoveries, something that even now leaves many in awe.

The Early Years

Born Esther Pauline Herschein in 1854 in Northampton, England to a Jewish family, she held an early passion for science and learning. However, not everything was easy for young Esther. At the tender age of six, she lost her father, leaving her family in dire straits. Her mother, Phoebe, took on a job as a dressmaker and, alongside this, ensured that Esther was given the education she longed for.

In contrast to the limitations placed upon women during this era, Esther excelled in mathematics, science, and engineering. She was particularly drawn to the elegant simplicity of mathematics and the boundless possibilities offered by science. Her thirst for knowledge did not go unnoticed, and in 1870, at the tender age of 16, she managed to secure a place at the Northampton College for Women, sparking her journey towards a bold and daring existence.

Unyielding Curiosity and a Burning Passion

By the age of 18, Esther had set her sights on the prestigious Royal College of Science in London, as well as the Royal School of Mines. But alas, the institution was dominated by men, and dared not open its doors to women. Esther was undeterred, and with the help of friends and mentors, she managed to enroll as an external student, eventually gaining a bachelor’s degree with distinction and even being granted with a prize for her ordeal.

As Esther’s journey into science progressed, she adopted the name Hertha Marks, signifying her decision to forge her own path and express her true self. She dove into various branches of science, from electricity and optics to acoustics, pharmacology, and the study of waves. A fast learner and brilliant mind, she contributed papers to scientific journals, began lecturing at various institutions, and even became a respected Fellow of the Royal Photographic Society.

Recognition and Revolutionary Ideas

Hertha Marks soon became a force to be reckoned with both in the world of science and in the personal lives of those who challenged her wit and intelligence. In 1884, she married William Edward Ayrton, a renowned scientist, and they worked closely together on electrical research.

Together they made unbelievable discoveries, such as Hertha’s work on the mechanism of the electric arc, known as the “Ayrton Perry interrupter”. This device was crucial in improving safety and efficiency in electrical generators and her findings were later recognized and applied by the Wright brothers in their early experiments with flight.

From laboratory research to innovative applications, Hertha Marks Ayrton’s work flourished. She penned scientific articles and delivered lectures, eventually earning her membership to the Victorian Institute of Civil Engineers, making her probably the first woman ever to do so.

A Life of Service

Throughout her life, Hertha’s contributions to science were remarkable, yet her adventurous spirit inspired her to make a difference in the lives of others. Besides her groundbreaking scientific work, she authored two books, “The Great Wallace” and “The Motor Wheel”, which made her a noted figure in the world of literature as well.

However, tragedy struck, as Hertha Marks Ayrton was diagnosed with breast cancer in 1908 and passed away in 1923. But even in death, her impact continued to resonate throughout the world. In 1972, the same esteemed Royal College of Science that had once refused to accept her, renamed its Hertha Standing Rocks as a testament to her unwavering spirit and profound influence on science. The good lady’s contribution to the scientific community was acknowledged posthumously as she was inducted into the National Women’s Hall of Fame in 1975.

The story of Hertha Marks Ayrton spans centuries, eras, and boundless horizons of knowledge. A testament to her resilience and unyielding spirit, she bravely shattered the glass ceilings one by one, providing a foundation upon which many other women could follow, soaring into realms once deemed inaccessible, thanks to the ingenious pursuits of Hertha Marks Ayrton.
