Unseen Walls: Fictional Tales About Confronting Racism


Maya stepped off the bus, the cool evening air folding around her like an old quilt. She clutched her bag tighter, her eyes scanning the unfamiliar streets of Willowbrook for a sign of the small apartment she’d be calling home for the next year. The neighborhood looked quiet, almost idyllic, but the subtle tension in the air whispered stories that the skyline couldn’t mask.

She had moved here for her graduate studies, excited to immerse herself in a new environment far from the warm, bustling city she grew up in. But as she walked, Maya couldn’t shake an uneasy feeling, as if invisible barriers lined the streets—walls no one dared to speak of, yet always present.

At the corner market, Maya overheard snippets of conversation that made her heart tighten. Mrs. Jensen, the attendant, muttered about “those new folks moving in” and “changing the character” of the neighborhood. It was the kind of language Maya had heard before, buried under polite smiles in her hometown, but here, it landed like harsh stones in her chest.

Her fingers traced the seams of her book bag, grounding herself. This was a new chapter—one where she decided she wouldn’t shrink away, no matter the invisible walls she might face.

Part Two: The Classroom Divide

Maya’s first week at the university was a whirlwind. The campus was sprawling, filled with students from all walks of life—or so it seemed on promotional posters. Reality, like the neighborhood, was layered with subtle separations.

In her seminar on American Literature, Maya noticed something immediately: the voices that carried the most weight were predictable. Discussions often revolved around classic Western works, sidelining authors of diverse backgrounds. When Maya tentatively introduced a novel by a Black woman writer, the professor gave a polite nod but quickly redirected the conversation.

During group projects, peers often hesitated to partner with her. When a white student finally joined forces with Maya, she caught an accidental slip—a whispered comment about Maya’s “different perspective” as if it were a novelty rather than a valued insight.

One afternoon, laden with frustration, Maya lingered after class. She approached Professor Hamilton, asking if more diverse literary voices might be included in the syllabus.

Professor Hamilton sighed, adjusting his glasses. “We do try to be inclusive,” he said, “but sometimes it’s about the canon—it’s not easy to change decades of tradition overnight.”

Maya wanted to argue, to explain how the canon itself was built on exclusion, but she bit back her words. Instead, she resolved to bring change herself where she could, beginning with the student literary magazine.

Part Three: The Hidden Hostility

Outside campus, the walls erected themselves with sharper edges. In the local café where Maya loved to write, baristas would forget her name or linger awkwardly when she ordered. When a group of patrons saw her sitting alone, they shuffled her way with whispered conversations that stopped abruptly.

One day, Maya spotted a flyer pinned to a bulletin board, advertising a community gathering. The event touted “local colors,” but as she read, the language felt exclusive. Words like “preserving tradition” were coded messages, thinly veiling critiques of the evolving diversity in the area.

Maya decided to attend anyway.

At the gathering, she was met with polite nods and cautious smiles but also with subtle messages of skepticism. An elderly man, Mr. Carmel, approached her during the refreshments.

“You’re new here, aren’t you?” he asked.

“Yes,” Maya replied.

He nodded slowly, eyes narrowing not unkindly, but with a curious weight. “Change isn’t always easy. Sometimes we hold onto what we know because it feels safe.”

Maya saw through the gentle words to the undercurrent of resistance. “I understand,” she said. “But sometimes, holding on too tightly breaks what could grow.”

Their conversation lingered in her mind long after the evening ended. It was a glimpse of the complexity tangled within the unseen walls.

Part Four: New Bridges

A few weeks later, Maya organized a workshop titled “Stories Beyond Invisible Walls,” inviting students and town residents to write and share narratives about race, identity, and belonging. The turnout surprised her—not just people of color, but also those who had rarely sat in such conversations.

During an intimate session, a white student named David shared his story growing up in a homogeneous community, confessing his struggles to recognize his unconscious biases.

Another participant, Latisha, a first-generation immigrant, described the isolation in spaces where culture was unseen or misunderstood.

Maya listened intently, recognizing a pattern: walls were not just built by overt hostility but by absence—the absence of understanding, representation, and acknowledgment.

The workshop became a platform for vulnerability and learning. From these shared stories, bridges began to form.

Part Five: Breaking the Silence

Inspired by the workshop’s impact, Maya collaborated with the student government to advocate for curriculum reform. Their campaign gained momentum, shedding light on the importance of diverse voices and experiences in education.

It was not an easy fight. Resistance came cloaked in bureaucracy, tradition, and subtle undermining. But Maya’s determination sparked conversations, shifting perceptions, one class discussion at a time.

At the end of the semester, the university announced the introduction of new courses centered on multicultural literature and social justice—an acknowledgment of the unseen walls and a first step toward breaking them down.

Epilogue: The Unseen Walls Within

As Maya walked through the neighborhood months later, she noticed change—not just in surroundings but in the atmosphere. The market owner greeted her with a warmer smile, and conversations felt more open, less charged with unspoken fear.

Yet Maya understood the journey was far from over. The unseen walls of racism were not always physical; they existed in hearts, minds, and systems. And while some walls would persist, the commitment to notice, confront, and dismantle them breathed hope.

She stopped beneath a streetlamp, looking up at the stars flickering against the night sky—a myriad of lights shining through the darkness. Maya smiled. Walls could be invisible, but so could the power to transcend them.

Unseen Walls is a reminder that confronting racism requires more than noticing the barriers—it demands courage to cross them, empathy to understand their impact, and resolve to build new spaces of belonging. Maya’s story is but one thread in the vast tapestry of change, beckoning each of us to recognize the unseen and bring it to light.
