Dancing in the Diwali Night: A Festival of Lights Story


Saanvi’s anklets chimed with breathless anticipation as she slipped through the crowded market, a sparkler of excitement fizzing in her chest. Diwali had arrived, transforming every lane in Lucknow into a carnival of color, laughter, and gleaming lights that scattered gold over the old city. Lanterns dangled from every doorway. Sweet shops beckoned with golden laddoos, pistachio cakes, and the rich perfume of rose and cardamom syrup. Saanvi, twelve years old, felt as though she moved through a fairytale, not a city that had felt humdrum and gray just yesterday.

She clutched her mother’s hand tightly, tugging towards a circle of dancers forming on the wide terrace outside Kumari Aunty’s house. “Can I join them? Please?”

Her mother smiled, the reflection of dozens of diya lamps flickering in her eyes. “Only if you don’t trip over your lehenga this time.”

Saanvi grinned, hiking up the scarlet folds of her dress (embroidered just yesterday by her grandmother), and darted barefoot into the open. The dancers were a whirlwind of spinning saris, flashing smiles, and limbs painted henna-red. Ritika, her cheeky neighbor, beckoned from the circle. Saanvi took her place, and at that moment, the music caught her like a swift current.

It was the kind of drumbeat that demanded motion. Clapping, stomping, swirling patterns traced by eager feet. The tabla thudded. The sitar arced overhead like the moon’s own song. Every note seemed to catch in her, urging her faster and faster. The older girls laughed encouragement, hair spinning in dark, streaming ribbons. Saanvi lost herself, swept by the joy of dancing under a heaven mapped with fireworks.

In the distance, the city exploded in color—rockets, chrysanthemums of gold and green. Each burst of light sent shadows reeling, chased darkness away. Saanvi leapt into the air, wishing she, too, could be a firecracker.

Hours before, the festival had begun with ritual and reverence. Saanvi and her family gathered to honor Lakshmi, goddess of wealth and fortune. Her father handed her a diya, its cotton wick floating, a humble gem of flame cupped between her palms. As the prayers ended, one by one, they set their diyas afloat on large brass plates and placed them along the sill, illuminating the house with points of gentle light.

Neighbors wandered in—Aunties in gold-trimmed saris, uncles balancing plates of sweets. Laughter spilled down the hallways. Soon, the children gathered on the back verandah for the annual lighting of sparklers. Saanvi’s uncle distributed long, slim sticks and matches, his knuckles dusted sugar-white from rolling barfi sweets. Saanvi watched the first spark flare, trembling with anticipation.

The little flame found the tip of her sparkler, which shivered into raucous hissing silver. Her brother, Nikhil, swooped through the garden, composing trails of light in looping letters. Someone produced a cone fountain, which spat out showers of emerald twinkles.

But all the fire outside seemed to pale next to the fire within her feet, now as she spun on that terrace beneath the stars, caught up in the ache of the music, the press of palms against hers, the urging shouts, “Faster! Again! Don’t stop!”

The song changed. Someone called, “Now Dandiya!” and thrust painted sticks into her hands. The dance grew sharper, more rhythmic, a living pattern, partners switching, faces whirling by like so many colored lanterns. Down below, in the square, streams of people passed carrying trays of sweets and stacks of marigold garlands. The festival had transformed the old world; Diwali made it possible for everyone to reimagine themselves radiant, for one night at least.

At the edge of the terrace, Saanvi paused to catch her breath. A tiny boy, face smudged with gulab jamun syrup, stared up at her, wide-eyed. He was holding a broken sparkler.

“Did you want another?” she asked, kneeling beside him. “We have some left!”

He shook his head but did not smile. “I can’t dance. My feet hurt.”

Saanvi considered. “Would you like to watch?” She swept her hand toward the crowd, the music rising as cousins and neighbors picked up the steps of another folk song. “Or you could drum?” Her cousin Arjun, in charge of the hand drums, caught her eye. Within minutes, the little boy was drumming, haphazard but beaming, surrounded by encouragement.

People arrived and left: cousins returning from distant cities, an elderly neighbor who could remember dancing here decades ago, a new family whose daughter tried every step and giggled at each mistake. Light wove through them: from the strings of electric bulbs, from moon-silver on saris, from lanterns flickering against plastered walls, and from the inexhaustible flames of their delight.

Later, after the last round of dance, the terrace shimmered with families trading stories, eating mango barfi and roasted cashews, laughter rising with the smoke of sparklers. Saanvi wandered to where her grandmother sat, wrapped in a deep blue shawl.

“Come here, little dancer,” said Dadi, patting the step beside her.

Saanvi curled up, sweat cooling on her skin.

“It was wonderful,” she said. “Like…everything was glowing inside me. More than lights, even.”

Dadi nodded. “That is what Diwali is meant to do. Long ago, there were more shadows than lights—difficult harvests, lost hopes. People lit diyas to remind themselves darkness never lasts, not when we’re together. When we dance, celebrate, and share sweets, we chase away every shadow.”

Saanvi watched the fireworks splitting the sky over the rooftops. Somewhere below, her friends and family picked up another song. She thought she could see the whole world, for one moment, reflected in a single candle flame.

As midnight neared, guests finally drifted away. Left behind was a faint hush of old songs, a scatter of wilted rose petals, the smokey aroma of burnt wicks and sweets long devoured. Saanvi and Nikhil slumped beneath the lanterns on the now-empty terrace.

“Tomorrow will be boring,” Nikhil sighed, toeing a spent sparkler.

Saanvi wearily reached out, drawing invisible patterns in the air—a dance only she could see. Her feet were sore, her belly full, her cheeks smudged with laughter. Yet the feeling lingered: a secret, golden warmth she could keep for the whole year, or maybe forever.

“But tomorrow isn’t here yet,” Saanvi said, and leapt up for one last spin. Her anklets winked like their own tiny stars. “There’s still tonight—and tonight, the lights never go out.”

Below, the moon hung low and bright. Someone on a distant rooftop sent a single, silent rocket roaring skyward. Saanvi watched it bloom, a brief, spectacular flower against the night.

In that shimmering second, she danced—not for anyone else, not even for the festival, but for the impossible, magical promise that comes at Diwali: that even a small light can set the darkness trembling, and that every step taken in joy is a step towards morning.

And so the festival, like its stories, continued—carried in memory, kept alive in the laughter of children, the three-step pulse of music, and the glimmering hope that every Diwali night brings.
