The Color of Injustice: Short Fiction on Racism


The sun hung low over Marigold Street, casting long shadows that stretched like silent fingers across cracked sidewalks and peeling storefronts. The air was thick with the hum of a city bearing the weight of its own history, the kind of history that whispered in alleyway corners and gritted its teeth behind polite smiles.

Elias Turner adjusted the collar of his worn coat, clutching the small manila envelope tightly in his trembling hand. The paper could have changed everything—or nothing. It was a list, but more than that, it was a story stitched together in faded ink, a story no one wanted to face.

For years, Elias had lived the invisible life of Black men in his neighborhood. A life where every nod from a police officer felt like a loaded gun aimed quietly at his back, where every glance was a silent accusation. He had learned early that the color of his skin was like a shadow that shifted with the light—sometimes faint, sometimes suffocatingly dark.

Today, he wasn’t just Elias Turner, mechanic and neighbor. He was the bearer of truth, a reluctant witness to a web of injustice entwined in the fabric of the city’s law enforcement.

Two weeks earlier, the events unfolded like a tempest. Marcus Jennings, a bright young man with dreams as vast as the sky, had been taken from the streets in a blink. Shot.

The official story was simple: Marcus had resisted arrest, had reached for a weapon, had endangered lives. The officers were cleared.

But Elias knew better. Marcus had been unarmed, terrified. He had been running—running from the very people sworn to protect him.

The evidence was there. Hidden surveillance videos, eyewitness accounts, murmurs that twisted through the neighborhood like the wind. Yet no one listened, not truly.

Elias pressed his fingers against the envelope, feeling the weight of a thousand unheard voices.

He walked to the corner store, where people gathered beneath the flickering neon sign, faces etched with worry and tired resilience. Children played on cracked steps, their laughter mingling with the distant sirens—a haunting lullaby.

“Have you heard anything?” Elias asked Marsha, the owner. Her eyes reflected a quiet storm.

“Only what the news says,” she replied, voice low. “But you know better.”

The color of injustice didn’t wear a single face. It was in every whispered doubt, every assumption, every blind eye turned away from the truth.

That night, Elias met with Lena, Marcus’s sister, in the cramped living room of their shared apartment. The walls were thin, papering over lives stretched tight by grief and hope.

“I don’t want to believe he’s gone because they didn’t see him as human,” Lena said, voice breaking. “I want justice.”

Elias nodded. “That’s why I have this.”

He slid the envelope across the table. “Proof. We need to make them see.”

Their plan was dangerous: exposing a system built to preserve itself, no matter the cost. But silence was complicity, and the color of injustice refused to fade into the background.

Days passed in a whirlwind of meetings, whispered phone calls, and cautious alliances. Elias and Lena reached out to journalists, activists, and legal advocates who understood the weight of the truth.

Rachel, an investigative reporter hardened by years of battling systemic corruption, became their unexpected ally. She poured over the evidence, drawing connections and exposing patterns buried beneath layers of denial.

Together, they uncovered not just the circumstances of Marcus’s death, but a wider tapestry of racial profiling, excessive force, and a culture that prioritized power over people.

The city’s response was predictably mixed. Protests erupted, fueled by a collective pain and a desperate demand for change. Some voices decried the unrest, painting it as chaos rather than courage.

Elias stood at the front lines, feeling the sting of tear gas and the solidarity of thousands. The color of injustice was bright now, impossible to ignore.

But within the flames of protest, he also saw embers of hope—the possibility that knowledge, courage, and unity might yet transform the narrative.

Months later, in a packed courtroom, the evidence was laid bare. The jury saw the videos, heard the testimonies, felt the pulse of a city demanding accountability.

The verdict was not the end, but a beginning—a tentative step toward justice in a system that had long forgotten its meaning.

Elias watched Lena outside the courthouse, her face a mixture of exhaustion and resolve. The color of injustice was not a single shade but a spectrum of struggle, pain, and determination.

And though the road ahead was uncertain, with every story told and every voice lifted, it grew just a little brighter.

In the quiet moments that followed, Elias understood the truth at the heart of it all: Racism was a wound that bled beneath the surface, staining everything it touched. But in facing that wound openly, exposing its color to the world, healing could begin.

The sun rose again over Marigold Street, casting light on cracked sidewalks, resilient faces, and the promise of a day where justice might finally shine in every hue.
