Existentialism Explored: A Short Story Dive into Meaning


Elias sat alone in his dimly lit apartment, the pale glow of a streetlamp seeping through the cracked window. Rain tapped softly against the glass, a rhythm that seemed to echo the beating of his own restless heart. Tonight was one of those nights when the universe felt vast and indifferent, a cold expanse indifferent to the questions stirring in his mind.

For weeks now, Elias had been grappling with a profound sense of emptiness. The routine of his daily life—a job that paid the bills but never fulfilled, friendships that felt shallow, and a city pulsing with life yet somehow alien—had begun to crumble under the weight of one relentless question: What is the meaning of it all?

He reached for his journal, fingers trembling slightly. Writing had always been his refuge, the place where thoughts scattered through his mind could take shape, find form. He began to write not just to document his feelings but to untangle the web of existential doubt that ensnared him.

The Absurdity of Existence

Elias’s thoughts floated toward the philosophy he had recently encountered — existentialism. The words of Jean-Paul Sartre and Albert Camus echoed in his memory, philosophers who spoke not of answers but of clarity in embracing the absurd.

The absurdity of existence, Camus had said, was rooted in the conflict between humanity’s desire for meaning and a silent, indifferent universe. Elias pondered this dissonance. The universe didn’t owe him a reason for his being. It simply was.

As rain continued to fall, he thought about the myth of Sisyphus, condemned to push a boulder up a hill only to watch it roll down again, endlessly. The image was grim, but Camus had argued that one must imagine Sisyphus happy. Because in embracing the struggle itself, in the act of rebellion against absurdity, meaning could be found.

Could Elias find meaning not in answers, but in the struggle—the effort, the living itself?

Freedom and Responsibility

In his research, Elias had stumbled upon Sartre’s assertion that existence precedes essence. That humans are not born with a given purpose; they must create their essence through choices. This freedom was exhilarating yet crushing.

The freedom to define who he was—and who he would become—felt like standing on the edge of a cliff without a safety net. Was he truly free to choose his path, or was he merely trapped by circumstances and past decisions?

He recalled the morning that had led him here, the choice he had delayed for weeks: whether to quit his job and pursue writing full-time. How much of that decision was true freedom, and how much was fear disguised as routine?

Sartre’s notion that with freedom came responsibility weighed heavily. Freedom was not just a gift but a burden—each decision etched the contours of his being, with no one else to blame or praise.

The Encounter

Elias pushed back from his desk and walked to the window, watching shadows merge with the rain-soaked streets below. The city seemed alive yet silent in some paradoxical way.

Just then, his phone buzzed. A message from Lila, an old friend who’d studied philosophy too, appeared: Coffee? I have a story about Kierkegaard that might help.

A flicker of hope sparked in Elias’s chest as he typed back eagerly. Maybe a conversation could assuage the turmoil within.

Kierkegaard’s Leap of Faith

At the small café filled with the hum of warm conversations and clinking cups, Lila greeted Elias with a smile that felt like a balm. Over steaming mugs, she shared Kierkegaard’s insight about the “leap of faith.” Unlike the rational, calculated approach to life, Kierkegaard argued that faith—whether religious or existential—required embracing uncertainty, a passionate commitment despite the lack of objective certainty.

The idea struck Elias deeply. Perhaps, he mused, life’s meaning was not to be discovered with cold logic but something to be chosen through conviction and commitment.

Lila elaborated, “It’s about embracing your own subjective truth. You have to make the leap, even if you don’t know what lies ahead.”

Her words wandered with him through the night, mingling with his thoughts on freedom and absurdity.

Confronting Angst

Later that evening, alone once again, Elias faced another philosopher’s shadow: existential angst—this profound anxiety stemming from the realization of one’s own freedom and isolation.

His heart raced as he considered the isolation each person endured at the core of their existence. No one could live his life for him; no one could bear the final responsibility.

But wasn’t this angst a kind of awakening too? A recognition that beneath the surface noise and distractions lay a raw, undeniable freedom—freedom to choose, to rebel, to create meaning.

Elias closed his eyes, breathing deeply. Fear was present, but courage flickered alongside it.

Meaning in Creation

Days passed, and with each, Elias began to write more consistently. Not just the melancholy reflections, but stories, poems, small bursts of creativity that expressed fragments of self.

Slowly, an idea took hold: meaning was less about uncovering a universal truth and more about the act of creating one’s own truth through lived experience.

Each word, each choice to write, to connect with others, seemed to push back against the abyss. The struggle was real and relentless, but so was the possibility of forging joy, purpose, and connection within it.

The Decision

One morning, Elias stood before his apartment window, the city alive with dawn’s soft light. The decision that had haunted him felt less daunting, the cliff less terrifying.

He would quit the job. Not because absolute certainty awaited him on the other side, but because the leap itself was an act of freedom and commitment, a way to live authentically.

There would be no grand epiphany, no algorithm to decode the purpose of existence. Instead, there was a choice—his choice—made in full awareness of chaos, ambiguity, and freedom.

Conclusion: Embracing the Abyss

In the end, Elias learned that the quest for meaning in an indifferent universe was less a search for answers, and more a journey of ongoing creation.

Existentialism, with its grappling of freedom, absurdity, angst, and faith, offered not despair, but a framework for embracing life’s inherent uncertainty and crafting significance through conscious choice.

The city outside continued its endless buzz, indifferent still, but within Elias stirred a quiet rebellion—a commitment to live, not despite the abyss, but because of it.

Elias smiled softly, his journal open beside him, the next blank page waiting.
The story of meaning was not written in stone. It was a story he would write every day.
