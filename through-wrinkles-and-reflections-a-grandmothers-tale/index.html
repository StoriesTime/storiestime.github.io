Through Wrinkles and Reflections: A Grandmother's Tale


There was a thick stillness that hung in the air of the old house, broken only by the occasional creak of the wooden floorboards or the faint tick-tock of the grandfather clock. It was the kind of house that seemed to breathe on its own, alive with memories etched into its every corner. The walls bore photographs long faded by time, their inhabitants caught mid-laughter, mid-dance, mid-life, looking out forever at a world they no longer lived in.

Zara sat cross-legged on the worn Persian rug at the foot of the armchair, tracing the faded pattern with her fingertips. To her, the room smelled both familiar and foreign, a mixture of lavender sachets, old books, and the peculiar air of history. The sound of a deep inhale drew her attention, and she glanced up.

Her grandmother, Noor, sat in the armchair, her fingers wrapped tightly around a small silver-framed mirror. Its edges were engraved with vines and blossoms, and though the glass was clean, it held a certain kind of distortion, as though it showed more than the image before it. Noor’s thumb traced the curves of the frame absently. Her face was lined with wrinkles, each crease telling its own story—a quiet testimony to decades of life lived with vigor. Her ash-white hair was tied neatly into a bun, though a few rebellious strands escaped its tether. But it was her eyes that always captivated Zara. They were sharp, brimming with secrets and fire, as though they held entire worlds concealed behind their gaze.

“Will you tell me now, Nani?” Zara asked, her voice soft but filled with curiosity.

For weeks, she had seen Noor with the mirror, cradling it in her lap as if it were fragile as spun glass. She would sometimes stare into its surface with such intensity that Zara had begun to wonder what she saw in its depths. Noor had only smiled when Zara asked, her response always some version of “Soon.” But today felt different. The air in the room buzzed faintly with unspoken energy.

Finally, Noor sighed and shifted in her chair. “Very well, child. But be warned: this isn’t a story of princes or fairy-tale castles. This is a story stitched from reality, folded into years.”

Zara nodded eagerly.

“Then listen closely,” Noor began, and her fingers curled once more around the mirror as though drawing resolve from its touch. “For this tale begins long before you or your mother ever existed, in a village far removed from the hum of the modern world.”

The village lay nestled in the crook of a valley, its thatched roofs rising like freckles against the emerald canvas of paddy fields. It was a simple place, unburdened by electricity or haste. Noor was barely sixteen when her life underwent its first upheaval. She had grown accustomed to the rhythm of the village, the whispers of the river that snaked nearby, the songs the wind brought from the mountains. But it was the year monsoons came too soon and stayed too long that etched the first deep lines into her young heart.

“Our home was swallowed by the river,” Noor said, her voice steady yet tinged with a distant ache. “One moment, it was standing firm. The next, the earth had crumbled beneath it, taking my dog, our warmth, and the only belongings we had known into the depths of churning waters. My father… he came and told me we must start over.”

Zara’s brows furrowed as she tried to imagine losing everything all at once. But before she could form the words that lingered on her lips, Noor continued.

“We left the village for the city. I had never seen a city before. Oh, how it clamored! A cacophony of wheels screeching against cobblestones, vendors shouting their wares, and fires crackling in makeshift stoves. It was nothing like the serene streams and whispering fields I knew. I detested it at first, but my father…”

Noor paused, a smile ghosting her lips though her throat bobbed as if swallowing something heavy.

“He had a way of seeing the light where others saw only shadows. He found work as a mechanic, despite having no experience. Every evening, he came home with grease under his nails and a grin that defied his exhaustion. He was my first lesson in resilience.”

A quiet filled the room, different from the one that had settled before—the kind born of respect and reflection.

Noor soon found work herself, stitching clothes in a factory alongside other young girls. Her fingers became swift and precise, and she learned how to whisper dreams into the stitches of every garment she touched. But the city was not kind to a young girl with big eyes and a trusting heart.

“There was a man,” Noor said, her voice dropping so low that Zara instinctively inched closer to hear. “He was one of the overseers. His hands were quick to grab, to take what he wanted, heedless of cries or pleading.”

Zara’s stomach curled, but she resisted the urge to interrupt.

“One rainy afternoon,” Noor continued, her eyes growing distant, “he cornered me near the back alley of the factory. I was trembling, my voice stolen with fear. But before he could lay a hand on me, an old woman appeared.”

This time, the tiniest smile flickered on Noor’s face. “She was bent over with age, her hair a wild halo of white, as though she had wrestled with light itself and won. She held a mirror in her gnarled hand—a mirror much like this one.” Noor held the small, ornate mirror closer to Zara.

“She held it out, forcing the man to look into it, and I’ll never forget the look on his face: horror, shame, regret. He stumbled back as though struck and ran without another word. The old woman pressed the mirror into my hand and disappeared.”

Life grew no easier, but the mirror became Noor’s guarded treasure. It wasn’t until years later, after the city had re-shaped her into a tougher version of herself, that she began to understand its power.

“My daughter—that is, your mother—was a child when it became clear the mirror was not ordinary,” Noor said, softly laughing at Zara’s jaw dropping slightly. “I had glanced into it one evening, exhausted from the day’s work. But instead of my reflection, it showed me something I had buried deep inside—a version of myself mired in anger and pessimism. I cried that night, felt as though it had flayed my skin to reveal who I was becoming.”

“So, it makes you see who you truly are?” Zara whispered, her curiosity piqued.

“Not just that,” Noor corrected. “It helps you see what you need to. At times it reflects truth. At others, it may reflect pain yet to come—or joy lingering just out of reach.”

Noor’s story unraveled further, moving through the decades of her life: her marriage to a man who softened her sharp edges but taught her that heartbreak often walked hand in hand with love; her struggles during lean years when her fingers stiffened but still sewed to keep her family fed; and her eventual decision to let old grudges with estranged friends dissolve, spurred by glances into the silvery glass.

“Every wrinkle,” Noor said, “carries a reflection, whether it’s one the mirror revealed or one I found on my own.”

By the time Noor’s voice grew quiet, the room had shifted slightly. The fading sunlight through the windows drenched everything in a soft, golden hue. Zara felt the weight of the story settle in her chest like a warm stone. She looked at Noor’s mirror, wondering what it might show her.

“What will I see if I look into it?” she asked.

Noor smiled, not answering directly. Instead, she handed the mirror to Zara. Its surface caught the light, gleaming with a mysterious sheen.

“I can’t tell you that, child,” Noor murmured. “But I can promise this—whatever you see will linger. And I hope it will guide you through your own chapters yet to be written.”

As Zara tilted the mirror to catch her reflection, her heart quickened. But what she saw… well, that is another story yet to be told.
