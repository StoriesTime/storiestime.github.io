The Colors of Dusk: A Memoir of the Holi Festival


I vividly recall the dusk of a Holi festival in my native village, tucked away in the lush green mountains of India. The thrilling colors of that day were astounding and unforgettable.

The village celebrations began five days before the main festivity, commencing with the sunrise. Each morning, people gathered at a traditional Thar Desert [-]:houses adorned with peacock feathers, brightly colored clothes hanging out to dry, and delicious smells wafting from kitchens. The highlight of these five days was a warm-up procession that impressed everyone with its unparalleled joyous spirit. A series of dances were performed to fill every inch of the village with vibrant energy.

On the eve of the Holi festival, we children gleefully engaged in the ‘phagwa’ dance, twirling and swinging our hips to the folk tunes. Our laughter echoed through the village, spreading waves of merriment.

As midnight approached, we ran down to the spring to pick ‘kola:’, symbols of love and fondness. In Holi celebrations, we collect droplets of water and then drips them onto closed doors of our homes.

The following day arrived in the absence of morning sunlight. Their absence surprisingly added to the mysticism of the day. The entire village embraced darkness that seemed to be trying to swallow the alive murmur of our village. The darkness was pierced by bonfires lite at some corners of our village - a customary fiery Holi return to normalcy, as if to demonstrate power over the mysterious night.

The rituals of this mystic evening were carried out under the light of candles and mirrors. In our village, this festival is not only about colors; it is an allegory for life.

Finally, Holi dawned, and we woke up to the first rays of the sun. We reveled in an atmosphere filled with a sense of anticipation and pure joy. Everyone was dressed in bright colors - reds, greens , yellows, purples, and blues.

Children darted around with pichkari [-: a traditional water gun used to celebrate Holi]s a tradition that reminds us to resolve conflicts with love. Meanwhile, elders viewed the dawn with an ambiance filled with calm, preaching wisdom, and peace.

As the dusk set in, a sudden chill whispered through the air, signaling the end of Holi. I later realized that just like the Holi festival, this silent awe-filled chill was life. The bonfire’s embers still glowed as we smothered each other with color. It depicted life: vibrant, chaotic, and beautiful, yet always embracing us in a meaningful silence at the end.

The festival was a symbol of life: such chaotic, intense, vivid, energetic, unpredictable. But also, calm and serene like the oppressive post-Holi darkness filled with quiet, peaceful whispers that tell stories of a future unknown.

Just like the Holi festival, life is full of an array of experiences. It’s very beautiful, but it can also be dull, dull like water, darkness. But, more than anything, just like Holi, life ends not with a bang but a chill. This chill forces us to return to our regular lives, even if it feels a bit oppressive.

The tools we use to celebrate Holi are simple yet profound: colors, water guns, and lamps are all just uteranisms of our emotions. This celebration of Holi is our way of beatifying and beautifying our life, which is much more profound than just any event.

From the depths of my heart, I remember that Holi festival with profound affection. And each time, this celebration revives my life, just like those cool windswept nights, rousing us from dreamy slumber, reminding us of our reality.

Holi memories are no distant memories, my friend; they are stories I tell myself to remember life better.
