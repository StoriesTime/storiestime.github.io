How Not to Flirt: Hilarious Tales from Awkward Dates


It was a Friday night, and Kevin, a 29-year-old marketing executive with a casual wardrobe and a reputation for awkwardness, found himself staring at his phone. It had been a long week, but he was still excited for his date with Sarah. They met on a dating app—one of those where people list their interests, and in a rare moment of honesty, Kevin had added, “I’m not good at flirting, but I’m pretty good at ordering pizza.”

Sarah had messaged him first, and he couldn’t help but be drawn in by her profile. She loved pizza too, and that was a shared passion that felt like fate. It seemed like a perfect match.

They decided on a casual meet-up at a local pizzeria, a cozy spot known for its wood-fired crusts and a “build-your-own” pizza option that encouraged conversation. Kevin was excited—maybe a little too excited.

As he walked into the restaurant, he spotted Sarah at a corner booth, looking effortlessly chic in a simple black dress. Kevin, in his usual jeans and a slightly wrinkled shirt, felt like a misplaced potato sack beside her. He gave a small wave, and she smiled warmly, which put him at ease for about a millisecond.

“Hey, Kevin! It’s so nice to finally meet you in person!” Sarah said, standing to give him a friendly hug.

“Yeah, totally! Same here! Uh… I mean, this is great! This pizza place is… pizza. It’s got pizza. You like pizza, right?” Kevin stammered.

Sarah raised an eyebrow, but her smile didn’t falter. “I’m a fan, obviously. Let’s grab a menu.”

As they sat down, Kevin immediately launched into the topic of pizza toppings. “Okay, so, I’m pretty basic with pizza—just pepperoni and maybe some mushrooms, but if you’re into pineapple, I guess we can be rebels together.”

Sarah laughed, a genuine, sweet sound. “I’m not a pineapple person, but I respect the boldness. How about you order, and I’ll let you surprise me?”

That was the moment. Kevin, in his eagerness to impress, ordered a large pizza with every topping imaginable—sausage, olives, green peppers, anchovies, and yes, even pineapple. As the waiter walked away with the order, Kevin couldn’t help but feel a surge of confidence.

“So, uh, how’s your week been?” Kevin asked, trying to steer the conversation away from his food choices.

Sarah smiled, but there was something almost sad in her expression. “It’s been alright. You know, work and life. What about you?”

Kevin, feeling like the conversation had hit a rough patch, decided to lighten the mood. “Well, you know, I spent a solid hour trying to figure out how to text my mom without sounding like a 12-year-old. It’s a talent.”

Sarah’s eyes widened, and she laughed. “Wait, you’re telling me you’ve mastered the art of pizza ordering but not texting your mom?”

Kevin nodded solemnly. “Exactly. I’m just a modern man, struggling in the digital age.”

When the pizza finally arrived, it was a glorious disaster—an absolute mountain of ingredients. Kevin smiled sheepishly. “Uh, so… this is what happens when I get carried away.”

Sarah blinked at the monstrosity in front of her. “It’s… impressive. How do you even eat that?”

“Very carefully,” Kevin replied, attempting to pick up a slice. The pizza, loaded with too many toppings, began to collapse under its own weight. Kevin, trying to salvage the moment, shoved a bit too much into his mouth and immediately began choking.

“Oh my god, Kevin!” Sarah exclaimed, reaching out, but in the chaos, Kevin tried to clear his throat and ended up knocking over his water glass, spilling it all over the table.

“I’m fine! Totally fine! This is fine!” Kevin gasped, wiping his face with a napkin, his cheeks bright red.

Sarah stared at him in wide-eyed disbelief, but then she burst out laughing. “You’re… you’re something else.”

It wasn’t exactly the smooth evening he had envisioned, but Sarah was laughing, which in Kevin’s book meant he hadn’t completely ruined it.

Chapter 2: The Botched Compliment

The next week, Kevin decided he had to redeem himself. He knew he had to take a more refined approach, so he planned a dinner at a slightly fancier restaurant. Nothing too extravagant, just a cozy spot with dim lighting and a menu that sounded like it belonged in a French film.

Sarah arrived dressed in a flowy red dress that accentuated her natural elegance. Kevin, in his usual style, had opted for a button-up shirt that was just a little too tight. He had spent the past hour in front of the mirror, trying to figure out how to compliment her without sounding like he was reading off a script.

“Hey, you look… wow. I mean, you look really… uh… good? No, wait, that’s not right… um, you look great,” Kevin stammered as Sarah approached the table.

“Thanks,” Sarah said with a bemused grin, clearly trying to make him feel at ease. “You look… nice too.”

“Nice! Yeah, exactly! You know, nice is the best word for it,” Kevin replied, nodding as if he had just given a TED Talk on compliments. “Nice is underappreciated. Like… pizza! You know what I mean?”

Sarah’s eyes twinkled, but she raised a playful eyebrow. “I think I do, but I’m still not entirely sure where we’re going with this.”

Just as Kevin was about to speak, the waiter arrived to take their order. Sarah ordered a salad, Kevin opted for a steak. The moment she left, Kevin felt his nerves creeping back in. He needed something to keep the conversation flowing, something that wouldn’t involve pizza, or choking on food.

“So, uh, I’ve been thinking… you seem like the kind of person who’s really good at… I don’t know, stuff. Like, life stuff. You have this whole vibe,” Kevin said, his hands gesturing awkwardly in the air.

“Life stuff, huh?” Sarah laughed. “I’m intrigued. What’s the ‘vibe’ you’re talking about?”

Kevin felt a bead of sweat form on his forehead. “You know… like, you have your life together. You do things with purpose. You know what you want… unlike me, who… uh… just bought a cactus last week and now has no idea how to take care of it.”

“Wait, you bought a cactus and don’t know how to take care of it? What’s the plan here, Kevin?”

“I’m just hoping it’ll survive… by some miracle,” he admitted, his voice now a bit quieter.

Their food arrived just in time to prevent further disaster. Kevin tried his best to cut his steak with finesse, but his knife seemed to have a vendetta against him. Every attempt was met with a dramatic squeak, like a failing violin string.

“Do you need help?” Sarah asked, leaning forward with an amused expression.

“No! I got this!” Kevin replied, giving up and cutting the steak into a million tiny pieces. When he finally managed to eat one of them, he tried to act like it was the most sophisticated bite of steak anyone had ever taken.

“Yup, nailed it.”

Sarah, ever the sport, took a small bite of her salad and smiled. “You’re definitely one-of-a-kind, Kevin.”

Chapter 3: The Phone Fail

As the date continued, Kevin’s nerves seemed to intensify. He was determined to salvage his charm, but everything he said felt forced. They were talking about hobbies when Kevin, in an effort to seem more cultured, reached into his pocket to pull out his phone.

“Have you heard of this new podcast? It’s about—”

Suddenly, Kevin’s phone started blasting “Eye of the Tiger” at full volume. It was set to his ringtone, and he had forgotten to silence it. The table froze. Sarah blinked.

“Is that… is that your ringtone?” she asked, trying not to laugh.

“Uh, yeah! It’s… an empowering choice," Kevin said, quickly fumbling with the phone. He tried to silence it, but instead, the volume increased.

“Looks like your ringtone is ready to go to war,” Sarah said, giggling.

Kevin turned even redder. “It’s for motivational purposes,” he muttered, “I swear, it’s not as bad as it sounds.”

By the time the date ended, Kevin knew this would be one to remember—though not for the reasons he had hoped. They parted ways with an awkward hug, both chuckling about the bizarre evening. And yet, Kevin couldn’t help but feel like he had learned something important.

Flirting wasn’t about being smooth, or perfect, or trying too hard. It was about embracing the awkwardness, laughing at yourself, and letting things unfold naturally. Maybe next time, he’d remember to silence his phone before the date. And, just maybe, order a pizza with a little more restraint.

Conclusion: Flirting Lessons from Awkward Dates

Through Kevin’s hilarious misadventures in the world of awkward dates, one truth became clear: the best flirting happens when you’re not trying too hard to be someone you’re not. While these tales are a little cringeworthy, they’re also a reminder that a sense of humor, a willingness to laugh at yourself, and a genuine interest in the other person can go a long way in making a date memorable for all the right reasons. So, the next time you’re navigating the world of flirting, remember Kevin’s story—sometimes, the most awkward moments are the ones that lead to the best connections.
