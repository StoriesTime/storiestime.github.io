Cooking My Way Through the World: A Gourmet Adventure


The idea struck me in a bustling café in Seville. It was a breezy autumn afternoon, the scent of coffee swirling around ochre-tiled floors and ancient stone walls. I scrolled through my newsfeed absentmindedly between sips, thumbing past yet another travel influencer balancing on a cliff or modeling a flowing dress by turquoise Mediterranean waves. For a moment, I wondered when travel became all about shots for the followers, and less about actually being there.

But it was my empanada that interrupted my reverie. Flaky, golden pastry — and the filling: beef, green olives, a tangle of sweet onions, spices I could only guess. The first bite was a revelation. My forks paused in midair. That’s it, I thought. That’s why I travel. I want to cook my way across the world — not just taste, but create these flavors.

Three weeks later, I was clearing out my apartment in Chicago, waving goodbye to the skyline one more time, and boarding a flight with only a backpack, a notebook, and a set of knives I swaddled in my grandmother’s embroidered kitchen towel. I had no route, little plan, and less certainty, but burning curiosity.

Chapter One: Spain — Chasing the Sizzle of Salamanca

My gourmet journey began in Salamanca, a Spanish city compact yet labyrinthine, where sunlight gilded the sandstone buildings every evening. I secured a spot in a local culinary school not as a student, but as an enthusiastic interloper. Chef Magdalena, with a voice like gravel and movements sharp as paprika, took me in after hearing my story.

The kitchen was a symphony of sizzling pans and shouted jokes in rapid, staccato Spanish. My job started simple: peeling garlic, cleaning greens, scraping crusts off well-used paella pans. But by night two, Magdalena let me prep the sofrito, the base of so many Spanish stews. Tomatoes, onions, and peppers stewed until melting — fragrant, hearty, deeply red. She refused to measure ingredients. “Tus manos son tus ojos,” she barked. Your hands are your eyes. I chopped, sniffed, tasted, and for the first time, cooked paella not by numbers, but music.

In the evenings, my classmates and I roamed street markets teeming with jamón Ibérico hung in fragrant rows, blood oranges heaped in pyramids, wheels of manchego cheese stacked like treasure. We swapped stories and secrets: how to coax sweetness from onions, the best way to crack almonds, why saffron costs a king’s ransom — and how a pinch changes everything.

Chapter Two: Morocco — Spice and Flame in Marrakech

Next, Marrakech: an explosion of color and rhythm, where snake charmers spun music between their fingers and smoke billowed from charcoal grills.

I joined Amina, a home cook renowned for her couscous and for never following a written recipe. We met before dawn in the medina. Bright-eyed, she led me past stalls fragrant with cumin and cinnamon, to tiny shops selling only preserved lemons or prunes or mint. She taught me to haggle; or, as she preferred to say, to charm the keepers until prices melted away.

Cooking tagine became a meditation in patience and layering: saffron blooming in broth, lamb softening beneath river rocks, apricots simmering until they surrendered to the savory spices. Amina’s kitchen was always filled with laughter and friends wandering in, drawn by the scent. “Never cook for one,” she said, “you might feed a city.” Every dish was a tapestry of flavors — sweet, spicy, crunch, melt.

Nights in Marrakech weren’t complete without street food: grilled merguez sausage, piping hot khobz bread, harira soup thick with tomatoes and lentils. I recorded recipes in my notebook, fingers sticky with honey and semolina.

Chapter Three: Italy — Learning Nonna’s Hands in Bologna

Italy beckoned with the promise of pasta. Bologna, a city devoted to food, was my pilgrimage. Through a friend of a friend, I met Nonna Lucia — a woman whose handshake was firmer than any chef’s grip.

Her kitchen was chaos: flour clouds, rolling pins, laughter over gossip. She taught me to make tortellini, kneading dough until silky, thin as paper. “You must feel when it is right,” she insisted, pressing my palm into the dough. Each tiny dumpling was hand-folded around pork and Parmesan, sealed with a twist only practice could perfect.

We cooked ragù for hours, meats and tomatoes simmering until the sauce turned velvety. We debated basil — before or after? — and why Italian food had to be slow-cooked and shared. I became part of her Sunday lunches, extended family and neighbors crowding around a long wooden table, wine tumbling into glasses. Each meal felt like communion.

In the evenings, I wandered arcades and trattorias, learning to taste olive oil blind, classify Parmigiano by scent, and recognize a “good” espresso the Italian way: a single, perfect ounce, just bitter enough to wake the soul.

Chapter Four: Thailand — Street Markets and Songthaews in Chiang Mai

I swapped Europe’s winter for the humid vibrancy of Chiang Mai. The air was thick with lemongrass and diesel, the city alive with motorbikes and monks clothed in saffron.

I joined a class at a tiny open-air kitchen owned by Mai, whose fiery wok skills defied her years. We started every day at the fresh market, squeezing kaffir lime leaves, snapping green beans, sniffing for the right young ginger. Mai’s philosophy was simple: balance. Spicy, sweet, salty, sour. A dish could be rescued or ruined with a single spritz of fish sauce.

I learned to pound papaya salad until sweet, tart ribbons merged with chili heat. I ladled coconut curries, adjusting flavors born in the tongue and nose. Night markets became a second classroom: skewers of grilled chicken, steaming bowls of khao soi, fat mangoes with sticky rice. I scribbled tips in my notebook, hands tingling from chopped chilies.

The hospitality was open-hearted. Mai’s family invited me to a festival dinner where we made sai oua — spiced northern sausages — twisting sticky casing beside grandmothers and giggling cousins. Laughter became the seasoning.

Chapter Five: Japan — Precision and Spirit in Kyoto

Kyoto drew me east with its promise of reverence for the ingredient. My teacher, Tomo-san, was a quiet master of kaiseki — traditional multi-course Japanese dining. His tiny restaurant, hidden in a side street, had no sign. Diners arrived by word of mouth or luck.

No flavor was an accident. We spent hours washing rice, perfecting knife cuts, shaping sashimi so delicate it shivered. Tomo-san explained umami as not just a taste, but a feeling — the resonance between your palate and memory.

We shopped at Nishiki Market, hunting the freshest yuzu and live uni. I learned the art of making dashi — kombu and bonito flakes simmered with patience — and folding tamagoyaki into golden layers softer than pillows. Plating was as crucial as cooking: each morsel a composition, color and texture balanced like a garden.

Cherry blossoms drifted past the window, as I drank clear miso soup, marveling at how so much flavor bloomed from so few elements.

Chapter Six: Mexico — Fire and Corn in Oaxaca

Oaxaca — land of moles, smoky mezcal, and street parades — pulled me south next. The markets were riots of color: baskets of corn in a dozen colors, chilies sharp enough to singe your tongue, avocado leaves smelling of anise.

I apprenticed with Elena, queen of mole negro. Making it was a test of stamina: roasting beans, toasting chilies, grinding chocolate with mortar and pestle. The mole’s secrets revealed themselves in layers: heat, earth, bittersweet smoke. “It’s not just food,” Elena told me. “It’s ceremony, memory, survival.”

Tortillas were ground by hand on stone metates, filled with Oaxacan cheese or grasshoppers fried crunchy. Every night, we shared tlayudas — crispy corn flatbreads — under strings of papel picado, sipping smoky mezcal and listening to marimba bands echo through the plaza.

Chapter Seven: India — Masala Dust in Kerala

India was chaos and poetry, turmeric and honking rickshaws. In Kerala, I found Amma, who welcomed me into her home on the backwaters. Her kitchen sang with cardamom, curry leaves, sizzling mustard seed.

I learned to make dosas, ladling batter onto a cast iron pan, coaxing it into lace-thin circles. We made fish curry with coconut and mango, our hands yellow with spice. Every meal was an invitation: join our table, break bread, tell your story.

I feasted on banana leaf thalis, spicy sambar, and payasam sweet with jaggery. The local fishermen brought fresh catch to our door, and I learned to cook by the tides.

Nights brought monsoon rain hammering the roof, and whisper-quiet conversations about family, faith, and why every dish tasted different, yet connected to every other by a thread of tradition.

Chapter Eight: Home Again — Stirring the Pot

After a year of wandering and cooking, my notebook bulged with recipes, ideas scrawled in several languages. My heart ached with homesickness for places I hadn’t left — but Chicago’s winter greeted me like an old friend.

I opened a supper club in my rented kitchen, inviting friends both old and new, promising a world tour on their plates. Each week, a new region: saffron-scented paella, hand-rolled tortellini, Thai curry as aromatic as any Chiang Mai market, Oaxaca’s smoky moles, Kerala’s spiced fish.

The table grew, people brought friends, strangers became confidants over shared bread. I realized I wasn’t just cooking dishes, I was recreating the feeling of each place: curiosity in Spain, warmth in Morocco, convivial chaos in Italy, harmony in Japan, exuberance in Mexico, generous hospitality in India.

The adventure never really ended. Every meal became a voyage, a story, an invitation. Cooking my way through the world wasn’t just about flavors. It was about discovery, memory, and the simple truth that when you cook for others, you build something lasting: community, belonging, love.

So my kitchen remains a crossroads, my table crowded, my door open. There are countless countries and recipes left untried. As long as there is a new spice to taste, a new friend to make, the adventure continues — one meal, one recipe, one story at a time.
