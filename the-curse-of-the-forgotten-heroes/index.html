The Curse of the Forgotten Heroes


The old bell tower loomed over the valley like a finger pointing to a sunless sky. Every morning, a thin mist crawled down its ancient stones, curling into the thatch and cobble of Noreldown’s sleepy streets, and every night—when the last candle guttered and windows winked into darkness—a moan, almost like a question, threaded through the bones of the town.

No one remembered why the tower was built. Alma Greaves, whose memory was sharp enough to recall the number of sycamore seeds swirling in a storm, always said it was simply “older than mud”, which was how she said ancient. But even Alma, sitting in her high-backed chair beside the fire, went silent and pale when the tower’s bell rang on its own, as it sometimes did at dusk.

The truth of Noreldown’s founding had unraveled with time, snagged and lost by generations content with comfortable fables. They preferred the tale that said the tower was built to honor the village’s saviors—a group of brave souls who had driven away bone-white wolves and shadowy kings. Each house supposedly descended from a different ‘Hero’, easily slotted into festival games and nursery rhymes. Yet no one could name these so-called champions, nor describe their faces, nor point to a grave or half-rusted sword as proof they ever existed.

To young Tessa Rowland, the stories were an itch she could never scratch. On her twelfth birthday, she sat on the low stone wall by the apothecary and watched the tower against the dusk.

“There goes that look again,” said her friend, Owen, slouching beside her. “You thinking about the bell?”

Tessa shook her head. “Don’t you ever wonder what’s in there? Or what happened to them?”

Owen’s gaze followed hers, his brown eyes wary. “Everyone knows. Heroes drove back the dark, blah blah. Then they left or died. It’s history.”

“But it’s not, is it?” she pressed. “No pictures, no names. Just stories. Maybe…” Here, Tessa lowered her voice—almost embarrassed by the enormity of her suspicion. “Maybe something happened the grown-ups don’t want us to know.”

Owen drew a line with his heel through the dirt. He didn’t reply, but he didn’t leave either.

They watched the shadow crawl up the bell tower, swallowing the old clock face until only the copper hands—perpetually frozen at 2:31—caught the last of the light.

That night, Tessa woke to the bell ringing.

She slipped from bed, feet sinking into cool flagstones. The night outside was crisp, stars bright, the tower standing a blacker shadow against the sky. She crossed the silent market square, swinging wide around the baker’s hut, and paused before the tower’s iron-studded door. The bell’s echo shimmered in the air, then faded. Tessa pressed her palm to the scarred wood.

The door groaned open.

Inside, cold and dust choked the spiral stairs curling upward. Light flickered from above, as if someone carried a lantern high on the platform. Tessa ascended, teeth clamped tight with nerves.

At the top, she expected ghosts. She half-expected nothing at all. Instead, she found a stranger—a woman in faded traveling clothes, hood drawn low, clutching a weathered book on her lap. At her feet, a candle guttered on bare stone.

“I know you,” Tessa said, voice trembling despite her efforts. “You’re…”

The woman smiled, sadness carved deep into the lines of her face. “I’m no one. Same as the others.”

“Are you a Hero?” Tessa persisted, inching nearer. “No one remembers your names.”

The woman’s laugh was soft. “That’s the curse, isn’t it? Memories slip, the tales twist. Once we were revered. Now we’re echoes.”

Tessa hesitated. “What happened to you?”

The woman rested a hand on the tattered book. “When the wolves came, when the King called shadows, we stood between Noreldown and ruin. Not for glory, not for song. But stories hunger for heroes, not people. Each time the bell rings, the world remembers a story. And a little more of us is lost.”

Tessa frowned. “But why?”

The woman’s gaze dropped to the candle. “There is magic in gratitude, and power in memory. When tales become legend, the real souls—who loved and feared and failed—are dissolved in the telling. We’re here, forever repeating, until someone remembers who we were—not only what we did.”

Tessa thought, then reached for the book. “Can I read it?”

The woman nodded. “If you can bear it, child.”

With a shiver, Tessa took up the book. The pages crackled—a ledger in a language half-recognized: names, tiny histories, sketches of faces and hands, hope and regret scribed in ink. Here was Aldric the baker, who had brought bread to the barricades. Syra who sewed wounds in the night. Brannick who wept after battle. None wore crowns, none bore swords in song. The more she read, the brighter the candle burned, as if memory itself was kindling light in the shadows.

When Tessa looked up, the woman was weeping.

“I remember you now,” Tessa whispered. “And all the others, too.”

The bell boomed once, twice more. The timbered room flickered: shadows stretching, condensing, then drawing together in warmth. For the first time, there was no cold in the tower. The woman—no longer a stranger—stood straighter, her face brightening with the certainty of being recognized.

“In all these years,” she said, voice trembling, “you’re the first to call us by name.”

Tessa, head swirling with truth, closed the ledger. “Will you go now?”

The woman shook her head, but there was peace in the gesture. “Perhaps, some day. But not tonight. Every memory feeds us—frees us.”

Down in the square, the morning broke golden. Alma Greaves, roused for her dawn walk, saw Tessa sitting on the tower steps, book in her lap, smile dawning on her face.

“Found what you were hunting?” Alma asked, cocking an eyebrow.

Tessa nodded. “I remembered something for everyone.”

Each day that followed, Tessa shared a story at market. How Aldric gave the first loaf of autumn to a neighbor. How Syra mended shoes as well as wounds. How Brannick wasn’t a warrior at all, but a father, scared for his son. The old tales softened, grew flesh and faces, hope and heartbreak. Gradually, others joined her. Owen spoke for a cousin who’d vanished in the woods. Alma herself remembered a song only her grandmother knew.

The bell tower grew quiet.

Years passed. Noreldown’s next festival honored names, not titles—voices, not legends. New songs joined the old. Tessa kept the ledger safe, its pages no longer fading, its ink sharper every year.

One dusky evening, an old woman limped to the end of the pier by the river, humming a tune Tessa had taught her. She thought she saw a woman in a tattered cloak step into the mist, smiling from the far bank. She blinked, but only the willow trees were stirring.

In the years to come, no one in Noreldown ever forgot the curse. They strolled past the tower, tipping hats not to stories but to the names that built them. Each new tale became a thread, woven into a tapestry both true and lasting.

All across the land, wherever legend lingered, a warmth bloomed in the coldest places. No hero was lost to time—so long as one person remembered their story, and spoke their name.
