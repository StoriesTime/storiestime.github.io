Around the World in 80 Dishes: A Culinary Exploration


Jules Delacroix stood at the corner of Rue des Martyrs, gripping a faded guidebook in one hand and a grocery bag in the other. The late afternoon sun dipped behind Parisian rooftops, casting a golden hue onto cafe terraces and flower stalls. He paused in front of a chalkboard menu advertising soupe à l’oignon, his stomach swirling with hunger and a familiar sense of restlessness.

Six years as a sous-chef under Chef Vincente, and still no whiff of the creative freedom he craved. From behind the tiled walls of Le Papillon Étoilé, Jules watched the world through the thin lens of mise en place and expectation. He needed more. If the legends of Chef Auguste Escoffier could weave culinary magic from the dregs of cardamom and venison, why not him? But Paris, for all its romance, sometimes tangled its lovers in loops of longing.

He ducked into the next alley, unfurling the latest text message from his sister, Marie. Her font choices were as brash as her advice: Life is delicious, if you dare to taste more than you know. Then, an idea as light and wild as saffron threads threaded its way into his brain: Around the World in 80 Dishes—not as a recipe book, but as a journey, savoring the stories behind the sustenance.

Chapter 2: The First Bite—Osaka’s Takoyaki

Two weeks later, Jules clutched his duffel bag on a rain-slicked street in Osaka. Neon kanji pulsed across the facades of izakayas, and baritone laughter spilled out as the noren curtains parted for salarymen. In a tiny stand, hunched over a cast-iron takoyaki grill, stood an elderly woman with hands as swift as pigeons. He ordered, and when the toothpick-skewered morsels arrived—crisp outside, molten inside, tentacle bits tangled with green onion and bonito flakes—history unfolded on his tongue.

The woman watched him for a reaction. He beamed. With a nod, she offered a soft tale of her grandfather, a street vendor during the postwar years, rolling batter beside bombed-out buildings. The dish wasn’t just octopus balls; it was resilience made edible, bite-sized proof that flavor can spring from despair.

Jules jotted notes, drawing a crude world map in his notebook, the takoyaki stall a pin marking memory and beginning.

Chapter 3: Saffron and the Souks of Marrakech

A week later, caramel light burned across the souks of Marrakech. The air vibrated with the aroma of turmeric and roasting lamb. Guided by a toothless merchant named Youssef, Jules wound his way to a home kitchen behind a tapestry shop, where a three-tiered tagine slowly exhaled clouds of cumin and ginger.

He was shown how to sink strands of saffron into hot broth, how the spice was worth its weight in dreams, harvested by hand at dawn. Each sticky apricot tucked into the lamb stew was an heirloom from a generation who had tamed the desert’s fickle bounty. The family ate together, tearing hunks of bread, debating football, politics, and trade—all woven into the deep orange sauce.

Jules recorded everything, savoring both the dish and the cadence of voices. He realized food is less a border and more a shared tongue.

Chapter 4: Buenos Aires—Coals and Conversation

Jules’s shoes scuffed the cobblestones of San Telmo, and his stomach pinged in anticipation. At a parrilla that opened directly onto the street, the scent of charred beef drew him toward Chef Lucia, her arms dusted with salt and chimichurri.

Together, they fed logs into the firebox, layering short ribs and chorizo on blistered iron. Lucia gestured that the best asado began with patience and ended only once wine and singing entered the blood. As dusk gathered, strangers huddled near, drawn to the flame and smoke. They discussed political heartbreak, football victories, and ancestral myths long after the meat was gone.

In Buenos Aires, Jules learned that trust is an essential ingredient—best marinated before the meal and cooked forever after.

Chapter 5: The Mystery of Kerala’s Spice Coast

It was a monsoon morning when Jules arrived in Fort Kochi, sandals squelching through red mud. Fishermen dragged nets ashore behind colonial facades, their voices lost in the squall. Behind a blue-washed shutter, matriarch Amma greeted him with a steel tumbler of chai.

She showed him the alchemy of a Kerala fish curry: tamarind soaking in sunlight, a grind of fresh coconut, mustard seeds popping in fiery oil. She spoke Malayalam, her phrases translated by her granddaughter, who likened pickled mango to “summer preserved.”

The meal was a harmony of sour, spice, and sweetness, the curry steeped with a legacy of Portuguese, Arab, and Dutch mariners. With every bite, Jules felt the current of migration and trade—every border crossed, every tongue adopted, every flavor inflected by centuries.

Chapter 6: Palermo’s Cannoli and Tough Goodbyes

In Sicily, everything began and ended with family. Jules arrived in Palermo’s Ballarò market on the Feast of Santa Rosalia, swept atop a tide of voices. He ducked into Nonna Marcella’s humble bakery as she piped ricotta into golden cannoli shells.

Cannoli, she explained, echo the fortunes of a city forever invaded: Norman almonds, Arab sugar, Spanish chocolate. Each crisp shell, a fortress; each creamy filling, a celebration that something sweet survives, no matter the storm outside.

They ate at the kitchen table, where shadows from the lemon grove flickered across the walls. “Everyone leaves Palermo,” Marcella said, “but they carry us in their sweets.”

She pressed a box of cannoli into his hands as tears prickled at the farewell.

Chapter 7: Seoul—Fermentation and Friendship

Jules next wandered narrow alleys of Seoul’s Insadong. Inside a tiny restaurant, he watched Chef Sun-mi layer napa cabbage in bathes of briny gochugaru, ginger, and garlic. Kimchi takes time, she said, as everything worthwhile does—pungency arising from patience.

They sat, sharing stone bowls of bubbling kimchi jjigae, the broth wrangling chili, funk, and depth. Sun-mi laughed at his watery eyes, slid soju across the table, and shared memories of a Korean war orphan who built his family on rice and kimchi.

In Seoul, Jules tasted memory preserved—simmered, salted, and fermented through sorrow and celebration.

Chapter 8: Hawai’i—Fusion on a Plate

Under a banyan tree on Oahu’s North Shore, Jules met Chef Kalea, her tattooed arms folding Spam musubi beside poke tossed with sweet onion and sesame. She explained how Hawai’i’s cuisine was a living mosaic—Japanese, Filipino, Portuguese, native Hawaiian—woven together in lunch plates and luau stews.

The flavors were bright, briny, and utterly new. Fish melted under soy glaze; mac salad carried more stories than syllables. Eating here was a reminder that roots travel, and what grows in volcanic soil reshapes the old world.

Chapter 9: Istanbul’s Endless Table

Jules’s footsteps traced the call of muezzin through Istanbul’s Fatih district. At Chef Elif’s table, dozens of meze plates arrived: smoky babaganoush, crispy sigara böreği, pilaf shimmering with spices. Here, eating was communal, cross-legged, a feast for both tongue and eye.

Conversations spilled between Turkish and French, weaving histories of sultans, exiles, and poets. Elif told tales of her grandmother, who baked for orphans during the Siege of Constantinople—flavors immortalized as sustenance in dark times.

At that endless table, Jules tasted the truth that tradition never sits still; it flows, an unbroken current from Anatolia to every hungry heart.

Chapter 10: Homecoming—A Table for All

Eighty dishes and nearly as many months later, Jules returned to Paris, his journal swelling with recipes and reverie. He opened a corner bistro not reliant on linen tablecloths or Michelin expectation. Each week, a card at every table listed today’s journey, and the menu sang with Burmese tea leaf salad, Lagos suya, Hanoi pho, Norwegian klippfisk.

Locals and tourists flocked for the flavors but lingered for the stories, spun by Jules with every pour of wine and flicker of candlelight. At night, as laughter spiraled up into the rafters, he understood—food is the thread binding strangers into family, history into now.

Epilogue: The Next Fork in the Road

Jules kept a battered map behind the bar—eighty pins, one for every dish, every memory, every friend. He watched newcomers study the lines crisscrossing continents and asked what they hungered for. Sometimes it was adventure, sometimes connection, sometimes just escape.

He’d learned that a meal is never just about ingredients; it’s about invitation and discovery—a passport, not a page. Which dish would be next? No one could say. But Jules had finally tasted what he yearned for: a world where every table is round, every meal a step into someone else’s story, and every bite a promise that the journey never ends.
