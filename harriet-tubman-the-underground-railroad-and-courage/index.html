Harriet Tubman: The Underground Railroad and Courage


In the shadowed fields of Maryland, where the dark soil bore the weight of enslaved hands, Harriet Tubman first dreamed of freedom. Born Araminta Ross, she was enslaved from birth, living under the harsh yoke of bondage that denied her name, her dignity, and her dreams. But from the moment she understood her chains, Harriet’s spirit stirred with an indomitable courage that no whip could break, no law could bind.

Roots of a Fire

Harriet’s early years were shaped by the brutal realities of slavery. She labored in the sweltering heat of the tobacco fields, her small hands blistered by the work that was never meant for a child. Yet what marked Harriet was not her suffering but her resolve. When she was a young girl, a severe injury from a heavy blow to the head left her with lifelong seizures, but even sickness could not quell the fire within her.

As she grew older, she watched her family torn apart by the cruel hands of slaveholders, relatives sold away like goods, and friends stolen into the night. Each act of violence, each piece of pain, became a brick in the invisible prison surrounding her. But it also became fuel—fuel for a daring hope that she would not remain a prisoner.

The First Step Towards Freedom

In 1849, Harriet made a choice that would alter the course of history. With the courage that only those desperate for liberty understand, she fled the plantation into the dense forests and across rivers, following a secret network of safe houses and signals known later as the Underground Railroad. This was no actual railroad but a covert operation of allies—free African Americans, abolitionists, and sympathetic whites—working together to help enslaved people escape to the free states and Canada.

Harriet’s first journey was perilous. She moved at night, guided by the North Star and the quiet whispers of freedom. Every snap of a twig, every creak in the darkness, held the threat of capture. Slave catchers haunted the roads, with dogs and guns ready to turn freedom seekers back into property. But Harriet pressed on, her heart steeled by the vision of a better life.

When she finally crossed into Pennsylvania, a free state, Harriet breathed a prayer of thanks. Yet for her, freedom was never just personal—it was a call to action. She could not ignore those still shackled behind her.

Mistress of the Railroad

What truly sets Harriet Tubman apart in history is not only that she escaped slavery herself but that she returned repeatedly—at great risk—to lead others to freedom. Over the next decade, she made approximately 13 missions back into the South, guiding some 70 enslaved people through the labyrinth of the Underground Railroad.

Many who traveled with Harriet spoke of her as a figure both fierce and tender. She carried a pistol not merely for protection but as a symbol of her unwavering authority—she would not hesitate to defend her freedom or that of the people she led. Her knowledge of the terrain and of escape routes was unmatched. She used codes and signals and relied on the kindness of strangers who believed in the cause.

Each successful rescue mission reinforced Harriet’s legend. She became known among enslaved communities as “Moses,” an allusion to the biblical figure who led his people out of bondage. But this was no myth—her courage was raw and tangible. To those she saved, she was life itself.

Courage Beyond the Railroad

Harriet’s bravery extended beyond the daring journeys of the Underground Railroad. When the American Civil War broke out, she found ways to contribute directly to the fight against slavery. She served as a cook, nurse, scout, and spy for the Union Army.

One of her most remarkable feats came when she became the first woman to lead an armed expedition during the Civil War. In the Combahee River Raid of 1863, Harriet helped guide Union boats to plantations where enslaved people were held, facilitating the rescue of more than 700 individuals. Her deep knowledge of Southern geography and her leadership skills were pivotal to the success of this mission.

Her involvement in the war underscored a truth far more profound than any single act of heroism: Harriet Tubman’s courage was not just physical bravery but a relentless commitment to justice and humanity in a world determined to deny it.

The Legacy of Light and Freedom

After the war, Harriet continued her work, advocating for the rights of African Americans and women. She helped establish schools and cared for elderly freed people. Despite the tremendous danger and hardship she endured, Harriet Tubman remained a beacon of hope in the darkest of times.

Her life reminds us that courage is not the absence of fear but the triumph over it. Harriet Tubman faced dangers unimaginable and overcame them with faith and fortitude. The Underground Railroad was more than a network—it was a symbol of resistance, humanity, and the powerful yearning for liberty that lives within every oppressed soul.

The story of Harriet Tubman continues to inspire generations. It is a tale of sacrifice, leadership, and unapologetic bravery. Through every hidden trail and every whispered code, Harriet carved a path from darkness to light, proving that even in the most unlikely of circumstances, courage can ignite a revolution.

Reflections on Courage and the Human Spirit

Looking back on Harriet Tubman’s journey, it becomes clear that her greatest strength was not just in her physical endurance but in her visionary leadership and moral clarity. She believed in the possibility of a world without chains, a world where equality was not a dream but a reality.

Her example teaches us about the power of individual action within the vast tides of history. The Underground Railroad was a collective movement, but it thrived because of figures like Harriet who dared to defy the violent systems of oppression head-on.

In remembering Harriet Tubman, we honor the countless unnamed souls who walked in her footsteps, who braved the darkness for the hope of dawn. Harriet Tubman’s legacy transcends time—it is a universal saga of bravery, resilience, and the unbreakable human yearning for freedom.

Harriet Tubman’s life is a testament to the indomitable courage that can change the course of history. Through secret ways, whispered promises, and fearless journeys, she showed what it means to be truly free—not because she was given freedom, but because she seized it, time and time again.
