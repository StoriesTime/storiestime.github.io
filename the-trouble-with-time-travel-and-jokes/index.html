The Trouble with Time Travel and Jokes


It was the year 2075, and time travel had gone from being a subject of theoretical debates to a full-fledged, everyday phenomenon. The technology had finally advanced enough for commercial use, allowing people to not only witness history but also interact with it. But there was a catch, of course. Time was fragile, and meddling with the past, even in the most minor ways, could have devastating consequences. Yet, for most people, the allure of seeing the past or experiencing future events was irresistible.

One evening, in a cozy café tucked away in a quiet corner of New York City, a young man named Ben sat at a table, tapping nervously on his smartphone. He wasn’t looking at news updates or checking his calendar. No, Ben was browsing time-travel tour options. His dream was to visit ancient Rome and witness firsthand the grandeur of the Coliseum, to hear the echoes of gladiators’ roars and watch emperors stroll by. But there was another, smaller, more peculiar dream that nagged at him — a curiosity that threatened to derail all reason.

Ben had always loved jokes. His father, an old comedian, had raised him on punchlines, one-liners, and witty banter. Ben’s sense of humor had always been quick, sometimes a bit too quick. He loved the idea of cracking jokes, and the thought of making people laugh was always a great joy to him. But with time travel becoming a possibility, he had a new, potentially disastrous idea: what would happen if he traveled back in time and told jokes to famous figures from history? Would their reactions be what he imagined? Would they laugh at jokes they had never heard before, or would his modern humor fall flat? It seemed like a harmless adventure. But, like all things involving time travel, it was not.

Ben had one rule: he wouldn’t mess with anything significant. No political upheavals, no changing the course of history. He just wanted to tell some jokes. Simple enough, right? After all, who could argue that humor wasn’t part of the human condition? It was timeless.

When Ben saw the time-travel tour for ancient Rome, it seemed like the perfect opportunity. The descriptions promised to take tourists on guided tours to the most iconic places in Roman history. And with the caveat that the experience would be carefully curated, ensuring that visitors wouldn’t interfere with anything major, Ben signed up. But deep down, he knew he was planning something a little bit more subversive than the brochure let on. He didn’t just want to see the Roman Empire; he wanted to make history.

The moment Ben stepped out of the time machine, his breath caught in his throat. He was no longer in the bustling, neon-lit streets of 2075 but in a world that could not have been more different. He stood in the center of a sprawling Roman marketplace, surrounded by towering columns and the thick scent of fresh bread. The sun blazed down, and people bustled around in simple, toga-like garments, laughing, shouting, and haggling. He marveled at the sheer scale of the world before him.

His tour guide, a stern-looking woman dressed in the traditional garb of a Roman citizen, waved him over. “Keep up, Ben,” she said. “Remember the rules. No time tampering, no significant disruptions. Enjoy the experience as a passive observer.”

Ben nodded, though his mind was already on his mission. As he walked with the group, he watched people pass by, occasionally stopping to ask the tour guide about life in ancient Rome. But his thoughts weren’t on architecture or politics. His mind was on jokes. Specifically, the idea of telling a joke to someone like Julius Caesar. Or perhaps even a philosopher like Seneca. Would their stiff, serious demeanor melt away in laughter? What if he could make them laugh at his 21st-century humor?

He spotted a man sitting near the fountain, scribbling on a piece of parchment. Ben’s heart skipped a beat. He recognized him immediately: Marcus Tullius Cicero, the famous Roman orator and statesman. His mind raced. Could he really pull it off?

Ben approached, his feet feeling suddenly heavier with the weight of what he was about to attempt. “Excuse me, sir,” Ben said, trying to sound casual. “I have a joke for you.”

Cicero looked up from his writing, raising an eyebrow. “A joke?” he asked, clearly puzzled. “What is this… ‘joke’ you speak of?”

Ben hesitated, then smiled, recalling one of his favorite lines. “Why don’t skeletons fight each other?”

Cicero frowned, clearly confused. “I am unfamiliar with the concept of skeletons fighting,” he said slowly, as though Ben had just spoken a foreign language. “But go on.”

“Because they don’t have the guts!” Ben finished, hoping for a laugh.

Cicero stared at him for a long moment. Then he chuckled—just a bit. It wasn’t the roaring laughter Ben had imagined, but it was something. The orator’s lips curled up into the faintest of smiles. “Interesting,” Cicero said, scratching his chin. “You… amuse me, stranger.”

Ben grinned, feeling a sense of triumph. He had succeeded. But as he turned to walk away, he felt a sudden unease. Had that moment altered anything? Could that small exchange of humor, so seemingly insignificant, have ripples through time?

As the tour continued, Ben grew more reckless. He had always dreamed of meeting great minds from history, but now he had the chance to interact with them directly. Each time he made someone laugh, no matter how subtly, he felt a thrill. He cracked a joke about ancient Roman roads to a group of builders, told a pun about gladiators to a local merchant, and even made a Roman guard laugh by suggesting he wear sandals with socks.

Each of these small moments seemed harmless, but there was one thing Ben hadn’t anticipated: time’s delicate balance. Each joke he told was a thread, pulling the tapestry of history in ways he could never fully understand.

It wasn’t until the next day that Ben realized the true consequences of his actions.

He had returned to the time machine’s station, eager to return home, but the tour guide met him with a stern expression. “Ben,” she said. “We need to talk.”

“What happened?” Ben asked, feeling his stomach twist.

“The timeline,” the guide explained. “It’s shifted.”

Ben blinked. “Shifted? What do you mean?”

“The jokes you told, the way you interacted with people—it wasn’t just a harmless distraction. It created a ripple. Small changes, imperceptible at first, but over time, they added up. We’ve traced the problem back to your conversation with Cicero. His reaction to you was… out of character. It caused a shift in the way Roman thinkers interacted with humor. It’s minor now, but if left unchecked, it could have enormous consequences.”

Ben’s heart sank. “I didn’t mean to change anything. It was just a joke. A joke!”

“That’s what we all think,” the guide said, shaking her head. “But jokes, Ben, they’re never just jokes. They’re ideas. And ideas are dangerous when they spread in the wrong context, or at the wrong time.”

Ben felt the weight of the situation crash down on him. Time was far more fragile than he had realized. A single slip—a single joke—could alter everything.

The days that followed were a blur. The time-travel agency launched a full investigation into the repercussions of Ben’s small interactions in the past. They worked tirelessly to “reset” the timeline, using every tool at their disposal to carefully undo the changes he had caused. It took weeks to isolate the effects of the jokes and repair the damage. But even then, the question lingered: had they truly fixed everything?

Ben never returned to the past, but the incident haunted him. He realized that the problem with time travel wasn’t the technology, nor the grand ambitions of history-altering figures. No, the trouble with time travel—and jokes—was the unintentional consequences of small actions, the unseen shifts that could spiral out of control. History was built on countless moments, and even a fleeting encounter could have lasting effects. A joke, it turned out, was a dangerous thing.

Ben learned his lesson the hard way: sometimes, the smallest ripple could cause the greatest storm.

Years later, Ben would look back on that time in Rome, reflecting on how one simple misstep—a joke—changed his view on the world. He still loved humor, but he had come to appreciate the delicate balance of time. After all, in a world where the past could be revisited, and where one word or one laugh could change everything, the joke was always on those who thought they could control time.
