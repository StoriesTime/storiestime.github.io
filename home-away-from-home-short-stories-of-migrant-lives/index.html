Home Away from Home: Short Stories of Migrant Lives


Fatima adjusted the hem of her scarf as she scanned the airport terminal, her heart beating faster than the announcement blaring from the overhead speakers. She clutched her passport and boarding pass, her fingers brushing the faded photograph tucked into her purse—her two sons, still children then, were smiling widely under the hot Lagos sun. That image had been her anchor in the ten years since she last saw them. Ten years of working double shifts in a bustling London café, cleaning houses on weekends, and sending money back home to ensure her family had a better life than she could ever offer within the limitations of her own country.

Now, after all those years of sacrifice, the threads of her life had finally woven back together. She was going home—not permanently, but long enough to see the men her sons had become. Long enough to remind herself why it had all been worth it.

The air was thick with the scent of hand sanitizer and tired travelers, but to Fatima, the terminal felt electric. She wasn’t the only one carrying a tapestry of sacrifice through the sterile halls of Heathrow. Around her, fragments of other migrant lives unfolded silently: a man from the Middle East adjusting his tie as he video-called his children; a woman from Eastern Europe cradling her baby while nervously shuffling documents; a young couple chatting quietly in a language Fatima didn’t recognize, their backpacks worn from years of use.

Fatima thought of the first months after she had arrived in the UK. She barely spoke English then, and the silences at work had felt like landmines she tried to sidestep. Every interaction seemed fraught with the possibility of misunderstanding. She’d spent her breaks sitting in the corner of the café’s kitchen, closing her eyes to block out the chaos of orders and clinking cups. The shame of being so out of place had been heavier than the physical exhaustion.

But tenacity had been a companion of hers long before she’d left home. She learned to fit her tongue around foreign sounds, practiced polite pleasantries to make herself less invisible, and grew comfortable with the ache of homesickness that accompanied her like an untiring shadow. When she finally landed a cleaning job for a family in Kensington, the quiet moments spent scrubbing countertops and vacuuming rugs became a small reprieve. The monotony gave her time to reminisce: the boys’ laughter as they argued over football, the warm aroma of jollof rice in the kitchen, and her late husband’s booming voice carrying across their neighborhood.

But even in those memory-soaked moments, the reality of her decision loomed. She was a mother providing from afar, her presence reduced to the money transfer notifications on her children’s phones. Every milestone—graduations, birthdays, even the death of her own mother—slipped by without her. Her boys understood, or at least they tried to. “Mama, we’re proud of you,” one of them had said last Christmas over a video call. But pride, Fatima knew, was not the same as closeness.

The boarding announcement snapped her back to the present. She moved toward the gate, clutching her bag tightly. As her feet carried her onto the plane, she couldn’t shake the feeling that she was stepping between worlds again. Only this time, rather than leaving something behind, she was reclaiming a piece of herself.

In the corrugated iron market stalls of Johannesburg, Nkosana arranged rows of vibrant beaded necklaces as he waited for the regular Saturday crowd to filter through. His entrepreneurial spirit was the only thing keeping his head above water since he’d left Zimbabwe two years ago to escape a country-long economic collapse. He often joked with his friends that he had an “MBA”—not a master’s degree, but a Massive Border Adventure. Packing everything he could into his battered backpack and crossing the Limpopo River in the dead of night had branded him with an edge that Johannesburg hadn’t managed to soften.

His stand was located in a cramped corner of the Yeoville Market, surrounded by vendors hawking everything from dried fish to secondhand electronics. But where others saw competition, Nkosana saw opportunity. His necklaces, bracelets, and earrings were carefully crafted—bright yellows, blues, and reds symbolizing the cultural heritage he wore like armor. He had learned to market himself to both tourists hungry for a piece of Africa to take home and locals who admired his skill.

Migration wasn’t new in his life. His father had worked in the South African mines before him, sending money back to the family until silicosis claimed him. Nkosana often wondered if the instinct to leave was etched into his DNA—a perpetual search for greener pastures even when the soil beneath his feet seemed rich enough.

But life as a foreigner in South Africa wasn’t easy. Johannesburg was a city of contradictions, where wealth rubbed shoulders with desperation. The locals’ resentment of migrants simmered just beneath the surface, sometimes spilling over into xenophobic violence. Nkosana had learned early on to tread lightly, to speak less in the melodic Shona of his ancestors and more in the clipped tones of Zulu and English. Even so, there were times when his accent betrayed him, when the hostility in a customer’s expression reminded him that he didn’t belong.

What kept him rooted was the blurry photo of his mother and younger siblings taped to the back of his stand. For them, he wore every insult, every sleight, like a badge of honor. Each sale he made brought him closer to sending the monthly remittance that kept them fed and, with luck, kept his sister in school. The dream of bringing them to Johannesburg, of being reunited in the teeming, electric city, fueled the long hours he spent in the sun.

On slow days, he found himself daydreaming of home—of the rich, earthy smell of the Zimbabwean countryside after a rainstorm, of his mother’s laughter as she stirred sadza on an open fire, of chasing crickets with his siblings. But home felt like a mirage now, a place he might never return to without first securing his family a future elsewhere. Johannesburg’s hustle had become his reality, and with it came both despair and possibility.

As the market buzzed with life around him, Nkosana looked at the passersby and wondered how many of their stories mirrored his own. Behind each face was a journey, a gamble, a quiet yearning to make foreign soil feel like home. He adjusted one of the necklaces on his stand and called out to a woman admiring his work, his voice cutting through the noise.

On the other side of the Atlantic, in a dimly lit New York City apartment, Ana carefully rolled dough onto the counter while her daughter Lina sat at the table coloring. The aroma of garlic and spices filled the room, as Ana prepared empanadas for her catering small business, which was still in its infancy but already their lifeline.

Ana hadn’t planned on fleeing Venezuela. She had been a nurse in her old life—respected, stable, content. But when the nation’s crisis deepened, her work became impossible, the hospital shelves empty of medicine and patients desperate for care. Leaving felt like tearing out her own roots, but Lina had been her priority. They had arrived in the U.S. with little more than a suitcase and dreams. Dreams that often felt crushed under the weight of the reality they lived in.

Every corner of her new life was unfamiliar. In Venezuela, she’d had a network—family, friends, neighbors. Here, the city swallowed her into its anonymity. There were times when she felt like an invisible thread in the expansive tapestry of immigrant lives that sustained New York. Still, she was determined to succeed, if for no other reason than to spare Lina from inheriting the struggles that defined her own adulthood.

The empanada business had been Lina’s idea, born from Saturday afternoons spent in their cramped kitchen. “Mamá,” she’d said one day, her bright brown eyes gleaming with a mix of hope and mischief, “why don’t we sell these? Everyone says they’re the best.”

It hadn’t been easy—navigating health permits, lugging heavy trays to community events, marketing on social media she barely understood. But little by little, the orders had trickled in. And with each dollar earned, Ana inched closer to something she hadn’t dared imagine since leaving home: stability.

“Mamá, look!” Lina interrupted her thoughts, proudly holding up her drawing. It was a picture of their tiny apartment, filled with colorful details, as though the cramped quarters were a palace. Ana’s eyes stung, a bittersweet smile crossing her face. Maybe, just maybe, they were carving out a home after all.

As Ana slid the empanadas into the oven, she thought about the countless migrants scattered across the boroughs. She wondered if they also carried memories of the places they left behind, memories that collided with the lives they were building. In New York, they were many things—workers, parents, dreamers, survivors—but above all, they were threads in the same quilt, each one stitched with longing and resilience.

In London, Johannesburg, New York, and beyond, the lives of migrants are testaments to the human spirit. They navigate uncharted terrain, stringing together pieces of their past and present to create something new—a home away from home. And in those homes, no matter how small or temporary, lies the quiet but unyielding hope for a future unbound by borders.
