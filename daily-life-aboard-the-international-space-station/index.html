Daily Life Aboard the International Space Station


Floating against the vast emptiness punctuated by glittering stars and the soft blue curve of Earth, the International Space Station (ISS) is more than a scientific outpost—it’s a home. Each day aboard this orbiting laboratory weaves a fascinating tapestry of routine, innovation, camaraderie, and continuous discovery.

Morning Awakening: The Soft Glow of Earth

The day begins with the gentle hum of the station’s systems and a soft alarm echoing through the cramped living quarters. Floating gently in the absence of gravity, astronauts slowly orient themselves as the Earth outside slips through its rapid orbit, completing a sunrise every 90 minutes. Unlike Earth-bound mornings, the ISS offers 16 sunrises and sunsets a day, an extraordinary reminder of their unique vantage point in space.

Astronauts begin by locating their floating sleeping bags tethered to walls or ceilings, carefully extracting themselves to avoid inadvertent collisions with equipment. Personal hygiene in zero gravity is a precise ritual: no running water, no shower. Instead, moist towels, rinseless shampoo, and meticulous care ensure they stay clean and refreshed. Brushing teeth involves special toothpaste that doesn’t require rinsing with water, highlighting the clever adaptations for living in microgravity.

Nutritional Challenges and Meals

Breakfast is a scientific act, as much as a culinary one. Pre-packaged foods, freeze-dried fruits, and specially designed beverages dominate the menu. Nutritionists on Earth carefully plan every calorie and nutrient, ensuring astronauts remain strong and healthy amid the taxing conditions of space travel.

Eating in zero gravity presents unique challenges. Food must be eaten carefully to avoid floating crumbs or droplets, which could damage instruments or be inhaled accidentally. Velcro, magnets, trays with bungee cords, and containers with specially designed lids become essential tools. Tomato juice or coffee might be sipped through vacuum-sealed tubes, while thermos-like pouches keep meals warm. The taste buds sometimes dull due to mild nasal congestion caused by fluid shifts in space, pushing astronauts to favor spicy or flavorful dishes.

The Science of Living: Experiments and Observations

Once nourished, it’s time to dive into the mission’s core purpose: scientific research. Each astronaut is part scientist, part engineer, and part explorer. Every day, a carefully orchestrated schedule guides them through experiments that could only be conducted in microgravity.

Zero gravity unlocks a remarkable world for science. Flames burn differently, fluids separate unlike anything on Earth, and tiny organisms reveal how space affects living cells. Astronauts monitor plant growth, test the properties of novel materials, conduct biological experiments on human physiology, and study cosmic phenomena using onboard telescopes and sensors.

Experiments necessitate precise documentation, often recorded via video, photographs, and detailed notes sent back to Earth. Data links allow ground control to advise and troubleshoot in real time, creating a seamless partnership between space and Earth-bound teams.

Maintenance: The Unsung Hero of Space Life

While science captures the imagination, maintenance is the backbone of station life. The ISS is a complex network of modules, life-support systems, communication arrays, and power regulators primarily dependent on solar energy. Like a self-contained spacecraft, routine system monitoring, repairs, and troubleshooting tasks keep the station alive.

Astronauts quickly become adept mechanics, replacing filters, reconnecting cables, patching leaks, and recalibrating sensitive instruments. These maintenance activities require dexterity and a calm mindset—small mistakes can cascade into critical failures.

Physical Fitness: Combating Microgravity’s Toll

Gravity plays a subtle but vital role in human physiology, and its absence sparks challenges. Muscle atrophy and bone density loss occur when bodies float weightlessly, requiring astronauts to dedicate around two hours daily to rigorous exercise.

The ISS houses specialized equipment such as the Treadmill with Vibration Isolation and Stabilization (TVIS), the Cycle Ergometer with Vibration Isolation and Stabilization (CEVIS), and the Advanced Resistive Exercise Device (ARED). These machines simulate weight-bearing workouts necessary to maintain cardiovascular health, muscular strength, and skeletal integrity. Exercise is both physical therapy and psychological relief—a way to stay fit and mentally grounded amid an alien environment.

The Emotional World: Isolation and Connection

Living on the ISS isn’t purely scientific; it’s deeply human. Confined to a space about the size of a six-bedroom house, with no windows except for limited observation ports, astronauts face isolation, confinement, and the stress of being millions of miles from home.

To combat loneliness and maintain morale, crews cultivate friendships and rituals. Video calls with family, digital messages, and sharing photographs from Earth help maintain emotional ties. Each crew member has personal quarters, small as they are, allowing moments of privacy and reflection.

The station’s cupola—a seven-windowed dome offering panoramic Earth views—serves as a sanctuary. Gazing at swirling storms, vast deserts, or the twinkling nightscape reminds astronauts of the fragile beauty of their home planet and the importance of their mission.

Science Meets Culture: Education and Outreach

The ISS is more than a laboratory—it’s a beacon for global cooperation and inspiration. Astronauts regularly engage in live educational events, speaking with students worldwide, demonstrating scientific concepts, or conducting simple experiments to ignite young imaginations.

Through social media posts, spacewalk videos, and candid photos of floating food or space pets like the tiny tardigrades sent aboard, the station bridges the gap between Earth and space. These outreach efforts foster appreciation for science, engineering, and international partnership.

Spacewalks: Dancing Beyond the Station

Perhaps the most dramatic moments of daily life are spacewalks—extravehicular activities (EVAs) where astronauts step beyond the safety of the station’s hull into the void. Clad in bulky suits with life-support backpacks, they meticulously follow choreographed procedures to repair components, install new instruments, or inspect external surfaces.

The world appears surreal during EVAs, an awe-inspiring juxtaposition of delicate human presence against vast cosmic darkness and the brilliant orb of Earth. The silence, save for radio conversations, and the sensation of drifting add to a profound sense of vulnerability and wonder.

Nightfall and Rest: Preparing for the Cycle to Repeat

After completing tasks, conducting experiments, exercising, and sharing moments of camaraderie or solitude, the crew winds down. Astronauts secure their gear, set their sleeping bags, and prepare to rest.

Sleeping in continuous daylight without gravity is a challenge. Crew members wear eye masks and earplugs to block light and noise, and are tethered to prevent floating into uncomfortable positions. Despite these challenges, many report vivid dreams and an uncanny sense of sleeping inside a spaceship that has become home.

The ISS rotates through countless sunrises and sunsets, a constant reminder of the endless journey they undertake. As sleep envelops them, the hum of life-support systems continues tirelessly, safeguarding this fragile bubble of life suspended in the void.

Daily life aboard the International Space Station is a complex blend of discipline and wonder, challenges and triumphs. It embodies human ingenuity, resilience, and the eternal quest to explore the unknown. Each orbit brings new experiences and insights, helping us understand not only space but also ourselves and our place in the universe.
