Home Away from Home: Short Stories Celebrating Immigrant Lives


Lin Zhu held his daughter Mei’s hand tightly as they maneuvered through the crowded terminal of San Francisco International Airport. His grip was firm—not just to keep her close but to steady himself. The plane ride from Guangzhou had been long and disorienting, but the cacophony of voices and rapid movements surrounding them now felt even more alien. Mei, wide-eyed and silent, clung to her worn backpack—the one with the frayed seam they’d stitched together months ago, knowing this day would come.

The United States. For years, the idea of starting over had been both a promise and a specter for Lin. As a schoolteacher in a rural Cantonese village, life had been predictable, but opportunities for a better future eluded him like a fragile wisp of smoke. Mei’s mother, lost to illness three years earlier, had always dreamed of emigrating. Lin had taken her wish and made it his purpose. Now, here they were—alone but resolute.

The chaos of baggage claim dissolved into the relative quiet of the taxi ride through unfamiliar streets. Mei sat with her nose nearly pressed to the window, trying to take in the cityscape—a blend of soaring skyscrapers and weathered townhouses bathed in a soft golden haze. Lin tried to ignore the unease in his chest, focusing instead on his mental checklist. Housing. Employment. Language. Mei’s schooling. The enormity of starting over from scratch was a weight he could not yet measure.

They arrived at a modest, two-story building painted beige with chipped shutters. Lin’s brother-in-law, Jun, had rented the compact apartment for them. Jun had emigrated years earlier and now worked long hours serving pho in a bustling restaurant downtown. When he opened the door, cheerful and robust, Mei hung back, hiding behind Lin’s leg.

“Ah, you made it!” Jun exclaimed over the rim of his glasses, clasping Lin’s shoulders warmly. “Come, come! It’s not big, but it’s yours.”

The apartment was as basic as Jun had promised—peeling linoleum floors, tan walls, a narrow kitchenette with a dripping faucet—but Lin thanked him as though it were a palace. Jun stayed long enough to help them unpack and show Lin how to work the old electric stove before departing for his evening shift. Mei flopped down on the thin mattress they’d set up for her in the corner of the single bedroom. There was exhaustion in her posture, but her eyes remained fixed on Lin.

“Are we staying here for long, Baba?” she asked, curling into a small ball.

Lin sat next to her, stroking her hair the way her mother used to. “For now,” he replied softly. “But we’ll make it better. This is just the beginning.”

The next weeks became a whirlwind of adaptation. Lin’s days started before sunrise, flipping through stacks of classified ads in free newspapers scavenged from convenience stores. His English was halting, a patchwork quilt stitched together from nighttime lessons and hastily memorizations. Still, he managed.

By sheer determination, he secured a dishwashing job at a diner on Mission Street. The hours were grueling and paid just enough to scrape by, but the monotony of suds and grease was oddly comforting. It gave structure to his days. Mei, meanwhile, was thrust into a public elementary school where the kids teased her for fumbling through unfamiliar consonants. She didn’t cry, though—not once. She returned each day with new words and stories that she haltingly shared with Lin as they ate their humble meals of rice and stir-fried vegetables.

One evening, as they huddled around their tiny kitchen table, Mei produced a crumpled flyer from her backpack. “Baba, my teacher said I should join this,” she announced, pushing it toward him. The page advertised an after-school mentorship program for immigrant children, led by a woman named Mrs. Morales.

Lin hesitated. Would Mei need him to stay with her? Would the program cost money? How would she handle being around people who spoke fluently when she was still finding her footing? But Mei’s earnest expression and the spark of hope in her voice nudged him forward. He nodded. “Okay, let’s try.”

Mei’s afternoons at the mentorship center proved transformative. Mrs. Morales, a spirited woman with a warm smile and a soft accent of her own, took an immediate liking to her. Through arts and crafts, games, and reading sessions, the program became a sanctuary where Mei’s confidence blossomed. She made tentative friendships with children like her—foreign in their surroundings but resilient in their dreams.

Meanwhile, Lin found solace in the camaraderie of his coworkers at the diner. Ahmed from Sudan taught him the quickest way to stack washed plates without breaking them. Marta from El Salvador corrected his English phrases with good-natured ribbing. For the first time since their arrival, Lin felt less alone in this vast, indifferent city.

But it wasn’t without challenges. One cold January night, Lin and Mei returned home to discover a notice slapped on their front door—they were behind on rent. The diner had cut his hours unexpectedly, leaving him scrambling to make ends meet. Mei’s schoolbooks were worn and borrowed; their supply of groceries dwindled faster than he could replenish them. He worked extra shifts where he could, but the perpetual exhaustion threatened to crush him.

Mei noticed. One evening, after clearing their meager dinner plates, she said, “Baba, I can help.”

Lin blinked at her. “Help? How?”

“I can draw. Remember those drawings I showed you from Mrs. Morales’ class? She said they were good—really good.”

Lin smiled despite himself. Mei always had an artist’s eye—a way of seeing the world in hues and shapes he often overlooked. “Yes,” he said gently. “You’re very talented. But how would that help?”

Mei bit her lip, then burst out, “I can sell them! At school, they sell candy bars for money. Why can’t I sell my pictures?”

Lin stared at her, his chest tightening. The prospect of Mei shouldering such a responsibility broke his heart, but her determination was undeniable. She reminded him so much of her mother in that moment that all he could do was nod.

By spring, their small, beige-walled apartment began to hold faint whispers of hope. Mei had joined the mentorship program’s art showcase, dazzling attendees—including Mrs. Morales—with her vivid watercolors that spoke of home and belonging. Her depictions of moonlit rice paddies and bustling village markets seemed to bridge the distance between her old life and her new one.

Lin, too, began to see faint glimmers of stability. A promotion to a line cook at the diner meant slightly higher pay, and through Mrs. Morales’ connections, he met a group of other Chinese immigrants who pooled resources to help one another.

Most importantly, Lin began to feel a sense of community. Though the city still loomed vast and intimidating, pockets of familiarity emerged—a Chinese grocer who set aside good vegetables for him, a neighbor who waved every morning as he left for work, Mei’s classmates and their shared laughter full of silly innocence.

Late one evening, as Lin stood by the apartment’s single small window looking out at the city lights, Mei approached him with a sketchbook in her hands.

“Baba?” she said, tugging at his sleeve.

He turned, glancing down at the colorful collage she’d drawn. It was their story. In the center, a humble figure of a man holding a little girl’s hand as she pointed toward the sky. Around them were crayon-painted scenes of the airport terminal, their apartment, a steaming bowl of noodles, and a city skyline capped with bright stars.

“It’s beautiful,” Lin said quietly, his throat tightening with emotion.

“It’s us,” Mei explained, leaning into him. “Because this is home too. Right, Baba?”

He pulled her into a strong hug, burying his face in her hair. “Yes, Mei. It is. Home is where we make it.”

And for the first time since their arrival, he truly believed it.
