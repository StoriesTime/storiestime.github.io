Likes and Lies: The Mask of Social Media


Maya sat on the corner of her bed, phone in hand, scrolling through picture-perfect images of people she barely knew. A warm cup of coffee sat forgotten on her desk, its steam curling up like silent whispers in the air. Her room was neat but unremarkable, blending into the sea of ordinary spaces that didn’t quite meet the Instagram-worthy standard. Today was just another day where she felt invisible in an ocean of headlines, hashtags, and heart-shaped currencies of validation.

It wasn’t always this way. Maya remembered a time when sharing something online was about connection—a moment preserved much like a photograph in a dusty album. Now, though, it felt different. It wasn’t about sharing anymore. It was about competing. The world had shifted, distorted through the glowing rectangle in her hand.

She stared at her latest post—a picture of her home-cooked dinner plated on a rustic wooden table. Her effort to emulate the aesthetic of food bloggers had garnered only twelve likes. Twelve. Maya’s stomach churned with a cocktail of disappointment, inadequacy, and jealousy. How did they do it? How did other people make their lives look so extraordinary? She let out a quiet sigh and opened an app she had found by accident months ago: PhotoHaven.

PhotoHaven was a lesser-known editing app but brimming with tools that transformed mundane shots into polished masterpieces. With a few taps and tweaks, her quiet dinner became an elaborate feast, dappled in soft golden light that Maya attributed wholly to the app’s filters. She stared at the new image. It was stunning, surreal even—but not hers anymore. The photo had become an idealized version of a meal she barely recognized. Still, if the likes increased, wouldn’t that make it worth it?

She uploaded the polished image. Within minutes, the likes trickled in. Her phone buzzed with notifications—heart emojis, compliments, shares. A surge of dopamine flooded her as validation washed over her insecurities. She refreshed the app compulsively, each new notification a fleeting affirmation that her life had meaning, that people noticed her.

Maya felt her heart swell when she saw a comment from Christine, one of the big influencers she followed: “This looks delish! 😍 Recipe, please?” Christine had over 50,000 followers—people who hung on to her every post, her every word. Maya felt like she had been noticed by royalty. Christine’s comment was all she needed to measure the success of her post against any self-doubt.

“Of course!” Maya replied enthusiastically. “I’ll DM it right now!”

The problem was, Maya didn’t have a recipe. The dinner she had snapped earlier wasn’t an original creation; it was a routine lasagna dinner she had put together using store-bought ingredients. But Christine’s request had planted a seed of panic. If Maya couldn’t deliver, then she’d have broken this fragile spell of approval. Her online facade would crumble into fragments of forgotten posts.

She started inventing—googling spices and sauces, scribbling notes, piecing together a recipe that sounded complex, sophisticated, believable. An hour later, she sent it over to Christine with a fabricated backstory about Italian culinary secrets she’d reportedly picked up during a summer abroad in Florence. Maya had never been to Italy, but her virtual persona told a different tale now.

As the days marched on, Maya found herself curating every aspect of her life for social media. She posted about adventurous hikes she never took, late-night books she never read, bespoke coffee shops she’d never actually visited. Her world was becoming ever more enviable and colorful, yet eerily detached from reality.

Each post gained traction. Her follower count climbed steadily—100, then 500, then breaking past 1,000. She became that person. The one whose life seemed curated perfectly, whose days glimmered under aesthetic filters and captions borrowed from poetry. Only Maya knew the truth: her life hadn’t changed. It was the same bedroom, the same coffee, the same mundane cycle, hidden behind a mask of curated deception.

Maya’s growing presence soon caught the attention of small brands that specialized in lifestyle products. Sponsorship deals trickled in: skincare companies, eco-friendly fashion lines, organic teas. At first, it felt like validation of her efforts—not just of her content, but of the person she had shaped herself into online. She celebrated each collaboration like a milestone, soothing her conscience with the idea that everyone “embellished a little” on social media. It wasn’t like anyone got hurt. Right?

But then came the first product review request: an expensive skincare serum whose quality was dubious at best. Maya had never tried it, yet the marketing team insisted it was revolutionary. They sent her a script filled with glowing praises, asking her to post a video claiming how radiant it had made her skin. She hesitated, scrutinizing her reflection in the camera. Her skin had been the same for years—flawed but familiar.

Still, the paycheck was enticing, and the prospect of further partnerships felt irresistible. “Everyone does this,” she murmured to herself, her voice quiet against the swirling storm of guilt. She recorded the video, polished it for hours, and hit “post.” Hundreds of likes poured in alongside comments of excitement and gratitude. Maya had convinced them. Her deception was imperceptible behind her cheerful tone and perfect lighting.

Weeks passed, and Maya found herself tangled ever deeper in her fabricated persona. She had turned her online profile into a spectacle of likes and applause, a cascade of illusions that grew larger and louder every day. But with the success came scrutiny—questions from followers, probing comments about her authenticity. It started small, with remarks like, “That lasagna recipe didn’t work for me. Did I miss a step?” As her posts amassed larger audiences, the occasional skeptic grew into a few persistent voices demanding answers.

One evening, Maya received an email, its subject line blunt and ominous: “Unmasked.” Inside was a single paragraph, accusing her of stolen recipes, stock photos, and fabricated reviews. Attached were screenshots and links to ingredients lifted directly from cooking blogs, side-by-side comparisons of pictures she claimed were her own. The message ended with a line that sent a chill down her spine: “Confess now, or someone else will.”

Maya stared at her laptop, the glow from the screen casting shallow shadows across her face. Her heart pounded, a cacophony in her chest that drowned out rational thought. She had built her empire atop this delicate tower of fabricated perfection; she hadn’t considered what would happen if it all collapsed.

That night, she drafted a post—a long, heartfelt confession explaining her insecurities, her need for online validation, her misguided attempts to fit into the online version of herself she had crafted. It wasn’t easy. Every sentence felt like peeling away a layer of armor, exposing the raw truth beneath: Maya didn’t have an enviable life, an adventurous spirit, or flawless skin. She didn’t have a recipe worth posting. She had only tangled herself in the mask of social media until she barely recognized herself.

When she finally hit “post,” Maya felt the weight leave her shoulders before dread took its place. Minutes passed like hours as she stared at the empty span of her notifications. Finally, they came—but not in the form she feared. Comments filled her inbox not to shame her, but to thank her. Hundreds of messages poured in—followers explaining how they had also felt the crushing pressure to perform online, how they too had pretended, exaggerated, and lied for the sake of likes.

One comment stood out in its simplicity: “I’m tired of pretending, too. Thank you for being real.”

The following months weren’t easy. Maya lost many brand deals, and her follower count dwindled. Yet what remained felt different. Her account wasn’t built on unreachable ideals anymore; it had become a space for honesty, community, and real connection. Likes still sprinkled her posts, but they no longer held a disproportionate sway over her emotions. Somewhere behind the follower count and analytics, Maya found herself again—a real person who didn’t need filters or falsehoods to matter.

The mask was gone for good.
