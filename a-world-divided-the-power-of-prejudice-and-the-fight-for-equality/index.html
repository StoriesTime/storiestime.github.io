A World Divided: The Power of Prejudice and the Fight for Equality


The city of Iridia shimmered on the horizon like a silver mirage, its spires wreathed in the morning haze, its heartbeat the echo of a thousand years of progress and pain. In Iridia, everything glistened: its glass walkways, its electric gardens, its memory towers that scraped the clouds and stored the stories of ancestors. But beneath the veneer, like so many great cities, Iridia’s soul was pocked with scars that no amount of light could truly heal.

I. Dawn in the East Quarter

On a sun-split morning, twelve-year-old Mara awoke to the sound of sirens, a distant, shrill wailing that clung to the streets of East Quarter like fog. Mara’s family lived in a four-room flat beneath the shadow of the city’s edge, where the walls bore the stains of generations of relegation. She pressed her face to the cracked window, peering down at the crowd gathering below.

They were her people—known as Vesari—identifiable by the pale blue birthmarks that trailed their arms like veins of quartz. In Iridia, the Vesari could not hold office, attend the famed universities of the city, or walk certain streets after dusk. Shopkeepers counted change twice when a Vesari hand extended; landlords tripled their rents. By law, everything was equal, but in practice, water found every crack—prejudice seeped into every pore of city life.

Mara’s mother, Lia, called from the kitchen. “Hurry, or you’ll miss the line for bread!”

Mara grabbed her faded satchel and darted down three flights of stairs, joining the current of Vesari snaking along Brick Street. Whispers rippled through the crowd—something had happened last night at the border with the North Quarter, where the Enari lived. The Enari—those with dark, iridescent freckles swirling on their cheeks—owned much of Iridia’s wealth and power.

Clutching her ration card, Mara turned her head. Two city guards watched the crowd, their silver uniforms unimpeachable, their eyes shielded by mirrored visors.

As the bread truck arrived and the masses surged, an Enari boy pushed through the throng—his clothes too fine for the quarter, his face flushed. The crowd recoiled, murmurs flaring. Before anyone could ask him why he was there, the boy—the son of a councilman, it would turn out—addressed the Vesari, voice trembling but passionate.

“I came to help,” he declared. His accent was crisp, unbowed by hardship.

The crowd jeered, tossed old fruit. The guards made no move to help the boy, but neither did they help the crowd. The city needed its divisions; a wound, after all, was easier to study when it stayed open.

II. Lines in the Sand

The boy’s name was Cassian. Only Mara watched as he was jostled against the curb, his expensive coat torn. Something inside her rebelled—not a fondness for the Enari, but a crack in the prescribed hatred.

That night, Iridia unfolded its procession of lights, the city split by its invisible yet unyielding border—a river called the Divide, where bridges existed in design more than reality. Mara could not cross to the North Quarter, nor could Cassian easily return once the guards shooed him away, a lesson in humility disguised as routine security protocol.

But for weeks, Mara caught glimpses of Cassian at the bread lines. He watched, listened, sometimes passed whispered notes to children who could read—stories from the “other side,” tales of wealth and education, threaded with an ache for something better.

Mara began to wonder—how real was this boundary? Was prejudice simply tradition refashioned as necessity? What might happen if someone laid a plank across the river, even for an hour?

III. Underground Currents

The engine of resistance in Iridia had always been the Whisperers—a loosely knit group, equal parts rumor and reality, committed to recording injustices and broadcasting them from clandestine radio outposts. Mara’s grandfather, Jale, was one of them, his memory sharp as a blade in his seventies.

Jale taught her to hunt for truth beneath the city’s gossip, to listen to the stories that did not make the sanctioned broadcasts. Each night, he and Mara tuned in, eavesdropping on transmissions that said what the day’s news never would: about arbitrary arrests, about businesses fined into closure for serving the “wrong kind” of customer, about the swift, cold violence that coursed down Alleys during those times the city called “incidents.”

It was through these broadcasts that Mara learned the shape of hope—hope measured not in mighty acts, but in the accumulation of small, brave choices.

One evening, with rain rapping the windowpanes, Mara whispered to Jale, “What if no one cares? What if it’s always like this?”

Her grandfather smiled, the wrinkles at his eyes deepening. “Change is a tide. It starts somewhere, even if you don’t see the moon that pulls it.”

Bolstered by her conversations with Cassian—now a tenuous friend—she proposed something radical: a children’s council, Vesari and Enari together, broadcast anonymously through the Whisperers’ network. Each would share accounts from their worlds, unfiltered and unedited.

Would anyone listen?

IV. Voices Across the Divide

The first transmission aired on a moonless night. Mara, her voice steady, described her day: how her school’s ceiling leaked in winter, how her friend’s mother lost her work permit with no explanation. Cassian spoke next, recounting his discomfort when his father’s colleagues joked about “dirty quarters,” and how his teachers discouraged friendships with Vesari.

The reaction was immediate and electric. Children in both quarters recognized each other’s voices. Some wept; some demanded why these divisions existed; some, emboldened, shared their own stories.

For the first time, the city heard the raw, beating heart behind the rhetoric of fear: a city’s worth of children, longing simply for open streets, for schools where they could study together, for the chance to see what the world looked like from both banks of the river.

Of course, not everyone applauded. Councilors denounced the broadcast as illegal. The guards swept through the East Quarter, searching for the source. But the Whisperers were too clever; their network danced through empty apartments, bounced from rooftop to rooftop.

V. A Crack in the City’s Armor

The broadcasts grew bolder. Together, Mara and Cassian organized secret deliveries—books, medicine, even vegetables—across the Divide. Rumors began that, just maybe, the Enari and Vesari children were not so different, that hatred was a script rather than an instinct.

One evening, an unexpected guest arrived at Mara’s home. It was Cassian’s mother, Anitra, trembling but determined. She had heard her son’s voice on the radio. Instead of scolding him, she listened. For the first time, she saw the East Quarter not as a wasteland, but as a neighborhood brimming with resilience, love, and quiet defiance.

Their conversation, awkward at first, blossomed into understanding. Anitra confessed her own isolation, how even among the powerful, she felt trapped in a cage of politeness and fear.

A gradual coalition formed. Teachers, artists, shopkeepers—people whose names weren’t known by the council—met in hidden rooms and lit candles against the city’s gloom. They carried stories like contraband, passing them downstairs and across the river.

VI. The Night of Broken Bridges

A year passed, and Iridia’s divisions deepened—a war of attrition. When a city guard shot a Vesari boy for crossing into the North Quarter with a loaf of bread, protests erupted.

This was where Mara and Cassian’s efforts met their test. The children’s council called for a march—not to the government’s gates, but to the Divide itself.

On the appointed day, thousands gathered at the riverbanks. Vesari and Enari, young and old, huddled opposite each other. At noon, Mara and Cassian, hands joined, stepped onto the forbidden bridge. Behind them, a wave of children followed. City guards shouted themselves hoarse, radios crackling in helpless alarm.

Some adults braced for violence. But Mara and Cassian pressed on, each step heavier than the last, until—like magic—the crowd began to cross from both directions. No force could hold them back. In an unplanned, unscripted moment, the river became a meeting place, not a boundary.

On the bridge, Mara handed Cassian a faded photograph: her family gathered at a birthday party beneath a leaky roof—a moment of joy, despite everything. Cassian showed her the book he had carried for weeks, a book forbidden in the East Quarter because it celebrated Vesari poets.

They exchanged gifts, a ritual older than the city itself—an answer, however small, to years of exclusion.

VII. Iridia at Dawn

After the march, the city could never quite return to its former silence. True, there were setbacks—a backlash by hardliners, more patrols, graffiti sprayed in venomous colors—but the air was different. Children could spot an enemy, or a friend, with equal ease now. The broadcasts continued, more inventive than ever; their signal, carried on the wind, reached districts no one had considered before.

Mara and Cassian grew older, and with them, a generation unafraid to challenge the lines drawn on old maps by fearful hands. Every week, more Enari and Vesari families joined the coalition. Each story shared, each secret picnic by the river, eroded the rhetoric of division.

Gradually, schools began to accept applications from Vesari students. The councilors were voted out, replaced by representatives from both quarters. It was not a perfect city; old habits clung like weeds, but the ground was braver, less brittle.

Iridia’s towers—now connected not just by bridges of steel and concrete, but by the invisible, resilient threads of empathy and understanding—gleamed brighter in the sunrise.

VIII. Epilogue: A Future Written in Their Own Words

In time, Mara and Cassian wrote their own histories—not of a perfect world, but of a determined one. They spoke to crowds who had once been kept apart, warning them of how easy it was to let fear masquerade as tradition. They reminded Iridia’s citizens that prejudice, left unchecked, built prisons invisible and inescapable.

But in the face of such odds, the city had chosen not to build higher walls, but broader bridges.

And in the squares at twilight, old songs—sung in both the Vesari and Enari tongues—rose like prayers above the city’s rooftops, winding through alleyways and garden courts, refusing ever again to be silenced by the dividing river.

No longer a world divided, but a city mending, one voice at a time.
