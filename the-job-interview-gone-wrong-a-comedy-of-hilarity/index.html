The Job Interview Gone Wrong: A Comedy of Hilarity


If there’s ever been a less auspicious start to a Tuesday, Lionel Burrows cannot recall it. The day begins at 7:15 AM, with a ferocious cat howling at the door as if auditioning for a horror film. Lionel lurches out of bed, steps barefoot into a puddle of what smells suspiciously like artisanal yogurt, and the day slides downhill with unrestrained momentum.

Today is the day of his Big Interview. He’s finally landed a shot at Quokkaly, Inc., the start-up everyone in town won’t stop buzzing about. Specializing in “cloud-based solutions for existential crises,” Quokkaly promises nap pods, kombucha on tap, anxiety therapy dogs, and a salary worth more than his entire apartment building. His life is supposed to change today. Lionel is supposed to become “Lionel: gainfully employed adult.”

But right now, Lionel is “Lionel: damp-footed, un-caffeinated, and muttering threats at a tabby.” He manages to flick on the kettle, untangle his only ironed shirt from the cat (“I will not hold a grudge, Persimmon, but you owe me rent and dry-cleaning”), and rehearse his answers to why he wants the job. (“My landlord would like this too.”)

At 7:38 AM, the kettle shrieks. Lionel pours water over yesterday’s coffee grounds. It tastes like betrayal. He tells himself: “It can only get better, right?”

He’s wrong, of course.

Act I: The Ill-Fated Commute

Lionel boards the bus at 8:03 AM. The bus lurches like a cranky walrus, the driver’s playlist an ear-splitting mix of polka and death metal. A kid kicks Lionel’s shin with the cheerful indifference of someone who hasn’t learned that pain is bad. Another passenger, beside a gigantic terracotta plant, informs the entire vehicle that “Starfish are the only creatures capable of real vengeance.”

Lionel’s phone vibrates: “Interviewer: Please let us know if you’ll be late!” He has twenty-seven minutes to cover six miles through morning gridlock—a logistical feat that only the most optimistic Google Maps algorithm could imagine.

He manages to spill lukewarm coffee on his shirt. It blooms across his torso: the world’s saddest Rorschach blot. A sweet old lady offers him a wet wipe and points out, “You look nervous. Don’t trip getting off. The curb is higher than it looks.”

Act II: The Arrival Calamity

He arrives at Quokkaly’s glass-enclosed, futuristic headquarters at 8:52 AM. The lobby looks like the bridge of a spaceship, all neon and chromed edges. The receptionist greets him with rehearsed cheer: “Welcome, traveler!” Lionel is fairly certain he’s stepped into a cult’s recruitment video.

The receptionist asks, “Do you have your visitor badge?” Lionel produces a wrinkled printout. She scans it, her eyebrow arching as the scanner beeps, then explodes in a shower of blue sparks. “You may need to stand back, Mr. Borrow—Browers? Burros?”

“Burrows,” Lionel corrects gently, only for a second badge to appear from a tiny chute with the name “Aloysius Bumbershoot, Third Mate.”

“Close enough,” says the receptionist brightly, handing him the badge and waving him through.

He steps toward the elevator, which opens to reveal four golden retrievers wearing tiny ties. Behind them is a looming 7-foot man with a diamond nose-piercing and a T-shirt that reads, “I Will Judge Your Fonts.” The man grins. “Don’t worry about the dogs. They’re here for everybody’s anxiety.”

Lionel stares at the dogs. The dogs stare back, wisdom in their eyes. It feels vaguely like being judged by the pantheon of workplace spirits. Tidying his coffee-stained shirt, Lionel gets into the elevator.

Act III: The Not-So-Quiet Room

The elevator chimes, hiccups, and delivers Lionel to the “Zen Interview Lounge.” It is neither Zen nor lounge: there are bean bags strewn across the floor, a fog machine softly wheezing in a corner, and an intern frantically sweeping up a mess of kale chips.

“Lionel!” a cheery voice rings out. A bespectacled woman in a business blazer and slacks strides over. “Melinda! I’ll be interviewing you for the Cloud Solutions role. You look… prepared.”

Lionel tries to smile, but a kale chip crunches under his foot. “Ready to cloud those solutions!”

Melinda blinks. “Let’s get started. First, tell me about a time you overcame a challenge.”

Which one does he start with? The cat. The death metal polka. The badge explosion. He begins, “Well, actually, this morning—” but Melinda’s phone buzzes. She glances down, grimaces. “One moment.”

She steps aside, whispering furiously into the phone. Lionel tries to sit, but the nearest bean bag sighs dramatically and flattens into the carpet. With all the grace of a tranquilized giraffe, he collapses onto it, knees to chin, resigned.

Melinda returns, looking frazzled. “Sorry about that. Lunch delivery crisis. Now: where were we?”

Lionel points at the fog machine innocently trailing a snake of mist toward the electrical socket.

“That’s new,” says Melinda.

He launches into his most recent triumph—“I once delivered a project two weeks early…”—just as the fog machine emits a sound like a dying goose and starts belching thick clouds. Melinda’s eyes water. “Let’s… maybe move to the Focus Aquarium.”

Act IV: The Focus Aquarium

Lionel follows Melinda through a maze of hallways into a glass-walled chamber filled with gently bubbling aquariums. A jellyfish undulates in the corner. An intern, ankle-deep in water, mutters about “bio-hazard pay.”

Melinda gestures at a dry chair. Lionel sits, back straight. She resumes. “Technical question: If you had to migrate a data center from a haunted mansion to a haunted cruise ship, what’s your first step?”

Lionel, sleep-deprived and smelling faintly of old latte, blurts out: “Hire an exorcist, check if the ghosts unionize, and backup everything twice.”

There’s a beat of silence. Melinda bursts out laughing, clutching her notebook for dear life. “Honestly, that’s fresher than the jellyfish!” Lionel laughs too. Some tension breaks—at least until a rogue fish leaps from an aquarium, landing with a dramatic flop near Lionel’s left loafer.

Melinda, unfazed, tosses the fish back. “Happens every hour.”

Act V: Office Parkour

The “brief skills assessment” takes place on the top floor, requiring Lionel to follow Melinda through a warren of indoor gardens, winding stairs, and an open-office swing set. They pass a yoga class meditating to whale song and a man in a banana suit typing furiously at two laptops.

Finally, they reach a conference room, where “The Panel”—three people and a goldendoodle—wait, each holding a Nerf gun. Melinda introduces everyone: “This is Dora, Marketing. Kaspar, Engineering. Sergey, Strategy… and this is Ted, the dog. He’s not a real judge.”

The assessment is a war of logic puzzles and pop quizzes, all delivered with the speed and deadpan of a sketch comedy. “You have two servers and three pies. Every time the cat meows, one server crashes. How much pie is left after 14 hours?”

Lionel launches into an answer involving equations and a very confused server with a lactose intolerance. Kaspar nods, scribbles something that looks like a pirate map.

Suddenly, Ted the dog lets out a bark and launches a foam dart that bounces off Lionel’s nose. There is a moment of stunned silence. Dora squeals, delighted. “It’s a hit! You passed the first test!”

Act VI: Snack Catastrophe

After the panel is satisfied (or simply hungry), Melinda ushers Lionel to the break room for “collaborative snacking.” The Quokkaly team, eager for their free-range, vegan, gluten-averse pastries, descends like seagulls on a trash barge.

Lionel, reaching for a muffin, accidentally sends a plate spinning off the counter and into the Chief Happiness Officer’s lap. The room falls silent. The happiness officer, an intimidating man with biceps that could intimidate steel beams, smiles. “Happens all the time. Please, call me Brett.”

Brett rises, grabs a fistful of muffins, and offers Lionel one. Crisis averted.

Act VII: The Final Exam (and Near Escape)

Melinda leads Lionel back to the Zen Interview Lounge. She sits, clipboard in hand.

“So—if you had to describe yourself with three breakfast cereals and a minor swear word, what would they be?” she asks.

Lionel’s mouth launches into overdrive before his brain can catch up. “I’m… Lucky Charms for energy. Shredded Wheat for honesty. Count Chocula for moments of mischief. And, uh, ‘bollocks’—because things go sideways, but I keep rolling.”

It shouldn’t work. Yet, Melinda claps, howling with laughter. “That’s the best answer I’ve heard all week. Let’s wrap up, shall we?”

As Lionel stands, his badge falls off. The dogs from earlier saunter by, tails wagging in approval.

“Final question,” says Melinda, deadly serious. “Can you start tomorrow?”

Lionel can hardly believe it; his brain whirrs, looking for the prank. “Was… was this all real?”

Melinda grins. “We like to see how people handle chaos. You handled it… spectacularly.”

Just then, the fog machine emits one last triumphant hiss. The power flickers. Somewhere, a polka song resumes on an unseen speaker.

Lionel looks down at his coffee-stained shirt, his “Aloysius Bumbershoot, Third Mate” badge, and the golden retrievers about to lead a yoga class next door.

He grins. “I wouldn’t miss it for the world.”

Thus concludes Lionel Burrows’s journey through the Great Job Interview Fiasco—the story of one man, five dogs, and a kingdom of chaos. In a world obsessed with workplace perfection, sometimes it’s the days that go entirely, deliciously wrong that set the stage for the best beginnings.
