Sundials and Spires: Hidden Stories of Big Ben


In the heart of London, where the river Thames flows like a silver thread through the vibrant tapestry of history and modernity, stands an icon known the world over—not just for its massive clock face, but for the stories whispered in its shadows. The tower commonly known as Big Ben, though technically the name of the great bell within, holds secrets beyond its famously precise chimes.

The grand Elizabeth Tower isn’t merely a monument of time; it is a silent witness to centuries of change, social upheavals, and architectural ingenuity. Beneath the famous clock faces and the booming bell lies a tale stitched together by sundials, spires, and the lesser-known anecdotes that escape typical historical recounts.

The Sundial That Never Told Time

Few realize that the precinct surrounding Big Ben once housed a sundial, an ancient residue from a London long before the industrial age fixed hours by the tick of a mechanical clock. This sundial was not merely ornamental. It was part of the early attempts to measure time based on the sun’s position—a practical tool navigating the day for those who worked nearby in the Palace of Westminster.

The sundial, carved from a single chunk of granite and embedded near the south entrance of the tower’s base, often went unnoticed by hurried tourists. Yet, it was a peculiar relic amidst the clock’s dominant reign. Why place a sundial near a colossal clock designed to render the sun’s verdict on time obsolete?

Legend has it that this sundial was a deliberate nod to tradition by Charles Barry and Augustus Pugin, the architects behind the Gothic Revival styling of the tower completed in 1859. They woven sundials into the design as an homage to mankind’s age-old quest to understand time—from the primal shadow cast by a stick to the intricate gears and pendulums of the clock.

Yet, the sundial never quite functioned accurately in that location. The massive spires and the tower itself threw shadows that confounded the simple science of solar time measurement. It was a sundial trapped in the shadow of the clock—both literally and figuratively.

The Gothic Spires: More Than Decoration

Look up beyond the clock face and you’ll notice the slender Gothic spires that crown the Elizabeth Tower. These are not just the product of Victorian architectural flair; they carry symbolic meaning and practical function.

Victorian engineers and designers favored the intricate spires as a way to link the modern structure to the medieval past, evoking the sense of continuity and resilience in a time of rapid industrial change. The spires, reaching skyward, seemed to echo the aspirations of an empire at its peak; each sharp pinnacle directed attention upward, a metaphor for progress and hope.

What’s less obvious is the function these spires served. They work as lightning rods, crucial in an era when lightning’s destructive power could easily incapacitate the clock’s delicate mechanisms. The spires channel storms safely to the ground, protecting Big Ben from harm. The combination of beauty and utility in the design reflects a Victorian ethos that sought to marry the poetic with the practical—a dance between art and engineering.

Interwoven Histories: The Hours Behind the Bell

The tower’s bell—Big Ben—was cast in 1858 and its powerful resounding chime has marked the hours ever since, serving as a sonic heartbeat for Londoners near and far. But behind that familiar sound lies a history intertwined with risk, innovation, and untold stories.

The bell was named, somewhat mysteriously, after Sir Benjamin Hall, the engineer in charge during its installation. Sir Benjamin was a man known for his imposing stature and voice, as well as the exercises he advocated for worker safety in mills and factories.

The bell’s weight and depth of sound posed unique challenges. Its hammer was designed carefully to avoid cracking the bell, a disaster that nearly happened on inaugural testing. After a small fracture did appear, the bell was silent for years before being repaired and tolled again on New Year’s Eve in 1859.

The story goes that during World War II, the tower was a target for bombing raids, given its iconic status. The clock faces and internal mechanisms sustained damage, but the structure endured. The resilience of Big Ben became a symbol of British steadfastness.

The Hidden Room: Where Time Stands Still

Not many are aware of a secret room tucked high within the Elizabeth Tower, accessible only by a narrow spiral staircase that spirals upward beyond the ringing bell gallery. This is the clock keeper’s chamber, a space frozen in time.

In this chamber, meticulous ledgers and journals line the wooden shelves. Handwritten notes from generations of clock keepers detail the trials of maintaining Big Ben’s clock mechanism through fog, war, and modernization. The room houses spare parts, tools, and a small library of horological texts, blending science with craftsman artistry.

One entry from the 1920s describes an instance when the clock stopped for three hours after a freak storm, triggering panic among Londoners who relied on the precision of Big Ben’s hourly call. The keeper’s log reflected not just technical notes but also a deep reverence for the clock as a guardian of daily life.

Beyond Timekeeping: Big Ben in Popular Imagination

Big Ben is more than a timekeeper; it is a cultural icon embedded in countless stories, films, and memories. It is the sound of celebratory fireworks on New Year’s Eve, the backdrop for political events, and a rallying symbol during times of national crisis.

Each bell strike marks not only an hour but a collective heartbeat of Londoners, British citizens, and visitors worldwide. The chimes echo through film scores and literature, striking a chord of familiarity and timelessness.

Despite its mass appeal, the layered nuances—the sundial’s shadow, the spires’ vigilant watch, the hidden room’s intimate records—remain known only to the few who delve deep into the history of this magnificent tower.

The Legacy of Sundials and Spires

Big Ben, with its blend of old and new timekeeping, stands as a testament to humanity’s relentless quest for order amid chaos. From the sundial acknowledging the sun’s movement to the clock’s mechanical precision, each element tells a piece of London’s evolving story, a chronicle of innovation, symbolism, and endurance.

The spires reaching towards the sky remind us that architecture is a dialogue across generations—a message carved in stone and metal that invites contemplation. Meanwhile, the bell’s thunderous voice narrates the relentless passing of time, the constancy amid a changing world.

Next time you find yourself in London, pausing in the shadow of the Elizabeth Tower, remember to look beyond the massive clock faces and the resounding bell. Seek the quiet sundial near the base, trace your eyes up to the lightning-catching spires, and imagine the keepers who, for over 150 years, have safeguarded the time.

Big Ben is not just a clock or a bell; it is a chronicle of human aspiration, resilience, and a testament to the stories hidden within sundials and spires.
