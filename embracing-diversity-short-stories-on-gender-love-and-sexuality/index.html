Embracing Diversity: Short Stories on Gender, Love, and Sexuality


Jules always noticed the windows first—St. Marion’s Cathedral with its dizzying panes, Gilmore Library’s mosaic skylights casting colored light on the hardback spines, the apartment on Sixth Street where sunlight danced through rainbow-hued glass. As a child, Jules thought looking through different windows could change the world’s shape, let them fit in places they weren’t sure they belonged. Even then, Jules kept notebooks full of dreams — drawings of stained glass, old poetry lines scribbled in neat, blocky letters, quick sketches of faces both familiar and imagined.

Jules did not have the words—at least, not then—for the fluidity inside them, the way their heart skipped and tumbled for both the soft curve of Marian’s laugh and the thrill woven in Eli’s warm, callused hand. Back then, people had words ready before Jules did, tossing them like coins in the school hallway: tomboy, sissy, experiment, phase.

At sixteen, Jules liked Marian Chase so fiercely it stung. She wore thick glasses and told improvised fairy tales, each more wandering and wild than the last. Jules wanted to catch Marian’s laughter in their palm and hold it like something precious — a keepsake for darker days. Once, at the autumn festival, Marian kissed Jules behind the lemonade stand. The taste haunted them for weeks afterward, somehow sweet and brave at the same time.

Jules worked up the courage to tell their older brother, Ned, who lived in the city.

“That’s wonderful, bug,” Ned replied, grinning, pouring Jules a mug of coffee. “You’re looking at me like you always did: like a mystery novel with half the pages missing. Want to talk about it?”

Jules tried. “I don’t know if I’m what people say girls are. Or boys. Sometimes I think I’m both. Sometimes neither. Maybe—maybe I’m just Jules. And I love Marian, but sometimes… sometimes I dream about boys too. Or people who are neither, or both, or neither and both.”

Ned squeezed Jules’ hand. “You’re just you. And that’s the most honest thing you can be.”

If only it felt so simple at school. By winter, rumors traced Jules’s name in icy whispers. One peer called Jules “lost in a phase,” another, in a nastier turn, “confused.”

It wasn’t until Jules met Sam that the world clicked sideways, offering another opening in the glass.

Sam transferred in sophomore year. Their hair was dyed the sharp blue of cartoon lightning, and they wore vintage boots decorated with patches. Everybody whispered about Sam, in that way small towns do. Jules envied that kind of brave.

One evening, after their art club finished painting the community mural, Jules stayed behind, sweeping stray paint flecks from the brick path. Sam lingered too, hands shoved in deep coat pockets.

“You did the pansies, right?” Sam said.

Jules nodded.

“They change colors in the light. Look—” Sam pointed, turning Jules toward the wall. The yellow petals blushed faint lavender beneath the streetlamp.

“I like things that change,” Sam continued. “People are like that too, aren’t they?”

Jules found it easy to talk then, easy to laugh, easy to admit that the landscape inside themselves shifted like soft clay. They sat on the mural’s edge, knees knocking together, and let the conversation take on its own living shape.

Over the next months, Jules and Sam became constellation friends: always orbiting, sometimes apart, but always finding their way back. They mixed thrift store finds for a gender-bending costume contest, staged living room karaoke, shared secrets. For the first time, Jules didn’t have to act a role. They could wear a dress or a blazer, tune their voice high or low, and Sam would just grin and say, “That’s you, Jules, all the way.”

Jules learned, through Sam’s stories, about the language that described what they’d always felt: nonbinary, pansexual, queer. Hearing these words out loud felt like unlocking doors in windows previously bricked over.

But not everyone responded this kindly. At the Valentine’s dance, Sam leaned over and kissed Jules on the cheek. The gymnasium lights flickered against sequins and rented tuxes. A sophomore snapped a photo. Within an hour, the picture swirled across group chats. The comments were a mosaic of confusion, mockery, curiosity. A bruised sort of courage pooled in Jules’s stomach.

That night, Jules cried. Not quietly, but with the kind of broken sobs that come when the world splits open. Ned drove over when he got the call.

Ned sat beside Jules on the porch, silence spiraling between them.

“I’m sorry,” Jules whispered.

“For what?”

“For making things complicated. For not just being… easier.”

“You’re complicated because you’re alive,” Ned said. “People think they want simple answers, but honestly, everyone’s living some secret they think no one will understand. And whoever said you needed to make sense to anyone but yourself?”

The next Monday, Jules painted over the hurt. They wore a purple jacket with silver pins, cropped their hair short. When someone whispered “freak” in the hallway, Jules smiled. Let them stare.

A few weeks later, Jules received an envelope—unsigned, the handwriting cramped. Inside was a note: I don’t know how to say I’m like you. Thanks for being brave enough to be seen.

Jules pressed the note into the pages of their old notebook. They didn’t know who sent it. Maybe Marian, or someone else at school who huddled alone at lunch. Maybe someone they’d never met but who’d seen them shining weird and proud through the crowd.

That summer, Jules thought about windows again: glass ticking with raindrops, the colored rays of the cathedral, the skylights flooding the library stacks. There were other ways to see things, other kinds of light. The panes were never just one color.

In July, when the city held its Pride parade, Jules, Sam, and Ned went together. Sam painted flags on everyone’s cheeks—trans, nonbinary, genderqueer, rainbow. They joined the march, surrounded by a river of color. Jules didn’t feel strange or lost. They felt held, swept into a crowd where every window let in the sun.

Jules met people at the parade whose stories felt like old echoing songs—Tessie and her wife, carrying twins in their stroller; Bashir, who came out late and wore badge pins on his jacket; Shira, who dressed in sequins and worked for the local radio. Each person was different, threads in a tapestry, and Jules mingled among them, asking questions, listening to old heartbreaks and the joy of new beginnings.

Standing on the sidewalk, the crowd roaring past, Jules realized: the world shifts depending on where you stand, who you stand beside. On some days, the glass is cloudy, and other times clear or rainbow-streaked, but always there’s a way to peer through, to offer your story and let someone else see themselves in the reflection.

At eighteen, Jules left for college in the city, a place where no one tossed their past as an anchor. There, Jules met friends who used all manner of pronouns, argued philosophy in the park after midnight, fell in love and out again, sometimes with boys, sometimes with girls, sometimes with people who refused categories altogether.

One night, lying on the dorm roof, Jules counted satellites blinking overhead, thinking about all the versions of themselves—child with the notebooks, adolescent fumbling for words, teen painting pansexual colors in a country gym. None of those selves had vanished. Each was a window, lined up side by side, hues shifting depending how Jules chose to see.

Jules laughed—soft and a little awed. They closed their eyes, and for a moment, the world behind every pane came alive in color: Marian’s easy grin, Sam’s wide-eyed questions, Ned’s tired, loyal patience. All those stories, all that love, weaving together. Today, tomorrow, always.

And in that shifting light, Jules knew: they never wanted to live on one side of the glass, never wanted to numb the colors or settle for the shadows. There’s no single right way to love, no universal definition of self. That understanding knits itself into the world’s fabric, not a pronouncement but a quiet, everyday miracle—one window, one story at a time.
