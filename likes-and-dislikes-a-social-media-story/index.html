Likes and Dislikes: A Social Media Story


Maya had always been fascinated by the way people presented themselves on social media. She had a thousand followers on her profile, though she wasn’t sure why. Her posts weren’t anything spectacular—just random snapshots of her daily life, occasional musings about books she was reading, or quirky moments she captured on her phone. Yet, something about the way she posted and how people reacted to it intrigued her. There was an invisible dance between herself and the world that she didn’t fully understand, but it was addictive, like an itch she couldn’t stop scratching.

The Birth of Likes

It was during a late-night scroll through her feed that Maya stumbled across an article about the history of social media likes. She had always wondered why people were so obsessed with them, why some posts would receive dozens of hearts while others barely registered any interaction. The article explained how likes originated as a simple mechanism to show approval or appreciation for content. At first, they seemed innocent enough—a way to say “I see you,” “I like this,” or even “I approve of this post.”

But, over time, the simple thumbs-up evolved. It became a powerful currency in the online world. The more likes a post received, the more valuable it seemed. It was like a social scorecard—except the rules were unspoken, and no one really knew how the game was played. Maya had often wondered whether people genuinely liked the posts they clicked on, or if they were just following some unwritten algorithm of what was considered “likeable.”

The article mentioned a pivotal moment—the introduction of the “like” button on Facebook in 2009. The idea was revolutionary, creating a sense of community and connection between strangers across the globe. But as the years passed, it also fostered competition. Who had the most likes? Who could generate the most viral content? Who could craft the perfect persona for the digital world?

The Shift in Perception

Maya’s curiosity grew deeper as she read through the article. She realized that likes had stopped being just about the content. It wasn’t about the quality or the creativity anymore. It was about how the post made people feel. The likes were a validation of one’s existence—a way to measure if your thoughts, actions, or ideas mattered. To some, it felt like the number of likes you got was directly proportional to your worth in the online world.

Maya, being someone who often thought deeply about the implications of social media, began to feel conflicted. She had always posted things that were meaningful to her, not necessarily aiming for likes. But over time, even she couldn’t deny the occasional rush she got when she saw her posts receiving more likes than usual. It felt good, like a small pat on the back from the digital universe.

Then came the dislike button.

Maya chuckled as she read about it in the article. The dislike button, which was tested on some platforms for a while, had sparked an uproar. Some saw it as a necessary counterbalance to the overwhelming positivity of likes. Others feared that it would breed negativity and division in the digital space. The idea was quickly abandoned, but the damage was done. People started to wonder: if we can’t dislike something openly, how can we express our discontent without just scrolling past or ignoring it altogether?

Maya thought back to the posts that had irked her over the years. The endless selfies, the overly edited pictures that showed a perfect life, the posts about what someone had eaten for breakfast—all of them seemed like hollow attempts to garner attention. Yet, even if she disagreed with these posts, she had no outlet to show her disapproval except for unfollowing someone or simply ignoring their content.

But that, Maya realized, was the problem. Social media had become a curated space, where only the most likeable versions of people and ideas were allowed to thrive. The posts that generated likes were often superficial, glossing over the complexities of life. No one wanted to post something messy, something real, something that might invite scrutiny. Everything had to be polished, perfect, and, most importantly, liked.

The Pressure of Perfection

It didn’t take long for Maya to notice the pressure building around her. The pressure to keep posting content that garnered likes. The pressure to fit into a mold that was both familiar and liked by her audience. She found herself second-guessing every post, wondering if it would get enough likes or if people would scroll past without a second thought. The weight of it was overwhelming at times.

Her friends had started talking about it more frequently. “Did you see how many likes Sarah got on her vacation post?” one would ask. “I can’t believe my photo only got 20 likes. I thought it was pretty good,” another would say, visibly disappointed.

Maya’s feed was filled with perfectly timed photos of sunsets, fashion outfits, and the latest trends. It was all so… predictable. She was growing tired of it. It was like everyone was following the same formula, trying to be the same person, just in different ways.

One evening, after yet another disheartening scroll through her feed, Maya made a decision. She would break free from the cycle. She would stop caring so much about the likes and focus on sharing what truly mattered to her. She wasn’t going to follow the trends anymore. If her posts didn’t get many likes, so be it. She would share her real thoughts, her genuine feelings, and let the likes fall where they may.

A New Beginning

Maya’s first post after this epiphany was a simple one. It was a photo of an old book she had just finished reading, with a handwritten note on the back that said, “I loved the way this book made me think about the world differently. I highly recommend it.” She didn’t expect much of a reaction. There was no catchy caption, no trendy filter, no glamour. Just her, being honest.

To her surprise, the post didn’t go unnoticed. It didn’t go viral, but it got more engagement than she had expected. People commented, sharing their own thoughts on the book, or offering recommendations for other reads. Some of her followers even mentioned how refreshing it was to see a post that wasn’t trying to sell a lifestyle or a product.

Maya realized then that maybe the likes weren’t the most important thing after all. It was the connections—the conversations—that mattered more. People were eager for authenticity, something they could relate to, something real.

The next few weeks were an experiment. Maya shared a mix of posts—some light-hearted, some serious, some personal, and some just for fun. She stopped caring about the metrics. She learned to appreciate the quiet messages and the private comments that were sent her way. She realized that the likes were just a number. The real joy came from the way people engaged with her content, from the connections they built over shared interests or ideas.

One day, she posted a photo of herself, looking unkempt and tired, with the caption, “This is me today. Imperfect and real. No filters, no edits, just life as it is.” The post didn’t get as many likes as her other content, but the comments were overwhelmingly positive. People appreciated the vulnerability, the honesty. Some even shared their own experiences of feeling overwhelmed.

That post, Maya thought, might just be the one that mattered the most.

Beyond the Like

As months passed, Maya’s relationship with social media changed. She was no longer trapped in the cycle of likes and dislikes. She understood now that social media could be a tool for expression, for connection, and for discovery. It wasn’t about fitting into a mold or seeking validation through a number—it was about sharing her truth, no matter how messy it might be.

The story of likes and dislikes wasn’t just about algorithms or approval—it was about the way people interacted with one another in a space that was both vast and intimate. It was about learning to embrace who you were, without worrying about how many people liked it. Social media, she realized, wasn’t the problem. The problem was how she had used it.

And now, Maya had learned to use it for something greater than herself.

To connect, to share, and to be unapologetically real.
