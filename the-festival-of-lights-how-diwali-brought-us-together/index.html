The Festival of Lights: How Diwali Brought Us Together


It began with a single, bright marigold tucked onto our doorstep, left silently in the weak dawn. No note, just a blossom—the kind my mother used to string together above the lintel back in Rajasthan. Here, halfway around the world and three decades removed, the sight startled me from my usual morning stride, coffee cooling at my lips.

I looked up and down the hallway of our apartment building, but found only closed doors and the muffled sound of distant breakfast routines. My neighbor, Mrs. Nguyen, passed me as she adjusted her coat and gave me a small smile, crinkling her eyes. “Pretty flower,” she said, in her gentle, lilting accent. “Today is a special day?”

I hesitated. Did I want to explain? The anticipation of Diwali swelled in my chest. Back home, the entire city would have been humming with last-minute preparations. Here, it felt secret, almost illicit—a festival carried quietly from the homeland, hidden between plane tickets and VISA forms.

But something about her kind curiosity nudged me to reply. “Yes,” I said. “It’s the beginning of Diwali. We celebrate with lights, sweets, and yes—flowers. Would you like to come by this evening? My mother is visiting, and she’s promised to make her gulab jamun.” I realized with surprise I wasn’t rehearsing the invitation for obligation’s sake, as I had so many times before. No, I was hoping she’d say yes.

Mrs. Nguyen grinned, the newness of invitation lighting up her face. “Of course,” she said. “I will bring something too. Spring rolls, maybe? Is that all right?”

“Perfect,” I replied. Suddenly, the hallway felt warmer. The marigold drooped in my hand, but inside, a bright thread had been tied.

The rest of the day was a flurry. My mother, who was visiting for her annual three-month-long “holiday” (her words), flitted between kitchen and living room in a parade of spices and utensils. She teased the dough for the sweets and fried bits of chickpea battered vegetables for the bhajis. Even my ten-year-old daughter, Anya, scampered about, stringing fairy lights on the window and drawing crooked little footprints on the floors with rice flour.

“You think anyone will come?” Anya asked, giving me a sidelong glance as she stuck another light to the wall with tape.

I shrugged, half-confident, half-ingenuous. “I think so. Maybe more than usual this year.”

We placed small terracotta lamps—diyas—along the threshold of our apartment, watching the flickering flames wink against the dusky evening. Through the hallway, bright trills and laughter began trickling past the doors.

The first knock came early, and it was Mrs. Nguyen, holding a plate mounded with aromatic spring rolls and salad. Her youngest, Lien, trailed behind, barely tall enough to peek over the plate, eyes wide at the golden lights. The two of them were soon joined by Rachel from 4B, whose family had moved in last month, and who brought chocolate chip cookies “because my daughter told me they’re good for every occasion,” she announced sheepishly.

We weren’t a large crowd, but as neighbors trickled in, it felt as if the light itself was calling to them. Each new person brought a dish, a story, or a curious question. The air buzzed with threads of languages braided together—Hindi, English, Vietnamese, Hebrew—and laughter spilled into the corridor.

My mother, delighted, took command. She talked of the old house in Jaipur where Diwali would illuminate every street, where children darted between houses trading mithai and fireworks popped like popcorn. She demonstrated how to swirl the batter, how to balance a diya so the wind wouldn’t snuff it out too soon. Emboldened, Mrs. Nguyen stepped forward to show how her family celebrated Tet, their own new year, and Rachel compared it to Hanukkah, offering anecdotes about spinning dreidels and rows of candles in the windows.

In the corner, Anya and Lien shrieked with delight as they sculpted tiny clay elephants and painted them gold. When it came time for sparklers, adults and children alike gathered in the courtyard, eyes glittering in the reflection of swirling light, each flame scattering its own confetti of hope.

Later, as the evening waned and the smell of sweets mingled with the last smoky trails of sparklers, we sat in a clumsy circle on colorful cushions. The noise softened to a low hum of shared contentment. The festival felt private and enormous at once, reaching from our small living room to the echo of celebrations across continents, families, and centuries.

“We’ve never had neighbors over like this,” Rachel confessed quietly, sipping spiced chai. “I always thought these things were very… personal. Not really for outsiders.”

My mother, who could not resist an opening for a story, smiled serenely. “But Diwali is about welcoming. Whoever steps into your light becomes a part of your celebration. What’s the good of light if you cannot share it?”

Lien, whose English had only recently found its footing, nodded with solemn wisdom far beyond her years. “Everyone’s house feels warm tonight,” she said, almost reverently.

That night, as the guests left—leaving plates piled with crumbs, drifted laughter, and the honeyed aftertaste of shared festivity—I thought of the marigold on the doorstep. Somewhere along the way, we’d all grown used to waiting behind closed doors, convinced that boundaries kept us safe and sacred. In opening the door, literally and figuratively, one ordinary marigold had begun to dissolve the lines between “us” and “them,” between festival and ordinary day.

In the weeks that followed, our building buzzed with new rituals. Neighbors lingered in the halls to swap recipes or check on a sick child. Anya and Lien exchanged stories about their holidays, sometimes making projects out of their differences—a joint scrapbook of family feasts, lanterns, and customs. Diwali had not just brightened our windows for a night—it had created friendships whose glow lingered in memory, like the afterimage of a single bright lamp in the darkness.

Months later, at the first promise of spring, Mrs. Nguyen invited us to Tet. This time, she left a small golden chrysanthemum on our doorstep—no note, just a flower, packed with silent invitation.

Anya, seeing it, grinned knowingly. “It’s our turn again,” she whispered.

We prepared rice cakes and cleaned the apartment, and this time when we opened the door, the warmth in the hall felt familiar—not just an echo of Diwali, but part of a new, uncharted festival, shaped out of many traditions and hands. As we lit new candles and celebrated together, all of us—old, new, and in between—belonged.

Even now, every time I find a flower on my doorstep, I think of that first Diwali in our apartment. How a simple gesture, a marigold left in dawn’s quiet, grew into something that made strangers into neighbors, and neighbors into family. In sharing the festival of lights, we discovered the real magic wasn’t the sweets, or the lamps, or even the stories—it was the door, opened at last, and the gentle, insistent light pouring out.
