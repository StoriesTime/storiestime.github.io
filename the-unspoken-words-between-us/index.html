The Unspoken Words Between Us


When Anya Delacroix stepped into the convenience store at half past midnight, the town of Merrow’s Crossing was dozing on its side. A single streetlamp outside buzzed. Traffic lights blinked stubbornly for empty intersections. The man behind the counter—Liam Porter, whose name she’d known since childhood—lifted his head as the bell above the door chimed.

She paused, the glass cooler doors frosting her reflection. In school, they’d written their names inside desk drawers: Anya & Liam, 2002, like an incantation for permanence. She pretended not to notice the way his eyes flicked up, or the needle scratch of tension filling the stagnant air.

Anya wanted a cherry soda. She grabbed two. She still remembered that soda was what kept her awake through exam season, back when Liam would sneak her bottles under the hum of library fluorescents.

Behind the register counter, Liam finished stacking newspapers with deliberate care. His hands hovered, not quite steady, familiar in their hesitations. These gestures had changed over the years, grown older, less certain. For a moment, she remembered how she’d tried to notice everything about him: how his lips twitched when he was about to say something funny, how he always pinched the bridge of his nose when he was annoyed.

Their dances around each other had once been punctuated with laughter, with words spilling over each other in long car rides and late-night walks. Now words hung, unsaid and heavy. Something had snapped between them—that final fight, the scraping clatter of a slammed door, a fleeting look of betrayal. Yet here they were again, summoned together by routine, drawn in by history’s undertow.

She set the sodas on the counter.

He rang them up, eyes fixed on the digital display. “You’re back in town,” he observed. His tone was flat, but in the tilt of his eyebrows she found a flicker of something—curiosity, accusation, relief? She couldn’t say.

“Just for a bit,” she replied, and left it at that. Words congested in her chest, straining against the dam she’d built. She found herself remembering the way autumn used to taste—the crispness, the aches, promises they no longer made. She counted her change, knowing she wouldn’t mention the city, the job, the apartment full of unopened boxes.

The old radio behind the counter crackled out weather reports for the week. Rain tomorrow. Maybe Thursday too. Conversation, once a current between them, now trickled down to an occasional exchange of facts: weather, prices, hours, news. They no longer talked about what came next.

As she took her bag, Anya hesitated. Wasn’t there something she should say to close the gap? Something about how he wore tiredness differently now, how his laughter lingered less in his eyes? Instead, she offered him a soda. Their knuckles brushed, an electrical jolt more eloquent than any language.

He accepted wordlessly, searching her face for an invitation or warning. Their silence spoke in paragraphs, dense with things unvoiced. Moments lingered where explanations should be: why she left, why he stayed, what had broken beyond repair.

“I sometimes think about…” she started, but the sentence tumbled and scattered. His half smile answered, apologetic.

“Yeah,” he said, the word thick with implication.

She lingered by the door. The bell jingled again, abrupt and final. Outside, Anya breathed in humid air shivering with possibilities, and tried to remember if there had ever been a time when they could say everything.

May 19th, 2:04 a.m.

The night after, sleep eluded Anya, her childhood bedroom shrunk by memories. She tiptoed downstairs, drawn as though by magnetism to the porch light filtering through the kitchen window. Her phone on the table glowed with a dormant message thread—Liam’s name still starring at the top, their last text from years ago: one word, “okay.”

She typed and deleted a dozen messages. “Sorry.” “Remember that night in August?” “Do you ever think about… me?” Nothing fit. How strange, she thought, this chasm between intention and speech. The bravery to leave and the cowardice to connect in the aftermath—they seemed to contain entirely different people.

She wandered outside, grass sharp with dew. A shape moved at the sidewalk—Liam, letting his dog nudge the weeds. Their eyes met, hesitant, conspiratorial.

“We really have to stop meeting like this,” she murmured, attempting mirth.

“You used to like coincidences,” he replied, letting the leash slacken.

They stood side by side under the brittle light. Above them, the sky was brined with unspoken questions. She wanted to reach for his hand, say, “Are we okay?” Instead, starlight glimmered overhead, too far to touch.

“Did you ever imagine we’d end up like this?” Liam’s question fell between them, more vulnerable for being voiced.

Anya tried to answer, but what could she say? That she pictured a version of them that didn’t hurt and hesitate, one where honesty was easy? That every silent moment since had made her wonder if she’d forgotten how to talk to the one person who’d once known her best?

Words gathered on her tongue, a plea for forgiveness, a catalog of regrets—rancid, raw, real. Instead, she said, “No, I didn’t.”

He studied the horizon. “Me neither.”

The dog tugged, anxious to move on. Liam gave a half-shrug, as though discarding possibility.

“See you around, Anya,” he said, voice thick with finality.

“Yeah.” Her throat ached with the words she withheld. “See you.”

He walked away, silhouette framed by streetlight. She stood, unmoving, until she couldn’t see him anymore.

May 22nd, 7:37 p.m.

Grocery store, aisle six. Both reached for the same box of cereal. They stopped, hands centimeters apart. This time, they laughed—an accidental, embarrassed sound. People passed, carts rattling, fluorescent lighting pooling in arcs that made the moment microscopic and intimate.

“You first,” she offered.

He shook his head. “Ladies’ choice.”

She grabbed the box, but their fingers touched again. Something softer passed between them—a reconciliation, tentative and unfinished. The rhythm of familiarity was there, almost.

They talked about small, safe things—mutual friends’ weddings, the new paint job on the school, inflation. Conversation circled around the crater, never approaching its edge.

“I should go,” she said.

“I’ll see you,” he replied. The repetition became a refrain, uncommitted and sad.

She rounded the corner, almost colliding with a stand of apples, heart beating loud enough to drown out everything but her own pulse.

May 23rd, 6:11 p.m.

Anya sat on her old bed, journaling by window light. The air was heavy with the scent of cut grass and the sense of things unfinished. From her window, she watched Liam mowing his front yard. He’d lived across from her since they were toddlers, their childhoods split by a single narrow street, a field of dandelions, and eventually, pride.

It occurred to her that every moment with him now was shaded by what they refused to say. All the apologies bartered for petulance, all the care unsaid because they both feared reanimating old hurt.

Her mother knocked, opening the door. “He looks well,” she observed without preamble.

Anya hummed in reply, wishing she could be someone who said what she felt instead of composing monologues in the dark.

May 25th, 8:58 p.m.

The annual Merrow’s Crossing festival churned. Food trucks clustered around the park. Neon lights flashed. Music from the temporary stage tangled with the laughter of children and the coyote keen of teens straddling independence.

Anya found herself by the popcorn stand, hands sticky with syrup. Across the crowd, Liam approached, waving to someone behind her. Their gazes connected, suspended for a fraction of a second.

He stopped beside her, out of breath from laughter or running. “It’s weird, isn’t it? How everything’s the same, but completely different.”

She nodded. Too loud for secrets here. But if she concentrated, she could pick out his voice from the cacophony—still warm in its timbre, still unwavering despite the awkwardness.

They talked as dusk settled—their words less filtered now, as though the festival allowed them a reprieve from consequences. They joked about their old teachers, recounted campfire misadventures, even reminisced about the many ways boredom made architects of hope.

For the first time, Anya felt the muscle memory of their connection returning. Glances curled around phrases as if trying to mend invisible seams. She almost dared herself to ask, “What would have happened if I stayed?” Instead she said, “I forgot you’re good at this.” Meaning, being here, being present.

He grinned. “I used to practice.”

They let silence tether them for a moment, a space in which confessions might germinate.

“There’s a lot I never said,” he began. It was uncharacteristic, raw.

She turned to face him, heart pounding. “Me too.”

He looked away, searching for privacy inside the crowd. The rawness in his voice made her want to reach out, but she kept her arms wrapped around herself instead.

“I think we hurt each other because it was easier than saying why we were scared,” he said finally.

She nodded. “I’m still scared sometimes.”

Surprised, he met her eyes. “So am I.”

They stood together, close but not touching, the crowd swirling like a moat. The words between them—fearful, heavy, imprecise—hung like particles illuminated only when the light hit them just right.

She smiled, a tentative offering. “We could try again, you know. At least talking.”

He let out a slow breath. “I’d like that.”

The crowd’s noise faded as the band struck up its closing song. Fireflies rose around them. They ventured down the path together, words slow and halting, but for the first time in years, actually trying.

May 31st, 9:13 p.m.

Late summer heat settled into the asphalt, making their sneakers stick faintly as they walked side by side. They strolled past houses with porch lights blinking Morse code into the thickening night.

They talked, in fits and starts, about small things and then the fractures. Regrets spilled out, clumsy and overwrought, sometimes tapering into apologies whispered into the grain of the night. They fumbled through explanations, sometimes contradicting themselves, sometimes leaving thoughts unfinished.

“Well,” she finally said, “at least we’re finally saying some of it.”

“Maybe that’s the trick,” he replied. “Not letting everything stay locked up.”

She grinned. “Easier said than done.”

He mocked outrage. “Hey, some things are worth saying, even if they come out wrong.”

They stopped at the edge of the field where they’d once built forts and dreamed up futures. The years since then—bruised, bright, ruins and renovations—felt softer in retrospect, less lethal in the warmth of honest company.

Neither claimed a fairytale reunion. Unspoken truths remained, some too entrenched to excavate.

But new words found their space. Tentative promises, subtle laughter, the vulnerability of “I missed you” without explanation or apology. They didn’t need to voice every hurt or hope. Sometimes the quiet communion said enough.

They stood together as the first stars punctured the inky sky, the silence now companionable, filled with understanding rather than regret. Each unspoken word between them didn’t feel like a loss anymore. Instead, it shimmered with the possibility of what they still could say, when they were ready, across the years and beyond the boundaries of fear.
