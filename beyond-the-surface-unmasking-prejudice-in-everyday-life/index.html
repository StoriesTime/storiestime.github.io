Beyond the Surface: Unmasking Prejudice in Everyday Life


The small town of Clearbrook was nestled between rolling hills and quiet forests, a postcard of serenity that one might expect to occur only in stories. Its Main Street was dotted with weathered brick buildings, snug cafes, and a clocktower that had long since stopped ticking but still managed to draw the eye. On the surface, Clearbrook was a community bound together by its charm and collective pride, but just beneath that polished exterior existed a current of discomfort—an unspoken underbelly of prejudice that many refused to acknowledge, often hidden beneath the most ordinary of interactions.

For Jasmine Denton, Clearbrook was home. She had spent twenty-eight years living in its rhythm, balancing her days between her job as the town’s librarian and helping her father with the flower shop they ran together. She knew the town’s quirks, its seasonal festivals, and even which streets to avoid during the peak of pothole season. But there were aspects of Clearbrook she’d never stopped noticing—its whispered judgments and the way it sometimes turned cold when you didn’t quite fit into its mold.

Jasmine’s mother had moved to Clearbrook years ago from Nicaragua and married her father, a Clearbrook native who knew to pave his movements in kindness to gain acceptance from the insular townfolk. Her mother’s darker skin and lightly accented English had been met with lukewarm smiles from their neighbors for as far back as Jasmine could remember. Though many came into the shop to chat with her father or compliment their flower arrangements, there always seemed to be an edge to some smiles—hidden but sharp, like the unspoken reminder that family roots didn’t fully bury themselves in soil unless they’d been planted there for generations.

It hadn’t always been dramatic moments of discrimination that gnawed at Jasmine’s peace but subtler acts: the waitress who took extra long to refill her coffee, the neighbor who asked her why her mother didn’t try harder to “lose her accent” after living in the U.S. so long, or an acquaintance’s passive remark that she didn’t “look Latina.” These were cuts so small they often healed unnoticed, but they still left their scars. And after years of being taught to “be the bigger person” and “give the benefit of the doubt,” Jasmine had begun to wonder what kind of world existed beyond those carefully conditioned responses.

Her moment of reckoning arrived unexpectedly on a chilly September afternoon, the day Jack Gardner visited the library.

Jack had been the editor of the Clearbrook Times for years, reporting on everything from juvenile fundraisers to the annual Harvest Fair, his tone always measured and uncontroversial. With every edition, he prided himself on reflecting the community exactly as it wished to see itself. He exuded the air of someone who thought they knew everything about their town, someone so tightly entwined with its perpetuation that they rarely questioned the foundation upon which it was built.

He arrived at the library in search of archival material for a series he was writing to celebrate the town’s founding. But as Jasmine prepared to assist him, he seemed hesitant, his tone drifting toward condescension.

“Are you sure these files are in order? This system seems… different from what I remember,” Jack said, gesturing toward the library’s meticulously maintained digital catalog.

“Yes, Mr. Gardner. Everything’s been updated recently, so it’s very efficient,” she replied, keeping her voice steady.

“Well, I hope it’s not too confusing. It used to be a lot simpler,” he said lightly, though the remark hung in the air like smoke.

Jasmine smiled, though her patience thinned. “I assure you, it works quite well.”

Jack’s presence came to feel symbolic of a deeper problem as the afternoon wore on. While selecting documents, he casually mentioned how much “simpler” life had been when he was young and motioned offhandedly to how different the town had become. Jasmine feigned polite interest but couldn’t help but notice the undertone of nostalgia for a time when people like her family hadn’t belonged.

By the time she finally ushered Jack out of the library, Jasmine felt like a mirror cracked from too much strain. Perhaps it was the culmination of years spent enduring these small, cutting exchanges or the realization that the polite smile she’d learned to wear since she was a child had protected nothing. Whatever it was, it kindled in her a desire not to accept these moments silently anymore.

The next morning, Jasmine decided to write an anonymized article and submit it to the Clearbrook Times’ opinion section—a fiery yet measured call for the town to reflect more deeply on its implicit biases, its tendency to overlook smaller acts of exclusion, and the discomfort of those forced to brush them aside. It wasn’t accusing or damning but a stark, honest portrayal of what it was like to live in Clearbrook while carrying the weight of being “different.”

She poured over every line, struck a few out, rewrote others, and finally submitted it late that evening. It felt like her way of unburdening herself, even if it only scratched the surface.

Three days later, the op-ed ran in the Times under the title “Beyond the Surface: Rethinking Community in Clearbrook.” The response was immediate and overwhelming.

Some praised the perspective; others dismissed it as overthinking or unnecessary. But what struck Jasmine most was the energy it stirred. A town meeting was scheduled just days after the article’s publication, and Jasmine quietly attended, melted into the crowd, her identity as the article’s writer safe under anonymity.

Clearbrook sat at a crossroads that evening. People rose to voice their opinions, some expressing regret for not seeing things more clearly while others fumed at the suggestion of societal flaws in a town they’d always idealized. But what mattered most was the conversation—messy, real, and undeniably alive.

Weeks after the article’s publication, Jasmine’s library became a hub; community members with new questions and realizations began seeking books and resources on empathy, racism, and systemic inequality. Conversations were tentative at first, but they blossomed. Parents asked about children’s books that focused on inclusion. Students requested historical texts for their essays. And slowly, Jasmine began to see the ripples of what could become a shift in Clearbrook’s culture.

Jack Gardner himself stopped by the library one afternoon with a copy of the article clutched tightly in hand. His expression was unreadable, but his words surprised her.

“That was brave,” he said softly. “I don’t agree with all of it, mind you…” He paused, as though weighing his next words. “But it made me think.”

Jasmine didn’t reply immediately. Instead, she handed him a book she’d tucked beneath the counter earlier that week: “Blindspot: Hidden Biases of Good People.”

Jack took it, nodded his thanks, and left.

Clearbrook didn’t change overnight, but Jasmine saw the cracks begin to form in its seamless facade. People started asking harder questions, challenging their preconceptions, and learning in small, deliberate steps to move beyond prejudice. It was slow, imperfect work, but it was real. In that process, Jasmine learned that unmasking prejudice didn’t require dramatic heroes or grand gestures. Sometimes, it began with a single voice daring to say, enough.
