The Discovery of America: Columbus and the New World


It was the year 1485, and Christopher Columbus, a skilled sailor and navigator, had spent years studying the ancient texts of the Greeks and Romans. He became fascinated by the stories of a vast, unexplored ocean separating Europe from the East Indies. Convinced that he could reach Asia by sailing west, Columbus spent countless hours making calculations, refinements, and adjustments to his theories. He was driven by a burning desire to prove the world was not flat and open to trade and exploration. A Meeting with a King One fateful day, Columbus boarded a carriage and arrived at the Palace of Alhambra in Granada, Spain. He hoped to obtain support and funding for his treacherous journey from King Ferdinand II of Aragon and Queen Isabella I of Castile. The monarchs were impressed by Columbus's confidence and conviction, but also perplexed by his theories. Columbus explained that by sailing west across the Atlantic, he could reach the riches of Asia in a shorter time and distance than traditional trade routes. After several meetings and deliberations, the monarchs began to see the potential of Columbus's plan and agreed to provide the necessary resources.
