Surviving the Change: A New World Amidst Global Warming


Teo squinted across the river’s shallow expanse, the sun glancing off its muddy veneer. The last painted turtle, once plump and king of this trickling domain, slid off a cracked stone.

“You see it?” asked Maya, drawing level at the edge, feet bare and cautious.

“Gone,” Teo answered. Insects buzzed around their legs, more animated than the lazy water. He flinched at the drone of another summer cicada—the last thing left deafening in the hush that came after the change. Everything else—frogs, birds, neighbors—had fallen to silence or migrated north.

They maneuvered upstream, where the bank rose to what had once been Evan’s cornfield—a brown wasteland, stalks crisped to ghosts by months of relentless heat. Beyond that, behind a ring of blackened cottonwoods, stood the farmhouse. Solar panels glimmered on the scalded roof like shingles of a dragon that nobody bothered to fear anymore.

“This section’s drying out more each month,” Maya murmured. Her gaze flicked to a scorched line in the silt.

“Salmon never ran here anyway,” Teo said, trying to tilt his lips in a smile. Humor had become a battered vessel, old but not obsolete. “It’s all just killifish now. If there are any left.”

“We’ll check around the south pond after this. See if the lilies made it,” she replied. It was ritual—plant, check, hope, lose, repeat. If they stopped, the world would slip away entirely.

Shelter in the Change

Inside the farmhouse, light filtered through windows corked with canvas scraps. Chunks of ice—the spoils of their battered freezer, on its last legs—rested in wide bowls near the workbench. A logbook fibrous and swollen from humidity lay open, clotted script filling its pages.

Teo picked up a pencil, scrawled a date: “August 23—river fallen. No turtles. Pond lilies, unknown.”

He paused as Maya set down collected bottles—each glugging with yellowed water, scavenged from the shrinking creek. The filters needed cleaning, the RO unit whining with exhaustion, like an old hound that never slept.

They sat side by side, backs braced by old cupboards, shoulders leaching salt onto dry shirts. Dinner would be rice—always rice—and sun-dried mulberries plucked from the last stubborn bush near the chicken wire fence.

“You think old Mrs. Bailey ever found her son?” Maya asked quietly. Some nights, these bouts of wondering kept them up longer than the heat.

Teo shook his head, pushing food around his bowl. “Nobody came back once New Galloway got the worst of the fires. Maybe they’ll find a community up in Aberdeen, up where there’s water.”

Maya nodded, eyes distant, mouth half-curled in wistful reverie. Before the change, these had been towns, bustling with fairs and football seasons, littered with corn-based snacks. Now, all maps felt speculative. Places shifted, names were memories pressed between aging pages.

New Seasons

The world had come apart not in roar but in a slow, ceaseless trickle. Ice caps vanished by day, inland storms swept off the faces of cities, the air choking down to its last particles. The seasons grew fraudulent; winter was what passed for a cool snap, rain a week-long rumor, spring a faint wish.

Out here, adaptation dictated rhythm. Mornings became precious, the only time when the ground didn’t scald bare feet. They rose before dawn to collect condensation as it beaded on plastic sheeting, capturing each drop like a promise. By noon, they hunted for shade, sheltering in root dens beneath wind-tilted trees or crawling down into the shallow well, only to emerge again when the sun fell ragged behind scorched hills.

Livelihood meant scavenging. Solar panels had been a blessing, their battered batteries stubborn as barn cats. Gardens frayed against pests—mostly pillbugs and wasps—and the occasional wild dog always nosed at their compost bins.

Teo spent afternoons patching their water purifier and tracing new cracks across the stonework of their barn. Each fissure felt dangerous, an invisible line between stability and collapse.

Maya inventoried supplies and read journals scavenged from town. Notes about rain-catch systems, mushroom growing in gutters, and how to desalinate groundwater. They marked up everything, developed their own language of arrows and circles—a shadow cartography of survival.

The Community Beyond

On Wednesdays, Maya walked the cracked state highway south, carrying a battered radio and a backpack filled with mash for trade. The road ran through what the maps called “Cedar Run,” but the signs lay in ditches now, splintered by storms. She met a man called Deke there, cheeks so leathery they could have been saddle straps.

He always wore a faded yellow jacket and carried stories in his laughter. Deke brought broth for their sick chicken, news from the bridge outpost—a collective of scavengers and farmers who pieced together a new world along what had been I-30.

“They’re fixing to build aquifers again,” he reported, swapping his broth for Maya’s handful of cornmeal. His voice was slow, thick with the weight of sun and expectation. “Got two working up near Harper’s Bend. Even got a pail-and-chain system.”

Maya grinned. “If you see anybody who can plumb, tell them we’ve got all the old copper pipe they could want.”

Deke cackled, more hope than truth. But the exchanges meant something: barter, words, shared struggle. Community knitted by need, not nostalgia.

Threats and Wonders

Certain creatures survived the shift better than people. Coyotes prowled night margins, leaner, quieter, noses strafing the dirt for traces of lizards and bird eggs. Hawks circled higher, keening over empty fields, their shadows like smears of paint across the dusk.

One morning, they found a patch of prickly-pear cacti flowering beside a spent irrigation canal. The fruit was tart, small, and brimming with sugar. For weeks after, purple-stained fingers and lips became the mark of fortune.

But that summer, the threat came not from predators or drought, but a different breed altogether: looters moving south from the mountains. At dusk, two figures appeared at the crossroads, hauling a broken wagon and armed with a sawed-off shotgun.

Maya and Teo watched from their window, hearts pulsing in tandem. The visitors picked through the dust, stole two hens, and left. Maya counted their steps retreating, tense with the knowledge that, next time, the strangers might not be so gentle.

They reinforced the barn with debris—old tires, iron bars, anything that could hold. For weeks, sleep came in slow patches, ears prickled for laughter that wasn’t theirs.

Adaptation

Time curved and settled. Fall was a memory; trees never turned, just browned, dropped, disappeared. They planted amaranth where the corn refused to sprout, and sweet potatoes in the shade of old tractors. Teo rigged a blue tarp to catch dew; Maya learned to dry and salt every edible green.

They joined the periodic trek north—a three-day crossing to find relatives of the ones who’d left before, perhaps trading for seeds resistant to this new, merciless world. In halls strung with LED lights and hand-sewn quilts, they exchanged stories and emergency rations, clustered around battered tables stacked with maps and hopes.

Maya’s brother, Etienne, sent word one day: their mother had made it to a settlement on the edge of the old lakes, a world rebuilt atop ruined suburbs. Their small collective took in strays, offered to share start-up seeds and instructions for hydroponics, all scrawled in patient loops on waterproofed paper.

Teo and Maya debated the risk—stay, with so much already clawed from the earth, or move, bet on new alliances amid wintry risks. At night, with spirits braced by prickly-pear wine, the choice haunted their dreams.

Discoveries

One clouded evening, after a rare storm rolled in and coughed up a half-inch of rain, they ventured outside to watch rivulets cut along the yard’s hardened hills. Maya followed a creeklet to where old garden beds slept beneath sheets of tarp. Life had sprouted—volunteer tomato plants, tough-skinned and resilient. They watched as a rabbit, thin and brave, nosed the shoots.

“Even now?” Maya whispered, as if the world might overhear. Teo squeezed her hand.

“I guess nothing gives up all at once,” he replied.

Each day became a new set of calculations: how to trap and store what water fell, when to risk planting beans, where to find shade for next season’s saplings. They learned patience—how to wait for pollinators, harvest in shifts, store every surplus for the seasons that offered nothing.

Rebirth

A year passed, then another. The nation that had been was gone—drowned or burned or divided. In its place, networks spread, cities of sod and canvas rising around every marsh left by receding rivers. Trade routes—once mere fantasy—connected survivors who had become gardeners, builders, repairers of both things and hopes.

Teo and Maya dug a pond rimmed by rocks scavenged from fields, filled it day by day with buckets of rain. They coaxed water lilies from ancient roots, found goldfish in the abandoned house up the ridge, and set them loose in the muddy water.

Old friends returned, drawn by word of harvest. Children ran barefoot in the delicate grass, tracking mud across new-woven mats. The radio sang again—messages crackling from distant settlements, voices mapping the new world.

No ceremony marked the transition, no sunrise broke with fanfare. But as the sun crept over the pond one morning, illuminating the trembling petals of lilies and the flicker of new fish, Teo and Maya sat together, hands mud-streaked and hearts steady.

They had survived the change and shaped, beneath the withering sky, the blueprint of a world remade.

Epilogue

From the community’s edge, the land rolling out in waves of stubborn green, they watched as distant clouds formed, heavy and dark over the north hills. Somewhere far off, someone else prayed for rain. Elsewhere, children mapped the earth with new dreams, and gardeners pressed seeds into the future, one germ at a time.
