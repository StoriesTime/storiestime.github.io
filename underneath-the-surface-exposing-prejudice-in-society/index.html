Underneath the Surface: Exposing Prejudice in Society


Lena always thought she understood the world. Growing up in a small, tight-knit town, she had learned the importance of kindness, the value of fairness, and the belief that everyone should be treated equally. Her family was well-respected, and their humble home echoed with laughter and stories of the good old days. However, it wasn’t until the summer after high school that Lena’s view of the world began to crack, and she discovered the deep-rooted prejudices lurking just beneath the surface of her seemingly perfect life.

It all started the day she went to the county fair with her best friend, Malik. Malik, with his warm smile and infectious laugh, had always been the one to make her feel at ease in any situation. He was different from the rest of the kids in town—not just in the way he looked, but in his understanding of the world. He’d been raised in the same town, yet his experiences were starkly different from hers. Malik was Black, and though Lena had never thought of that as anything more than a fact, her town didn’t see it that way.

They arrived at the fairgrounds, the sounds of rides whirring and people laughing filling the air. The smell of cotton candy and popcorn mixed with the warm summer breeze, creating an atmosphere of carefree fun. They had a plan to visit every ride and eat their weight in fair food. But something felt off. Lena couldn’t quite put her finger on it at first.

As they walked past the ticket booth, a woman with blonde hair and a bright smile greeted them. “Two tickets, please,” Lena said, pulling out cash from her wallet. The woman’s eyes flicked to Malik, and her smile faltered for just a moment. It was barely noticeable, but Lena saw it. The woman handed over the tickets without a word, but as they moved away, Lena could feel Malik stiffen beside her.

“You okay?” Lena asked, not understanding why he seemed upset.

“Yeah,” Malik muttered, his eyes focused straight ahead. “Just tired of it, you know?”

Lena looked at him, confused. “Tired of what?”

He sighed, his voice low. “Tired of the looks. The way people treat me like I don’t belong. Like I’m an outsider, even though I’ve lived here my whole life.”

Lena’s heart sank. She had never noticed it before. How could she have missed it? She had always known Malik was different in appearance, but she never once thought about how that might make him feel. She thought everyone was just like her—innocent, unaffected by the invisible lines drawn by society’s prejudices. But now, as they walked through the fairgrounds, she noticed how people avoided Malik’s gaze, how children clung to their parents when they walked by, and how a group of teenage boys across the street whispered and laughed when they saw him.

The deeper Lena went into the fairgrounds, the more she felt an overwhelming sense of discomfort. She began to question every interaction, every glance, every word that passed between her and the people around her. She had lived in this town her whole life, yet she had never seen it through Malik’s eyes. The prejudices that had been woven into the fabric of her society were so deeply ingrained that she had never even considered they existed.

Later that evening, after the fireworks had faded and the crowds had thinned, Lena and Malik sat on a bench near the Ferris wheel. The world around them was quieter now, the stars twinkling overhead. Malik spoke first.

“People think I don’t notice, but I do. I notice when they treat me like I’m invisible or like I’m something to be afraid of. It’s exhausting, Lena. You think that being in a small town means everything is simple and pure, but it’s not. There’s a lot of ugliness hidden beneath the surface.”

Lena was silent for a moment, her mind racing. “But how can they think that? You’re one of the kindest, most genuine people I know. How can anyone judge you based on the color of your skin?”

Malik chuckled bitterly. “That’s the thing, Lena. They don’t see me. They don’t see who I am. They see the color of my skin, and that’s enough for them to decide what kind of person I am.”

Lena felt the weight of his words settle in her chest. She had been so blind to the world outside her bubble, so unaware of the struggles her best friend faced every single day. She thought about her own experiences—how she had been sheltered from the harsh realities of prejudice because of her privilege. Her skin was white, her family well-off, and she had never once been judged for the way she looked or where she came from. The reality of Malik’s life was a stark contrast to everything she had ever known.

The conversation lingered in Lena’s mind as the days passed. She began to pay closer attention to how people treated her friend, noticing the subtle, almost imperceptible signs of prejudice. The way people crossed the street when they saw him walking alone. The way shopkeepers followed him with wary eyes when he entered their stores. The uncomfortable silence when he was introduced at social gatherings. It was all so small, yet it added up to something much larger, something that was ingrained in the culture of the town.

One afternoon, Lena found herself sitting with her mother in the kitchen, peeling carrots for dinner. The conversation had drifted to the fair, and Lena couldn’t help but bring up what had happened with Malik.

“I don’t understand, Mom,” Lena said, her voice tinged with frustration. “Why do people treat him like that? He’s the same as everyone else. It’s not fair.”

Her mother paused, setting down the knife in her hand. “Lena,” she said softly, “you don’t always see the prejudice that exists in this world because it doesn’t affect you. It’s easy to live in a bubble, thinking everything is fair and equal. But the reality is, not everyone is treated the same. There are systems in place that make it harder for some people to succeed, simply because of things they can’t control—like the color of their skin, where they were born, or what they believe.”

Lena felt a lump form in her throat as her mother’s words sank in. “But what can we do? How do we change it?”

Her mother’s gaze softened. “We start by acknowledging it. We start by having conversations like this. We speak out when we see injustice. We teach others to recognize their own biases and challenge them. It’s not easy, and it won’t happen overnight, but change starts with awareness.”

That night, Lena lay in bed, unable to sleep. Her mind replayed everything she had witnessed with Malik—the subtle prejudices, the uncomfortable silences, the exclusion. She realized that the world she had always believed to be fair and just wasn’t as simple as it seemed. Prejudice wasn’t always loud or violent—it was quiet, insidious, and deeply ingrained in the fabric of society. And Lena, for the first time, understood that true change would require more than just kindness. It would require confronting the uncomfortable truths about the world and actively working to dismantle the systems that allowed prejudice to thrive.

The next day, Lena decided to act. She began by talking to Malik more openly about his experiences, listening with a heart that was ready to learn. She spoke to her family and friends, encouraging them to examine their own biases and reflect on how they might unknowingly perpetuate harmful stereotypes. Slowly, the conversations began to spread, and the small town that had once felt so comfortable and familiar started to shift.

It wasn’t easy, and it wasn’t perfect. But as Lena continued to expose the prejudices hiding underneath the surface of society, she began to see that real change was possible—one conversation, one action at a time. And with each step forward, the world became a little more just, a little more equal, for everyone.
