The Unbreakable Spirit of Harriet Tubman: Freedom Fighter and Women's Suffragist


Once upon a time in the Southern soils of the United States, there lived a woman who would go down in history as more than just an enslaved individual trying to find freedom. Her name was Harriet Tubman, and she was to become a beacon of hope for countless African Americans seeking a life free of slavery.

In the early hours of dawn, beneath the sun’s gentle whisper and the shadows of the confining plantation, Tubman’s heart ached for the chains that held her people forcibly captive. To her family and friends who lived in that same tight circle of misery, she wasn’t just another name, not just another face lost in the endless rows of cotton fields. She was Harriet Rosetta Tubman, born Araminta Ross, and an unbreakable spirit lay within her.

Born in 1822 on a plantation in Maryland, her life was marked with a series of hardships right from the beginning; near-death experiences, the loss of her siblings to disease, and of course, the agony of living in bondage. Little Harriet bore witness to the cruelty and brutality that the enslaved people faced every day. Yet, the fire within her, unquenchable and unyielding, engendered a desire for justice and equality.

As she grew older, Tubman united with, rather than fought against, the strength of her faith. Her devout prayer life would give her a sense of solace and hope during her darkest moments, and she developed a strong connection with the Lord. In her moments of introspection, she sought guidance and relentlessly questioned the universe regarding her purpose.

The moment that would ultimately catalyze Harriet into action came one icy winter eve when a fellow slave was beating an enslaved woman named Titus. As she and Titus locked eyes, Tubman knew she had been given a sign: she should break her chains of servitude and fight for the freedom of others.

Risking her own life, Tubman made her daring escape in the chilly winter months of 1849. With a trusty steely resolve and a map in hand, she used every ounce of her knowledge about the land and its terrain to navigate her real-life ‘Underground Railroad’, a secret network of conductors who would aid her passage to the freedom of Canada.

Her journey was no less than a saga; she braved the ferocious rapids of the Black River, nearly losing her life, but it only steered her resolve stronger. Harriet Tubman would never return to the life of a slave; her mission was now etched in her heart - to help others attain the freedom she had fought so hard to attain.

In her relentless pursuit of liberation, she made her way back to Maryland several times, conducting countless freedom seekers to the northern shores. Despite the constant threat of retribution from her former captors, she never glimpsed an ounce of fear, her faith guiding her every step of the way.

As the scourge of the Civil War tore apart the United States, Harriet Tubman stationed herself on the battlefront, using her knowledge of the area to become a spy and scout for the Union Army. Her skills in reconnaissance helped free over 700 slaves while playing a central role in the Combahee River Raid. Arriving at a checkpoint near the river, she delivered her all-encompassing words, ‘If I am to be killed, I had rather die in the service of God than to live in slavery.’

Even after the screeching of bullets subsided, her fight for freedom wasn’t over yet. Once the 13th Amendment to the United States Constitution was passed, abolishing slavery, she channeled her indomitable spirit into the Women’s Suffrage Movement and the Universal Negro Improvement Association. Harriet Tubman was undeterred, never allowing herself to waver from the unshakable pursuit of equal rights for all individuals.

Harriet Tubman’s journey was chronicled with moments of profound darkness, hardships, and perseverance that sprang from the heart of the very fabric she was woven from. Despite all odds, she became what the world knew her to be: a freedom fighter and the epitome of an unbreakable spirit.

As time marched on, Harriet Tubman stood resilient against the ever-changing tides of history - a warrior who refused to bow down to the labels and shackles of the world around her. Through her battles, she showed the world that freedom and justice should be the birthright of every human being.

And thus, if there’s one lesson that the unbreakable spirit of Harriet Tubman has left for us, it’s that hope, freedom, and justice are ultimately found through the relentless pursuit of it — and there is no limit to what an unyielding spirit can achieve.
