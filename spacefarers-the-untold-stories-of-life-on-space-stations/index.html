Spacefarers: The Untold Stories of Life on Space Stations


As I float through the cramped corridors of the Ubuntu Space Station, the hum of the life support systems and the gentle hiss of the oxygen generators create a soothing melody that's become familiar to me over the years. The station's dim lighting casts an otherworldly glow on the recycle bins, the hydroponic gardens, and the durian-smelling storage compartments. I'm Marcus, the newest addition to the Ubuntu crew, a group of forty scientists and engineers who call this tight metal spire home for the next eighteen months. The six of us are on a mission to gather data on a newly discovered exoplanet, Xylophia-IV, hidden deep in the Orion Nebula. Our station is about to serve as both a science laboratory and a human habitat, floating in the vast expanse of space. Station Living Our living quarters are compact and utilitarian, designed for efficiency rather than luxury. Each of the four crew members has their own quarters, no more than 4 meters square, containing a sleeping pod, a personal hygiene station, and a food synthesizer. The Synthie , as we call it, can produce a varied diet of tasteless but nutritious meals. Breakfast is usually a humble affair of powdered eggs, rehydrated fruits, and spinach-flavored porridge. Team meetings take place in the observation lounge, where the curved windows offer breathtaking views of the starfield. It's where we review our progress, analyze data, and make decisions for the mission. Through windows of sightlines, I see our supplies pulsing with solar energy collected from orbiting solar panels and recycled by the station's famous Pslungan field.
