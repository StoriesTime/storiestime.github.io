Meanwhile, the Cordeliers Club, a group of radical Jacobins, was in full swing, organizing meetings, rallies, and demonstrations. Led by Georges Danton, a young lawyer and orator, the club's members were determined to bring about radical change and throw off the yoke of aristocracy once and for all. As the days turned into weeks, the tension in Paris continued to escalate. The people were demanding bread, justice, and liberty. The Assembly, scrambling to respond to the crisis, proposed a series of reforms, including the creation of a National Assembly and the abolition of feudal privileges. But the revolutionaries were not to be satisfied with half-measures. They demanded, and eventually got, the absolute monarchy itself abolished and the establishment of the First French Republic. The king, Louis XVI, was forced to accept a constitution that severely limited his powers, and a new government, the National Convention, was established to oversee the transformation of France into a democratic state. Despite the internal divisions and power struggles that would ultimately tear the Revolution apart, the French people had won a great victory on July 14, 1789. Liberty, Equality, Fraternity - the values that would guide the Revolution - had been proclaimed to the world, and a new era of freedom and democracy had begun. As the news of the fall of the Bastille spread throughout the city, the people of Paris erupted in joyful celebrations. The once-feared fortress, the symbol of absolute power, was now a potent reminder of the power of the people. For the first time in centuries, the French people had a government of their own, a government that was accountable to them and not to the monarch. But the honeymoon was short-lived. In the months and years that followed, the Revolution would be torn apart by internal conflicts, ideological debates, and the rise of new foil groups.
