Unlikely Superheroes: Ordinary People with Extraordinary Problems


In the city of Edgerton, nothing spectacular ever happened. The sky was mostly the color of old oatmeal. Buses rattled and banged along pothole-broken streets, and the corner diner, Mabel’s, brewed coffee strong enough to strip wallpaper. But behind the cracked stucco and peeling paint, a story of extraordinary struggle waited, like the awkward moments before a thunderstorm.

Chapter 1: The Night Owls

Arnold Jenkins had never seen himself as anyone’s hero, least of all his own. At 52, his knees creaked like floorboards each morning. He delivered the mail on the south side, which wasn’t a glamorous job. But what Arnold remembered most from childhood was his mother’s mantra: “Everyone’s got a battle.”

Arnold’s battle wasn’t obvious. He’d been plagued by insomnia since his wife died three years ago. No pills, no meditative podcasts, no herbal teas had lured sleep from her hiding place. Instead of rest, he spent long nights walking Edgerton’s back alleys and forgotten corners.

Lately, the quiet was different. Shadows lingered where there should have been none. Footsteps echoed behind him, so cleverly that he was never sure if they actually belonged to someone, or if his mind was lurching at ghosts.

One midnight, near Broad and Sixth, Arnold noticed a glint—a shimmer by a dumpster. He moved closer, muttered under his breath, “Not tonight,” and flicked his flashlight on. The beam landed on what looked like a person in a cloak made from hundreds of reflective bicycle strips.

The figure twitched, stood, and brushed themselves off. “Oh, brilliant,” they said, their voice young and taut with nerves. “It’s you. The postman.”

Arnold squinted. “You’re the one who’s been leaving weird chalk drawings in the park?”

“Only on Thursdays. The other days are for the poetry magnets,” the stranger replied.

Chapter 2: Gloria’s Middle Shift

A few blocks away, Gloria Ramirez was stacking boxes in the faded backroom of Ray’s Thrift. Gloria’s exceptional talent was not in finding good deals—though she was excellent at that too—but in her ability to remember every single item—size, color, condition—that had passed through the store in seventeen years. If anyone wanted to know if a pink cardigan with elbow patches had been donated last winter, Gloria could recite the day it arrived, who dropped it off, and how much it sold for.

Yet, Gloria struggled with a deeper fear: panic attacks. Sudden, sharp, inexplicable surges of heart-racing dread. They struck as randomly as lightning, especially while pushing a squeaky cart between aisles.

She dealt with it in secret, hiding behind the “Employees Only” door to knead her wrists and whisper, “Breathe, Gloria. Four in, six out.”

Her super memory was revered but, at times, she would have traded it just for an hour’s peace.

One Saturday, while Gloria was breaking down boxes in the alley, she heard voices—a weird poetry about “moonlight licking windows like old cats,” and someone else, laugh-coughing.

Gloria tiptoed into the shadows, saw Arnold, the insomniac mailman, with the strange, shimmering figure.

Chapter 3: Uneasy Alliance

On the cracked curb, the three looked at one another with wild uncertainty. The cloaked figure swept their hood off. A teenager. Brown curls, cheeks flecked with acne, and those anxiety-wide eyes.

“I’m Rae,” the teen said. “And I need help. I think someone’s watching me.”

Arnold blinked, considering turning and walking away. But he saw Rae’s hands—shaking, raw-knuckled. He nodded. “Alright, kid. Who?”

Gloria leaned in. “Are you being followed by someone dangerous?”

Rae glanced around, lips trembling. “Maybe. It’s hard to tell—from the night shelter, I think—I just know they keep appearing in reflections. Sometimes, things move where they shouldn’t.”

Arnold and Gloria exchanged a glance. Edgerton wasn’t the safest city, but this had a chill.

Arnold took the lead. “Let’s sit. Tell us everything.”

Chapter 4: The Diagnosis

It turned out, Rae was staying at the shelter after leaving home. Being nonbinary in a rigid family didn’t leave much room for celebration. But Rae’s real secret wasn’t about names or pronouns; it was about perceptions. Rae had a knack for seeing the connections between things—tiny threads linking people, places, and intentions.

They called it “the lattice.” Sometimes, lines would thread between strangers on the street, drawing Rae’s focus to conversations not meant to be overheard. Other times, patterns in puddles or spiderwebs would “warn” Rae of coming events. Fatigue blurred the edges, but the ability had saved them more than once from dangerous situations.

Except now, these visual threads were tangled, knotted, sometimes leading Rae to dark corners where threat pulsed, invisible to everyone else. Rae wasn’t sure whether it was intuition, sensory overload, or the edge of some unknown psychic ability.

Gloria, hearing all of this, thought of her own panic attacks. Arnold’s quiet battles with sleep. None of them fit in the boxes the world assigned them. But maybe that was okay.

“We need each other,” Gloria said softly.

Chapter 5: Edgerton’s Unofficial Guardians

They started small. Rae would spot the strange lines, the odd patterns licking across Edgerton’s walls. Arnold, with his endless walks, knew everyone, could relay quiet warnings, disperse a calm presence among night owls and insomniacs. Gloria wielded her memory to keep track of odd events—rumors of a man with two shadows, dogs barking at empty streetlamps. Together, they became Edgerton’s unofficial guardians.

They called themselves the Night Owls, stitched together by necessity.

Most nights passed uneventfully. But soon whispers reached Rae: a low-level fixer named Mr. Thorne was creating tension among the city’s unhoused population, pitting desperate neighbors against each other for favors and safe beds.

Gloria tracked the fixer’s donations at the store—sudden, frequent, and always expensive brands, with tags left on. The names on the receipts jogged her memory. Something didn’t fit.

Arnold watched people drift in and out of the shelter, noting who lingered in the alleys after curfew. He spotted cars idling too long, men in hats making handoffs by the door.

Rae’s lattice burned bright at dusk. Thread after thread pointed to Mabel’s Diner, the only place left open all night.

Chapter 6: The Diner Standoff

One Tuesday, wind howling, the trio met in the corner booth at Mabel’s. Rae shivered, tracing patterns on the Formica table. “He’ll be here. The threads pulse—like a power line about to snap.”

Arnold’s hands curled around a mug. “If he’s hurting people, we need a plan.”

Gloria thought back. “Most of his donations are early morning. We could look around the back room after he drops them—see if he’s hiding anything.”

Arnold nodded, eyes sharp. “I’ll walk the route. If he’s watching, I’ll know.”

The bell over the door jangled. A tall, pale man entered, trench coat bone-white, eyes like a pair of empty wells. Mr. Thorne.

He ordered coffee, spoke quietly with the waitress, then slid into a booth facing the street. Rae watched the thread connecting Thorne—pulsing, black, thick as rope—to a figure outside, one of the shelter regulars, Sam.

Rae’s voice shook. “He’s threatening Sam. Sam owes him.”

Gloria made a decision. “I’ll get Sam. Arnold, you talk to Thorne.”

Arnold stood, his insomnia now a shield. No one outwaited a man who didn’t sleep.

“Mr. Thorne,” Arnold said, voice even. “You’re the one offering charity and taking cuts out of people with nothing left.”

Thorne’s eyes narrowed. “You’re the postman. Best keep to your letters.”

Arnold sat, holding Thorne’s gaze. “People talk. If Sam’s in trouble, I’ll see him safe before I clock out. If you’re making things worse for folks down on their luck…well, Edgerton doesn’t much like outsiders causing trouble.”

Gloria, meanwhile, was already out the back, whispering to Sam behind the dumpster. “Come on. We’ll get you to the library. Thorne won’t come after you there; too many people, too many cameras.”

Rae’s fingers burned as the threads shimmered—a final, violent tug. Across the street, flashing lights: a squad car parked. Before Thorne could move, Arnold lifted his phone and dialed the precinct. “Got a man here—maybe you’d like to ask about counterfeit donations.”

Chapter 7: Victory in Minor Keys

That night, Thorne was questioned by police. Nothing major, just enough to put a chill in his campaign. Sam was safe. Rae watched the dark threads flicker and die, replaced with soft, humming golds and greens. Gloria’s heart did not race in her throat for once; she stood tall in the library, helping Sam fill out a job application.

As dawn painted Edgerton faint neon, the Night Owls met again at Mabel’s, sipping lukewarm coffee, feeling stronger, if not less troubled.

“We’re not heroes,” Arnold said, “but this city needs someone awake at night.”

Gloria smiled. “Sometimes extraordinary problems demand ordinary courage.”

Rae beamed, the world’s threads glowing anew. For the first time in a long while, what was broken didn’t feel so hopeless.

Chapter 8: Epilogue

The trio kept meeting. Rarely did anyone know their names; on police reports, they were “concerned neighbors.” On neighborhood forums, they were shadows. But Edgerton grew safer in the night. The pawn shops stopped exchanging obviously stolen goods. The shelter lines thinned with more dignity than desperation.

Three unlikely superheroes, their struggles woven into the tapestry of a city too ordinary to notice how special its defenders had become.

If you ever walked Edgerton after midnight and caught a glimpse of threads shimmering in puddles, or saw an unassuming mailman, a thrift store maven, and a teenager with a jacket like a disco ball traveling together, don’t look away. You just witnessed the heartbeat of a place mending in the dark—a city protected by those fighting extraordinary problems …and choosing to help anyway.
