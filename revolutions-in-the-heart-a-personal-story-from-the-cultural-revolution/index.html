Revolutions in the Heart: A Personal Story from the Cultural Revolution


The smoky scent of burning textbooks hung over our neighborhood like a shroud. It was the spring of 1967, and even at thirteen, growing up in the heart of Shanghai, I recognized the acrid note of fear that drifted through our building’s thin walls. My mother, tall and straight-backed, hid her tears as she spooned bland, thin congee into bowls we cradled like secrets.

The Red Guards were everywhere, in stark red armbands and black boots. Their chants echoed past my window long after midnight, rattling the windowpanes and my nerves in equal measure. When my father, a high school physics teacher, whispered to me not to recite poetry aloud, his breath tickled my ear; his voice was so hoarse it sounded like a warning from the afterlife.

One afternoon, as the oppressive heat pooled in our courtyard, a commotion unfolded two floors below. The sound of angry voices, furniture scraping on cement. Mother told me to stay inside, but I pressed myself to the cool wall, listening.

“Counterrevolutionary!” screamed a girl’s voice. A crash followed. I pictured my neighbor, Old Mr. Ye, who traded stamps from the safety of his tiny apartment. He loved to show me his battered memoir, its thin rice paper pages stamped with wiry calligraphy. Now, the words he had cherished would likely go up in flames, or worse, in a public denunciation.

That night, I dreamed of books with wings, fluttering around our city, pursued by torch-wielding boys. When I woke with a start, my father’s side of the bed was empty. The sound of distant drums echoed in the summer air.

By that summer, school had become a theater of suspicion. Our classroom, once filled with the soft scratch of pencils and the quiet rhythm of recitations, vibrated with slogans. “Destroy the Four Olds!” shouted banners, which hung like judgments over our heads. Teachers once loved for their wisdom became suspects overnight.

Miss Zeng, who’d introduced me to the poetry of Du Fu, arrived clutching a wrinkled handkerchief. Her eyes darted to the new placard hanging above the blackboard: “Down with revisionists!” We, her students, kept silent. The Red Guards forced her to kneel on the linoleum, reciting her supposed crimes: elitist, bourgeois, anti-Maoist. I looked away when she begged for forgiveness. Even now, the shadow of her frail body on the floor rises in my mind—trembling, defeated, but unbroken.

At home, my father burned his university diplomas in the stove, feeding the blaze with trembling hands. “You must forget all this, Xiu,” he said, his voice like gravel. “We are peasants now, all of us.”

I nodded, but I knew there was no erasing the lines knowledge had etched in my heart. That night, I traced the shapes of forgotten characters on my forearm with a piece of chalk, erasing them before anyone could see.

My family’s story mirrored those of many others, swept along by the tide of chaos and ideology. Our next-door neighbor, Mr. Sun, vanished overnight. Rumors swirled: he’d owned land before the revolution, he’d spoken out against the new textbooks, he’d hoarded foreign stamps. No one dared ask, and the authorities never explained.

Mother and I kept to ourselves, tending the threadbare garden where peppers struggled through parched soil. There was solace in routine: watering, weeding, watching seedlings push through. The garden became an oasis, a space where politics could not intrude. At dusk, dragonflies flickered above the bean vines, their silent flight a reminder of ordinary beauty.

But peace was always fleeting. One morning, Red Guards marched through the neighborhood again, banging pots, demanding the destruction of “old” relics. They thundered at our door, searching for anything suspicious—a foreign book, a photograph, a letter in English. Mother handed over her wedding dress, pale blue silk saved from a distant cousin. They ripped it apart, flakes of fabric drifting to our dusty floor.

I tried not to cry, remembering my father’s words: “steel your heart.” But as I swept up the shreds, something cold settled inside me.

The revolution pressed even harder. In autumn, my father was sent to the countryside for “re-education.” There were no goodbyes, just a hurried embrace before dawn. He left behind his glasses, his suit neatly folded, his calligraphy set hidden in a tin behind loose bricks. “Don’t let them find these,” he had whispered. Each night, I ran my fingers across the inkstone, breathing in the memory of ashen poems and lost afternoons.

Mother and I survived, rationing cornmeal and hope in equal measure. Our block meetings became trials, each neighbor denouncing another. When it was Mother’s turn, she kept her chin up, voice steady, eyes flat. “I will learn from my mistakes,” she recited, “and follow Chairman Mao’s teachings.”

Yet in our tiny apartment, she sang me lullabies plucked from forbidden melodies, her voice thin but stubborn. At such moments, the world felt smaller, like a stage set, and my own heart’s revolution beat an angry, tender rhythm.

One winter evening, as frost feathered our window, a package arrived, stamped with unfamiliar characters and splattered with rain. Inside, wrapped in a handkerchief, was a single letter from my father. Written in coded phrases—part folk song, part arithmetic—it told of fields frozen, back-breaking labor, nights spent worrying. Though he could not ask after us directly, I read his yearning between each carefully inked line.

Mother studied the letter under lamplight, her face unreadable. “We wait,” she said finally, “and we carry on.”

I started to write my own letters, never daring to send them. In the margins of cast-off newspapers, I scribbled down everything I couldn’t say aloud: my longing, my fury, snippets of the poems Miss Zeng had taught me, stories of our silent dinners and wilting garden. When anxiety crushed my chest, I pressed my palm to my ribs and imagined my pen flying across endless pages, writing our story into memory, refusing to let it be erased.

The years blurred: ration cards, purges, slogans painted anew every spring. Shanghai seemed to shrink, every street corner monitored, every friendship a potential betrayal. Yet private worlds survived, stitched together from memory and art.

Once, I found a tattered book pressed into a crack in the wall beneath our stairs—Laozi’s Dao De Jing, its cover torn, its pages soft with use. Tentatively, I opened it, savoring the quiet wisdom: “To yield is to be preserved whole.” I copied verses down, using the stub of a pencil, hiding the scraps among my mother’s sewing patterns.

Secretly, I met with Wen, a boy from my class whose father had vanished into a labor camp. We traded stories in hushed voices, wove jokes into the spaces between caution and despair, shared steamed buns when we could spare them. One afternoon, he pressed my hand and whispered, “Someday, this will end. They’ll write new slogans, and we’ll still be here.”

Hope was a fragile thing, but I clutched it anyway.

By 1972, the fervor began to wane. Slogans faded and denunciations became rare, though suspicion trailed after us like a stray dog. My father returned home one humid dawn, thinner than before, his clothes loose and his face deeply lined. Yet somewhere in his eyes, a spark still burned.

The city, too, seemed changed—a little wearier, quieter somehow, as though exhausted by its own fever. I gathered my old letters, the hidden poems, the rescued book, and laid them out for my father. Together, sitting on the cracked stoop under the weakened sun, we read silently, our thoughts knitting a tapestry from pain and resilience.

“I was afraid you would forget,” he said, after a while.

“I couldn’t,” I replied, tears thickening my words. “You can hide stories, but not erase them.” My heart hammered in my chest—defiant, alive.

That spring, the first laughter I’d heard in years drifted through our kitchen window, carried by a group of teenagers returning from their own re-education. They joked, skinny and proud, their faces open to the sun.

We rebuilt, slowly—my father found work translating instruction manuals for a new electronics factory, and my mother grew beans again, the vines curling toward the sky. Wen left to study in Beijing; I stayed behind, tending the earth, writing lines where none could hear.

Sometimes, when the city quieted at dusk, I climbed to the roof and watched the sun slant golden behind distant smokestacks. The air still held the scent of burnt paper, but it mingled with jasmine, with possibility. My revolution did not lie in banners or chants; it lived in each story I chose to carry forward, in each secret act of tenderness, in every refusal to forget.

Shanghai was changing—slowly, warily. New slogans would come, and new rules, and the history written by the powerful would shift again. But in our garden, beneath the climbing beans, under the tiles and between the lines of every memory, lay the true revolution: stubborn as a seed, hidden but alive, breaking through the stone with silent, unbreakable hope.
