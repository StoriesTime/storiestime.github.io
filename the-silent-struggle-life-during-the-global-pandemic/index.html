The Silent Struggle: Life During the Global Pandemic


The street outside Priya’s window had fallen silent, a hush that felt at odds with her memories of the same avenue—its usual parade of laughter, honking scooters, and the music of neighborhoods mixing in the air. She stood hunched, mug in hand, feeling the press of stillness all around her cramped Mumbai apartment.

Work emails had trickled in for the morning. Notifications blinked, each one a reminder of abrupt changes: meetings moved online, goals reshuffled, new crisis protocols forwarded from senior management. Priya stared for a long beat before shutting her laptop with a click. The truth was, nobody expected much from anyone now. Output would have to wait. Survival, in all its quiet forms, had become the only real deliverable.

Priya took her mug to the balcony, the one space left where sunlight still belonged to her, and considered her neighbors through rows of concrete and railings. She saw Mr. Mehra, her next-door neighbor, hesitate before touching his mailbox with his sleeve. Across the street, a little girl bounced a ball in a masked, lonesome rhythm. A mother watched from an open window, dread and exhaustion visible even from a distance. It brought to mind the thousands of windows across the city, millions across the world—a patchwork of stories unfolding in parallel silence.

Days earlier, the news had shown evacuation flights from faraway places, footage of overwhelmed hospitals, anxious lines for food rations. But Priya’s own struggle felt smaller, harder to pin down. There was no headline for waking up with a racing heart, no camera crew for evenings spent listening for ambulances in the distance. Isolation was a different kind of crisis: drawn-out, invisible, relentless.

At first, she had tried to keep up with the world outside—doomscrolling through alerts and numbers, poring over statistics, desperate for certainty. Each morning, a barrage of headlines hit her with secondhand panic. She learned new words every day: contact tracing, aerosol spread, flattening the curve. They became a kind of incantation, a futile attempt to summon back normal life.

But the worst moments came, oddly, in the quiet—when she realized, with a stab of guilt, that she was among the lucky ones. She had her job, her apartment, her health. Some evenings, as dusk painted the city an impossible shade of blue, she reached for memories of her parents’ laughter at the dinner table, the steady friendship of her old college gang, the freedom of walking home beneath a sky full of stars. What to do with longing that had nowhere to go?

Sometimes, she tried to soften the hours. She video-called her college roommate in Delhi, their conversation looping through shared worries and childhood gossip. They laughed at their own paranoia—who knew a cough could ring so many alarm bells? They sent memes, shared recipes, debated the world’s future as if they might, somehow, solve it together. It wasn’t the same, but it helped.

On weekends, Priya caught herself cleaning cupboards she did not care about, sorting clothes she rarely wore, as if the act of organizing space might bring order to her mind. Books half-read tumbled from shelves, old festival photographs slipped out of envelopes, dust from another lifetime danced in patches of afternoon sun. She played old songs, let herself cry. Let the music and memories collide, washing through her like a stone turned by the river.

The world outside grappled with its own heartbreaks. She saw news of migrant workers walking home by the thousands—barefoot journeys across locked borders—and felt the weight of their exhaustion, their uncertainty. Social media filled with pledges and petitions, pleas for oxygen, food, shelter. Every act of kindness sometimes felt too small, yet these efforts slowly knotted people together in invisible threads of hope.

Priya donated what she could to a local NGO, pressed her hands flat against a wall—her way of sending strength into the world. At a distance, she watched neighbors help each other, cooking and sharing supplies, words traded over phone lines and WhatsApp groups. Those who could, offered shelter to strangers; those who couldn’t, broadcast their outrage until officials answered. Compassion was improvisational, urgent.

Quiet resilience began flickering in unexpected places. Children learned to greet birthdays through glass doors and screens. Grandparents, once mystified by smartphones, now thumbed their way through video calls—stumbling, laughing, learning. Kitchens became temporary offices, schools, sanctuaries. People rediscovered the patience to listen, really listen, to one another’s worries. When the applause for healthcare workers rang out at sunset, Priya joined in from her balcony, hands stinging with gratitude, tears wetting her cheeks.

Later, when the night deepened, she sometimes let herself imagine the city’s collective dreams. She pictured strangers lying awake, just like her, staring at cracked ceilings. How many sleepless souls drifted through the hours, tangled in anxiety, love, fear, gratitude? She pictured the millions—each bearing private griefs: missed funerals, lost jobs, the sharp ache of distance. And the quieter wounds—plans postponed, rituals suspended, joys set aside for another year.

Days blurred. Priya marked them with little rituals—a different pair of earrings, a new book, saffron steeped in hot milk. When panic clawed its way in, she painted sets of watercolors in messy swirls, a private rebellion against the formlessness of time. Occasionally, she posted poems online, stitched together from stray thoughts and hard afternoons. Sometimes friends messaged to say the words had helped them breathe. Sometimes they just left a heart emoji—they understood the limits of language.

Her brother phoned from two cities away. He whispered updates about their parents, the subtle ways life had changed: visits from the vegetable vendor now conducted behind screens, festivals celebrated with neighbors waving from balconies, the family dog growing curious about all the extra time at home. He asked about her, too. They discussed politics, shared film recommendations, stayed up too late swapping stories that skipped through memory and back again. Each call felt like a tightrope across the unknown.

One evening, Priya’s manager messaged unexpectedly: “How are you holding up, really?” It wasn’t a perfunctory question; she sensed it in the delayed reply, the willingness to linger past protocol. For the first time in months, Priya admitted exhaustion, confessed to difficulty focusing, the weight of both fear and gratitude. The conversation turned, slowly, to ways they might rework workloads, little accommodations that offered breathing room. Empathy became a new kind of productivity.

Outside, the monsoon arrived early, beating against the city’s high-rises. Priya sat at her window, watched the rain sheet over empty playgrounds, listened to the hush of water against concrete. She tried to remember how this season had felt before—the rush of umbrellaed crowds, laughter skipping through puddles. There was no easy way back.

In time, the world adjusted, cautious and strange. Priya’s building hired a new security guard, brimming with nervous warmth, who sanitized his station every hour and called each resident by name. The little girl across the street decorated the stairwell with colored chalk, hearts and smiley faces cheering silent corridors. A neighbor’s puppy barked from their window, eager to befriend every passer-by.

Priya found herself writing long emails to friends, messages brimming with the kind of honesty that had always waited in the wings. They confessed to loneliness, dreams deferred, new rituals—midnight baking, dawn walks, rooms transformed into miniature jungles. There were confessions of depression and fear, but also wonder at fleeting beauty: the return of birds, the clean smell after rain, the rediscovered pleasure of slow mornings.

The world outside began to pulse with cautious hope. Scientists raced for vaccines, communities organized pop-up clinics, volunteers delivered meals and smiles. The internet, so often a source of anxiety, became an extraordinary web of support: strangers singing across continents, sharing home workouts, passing along sources for good masks and jokes about zoom mishaps, swapping tips for sourdough starters. A patchwork planet, battered but unbroken.

Priya’s own routine shifted. She cooked for herself with more care, Mandarins and spices and fresh ginger folded into the same old rice, made new. Her daily walks became meditative, her apartment a living collage of painted notes and blooming plants. Sometimes, she played board games with her roommate, laughter looping in circles around the tiny living room. Small joys began to matter more.

There were setbacks, too—new spikes in cases, revised restrictions, tears for losses that news anchors measured but never truly captured. But more and more, those setbacks met a population seasoned in resilience. The rituals of grief and gratitude, of checking in, of offering compassion, became woven into everyday life.

Sometimes, Priya would stand at her balcony during golden hour, sunlight slanting through restless clouds, and imagine what she might tell her future self about these years. She’d remember the terror, yes, but also the ingenuity—parents learning to juggle work and play, exhausted doctors teaching the world to care from behind layers of plastic and cotton. She’d remember the silence, but also the fragile strength it bred: strangers growing bold in kindness, plans shifting and remaking themselves a hundred different ways.

She’d remember the patience that grew of necessity, the difficult lesson that life is neither fair nor predictable but always, somehow, worth defending. The unexpected gifts: time alone with herself, proof that love can leap across continents, evidence that the world’s capacity for heartbreak is only matched by its appetite for healing.

When news finally broke that relief was on the horizon—vaccine trials declaring hope, cities announcing cautious reopening—Priya let herself feel a cautious joy. But she knew scars remained, not all visible, and that the silent struggle of these years would shape her in ways she could not yet name. She also felt, for the first time, the stirrings of peace.

One late evening, when the city trembled on the cusp of recovery, Priya stood at her window and listened. The old noise had not returned—no parades of honking, no music drifting down from rooftops. Instead, she heard something softer: the hum of resilience, laughter threaded with loss, neighbors waving from windows, trading small kindnesses. A city, a world, learning to breathe again.

Inside, Priya pressed her palms together, bowed her head to the gathering dusk, and whispered a quiet vow to remember. To never again take the ordinary for granted—laughter at a café, the clasp of a friend’s hand, the simple freedom to move through the world unafraid.

And somewhere far below, a little girl bounced her ball, counting each echo as both victory and promise: the world, bruised but alive, would meet the dawn again.
