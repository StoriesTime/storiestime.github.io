Existential Questions in Short Story Form


In a small town cradled by misty hills and whispering forests, there lived an old clockmaker named Elias. His workshop was a labyrinth of ticking mechanisms and fading wood, where time itself seemed to slow down, tangled within the intricate gears of the countless clocks he had crafted over decades. Elias was a man who traded in moments—assembling seconds, minutes, hours—yet he harbored a peculiar obsession far beyond mere workmanship. He sought to understand the essence of time, questioning its nature and, more profoundly, the nature of existence itself.

Elias’s daily routine was a ritual of precision. As dawn’s first light poured through stained glass, casting colorful patterns onto his workbench, Elias would wind up his clocks, meticulously attuned to never miss a tic. Outside, the town awoke with the murmur of life; inside, the workshop was a sanctum of quiet contemplation. Between repairing watches and crafting new timepieces, Elias pondered a seemingly simple but eternally complex question: What is the meaning of existence?

His workshop was filled not only with clocks but also with dusty tomes and philosophical essays. Books by thinkers like Camus, Nietzsche, and Kierkegaard lay open and scattered, their margins full of Elias’s handwritten notes and connections. He had spent years trying to dissect their perspectives, integrating the human search for meaning with the inexorable passage of time.

One evening, as a fierce storm battered the town, tearing leaves from trees and shaking windowpanes, Elias sat by the flickering light of an oil lamp. He was working on a peculiar clock—a grand contraption unlike any he’d built before. This clock didn’t just keep time; it was designed to depict the life cycle: birth, growth, decay, and death, with hands that moved not by hours and minutes, but by seasons and milestones.

As thunder rolled through the hills, Elias locked eyes with his creation. “If time is the measure of life,” he mused aloud, “then what does it mean when time ends? Does death conclude the question, or is it simply another turning of the gears?”

He realized that time, with all its measurable aspects, could not fully contain the essence of existence. The clock ticked forward tirelessly, but Elias knew that human life was punctuated by experiences far weightier than seconds counted.

The philosophy of existentialism had always fascinated Elias because it forced him to face the absurd: the idea that life might have no inherent meaning other than the one we give it. For a craftsman of time, that notion was both unsettling and liberating.

On a particularly chilly morning, Elias decided to step outside his workshop to visit the nearby lake. The surface was still, a perfect mirror reflecting the gray sky above. Sitting on a worn bench, Elias spotted a small boy skipping stones across the water—each splash rippling outward, disturbing the glassy calm before settling again.

The boy’s laughter was infectious, and for a moment, Elias let himself feel the freedom of pure joy detached from time’s constraints. Then a question arose: Are we merely ripples in the vast ocean of time, or do our actions etch permanence in the flow?

Back in his workshop, this insight sparked a new project. Elias began constructing a clock unlike any other: a “Narrative Clock,” where each hour symbolized a human story rather than a segment of time. He envisioned a mechanism that would rotate through tales of joy, sorrow, love, despair, and triumph—reminding the onlooker that life is woven from moments that transcend mere chronology.

As he worked, Elias pondered the ethical implications of his invention. Was he playing god, deciding which stories merited time? Or was he offering a mirror to humanity, urging people to find meaning not through external dictates but through their own lived experiences?

One afternoon, the doorbell rang, and a young woman entered Elias’s shop. She introduced herself as Clara, a philosophy student intrigued by the rumors of the clockmaker’s deeper inquiries into time and life.

Over cups of steaming tea, Elias and Clara delved into conversations about existentialism, the absurd, freedom, and responsibility. Clara spoke of existential dread—how confronting the void sometimes left her paralyzed with fear. Elias replied that dread could also be a catalyst for authenticity, a signal awakening one to the freedom to craft their own path.

“You see,” Elias said, gesturing to the half-finished Narrative Clock, “life isn’t just time passing. It’s the stories we choose to tell, the meaning we assign to the moments in between the ticks.”

Clara nodded, her eyes bright with understanding. “Perhaps meaning isn’t found but created—by us, in collaboration with time.”

Weeks passed, and the Narrative Clock was completed. Its face was a collage of images and symbols, each representing facets of the human condition. The hands moved slowly, cycling through stories inscribed in vibrant patterns, a celebration of life’s multifaceted nature.

Elias arranged a small exhibition in the town’s gallery. People came, curious and contemplative, drawn by the unusual clock. As they observed, discussions flourished about what it means to exist and how one might find purpose amid uncertainty.

One elderly visitor whispered, “I never thought of life as a story shaped by my choices before. Maybe I still have chapters to write.”

As the seasons turned and years unfurled, Elias’s clockwork philosophies became woven into the fabric of the town. His workshop echoed no longer just with ticking gears but with conversations, reflections, and a shared quest to understand existence.

Elias, now content with his quest, realized that perhaps the greatest existential question—Why are we here?—was not meant to be answered definitively. Instead, it was a calling to live deliberately, embracing the mystery of time and the stories that comprise a life well-lived.

As he wound his old clocks one last time, Elias smiled. The paradox of the clockmaker was that while he measured time, he knew the true measure of existence was not in seconds counted but in meaning created. And that, perhaps, was the most enduring ticking of all.

In the silent hum of the workshop, the clocks chimed together—a timeless symphony of questions, reflections, and the human heart’s eternal dialogue with existence.
