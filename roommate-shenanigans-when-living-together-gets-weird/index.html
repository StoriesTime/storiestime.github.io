Roommate Shenanigans: When Living Together Gets Weird


The ad was clear: “Seeking third roommate to join two chill housemates, must love plants, music, and pasta. Rent negotiable for the right weirdo.” Rachel had scrolled past it twice because “love plants” sounded like code for “clean up after my Amazon jungle.” But her desperation outpaced her dread, and after a string of silent tours through empty, personality-vacant rooms, this one’s quirk spoke to her. Maybe she’d find kindred spirits. Maybe they’d sacrifice a goat. Either way, it sounded more interesting than her parents’ garage.

She met her future cohabitants—a wiry, wide-eyed guy named Drew and a PhD student with curlier hair than should fit on one head called Lila—at 7:00 on a Thursday, just as the sinking sun bled orange through the living room blinds. Drew was microwaving something in a mason jar. Lila had a calico cat on her shoulder and a bandage on her forearm, apparently unrelated.

“So, Rachel,” Lila said, gesturing with a glass of orange juice that looked suspiciously pulpy, “you left hobbies blank on your application.”

“I…did?” Rachel stammered, trying not to watch the microwave. “Oh, I read a lot. Some gardening. Definitely into pasta.”

Drew grinned. “That’s a better answer than Aiden last week. Claimed his hobby was ‘cryptocurrency ethics.’”

Lila shuddered. “He also tried to install three Ring cameras during the tour.”

Rachel nodded and smiled, feeling already, in some surreal way, inducted into a rotating circus act. She’d get keys next day, they told her—after, as Lila reminded her gravely, the “smell test.” Drew just grinned wider.

Chapter 2: The Pasta Pact

Moving day arrived. Rachel squeezed her clothes into the smallest available suitcase and crammed her books into a battered cardboard box. Her entire adult life, she realized, could fit on the passenger seat. She slid in after the box with a jolt and set off for her new home in the heart of a neighborhood with mural-covered walls and at least four taco trucks within stumbling distance.

Drew helped haul a mini-fridge up the stairs, panting, “You can decorate the fridge if you want. Lila already claimed the freezer for her weird bread.”

Lila breezed past a few minutes later, trailing a scent of rosemary and band-aids. “We’re doing pasta nights,” she announced, as if Rachel had signed a contract. “Twice a month. Anyone can choose the pasta shape, but the sauce has to be voted on.”

Rachel, mid-unpacking, snorted. “What happens if there’s a tie?”

Drew’s eyes twinkled. “You don’t want to know.”

“Last time,” Lila said drily, “someone tried pesto ice cream.”

Rachel blinked. A joke? Not a joke? Either way, she was intrigued.

Chapter 3: Unraveling Normal

The first week, it became clear that “chill” in this house was a relative measurement. Lila’s cat, Nightjar, belly-flopped onto Rachel’s laptop mid-conference call, then looked her straight in the eye and deliberately pressed the space bar. Drew’s bonsai trees populated every other flat surface and were adorned with googly eyes; every morning, one of the trees wore a different hat. Bowler, beanie, or baseball cap. No explanation was offered, and Rachel discovered that asking only made Drew more cryptic.

Friday night was officially Declutter Night. Not for tidying (that happened whenever something started to smell) but for emotional baggage. Lila set out a “Feelings Jar”—a pickle jar with a Post-It note and a pen. The idea: write a secret, fold it, and toss it in. None of them expected anyone to be honest, but someone wrote, “I think Nightjar is plotting to murder me,” folded twice.

Another: “I hid an avocado in the living room to see how long it would take to rot. Update: 8 days.”

The next morning, Rachel realized she felt the house’s oddness slowly infecting her. She drew a face on an orange, gave it a wig, and left it by the French press. She didn’t admit to it. But the day after, the orange had a painted-on bowtie and someone had started gluing googly eyes to the toaster.

Chapter 4: The Pasta Disaster Olympics

The first household pasta night landed on the last Sunday of the month. Lila had chosen orecchiette. She also produced a jar of “homemade sauce,” which she claimed had once won a blue ribbon at the CSU Boulder Farmer’s Market—as a fertilizer additive.

Drew contributed “experimental garlic bread” with a suspiciously green tint, courtesy of, he explained with pride, his countertop hydroponic basil. Rachel contributed salad, classic Caesar, figuring it would offset whatever else happened. The air fogged with the aroma of butter, garlic, garden basil, and a whiff of burnt cheese.

After three bites, all three burst out laughing. The sauce was so spicy Rachel was positive she could see through time. Drew’s bread, on inspection, held a single unbaked clove of garlic per slice. Lila’s salad dressing had, somehow, a faint but distinct aftertaste of grape jelly.

They stayed around the table for almost two hours, trying to compute what had gone so bizarrely wrong. The cat, disgusted with the offerings, cleared a glass of water onto Lila’s lap.

Somehow, no one mourned the ruined food. The stories spun free around the table, reminiscing of past pasta failures, and, not for the first time, Rachel wondered if this was actually better than good food—whatever was outside the window, tension and all, belonged to other people.

Chapter 5: Quirks Multiply

The weeks blurred with new peculiarities. The shower let out a shriek like a banshee every time someone used the hot water, to which Drew responded by composing a new song each day, performing it with shampoo bottles as percussion instruments. Lila began hosting a monthly “book swap and potion night,” blending herbal teas while encouraging everyone to “cleanse their energies and swap a used paperback.” Rachel’s copy of ‘Dune’ vanished. She replaced it with an even thicker copy of ‘The Stand’ just to watch Lila’s reaction.

Rachel found herself taking the long way home, hopeful that by the time she arrived, Drew would have another cryptic scavenger hunt posted to the fridge, or Lila would have overcooked vegan muffins to be consumed only as doorstops. The boundaries of her life, once sharp and cautious, smudged into weirdness and laughter.

But not all was harmonious. Rachel’s sleep schedule crashed into Drew’s nightly “creative time,” which often began at 2 a.m. and ended, if she was lucky, before dawn. Lila, meanwhile, left half-finished science experiments in the refrigerator and snapped at anyone who tampered with the mysterious mason jars labeled only with runes.

One particularly harrowing Monday, Rachel opened the fridge for yogurt and was confronted by what appeared to be an attempt at home-made tofu towering in a pickle jar. Drew, seeing her shriek and leap back, claimed it was his “fermentation research.” Rachel instituted a new household rule: labels must be in English, and mold must stay sealed.

Chapter 6: The Breaking Point

Disagreements escalated. Rachel’s resentment simmered with every late-night jam session; she left passive-aggressive Post-Its on every amplifier. Lila’s potion night was interrupted one evening when Drew and a couple friends began filming a stop-motion movie using all the houseplants. Rachel’s salad from the night before became an unwitting prop.

After three weeks like this, the storm broke. Pasta night descended into an argument over chickpeas of all things—should you put them in fettuccine? Rachel lost it.

“Look,” she snapped, banging down her fork, “If anyone microwaves cilantro again, so help me, I’m moving to a tent in the backyard.”

Lila and Drew stared. Even Nightjar, sprawled on Drew’s shoes, went still.

Then Drew burst out laughing. Lila joined in, and soon Rachel was chuckling, the pent-up strangeness and miscommunication dissolving as if released by some shared secret code. The next day, Rachel drew a “peace treaty” on the whiteboard beside the fridge: “No cilantro in the microwave. Experiment jars go on the bottom shelf. Quiet hours: 1-7 a.m.”

Drew counter-signed. Lila amended it: “If you see me sleepwalking, put me back in bed, not the kitchen.”

They all agreed.

Chapter 7: Acceptance

The remainder of the year was a kaleidoscope of offbeat traditions, half-baked baking, feral plants, and increasingly elaborate scavenger hunts. Rachel’s blood pressure dropped, despite (or perhaps because of) the chaos. Somewhere along the line, the lines between “weird” and “comfort” faded. The quirks that irked her in the beginning blossomed into a symbiotic pact: no one in this house was going to judge the others for being a little off. Life overflowed the usual boundaries, and for the first time in years, Rachel had stories worth retelling—stories that could only come from the slow, strange miracle of living together with other wildly original, unpredictable people.

Through arguments, laughter, pasta disasters, and stray bits of “fermentation research,” the three roommates crafted a peculiar, endearing family unit. Their shenanigans were the currency of their connection—and by year’s end, Rachel couldn’t imagine a better measure of home.

Living together gets weird, but sometimes, weird is exactly what you need.
