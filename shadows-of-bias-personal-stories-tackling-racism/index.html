Shadows of Bias: Personal Stories Tackling Racism


The morning sunlight barely pierced through the mist that hung in the air as Mara walked down the street toward the bus stop. She had always taken this route, a familiar path she had walked for years, but today it felt different. Today, something weighed heavily on her chest, a subtle unease that lingered in the pit of her stomach.

Mara had grown up in a small town, one where people looked like her—people who understood the quiet power of shared experience. But over the years, as she ventured further into the city for work and study, she had been confronted with a reality she had not fully prepared for: the existence of bias. It was there, in the looks she received from strangers, in the dismissive tone from her colleagues, in the subtle jokes that weren’t so subtle.

Today, she could feel the weight of it more than ever. She had heard the whispers, felt the eyes of others tracking her movements, had overheard the words that made her wonder whether she was just another figure to be judged.

As she approached the bus stop, she saw an older woman sitting on the bench. Her skin was dark, her hair streaked with gray, her face marked by the wisdom of years lived through the challenges of life. Mara smiled at her, but the woman’s eyes remained fixed on the ground. It was a look Mara knew well—one of exhaustion, of someone who had seen too much. Mara could feel the subtle tension between them. She sat down next to the woman, not out of necessity, but because something inside her urged her to try and break the silence.

“Good morning,” Mara said softly.

The woman glanced up, a slow, knowing smile spreading across her face. “Morning, dear. Long day ahead?”

“Seems like it,” Mara replied, trying to read the woman’s face. It was a quiet moment, but it held so much. The connection they shared was unsaid, but it was there.

As the two women sat in the stillness, a young man walked past them, a briefcase in hand. He looked Mara up and down, his eyes lingering just a little too long on her skin, and then continued on his way, not bothering to acknowledge her presence further.

The woman on the bench tensed slightly, her grip tightening around the strap of her purse. Mara felt the familiar sting of judgment, that invisible weight that came with being seen through a lens of bias. It wasn’t new, but it was never easy to bear.

“I see that look in his eyes,” the woman said quietly. “It’s the same one I’ve seen all my life. People who don’t really see you—they just see what they expect to see.”

Mara nodded, her throat tightening as she absorbed the words. It wasn’t the first time she had encountered such behavior, but it still hurt. The young man’s look was nothing compared to the intense scrutiny she sometimes faced. In the workplace, she had been passed over for promotions, her ideas dismissed as “too radical,” and her presence often overlooked until it became impossible to ignore. She had worked hard to prove herself, but no matter how hard she tried, there was always an invisible barrier that seemed to keep her from achieving the same recognition as her peers.

But she wasn’t alone in this. The woman beside her had lived through it too. Their shared experience, though unspoken, created a bond—one of understanding, of silent solidarity.

“What do you do when that happens?” Mara asked, her voice tinged with frustration.

The woman shrugged, her face turning reflective. “You learn to carry it, dear. You learn that people’s judgments don’t define you. They may try to box you in, label you, make assumptions about your worth, but you know who you are. That’s what matters.”

Mara felt a deep sense of respect for the woman, whose voice held the weight of years of survival. It wasn’t the first time Mara had heard these words—“don’t let them define you”—but hearing them now, in the presence of someone who had lived through so much, felt different. She thought about her own experiences, the challenges she had faced, the times she had let herself be defined by the bias others had placed on her.

“I wish it were easier,” Mara admitted, her voice barely above a whisper. “I wish it didn’t hurt so much to feel like I’m always having to prove myself.”

The woman nodded knowingly. “It’s hard, no doubt. But don’t ever lose sight of who you are. Racism doesn’t just come from strangers. It can seep into your own heart if you’re not careful. The world can make you doubt yourself, make you feel small. But don’t let them steal your sense of self-worth.”

Mara sat in silence, absorbing the woman’s words. She thought about the times she had questioned her worth, the doubts that had crept in during moments of exclusion. She remembered how she had been passed over in favor of someone else, simply because of the color of her skin. Those moments had piled up over the years, shaping her into someone who was constantly looking over her shoulder, wondering if she was truly accepted or just tolerated.

But the woman was right. She couldn’t let those experiences define her. She had to find a way to rise above them, to embrace her own power and continue to strive for the things she knew she deserved.

A bus pulled up in front of them, its brakes hissing as it came to a stop. The door opened with a creak, and passengers began to shuffle off and on. Mara stood up, ready to board. She turned to the woman on the bench.

“Thank you,” Mara said, her voice steady now. “You’ve really helped me see things differently.”

The woman smiled, her eyes gleaming with a quiet pride. “You’ll get there, dear. Just remember—you are enough.”

Mara nodded, a sense of clarity washing over her. She had always known that the fight against bias wasn’t easy, but now, more than ever, she felt determined to continue pushing forward. She boarded the bus and found an empty seat, her mind racing with the conversation she had just had. It wasn’t just the words themselves, but the unspoken understanding they carried. Mara wasn’t alone. There were others like her, others who had faced the same shadows of bias, and together, they could push against the weight of prejudice and change the narrative.

As the bus drove through the city streets, Mara looked out the window, watching as the world around her unfolded. She saw the faces of others, all of them living their own stories, facing their own challenges. Some were the same as hers, others were different. But each person had a story to tell, a journey that was uniquely their own. And it was in these stories that the power to overcome bias, to challenge discrimination, and to reshape the future lay.

Mara knew that the road ahead would not be easy. The shadows of bias would always be there, lurking in the background, but she was ready to face them. She would no longer allow them to define her or her worth. She would continue to rise above them, to break down the barriers that others tried to place in her path.

And one day, she hoped that the world would see her not for the color of her skin, but for the strength of her character and the courage to stand up in the face of adversity.

In that quiet moment on the bus, Mara made a silent promise to herself: she would keep fighting. For herself. For the woman on the bench. For every person who had ever been silenced by the shadows of bias.

And she would never stop until those shadows were gone.
