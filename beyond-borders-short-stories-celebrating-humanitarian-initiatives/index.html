Beyond Borders: Short Stories Celebrating Humanitarian Initiatives


In the heart of a primitive village nestled within the mountainous terrains of northeast Nepal, there existed an ancient tradition that reflected the community’s unwavering dedication to charity and humanitarian initiatives. Every year in the harsh winter months, when the snow would engulf the village in a cover of white, the rituals of this tradition were performed in a procession of carefully choreographed steps.

The village elder, Maina Didi, handed over a round-brimmed lantern to each family in the village. Each lantern was distinct in design, bearing intricate local motifs and adorned with small bits of precious stones and beads collected over years by the families. The lanterns had been passed down from generation to generation; influenced by the history and culture of the village, reminiscent of stories of their forefathers.

“The lanterns, our ancestors called Chhahara, serve as a beacon of hope,” Maina Didi would say, emotion glistening in her eyes, “As darkness looms over us, they illuminate the path ahead, just as our ancestors before us did.”

When darkness had fallen, the lanterns would be kindled. In unity with the village, each family would place their lanterns in the windows, dancing a pattern of quickly flickering and finally dimming lights in the night sky. This electrifying sight brought an enduring sense of camaraderie and pride to the villagers. The lanterns symbolized more than a commitment to not let pessimism gain ground; they represented the zeal of a community growing stronger despite their isolation, the plight of individuals, and a yearning for a brighter future.

But the harsh winter of 1943 brought an unpredicted calamity. A severe snowstorm hit the village, bringing torrential snowfall. The storm raged for days, suffocating everything under its ruthless fury. Despite the village’s relentless efforts, the snow only kept piling, threatening to engulf the village with its creeping chill.

Recognizing the gravity of the situation, the villagers heeded Maina Didi’s call. They sent word to the neighboring villages for aid and relief, the first time in their history they had to reach out for help.

The response was overwhelming. Villages from far and near came forth, each carrying their Chhahara lanterns in solidarity with the beleaguered community. As the storm raged on, the Chhahara lanterns guided the relief workers through the snowy torment, saving lives and ensuring the village did not succumb to the icy despair.

Families from outside the region who had helped in the relief work eventually settled in the village, enrichening the community with their skills and trades. More actions were initiated to reach out to other isolated settlements, and many came forward to help build a bridge between the scattered valleys.

The village that was once confined within its mountains now stood resonating with the spirit of unity and humanitarian initiatives that once lit the Chhahara lanterns.

Though the storm of 1943 had tested their limits, the villagers discovered that beneath the snow and frost lay an underlying warmth waiting to be shared. The lanterns’ enduring legacy sunk deep within the village, lighting the way for more than just the Chhahara ritual. Each flicker framed in the.windows served as a constant reaffirmation of the unbreakable bond the villagers forged with their newfound brothers and sisters.

These lanterns, the Chhahara, became a symbol of a sacrificial humanitarian tradition encompassing everything that the village stood for; bound by their unity and the common vision to strive against the odds.

For decades to come, the Chhahara lanterns would remain the village’s pride, the epitome of their dedication to humanitarian initiatives, a guiding light in times of need, and a hopeful reminder that even the darkest days could be overcome with unity and compassion. And as the Chhahara lanterns continue to illuminate the skies each winter, so shall the echo of their legacy reverberate through the vast mountains supporting the villages that now stand beyond the borders.
