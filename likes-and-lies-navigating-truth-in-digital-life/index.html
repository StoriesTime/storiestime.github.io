Likes and Lies: Navigating Truth in Digital Life


In the soft glow of her bedroom, Claire sat hunched over her phone, her thumb flicking lazily across the screen. Her mind was elsewhere, lost somewhere between the posts and the faces of friends she barely knew anymore. A small ding pulled her from her reverie: a notification. It was from Marissa, her college roommate, who had posted a photo on her story. Claire clicked on it without thinking.

The image was perfect. Marissa, in a flowy white dress, standing in front of a rustic café bathed in the golden light of a setting sun. She looked effortlessly beautiful, with a genuine smile that could have been ripped straight from a magazine. Underneath the photo, there was a short caption: “Living my best life, one cup of coffee at a time.” Claire couldn’t help but sigh. She didn’t think it was fair.

Her thumb hovered over the “like” button, and for a moment, she considered not engaging. The photo was beautiful, sure, but it felt fake. It wasn’t just the posed nature of the picture that bothered her. It was the subtleness of it—how easily everyone could lie with a click, how a person’s entire identity could be constructed with a series of snapshots and a couple of strategically placed words. Claire had been in that café with Marissa last week. It was rainy, and Marissa had spent most of the time scrolling through her phone, only half-listening to Claire’s stories about work. But in this image, it was a different story altogether.

With a soft groan, Claire tapped the heart-shaped icon. A notification appeared: “You liked Marissa’s post.” It felt hollow. Almost too easy.

A few months ago, she wouldn’t have thought twice about it. A like was just a like, something everyone did to show support, to acknowledge the existence of others. But over the past year, Claire had begun to wonder: were they really supporting, or were they just participating in a performance? The more she scrolled, the more she felt like the walls around her were closing in. The digital world had started to feel less like a community and more like a stage, and Claire was stuck playing a part she never signed up for.

It wasn’t just Marissa. Claire’s entire social media feed seemed to be a carefully curated gallery of smiles, vacations, accomplishments, and picture-perfect lives. There was Jasmine, the girl from high school who now posted daily fitness selfies and motivational quotes. Then there was Ethan, her old coworker, whose feed was a testament to his successful career. It was easy to get caught up in the illusion of it all.

Claire had tried once to post something “real.” A snapshot of her lunch, taken without any editing, with a caption about how stressed she was at work. It was meant to be a joke—something self-deprecating to remind her followers that life wasn’t as perfect as it appeared. But the post received only a handful of likes. No comments. She deleted it within an hour.

“Why is it so hard to be honest?” she muttered to herself as she scrolled through her feed again.

Her thoughts were interrupted by a message. It was from her best friend, Amelia.

Amelia: “Hey, what are you doing tonight? Want to grab dinner?”

Claire thought for a moment. She wasn’t particularly in the mood for socializing, but the idea of spending time with someone who knew her beyond the screen of a phone was appealing.

Claire: “Sure! Let’s do it. I need a break from the endless scrolling.”

When Claire arrived at the restaurant, she immediately spotted Amelia sitting at a corner table, her phone face down on the table. There was no need for awkward greetings, no need for “status updates” or social media banter. Amelia was present, grounded in the real world. It was one of the things Claire cherished most about their friendship.

They talked about everything and nothing—work frustrations, their latest Netflix binge, and their mutual love of coffee. It felt so different from the text-heavy, emoji-filled conversations she had on her social media apps. There was something deeply satisfying about having a conversation without worrying about how every word might be interpreted or how much validation she would receive after it was over.

“You know,” Amelia said, sipping her wine, “I’ve been thinking a lot about social media lately.”

Claire raised an eyebrow. “Really? What about it?”

Amelia leaned in, her eyes sparkling with curiosity. “I feel like we’ve all fallen into this trap. We’ve turned our lives into this weird, curated highlight reel. Everything is about how it looks, not what it is. And it’s exhausting.”

“I know exactly what you mean.” Claire slouched in her chair, the weight of her thoughts pressing down on her shoulders. “I’ve been thinking the same thing. Like, we’re all posting things that are meant to look perfect, but it’s just… a mask. A lie.”

“Exactly. And the worst part is, we don’t even notice when it becomes our reality. The likes, the comments, the followers—it all starts to define us. But in the end, it doesn’t mean anything, right? It’s just a bunch of numbers.”

Claire took a deep breath. For the first time in weeks, she felt like someone truly understood her frustration. It was comforting, but also slightly unnerving. “But it’s hard to escape from it. It’s everywhere. Even when I try to take a step back, I feel like I’m missing out. Like I’m not part of the conversation if I don’t engage with it.”

Amelia nodded. “I get it. I think a lot of people feel that way. It’s like we’re all playing a game, but none of us really know the rules. And at the end of the day, we’re chasing validation from people we don’t even know.”

Claire thought about it. Was this all just an elaborate game of pretend? A charade where everyone was vying for the same thing—attention, validation, a fleeting sense of belonging?

“I guess I’ve been thinking about it all wrong,” Claire said after a long pause. “I’ve been measuring my worth by how many people like my posts. But maybe… maybe my worth isn’t tied to that at all.”

Amelia smiled softly. “I think you’re onto something. You’re more than your feed, Claire. You always have been.”

The following week, Claire made a conscious decision to change the way she interacted with social media. She didn’t delete her accounts—no, that would be too drastic—but she began to unfollow the accounts that made her feel small, the ones that perpetuated the illusion of perfection. Instead, she sought out people who were open about the messiness of life—the ones who shared the ups and the downs, the victories and the struggles.

And then, one afternoon, she did something completely out of character. She posted a photo—just a photo of her messy desk at work, piled high with paperwork and half-empty coffee cups. The caption was simple: “Some days are better than others. Here’s to surviving another week.”

It wasn’t glamorous. It wasn’t curated. It was real.

The response was unexpected. Dozens of comments came in, each one filled with empathy, encouragement, and shared experiences. Her friends didn’t just “like” the post; they commented, engaging in a real conversation. Some even posted their own “messy” photos, showing the world that not everything had to be perfect. That, in itself, was enough.

Over the next few weeks, Claire began to notice something remarkable. The more authentic she became in her online presence, the more connected she felt to those around her. The pressure to maintain a flawless image began to dissipate, replaced by a sense of freedom. She no longer worried about how many likes she received or whether her posts fit some curated aesthetic. Instead, she focused on what truly mattered: real connections, genuine moments, and honest interactions.

It wasn’t always easy. Sometimes, she still found herself slipping into old habits, checking her follower count or obsessing over the perfect caption. But each time she did, she reminded herself that life wasn’t about the number of likes you had, but the people who truly saw you for who you were.

As Claire closed her phone one night and placed it on her nightstand, she realized something important: the digital world was not a reflection of reality. It was merely a lens—a way to connect, share, and express. But the truth of who she was? That could never be contained within the frame of a picture or the click of a like. It was something deeper, something real, something that could only be found when the screen was turned off, and the world outside beckoned her to live.
