Deepfake Diaries: When Truth Can't Be Trusted


Laszlo Tomlinson first glimpsed himself on the news in circumstances he could not explain. The “him” on-screen, mouth hard-set and eyes rimmed in shadow, was delivering a speech at a rally he had never attended, from a lectern decorated with symbols he had never chosen to endorse. Laszlo muted the television. For one librarian in the crumbling heart of Boston, the world tilted open into the uncanny.

He leaned in, squinting for flaws: Was the jaw movement off? Did his voice stutter, replayed in endless clips? But the footage rolled smoothly, down to the knotted brow he pressed when he was forced to shelve the Dostoyevsky collection for the third time in a month. No one spoke to him about it for days. Convinced it was an anomaly—one tech slip, some Internet joke—Laszlo pushed it from his mind until his phone vibrated with a dozen messages late Thursday night.

What is WRONG with you?
I didn’t know you felt that way about them.
Are you in trouble?

He clicked a link from his sister: another video. The digital Laszlo delivered a spitting condemnation of a local politician, laced with threatening language foreign to his tongue. Everything was warped, and yet: the memories, the lips, the hands—wrong but his.

He stared at his phone, stomach pooling with nausea. It was then that he noticed the account: @DeepfakeDiaries—an anonymous handle, profile all black save for his pixel-stretched smile. The caption: “Truth comes in many faces.”

The World in Shadows

The next morning, Laszlo found his apartment building’s front door sprayed with graffiti: BENEATH THE MASK, THE KNIFE.
Nearly midnight before he reached his roommate, Noelle, valiantly immune to panic. Noelle was a machinist by day and spent nights modding her own VR headsets or baking weirdly dense fudge. When he showed her the video, she frowned.

“It’s good,” she admitted, “Way beyond those crummy face swaps. They’ve layered your posture, speech. And—wait, that’s your living room.”
He felt a spike of something bitter. “I was at work. That never happened.”
“They’re using your background.” She sighed, rolling her eyes at the universe. “You know, technology is an eternal arms race with nonsense. It’s why I stick to the hardware, and screw the algorithms.”
Laszlo put both hands in his hair and pulled, gently. “How do I prove any of this isn’t real?”
“Proof is up for grabs, but we can dig in.” She snapped on her tablet. “We start hunting tracks.”

They ran searches. The videos were everywhere: social feeds, message boards, targeted emails to prominent city groups. Each day, a new snippet: Laszlo refusing kids entry to the library; Laszlo trashing rare books; Laszlo demanding money from his boss. The comments below stung. Even his mother, anxious and reserved, left a voicemail asking if he needed to “see someone.”

Noelle mapped out the digital signature, following it back to a Dutch server and then a Vietnamese connection, which broke into ten more anonymous nodes. Traceback became a hydra. She groaned.
“They’re pros. Maybe criminal types, or viral marketers. Could even be a bored teenager with a grudge and too much time.”
Laszlo stared past her tools to the windows, where the city’s lights smeared watery across the glass. “You think they’ll stop?”
Noelle shrugged, her face softening. “It’s about control. Attention. Once you stop responding, most people get bored. But this? This feels… surgical.”

The Soft War

Within weeks, Laszlo’s life underwent a bizarre transformation. Though his library manager issued a gentle public statement of support, she assigned him to the back rooms, “just until the fuss dies down.” Friends stepped away after one viral clip suggested he had defaced a community mural. Dating? Off the table—no one wanted headlines with their brunch.

A woman stopped him outside the market, clutching her phone like a weapon. “How dare you? People like you destroy everything good.”
“I didn’t—”
She snapped a picture, stormed off, fingers working at the screen.
Laszlo began tracking every video, every manipulated utterance. Sometimes, he heard himself confessing to crimes: vandalism, political hacking, even a phoned-in bomb threat. His likeness sold conspiracy theories and dietary scams on late-night channels. Each episode became more convincing, massaging small, known details—his books, his love of jazz, the tattoo under his sleeve—and weaponizing them into fiction.

Noelle set up a signal system: “Green is okay, amber is chill at home, red is hide in the stairwell.” Most days, she nudged him out for brief walks. “It’s not really about you,” she smirked. “You’re just a particularly convenient canvas.”
When Laszlo asked, “What happens if nobody can tell truth from lies?” Noelle shrugged. “Then we all float in the soup, bud. And soup doesn’t tell stories. People do.”
He exhaled, long and measured. “But if people believe the soup?”
“That’s how you poison the well. Not the soup, but the stories about the soup.”

A Meeting with the Enemy

On an impulse, Laszlo called a lawyer, Veronica Kaiser, whose website led with “Digital Defamation: Fight Lies with Litigation.” Over coffee, she took colossal notes without breaking eye contact.
“They’re masterful,” she said, clicking through the archives with clinical indifference. “But here’s the hitch—we’d have to convince a jury it’s manufactured, and even tech-savvy folks are starting to blur real from fake. You got any enemies?”
“None I know,” Laszlo replied, thinking of overdue library fines, a couple of failed romances, one bad breakup in college.
“Could be random. Could be big,” she said. “Tell you a secret: most folks don’t care if something’s true; they’ll share it if it itches what they want to believe.”
He laughed, hollow. “How do you defend against that?”
Veronica grinned. “Sunlight and noise. You blast your real self everywhere. Dominate the narrative.”

Laszlo, numb, agreed to a plan. He would release video counterstatements—at first awkward, rehearsed, but gradually more fluid. Noelle engineered a studio corner in their kitchen, adding timestamps and on-screen security signatures. Every post included open access to raw footage and a running “diary” of daily events. He embraced transparency as armor.

The reactions were mixed. Some called him a liar. Others rallied, “Support Laszlo!” Stickers appeared on lampposts, a local podcaster declared, “He’s the perfect test case—who do we trust when every face comes in digital clothing?”

A national morning show invited him for a virtual interview. He wore his most somber sweater. The anchor, bright and polished, asked, “Laszlo, do you believe anyone will ever know who the real you is?”
He paused. “I think that’s up for grabs. Most days, I trust myself. Some days, I don’t.”
The anchor blinked—just enough to betray surprise. “Do you see an end to this?”
“No. I think we figure out how to swim. Maybe never touch ground.”

Thin Ice, Deep Water

Months passed. The campaign shifted: now, the fakes experimented with empathy. They released deepfaked apologies, Laszlo weeping on cue, expressing sorrow and new resolve. It blurred boundaries, making allies suspicious. A subreddit spun off to analyze his “micro-expressions,” arguing whether his left eye consistently twitched in guilt. Accusations blossomed with the weeds in cracked paving—the videos spread faster than the refutations.

Real-world consequences rippled. A kid was caught vandalizing the library and blamed “Laszlo’s new attitude.” A city councilor dropped Laszlo’s name in a rant about “unaccountable librarians.” At concerts, strangers alternately greeted him with warmth or sidelong glares. Even his coworkers stopped meeting his eye.

Stranded between digital invention and analog existence, Laszlo’s world shrank to late-night walks with Noelle, silent movies played on scratched disks, and an endless string of encrypted emails to international watchdogs and academic experts.

He learned about authenticity verification tools—watermarks, forensic audio analysis, distributed ledgers recording video origin. He tried everything. But the arms race kept pace: the fakes grew subtler, finding fresh fissures to widen.

His psychologist coined a new term: “Existential erosion—when the ground beneath your lived experience feels mined by outside forces, constantly shifting.”
Laszlo, always reluctant with labels, conceded the accuracy.

The Tipping Point

The break came not with an online revelation, but a stumble back into community. Marian, the branch janitor—unfazed, pigeon-feeding matriarch—paused him one Tuesday, as he vacuumed the stacks.

“My nephew says you’re a villain on the Internet,” she said. Her eyes crinkled with skepticism. “But that can’t be right. You helped me fix my transit pass.”
He stammered, unsure if this was an ambush, but Marian smiled. “Boy, the world’s always had liars. Now they’ve just got faster shoes.”

Emboldened, Laszlo found himself seeing people not as vectors of suspicion, but as collaborators in a shared uncertainty. He leaned into his own voice—in the messages he left, the conversations he braved, the classes he taught. Colleagues started pulling him back into projects. The city newspaper ran a piece titled, “The Disinformation War Is Local: One Librarian’s Fight,” and—miracle of miracles—readers shared verification tips in the comments.

Noelle assembled a “Soup Club”—in-person gatherings for anyone feeling digitally adrift. They drank actual soup, swapped stories, and practiced spotting fakes. No one ever resolved who the realest version of Laszlo was, but laughter kicked at the numbness.

Truth Among the Noise

By spring, a fresh series of fakes appeared—this time, Laszlo advocating for wild, impossible things: dogs running for mayor, mandatory interpretive dance at city hall. The jokes caught on; memes spun off in dizzy numbers. People recognized the absurdity, started tagging the fakes as satire, the line between threat and parody crumbling.

Laszlo discovered his old defensiveness giving way to something lighter. He still checked his feeds and flagged impostors, but he began to answer questions with gentle honesty. When asked by a classroom of kids, “How do we know who’s telling the truth?” he replied,
“You learn how to ask questions. And you never stop asking.”

People forgot to fear him, in time. The outside world, always a step ahead, found fresher targets. Deepfakes only worked so long as audiences cared about the friction—tiring quickly, they sought new drama elsewhere.

Laszlo’s life remained changed. Trust was a negotiation, and identity a mosaic, constantly arranged anew at each meeting. He learned to document his days with a humility that made denying the forgeries easier for some, even though certainty was forever lost.

Noelle, champion of loyalty, summed it up as she passed him the soup bowl.
“You build your truth like scaffolding. Show up, show people you’re real. The rest?”
She shrugged. “Might not matter soon. But soup’s hot and the company’s good.”

In the end, Laszlo accepted what he’d once feared: in a world where the image of a face could be forged and reputations rerouted with a click, truth took the shape of small, persistent acts—maternal advice, a shared lunch, the patient restoration of someone else’s coat lost in the returned books pile.

You live, you endure, and you continue. Not because proof was possible, but because the only real answer to the endless churn, the only defense against eroding certainty, was to remain visible in all your imperfection. To tell your story, and hope it landed—however briefly—on open ears.

Epilogue: A Different Diary

When he was alone, Laszlo kept a diary offline, ink on heavy paper. The entries were scattershot—lists of “what I’ve done, what I’ve been accused of, where the two do not match.” Sometimes, he’d flip pages to read the worn lines out loud, reminding himself of the difference between certainty and faith.

He could not stop the world from doubting. But each day, one true page: that was his resistance, quiet, razor-sharp.

In a world where truth itself could be made of static, the will to persist—to say, I am here, and this is my version—became both defiance and refuge.

And that, Laszlo thought, was enough.
