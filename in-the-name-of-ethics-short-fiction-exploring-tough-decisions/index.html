In the Name of Ethics: Short Fiction Exploring Tough Decisions


Mara Blake sat alone in the dimly lit conference room, the soft hum of the air conditioning the only noise filling the silence around her. Outside the tinted glass, the city buzzed unaware of the crisis unfolding inside the towering skyscraper of MedEvo Pharmaceuticals. Today, Mara wasn’t just a research scientist—she was a decision-maker at a crossroads where science, morality, and personal conviction collided.

Her finger traced the edge of a confidential report marked Top Secret — For Internal Review Only. Inside, it detailed a revolutionary drug, Serifen, meant to drastically reduce the progression of an aggressive neurodegenerative disease. The clinical trials so far were promising. Patients had shown remarkable improvements. Yet buried within the data were side effects some would call catastrophic: in roughly 10% of test subjects, Serifen caused unexpected cardiac arrhythmias, occasionally fatal.

Morally, her job as a scientist was clear—protect human life and further medical knowledge. But ethically, it was a labyrinth. If Serifen was released, thousands could be saved or given a quality of life they otherwise wouldn’t have had. But to do so meant knowingly exposing some to potential death.

She glanced around. The board members’ faces, projected on a video call, each showed a mixture of skepticism, concern, and greed. Investors pushed hard for approval. The public wanted hope. The media smelled a breakthrough, ready to praise or vilify.

Mara exhaled deeply.

The Genesis of the Dilemma

Six months prior, during the earliest Phase 3 trials, Serifen had appeared miraculous. Patients with deteriorating motor functions regained movement; cognitive decline slowed; families dared to dream of futures not written off. Mara had poured years into the molecular pathways of this drug, using gene therapy to target specific neuron receptors.

But when the first fatal cardiac event occurred, the trial halted. Detailed data reviews revealed a pattern linked to a secondary metabolic reaction in a small portion of patients, often linked to an unnoticed genetic polymorphism.

The question quickly transformed from medical to ethical: Should the drug be shelved indefinitely, potentially postponing a lifesaving therapy for the many, or be advanced with stringent monitoring, risking the lives of some?

Meeting after meeting, the company’s executives leaned into financial forecasts and shareholder returns. Regulatory consultants suggested manipulating data interpretations to favor faster approval. Media liaisons prepared statements designed to emphasize hope over risk.

Mara felt herself pulled in directions none aligned with her core value: truth.

Haunted by the Faces

That evening, Mara walked through the hospital ward where recent trial patients stayed. In bed 12 was Mrs. Eleanor Briggs, an 82-year-old former gardener, who had returned movement to her fingers for the first time in years.

“Will it be safe, Dr. Blake?” Eleanor asked softly, gripping Mara’s hand. “I want to dance at my granddaughter’s wedding next spring.”

At table 7, a young man named Luis, 48, whose Parkinson’s had rapidly worsened, had shown dramatic improvement but had begun experiencing irregular heartbeats—something the team monitored anxiously.

Mara’s heart ached. Each patient represented the stakes of the upcoming decision.

The Pressure Mounts

The board meeting scheduled for the next morning felt like a trial in itself. Mara knew her presentation could shape fate.

She had three choices:

  1. Recommend immediate approval, with full transparency about the risks and strict patient screening.
  2. Advocate for continued trials, postponing approval until safer protocols or alternative molecules were developed.
  3. Resign publicly, refusing to endorse what she personally saw as reckless.

Each held consequences. Approval might save thousands but cost some lives. Delay might protect risk groups but doom many patients to worsening disease. Resignation might bring public scrutiny but leave the company vulnerable to profit-driven decisions without scientific oversight.

Her phone buzzed. A text: “Remember the impact. Ethics isn’t just black and white. — J.”

It was from Jonah, her mentor, a bioethicist who taught her the delicate balance between idealism and pragmatism.

A Moment of Clarity

In bed that night, Mara reflected on Jonah’s words while drafting her speech. She thought about the Hippocratic oath—the commitment to “do no harm” — a principle often challenged by the realities of medicine. Sometimes, harm might be an unavoidable consequence of pursuing good.

Were those consequences acceptable? Who decides?

She decided her responsibility was to advocate rigorously for transparency, patient autonomy, and ongoing data collection. The world deserved to make an informed choice, not blind trust or coerced hope.

She would:

  • Present the full clinical data honestly.
  • Recommend a phased national release with mandatory genetic screening.
  • Propose an independent ethics board to monitor adverse effects publicly.
  • Demand patient consent forms explicitly acknowledging risks.

Her approach balanced scientific progress with ethical caution.

The Boardroom Decision

The next day unfolded tensely. Mara stepped to the virtual podium, face calm but voice steady.

“Ladies and gentlemen, Serifen has the potential to redefine treatment for neurodegenerative disease. However, it carries inherent risks that cannot be minimized or ignored. Withholding it from all patients delays hope; releasing it without safeguards endangers lives. I propose a carefully controlled release, coupled with genetic testing to identify risk factors. This respects patient autonomy and maximizes benefit while minimizing harm. I urge the board to choose courage tempered by responsibility.”

There was silence, then murmurs, some approval, some challenge.

The CEO leaned forward. “Are you confident this will satisfy regulatory agencies? Investors?”

“It won’t please everyone,” Mara replied. “But it preserves our integrity and, ultimately, our patients’ trust.”

After heated debate, the board voted narrowly in favor of Mara’s recommendations.

Epilogue: The Weight of Ethics

Months later, the controlled release began. Patients were screened meticulously. Side effects tracked exhaustively. A public forum convened regularly, allowing patients, doctors, and ethicists to voice concerns and celebrate successes.

Mara often visited trial sites, watched cautiously as science and humanity intertwined. Not all outcomes were perfect, but the approach fostered a collaborative environment where difficult truths were faced openly.

One evening, Mara stood in the hospital garden. Eleanor appeared, cane in hand, brighter than before.

“Thank you for fighting for us,” Eleanor whispered.

Mara smiled. “Thank you for reminding me why ethics must guide every decision, even when the path is hardest.”

In the name of ethics, tough decisions are never easy, but they shape the future worth living.
