The Trolley Problem: A Philosophical Examination of Ethics in Crisis


It was late in the evening, and the air was thick with mist. A soft breeze rustled through the leaves of the trees lining the tracks. An old trolley hummed along the railways, its wheels screeching slightly as it made its way through the winding, desolate countryside. Onboard, there was only one figure—a solitary man sitting by the window, gazing out into the obscurity.

His name was Professor Marcus Thorne, a man renowned for his philosophical acumen. Over the years, his lectures had captivated many students, each one walking away with a sense of wonder about the nature of moral choices. The trolley problem, a well-known thought experiment in ethics, was something he had studied for decades. But for the first time, Marcus found himself face-to-face with the dilemma, no longer confined to the ivory tower of academic discourse.

The night had taken an unexpected turn. After attending a conference in a far-off city, Marcus had caught the last train back home. The trolley was the only one running at this hour, and he was the only passenger. As the train sped through the desolate landscape, the sudden jolt of the trolley breaking through the stillness sent him tumbling from his seat. His mind, trained in the art of logic and reasoning, scrambled to make sense of the situation.

Suddenly, an alarm rang out from the intercom above. A voice, calm and steady, echoed through the empty car.

“Attention, passenger. This trolley is about to enter a critical juncture. You are currently in the position of control. Please listen carefully. Ahead, the track splits into two directions. A situation has arisen that requires immediate decision-making. Your actions will determine the fate of five individuals. Please be prepared to make a choice.”

Marcus’s heart skipped a beat. He had heard of such hypothetical scenarios before—the philosophical exercise known as the trolley problem. But never had it seemed so real, so immediate. His thoughts raced, analyzing the problem that had been posed to him.

A loud click sounded, and the intercom voice continued.

“On one track, five people are tied to the rails. They are unable to move and will be unable to escape in time. On the other track, there is a single individual. You have the ability to divert the trolley by pulling a lever. The question you now face: Do you pull the lever, sacrificing one life to save five? Or do you refrain from action, allowing the trolley to continue on its course, resulting in the death of five?”

Marcus swallowed, his mind clouded with conflicting thoughts. The theoretical trolley problem, which he had discussed with students for years, was nothing like this. Here, in the real world, the consequences were tangible, the stakes impossibly high. His breath quickened. Time seemed to slow, and his hands trembled as he grasped the lever beside him.

He could feel the weight of the choice pressing on his chest, suffocating him. The fundamental question was simple—what was the right thing to do? The classic formulation of the problem had always been straightforward in the classroom. Should one act in a way that maximized overall good? Or should one respect individual rights, no matter the consequences?

As he stared at the two tracks, Marcus’ mind began to formulate answers from all his years of study.

In one scenario, pulling the lever would save five lives. This was the utilitarian approach: the greatest good for the greatest number. But the cost of saving those five lives came with the blood of the single individual. That person would die as a result of Marcus’s action. The ethical justification for this choice relied on the premise that the overall good—saving more lives—outweighed the harm caused to the one.

But Marcus was no stranger to the criticisms of this approach. What of the moral cost of causing someone’s death? What of the violation of that individual’s rights? Could a decision made for the sake of efficiency or utility ever truly justify the harm it caused to an innocent? This question gnawed at Marcus as he considered the alternative.

The other track offered a different possibility: to do nothing, to refrain from action, and let the trolley continue on its current path. In this case, five people would die, but Marcus would bear no direct responsibility for the outcome. The trolley would kill them by its own momentum, and his inaction would be the cause, in a sense, of the tragedy. Yet, this option allowed the moral integrity of non-interference. It would be a violation of the principle that one should never cause harm directly.

The intercom crackled again, breaking Marcus out of his reverie.

“Please make your decision. You must choose now.”

Marcus’s gaze shifted from one track to the other. The faces of the five people tied to the first track were invisible, but he could feel their presence—unknown individuals whose lives hung in the balance. The one person on the second track, equally faceless, equally unknown, would also be lost if he chose to act. His fingers hovered over the lever, the weight of his academic training pushing him toward one answer, while his deep-seated empathy led him in another direction.

What is the right thing to do?

The trolley problem had always been an abstract question—an opportunity for students to practice ethical reasoning. But now, faced with this impossible choice, Marcus found that his philosophical framework faltered under the crushing reality of human lives. He thought of his wife, his children—would they forgive him for the decision? How could anyone possibly forgive him for pulling the lever, knowing that a single death would be his doing?

He could hear the whirring sound of the trolley’s wheels as it approached the split. The seconds felt like hours. The trolley was now so close that he could almost feel the heat from its engine, the inevitable crash that would come no matter what.

Marcus took a deep breath. He understood the theoretical arguments for both sides. He knew that, as a philosopher, he could reason his way into a justification for almost any course of action. But there was a deeper truth that gnawed at him now—the human cost of his decision. In the classroom, one could argue endlessly about the merits of utilitarianism versus deontology, but in the heat of the moment, Marcus knew that no argument would undo the reality of the lives lost.

The trolley continued to rumble closer.

And then, without a word, he pulled the lever.

The trolley lurched and shifted onto the second track, and the sound of the trolley’s wheels changed as it veered away from the five individuals. Marcus felt a brief sense of relief. It was over. He had acted. He had made the choice.

But as the trolley continued on its course, his heart sank. He could not see the results of his decision, could not know whether he had truly saved anyone or if his choice had been the wrong one. His mind raced with what-ifs and if-onlys. Was there an alternative he had overlooked? Was there a way to avoid the suffering entirely?

The train slowed to a stop. For a long moment, Marcus simply sat, his mind heavy with the weight of what had just happened. He closed his eyes, trying to clear his thoughts. As the minutes passed, the intercom crackled again, its voice calm but unyielding.

“Decision complete. You have successfully altered the trolley’s course. The five individuals on the first track have been spared. The single individual on the second track has perished.”

Marcus felt his heart sink even further. He had chosen the utilitarian path, the one that prioritized the greatest good for the greatest number. But the reality of his decision felt nothing like the clean, rational outcomes of philosophical theory. There was no solace in the fact that he had saved five lives if it meant taking one. No neat moral framework could explain the pain of that choice.

He sat in the silence of the empty trolley, haunted by the faces of those whose lives he had touched with his actions. In his mind, they were no longer abstract concepts. They were real people—just as real as anyone he had ever known. And their deaths, though abstract in theory, were now forever part of his reality.

The trolley began to move again, this time heading toward its final destination. Marcus sat motionless, feeling the weight of the moral consequences of his actions pressing down on him with every passing mile. There was no escaping the knowledge that, in the end, there were no perfect answers, no clear solutions. There was only the human experience of making decisions, of living with the consequences, and of knowing that no matter what choice one made, there would always be another track ahead, with its own ethical dilemmas, its own painful truths.

In the end, the trolley stopped. The lights flickered off. And Marcus knew that the question of morality was never about finding the perfect answer—it was about living with the questions, and learning from the lives we touch along the way.
