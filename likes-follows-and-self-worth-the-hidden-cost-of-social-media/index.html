Likes, Follows, and Self-Worth: The Hidden Cost of Social Media


Mallory didn’t see the darkness of her room anymore; it had faded into the velvet blue of her phone screen. Each night played out the same orchestration of digital flickers: the soft clack of her nails as she scrolled, the quick heartbeat as she watched the numbers change. Hearts. Comments. Shares. At twenty-three, her life was measured not in memories but in reactions, and Mallory was very close to the currency she craved.

Six years earlier, she’d chanced into her first viral post: a blurry shot of her in thrift-store colors, beaming as her dog upended a picnic basket. She hadn’t tried to be clever—just honest. The notifications poured in, an invisible flood. It felt good, that first time; the affirmation of people laughing, marveling, liking. Social platforms—her little universe—felt like magic.

Now, the game had changed, and so had she.

It began with small choices. She’d retake photographs to get the lighting exactly right, adjusting herself, the background, the dog, and the plate of food. She reframed posts twenty, maybe thirty times. Did those five extra filter swipes cross into the territory of ‘lying’? Did anyone else’s face really look like that, or did everyone wake up beautiful except her?

Mom would say she was fishing for attention. But it wasn’t attention she wanted—was it? She wanted to matter. And these platforms had provided rules that seemed clear and fair: if you’re good enough, clever enough, pretty enough, more people will care.

She started studying the patterns. What hashtags rose in the morning? Which celebrities’ posts to piggyback on? She learned the science: in the digital ecosystem, authenticity was praised, but curation was rewarded. Every day, people scrolled, double-tapped, followed, unfollowed, boosting those who made them feel something. If you hit the algorithm’s sweet spot, your content soared upward, battered forward by love or by envy. Either way, the numbers grew.

And Mallory grew with them—at least, her online self did.

The Echo Chamber

Her real world began shrinking as her feeds expanded. Texts from friends—“Coffee this week? Come over?"—sat unopened, then disappeared like fallen leaves. Hanging out in person made her nervous: her friends saw her unfiltered, unscripted, liable to say something awkward or blank out entirely. She liked her avatar better; it was glossier, neater, more charming than she could ever manage at brunch.

She met new people, too. Fellow “creators.” At first, they cheered each other on—liking, commenting, offering support messages deep in the DM maze. But soon the lines blurred. Who was genuinely happy for whom, and who was checking their own stats after every exchanged emoji? Mallory found herself measuring every interaction: What did they want from her? How did they help her numbers?

When her audience spiked, an email arrived, as predictable as a sunrise.

“We’d love to collaborate—our brand is all about empowerment and bold colors!”

For the first few campaigns, she felt a spike of pride. Later, she felt only the algorithmic hum of calculation. Was she diluting her brand by shilling for hair vitamins? If she posted about mental health, was she being “real,” or just capitalizing on a trend?

Sometimes, Mallory wondered what would happen if she stopped posting. Would anyone reach out, or did her worth exist only inside red bubbles and notifications?

Behind the Curtain

She couldn’t remember when the sleep first abandoned her. Maybe around the time she started waking up to check overnight engagement, daylight peering around the blue glow. A missed notification felt like missing an opportunity—someone else would trend while she rested. Her followers wanted new things every morning, and the cycle spun faster.

Even when she had nothing to say, she’d scroll through other people’s profiles. Everyone seemed busier, prettier, more successful. Sometimes, she zoomed in on their photos, looking for evidence of the facade—awkward edits, reused backgrounds. Instead, she found herself scrutinizing her own face in their photos: Do my eyes really look like that? Is my hair always so flat?

Comments praised her for her “openness,” her “inspiration.” Yet their words felt two-dimensional, unable to fill the hollow in her chest that grew a little deeper after every scroll. A particularly harsh reply—“Fake”—burned longer than any heart emoji.

One night she asked herself, aloud in the empty room, “Am I real if nobody sees me?”

The Unraveling

She tried to take a weekend off. Her phone slipped beneath the pile of T-shirts. Instead of freedom, she found withdrawal: heart thumping, mind whirring over what she was missing. Ghosts of notifications flickered in her imagination, each one a reminder that the world might move on without her.

On Sunday, when she finally looked, she’d lost seventy-two followers.

The numbers swelled and dipped, validating and punishing her simultaneously.

DoorDash bags multiplied in her trash. Her world condensed into a triangle: bed, bathroom, ring light. Her plants withered. Sunlight reached her only through cracks in the blind when she moved to film a video.

Sleep became an afterthought. Food lost its flavor. She kept her camera and phone between her and the world—generating content in real time, desperate for affirmation that she was not fading into irrelevance. Sometimes, she accidentally caught herself on her phone’s front-facing camera and didn’t recognize her own exhausted face.

The people closest to her noticed the change before she did. Friends who’d been relegated to group chats made attempts: voice messages, cards in the mail, “let’s talk.” For a while, she let them orbit around her digital sun, not wanting the messiness of real conversations.

One day, her mother called, not to talk about posts but to ask about her sleep and her meals. Mallory bristled, then softened. “I feel like I don’t know who I am if I’m not showing other people,” she admitted, voice thin with the confession.

Her mother, puzzled but loving, said, “You are still you, honey, even if the phone is off.”

Mallory wasn’t so sure.

Searching for Silence

The unraveling wasn’t dramatic. It was the slow fading of a song she could no longer remember. Posts felt forced; captions, empty; smiles, rehearsed. She wondered about the version of herself before she became obsessed with curation and validation.

Mallory started therapy, though not out of epiphany, but exhaustion. There, she pieced together the reality: her digital self had become a mask she wore so long, she’d forgotten the texture of her own face.

She practiced taking walks without her phone—awkward at first, liberating when she noticed leaf shadows dancing across concrete. She deleted comment threads instead of engaging trolls. She messaged a friend and met her at a coffee shop; they talked, and when Mallory fumbled a joke, her friend laughed, and it didn’t matter that it couldn’t be edited.

Piece by piece, she began reclaiming herself from the marketplace. Some nights, she posted anyway, but with honesty, not calculation. Sometimes that honesty brought in smaller numbers, and sometimes it brought an outpouring of gratitude from others searching for the same escape.

She learned to measure self-worth in ways that couldn’t be quantified: the warmth of being seen in person, the relief of not scripting every moment, the ease of living without witnesses.

Moving On

Mallory still lived online—her job, her circle, her humor all belonged to the world of sharing and posting. But she allowed imperfection, and sometimes even silence, to return. She watched her numbers rise and fall with the tides, no longer driven by the relentless need for more.

She found new communities that cared about connection, not just engagement. She started sharing behind-the-scenes snippets: the failed attempts, the tired evenings, the times when she didn’t know what to say. Followers trickled in, not by thousands, but enough. Friendships deepened. The algorithm, forever a spinning slot machine, faded into the background.

Offline, she tended her plants, called her mother, spent long afternoons at the park just observing, breathing, being. She learned what it felt like to belong to herself, independent of a browser tab.

Epilogue

Mallory never found a balance that lasted forever. Like anyone in the modern world, her identity would always be part public, part private—a collage of images and in-betweens. But she kept searching for meaning that didn’t depend on likes or follows, holding space for the unshareable truths and small, offline joys that made her real.

And each day, the roar of validation outside grew quieter, until only her own voice remained, clear and true, guiding her forward.
