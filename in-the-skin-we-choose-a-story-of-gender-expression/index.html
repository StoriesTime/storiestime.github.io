In the Skin We Choose: A Story of Gender Expression


It was an ordinary afternoon, the kind where the sun hung low in the sky, casting a soft golden hue over the town. Yet, for Eliana, this was no ordinary moment. She sat in front of her bedroom mirror, a place that had been both a refuge and a battlefield. The reflection staring back at her was one she had known for years, yet never truly recognized.

Her hand hovered over the drawer where her favorite clothing lay—a collection of brightly colored shirts, oversized sweaters, and skirts with playful patterns. She loved the way they made her feel free, yet constrained. She had worn them since she was a young girl, but as she grew older, the comfort of these clothes felt increasingly out of place. There was an itch beneath her skin, a nagging feeling that the fabric wasn’t aligning with the person she was becoming. The gendered expectations that had always been placed upon her felt more like a cage, and she was ready to break free.

Eliana had always been a “girl” in the eyes of the world, but the term had never fully encapsulated who she was. The concept of gender, which was often so rigidly defined for others, had always been fluid for her. She remembered when she was younger, trying to force herself into dresses and high heels because that was what society said girls wore. But as the years passed, she found herself sneaking into her brother’s closet, trying on his shirts and sneakers, feeling the freedom that came with those clothes. She felt strong in them, yet there was always the undercurrent of guilt, as if she was violating an invisible rule.

It wasn’t until she discovered the term “gender expression” that things started to click. Gender expression was how someone chose to present themselves to the world—it was an external manifestation of how one felt internally. The moment Eliana learned that this was something she had control over, a new world opened up to her. The idea that she didn’t have to conform to the traditional expectations of femininity was liberating. She could choose to express herself in ways that felt right, not based on society’s arbitrary rules.

But choosing to express herself differently was not without its challenges. There were moments when Eliana felt the weight of others’ expectations pressing down on her. Her friends and family were accustomed to the way she had always presented herself—feminine, poised, and delicate. Changing that, even in small ways, felt like an act of rebellion. The first time she wore a shirt with a more masculine cut, she could feel the eyes on her. Some of her friends complimented her, others remained silent, unsure of what to say. Her mother, always a beacon of kindness, asked her if she was feeling “okay” when she noticed the shift in Eliana’s wardrobe. It was a small comment, but it carried a heavy weight.

Eliana didn’t have an easy answer. Was she okay? Was she unwell? What did any of these terms mean when it came to her own sense of self? She didn’t feel unwell; she felt more herself than she ever had. But she also felt the pressure of living up to others’ ideas of what it meant to be a woman, even if those ideas didn’t fit her.

She spent countless hours grappling with these questions. She read books, watched documentaries, and spoke with others who had gone through similar journeys. She learned about non-binary people, those who existed outside the rigid categories of male and female. She learned that gender wasn’t a fixed point—it was a spectrum, a fluid and ever-changing experience. She read about people who embraced both masculine and feminine qualities, blending them into something uniquely their own.

But as much as this knowledge helped her understand the world, it also made her realize how much there was left to explore within herself. Gender expression wasn’t just about clothing or appearance; it was about how she felt in her own body, how she walked through the world, how she interacted with others. It was about feeling comfortable in her skin, no matter what shape or form that took.

One evening, as Eliana sat on her bed, surrounded by clothes that no longer seemed to fit her, she made a decision. She would no longer hide behind the label of “girl” or “woman” if it didn’t reflect who she truly was. She wasn’t going to settle for a gendered box that didn’t contain all of her complexities. Instead, she would dress, speak, and move through the world in the way that felt right for her—no labels, no expectations.

The next day, Eliana went to the store, not with a plan, but with an openness to whatever called to her. She picked out pieces that felt empowering—some were dresses, others were loose pants and graphic tees. She didn’t care whether they were labeled “feminine” or “masculine.” She cared only about how they made her feel. When she tried them on at home, she realized that the clothes didn’t define her. It wasn’t about whether she was wearing “women’s” or “men’s” clothing. It was about how those clothes allowed her to express herself authentically.

As she stood in front of the mirror once more, Eliana saw not a girl or a woman, but a person—a human being who was not bound by societal constraints or traditional gender roles. She felt both powerful and vulnerable, and that was okay. It was in this balance that she found her true expression.

Over the following months, Eliana continued to experiment with her style and presentation. She played with different looks, blending femininity with masculinity, softness with strength. She experimented with her hair, cutting it shorter one week and growing it out the next. She wore makeup when she felt like it, and skipped it when she didn’t. She allowed herself the freedom to explore without the burden of fitting into any one category.

But the most important change was in how she carried herself. She began to move with a sense of ease and confidence, no longer worried about how others saw her. Her expression of gender was hers alone to shape. She was still learning, still growing, but she felt a deep sense of peace in knowing that she didn’t have to fit into anyone’s mold. She was free to be her authentic self, and that was more than enough.

It wasn’t always easy. There were moments of doubt and discomfort, especially when others didn’t understand or when she felt the sting of judgment. But Eliana had learned that her expression of gender was not for anyone else—it was for her. She didn’t owe anyone an explanation, and she didn’t have to seek validation from the outside world. The skin she chose to live in, the skin that she crafted for herself, was enough.

As she walked through life, Eliana found that the more she embraced her true self, the more others did too. Some of her friends began to experiment with their own gender expressions, drawing inspiration from her courage. Her family, though initially hesitant, came to accept and support her in ways she never imagined. It was as if her own transformation had created a ripple effect, encouraging others to explore their own identities.

One day, as she sat in a café with a group of friends, someone mentioned a term Eliana had never heard before: “gender fluid.” It wasn’t a term she identified with, but it resonated deeply with her. Gender, like water, could flow, shift, and take different forms. It was not a static thing—it was something alive, something that could grow and evolve. She smiled to herself, realizing that her journey of gender expression was not a destination, but an ongoing process. It was about embracing the fluidity of her identity and allowing herself the grace to change, evolve, and redefine who she was at any given moment.

As she walked home that evening, the city streets buzzing with life around her, Eliana felt a quiet strength within her. She had finally come to understand that gender wasn’t something to be confined to a box—it was something to be experienced, explored, and celebrated. In the skin she chose, she found the freedom to be unapologetically herself.

And for the first time in her life, she knew exactly who she was.

The end.
