Under the Northern Lights: A Cultural Expedition in Iceland


The wind howled across the barren expanse, carrying with it the scent of salt and snow. Elísabet tightened the scarf around her neck and pulled her woolen hat lower over her ears. She had grown up hearing stories of these lands, the same ones that her great-grandparents had traveled, but this journey wasn’t for survival. It was for curiosity, for connection, for discovery. Every step she took carried her deeper into Iceland’s majestic landscapes, and closer to a heritage she only barely understood.

When her grandmother had told her, “You won’t find Iceland in books,” Elísabet had scoffed. But the old woman had been right. The stories, though vivid, lacked the biting chill of the country’s winter winds or the surreal silence that draped itself like a blanket over the valleys. To truly know Iceland, she would have to experience it. But even in her wildest imaginings, she hadn’t expected to be chasing the Northern Lights through the veil of a dark Arctic sky.

Her journey had officially begun two days prior, when she had landed in Keflavík Airport. The air was crisp and electric, carrying the promise of something more than the world she’d left behind. Reykjavík, though small as far as capital cities go, had pulsed with life when she arrived. Bright murals adorned the sides of centuries-old buildings, and the smell of cinnamon pastries mingled with that of smoked fish and brine. After checking into a modest guesthouse tucked down a quiet side street, she had wandered the city’s winding paths, peering into artisan workshops, sampling skyr with wild blueberry jam, and marveling at the looming Hallgrímskirkja church that stretched towards the heavens.

The locals had been impossibly warm. A shopkeeper had told her about the Yule Lads—troll-like figures from Icelandic folklore—and how their mischievous antics still kept the country’s children on their toes during the holidays. Another had explained the significance of the turf-roofed houses she would spot later in her travels, relics of a time when people built with what the Earth could provide. Yet, despite Reykjavík’s undeniable charm, Elísabet knew her true destination lay far beyond the city lights.

Her path led her east, over rugged lava fields blanketed in thick moss and past glacial rivers whose luminous blue waters seemed like something out of a dream. She drove along Route 1, the famous Ring Road that circled the entire island, her vehicle one of only a handful that punctured the raw wilderness. The sky above was a perpetually shifting canvas: one moment draped in melancholic grays, the next bursting with a kaleidoscope of pinks and golds as the sun kissed the horizon.

Each stop along her route unraveled another thread of Icelandic culture, weaving together a tapestry of history and myth. At Þingvellir, she stood in the shadow of the Alþingi, the ancient parliament site where chieftains once gathered to settle disputes and forge alliances. The tectonic plates of North America and Eurasia met beneath her feet, a silent reminder of the ever-shifting forces that shaped this land. Elísabet had crouched by the icy waters of the Silfra fissure, the thought of glacial purity mingling with awe as her fingers grazed the edges of the jagged rock.

At Geysir, she had waited in eager anticipation as bursts of boiling water rocketed skywards. Something about the rhythm of it—the earth building its pressure and then releasing in a powerful surge—mirrored her own feelings. Iceland felt alive, pulsating in time with her heartbeat. Surreal basalt cliffs at Reynisfjara Beach had left her breathless, as had the turquoise lagoon at Jökulsárlón where ancient icebergs drifted serenely towards the sea.

But her journey wasn’t just about sights. It was a story told as much by the people as it was by the land. A few nights earlier, she’d walked into a homely village hall where locals had gathered for an evening of storytelling and song. The air was thick with the aroma of traditional Icelandic lamb soup, and the lilting tunes of folk ballads filled her chest with a warmth that no fire could imitate. An elder recited tales of the huldufólk, the hidden folk believed to inhabit the rocks and cliffs, their presence shaping Icelandic customs in ways that no modern mindset could deny.

She learned that to the Icelanders, nature wasn’t something to be conquered or extracted—it was to be revered. Crossing a barren stretch of land required respect. Forgetting to acknowledge the spirits of a place, or the stories they carried, was akin to forgetting yourself.

On the third night of her expedition, the Northern Lights made their appearance. Elísabet had been waiting. Standing under the starry dome of the heavens in a secluded valley near Mývatn, she had felt the cold biting at her cheeks, but her anticipation overshadowed any discomfort. The night sky was a yawning abyss, vast and silent except for the occasional whistle of the wind. And then, deftly and without warning, it began.

A faint shimmer of green stretched across the heavens, as if someone had pulled a veil of light over the stars. It flickered for a moment, and then grew, evolving into dancing ribbons of green, pink, and purple. The aurora borealis seemed almost sentient, waltzing across the darkness, swirling and twisting in ways that defied comprehension. Elísabet’s breath caught; her hands trembled. No photograph, no anecdote, no poem could prepare her for the raw beauty of what she was seeing.

The villagers had told her the Northern Lights were more than just a natural phenomenon. They were a phenomenon woven into folklore and belief. Some said they were the souls of ancestors, celebrating in the afterlife. Others said they were bridges to the gods, a fleeting glimpse of something greater than themselves. As Elísabet stood transfixed, she wondered if the truth transcended the words altogether.

Her return to Reykjavík marked the end of her expedition, but also the beginning of her understanding. She stopped one last time by Harpa Concert Hall, its glittering glass façade reflecting the harbor lights. As she gazed out at the North Atlantic, she felt a shift within herself. Iceland had unraveled something in her—perhaps it was a connection to her lineage, buried deep and forgotten, or perhaps it was something broader, a rekindled appreciation for the interconnectedness of Earth and humanity.

The Northern Lights had danced their way into her life, leaving an indelible mark. They had whispered to her of mysteries she couldn’t fully comprehend, of stories as timeless as the glaciers and as fleeting as the auroras. As Elísabet turned towards the airport to return home, she carried these lessons and stories with her, knowing that Iceland’s spirit had left its own aurora etched into her being.
