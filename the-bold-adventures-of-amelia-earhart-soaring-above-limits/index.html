The Bold Adventures of Amelia Earhart: Soaring Above Limits


Amelia Mary Earhart was born on July 24, 1897, in Atchison, Kansas, where the American heartland meets the vast plains. Her father, Samuel, was a lawyer, and her mother, Amy, encouraged the interests and ambitions of their only child. Growing up amidst the wind-swept prairies of the Midwest instilled in Amelia an affinity for the open skies and a pioneering spirit. Amelia's life was one of adventure from the outset. As a child, she loved listening to stories of the pioneers who had crossed the Great Plains on horseback, braving harsh weather and endless distance. Her admiration for the Dauntless females who had overcome odds and achieved the impossible would forever shape her path. She attended Ogontz School for Girls in Pennsylvania and later moved to Hyde Park, Chicago, where she took a job as a social worker and enrolled in the Spokane Flying Service. The First Flight On January 3, 1921, Amelia embarked on her first flying lesson in a Kinner Airster, a rickety old biplane with a foot-operated gas pedal. Curtis Pitts, her instructor, taught her the ropes in a dusty field in Los Angeles. Amelia's first foray into aviation lasted about 10 minutes and twenty-five loops over the mechanic hangar as she activated the throttle too hard, giving everyone involved a scare. This experience strengthened Amelia's resolve; the thrill of flying had gripped her, and she vowed to keep pushing the boundaries of what seemed possible. The Cross-Country Flight In 1928, Charles Lindbergh's historic solo flight across the Atlantic caught the imagination of the American public. Intrigued by the possibility of crossing the world's largest ocean, Amelia joined the Soroptimist Foundation of Great Britain's campaign, aimed at creating a non-profit organization dedicated to the education and communication skills of women. Her travels in the skies turned out to be merely the beginning of a lifelong quest to explore the wide-open blue. While preparing to fly her Lockheed Vega all-metal monoplane across the Atlantic, Amelia's assessment revealed immense risks, from unpredictable weather to inadequate support systems. But when several World Airlines partners were able to help launch a specially designed trans-Atlantic service, Amelia skywrote her skepticism, filing suit for a more efficient plane capable of punching through isolation, climate failures, and the pressured bearers that beset such unparalleled travelers.
