Through Their Eyes: Understanding Racism and Empathy


In the small town of Maplewood, towering oaks lined the streets in a canopy of green, and white picket fences adorned the neat homes. This quaint town had long prided itself on its acceptance, its charm laced with the unspoken truth that the comfort it provided some was a shield against the struggles of others. For Olivia, a high school senior with dreams of becoming a journalist, this dichotomy became painfully clear when she decided to undertake a project that challenged her perspective—an exploration of racism through the eyes of her classmates. A Project Born of Curiosity As part of her senior project, Olivia's teacher, Mr. Harris, encouraged students to choose topics that resonated with them. With a rich cultural backdrop and a variety of personal experiences among her peers, Olivia felt drawn to an issue she had long perceived as distant. She was enveloped in her thoughts when she saw Amir, a classmate from her history class, sitting alone at lunch. With skin the shade of caramel and eyes that sparkled with humor, Amir was beloved by his friends but often faced insidious comments that went unnoticed by most. "Why don’t you ask Amir about his experiences?" Mr. Harris suggested one afternoon. The thought made Olivia nervous. She had never crossed that invisible line that divided 'friend' from 'acquaintance' when it came to deep conversations. But the seed had been planted in her mind. The Conversation Begins Days passed as she pondered the best way to approach Amir, and on a sun-kissed Tuesday afternoon, Olivia found him gazing out a classroom window. Summoning her courage, she approached him. "Hey, Amir. Can we talk for a minute? I want to ask you something for my project." He turned towards her, curiosity in his eyes. "Sure, what's up?" With a racing heart, Olivia broached the topic. "What’s your experience been like living in Maplewood as a person of color?" For a moment, Amir was silent, then he looked down at his hands. “You mean like… racism?” Olivia nodded, her stomach knotting in anticipation. "Well, it’s complicated," he started, “Most people think it’s just the obvious stuff—like slurs or people acting weird around you. But sometimes it’s the little things that hurt the most.” Unpacking Experiences As Amir shared his story, Olivia was transported into his world. He spoke of the time a group of boys had laughed at him in gym class because he was wearing his cultural attire for a presentation. He recounted stark moments where he found himself the only person of color in parties, feeling both visible and invisible at the same time. "I remember walking down Main Street and overhearing someone say that I didn’t belong here," he said quietly, his voice barely above a whisper. “It’s like being in a bubble where everyone can see you, but no one really wants to understand you.” With each word, Olivia felt a mixture of discomfort and empathy for Amir. It was as if she were peeling away layers of blissful ignorance she didn’t know she had. She realized that understanding racism was not merely about acknowledging blatant acts; it was about recognizing the insidious, everyday microaggressions that plagued individuals like Amir. The Ripple Effect As her discussion with Amir deepened, Olivia felt a commitment growing within her. She resolved to reach out to others in her school and document their stories. Her canvas was expanding, and so was her understanding. Over the next few weeks, she spoke to several classmates and community members, each narrative contributing to a tapestry woven with resilience, pain, and hope. Ella, a biracial student, recounted her struggles of identity. “I’ve faced questions about whether I’m ‘Black enough’ or ‘White enough.’ It’s exhausting trying to justify your existence,” she said with a sigh, her brown eyes welling with emotion. Julio, an immigrant from Peru, discussed the troubles of language barriers and cultural misunderstandings. “Sometimes people think they’re being funny, but they just don’t understand that their jokes hurt. They don’t see me, they see a stereotype.” Each story was a thread, knitting together a larger picture of the intricate experiences of racism in their community—a shared human experience that unified them through their differences. The Empathy Movement By the time Olivia compiled her research and interviews, she felt an overwhelming desire to present it in a way that would resonate with not just her classmates but the entire community. With help from Mr. Harris, she organized an empathy event at the school, inviting her peers to hear the stories firsthand, fostering a dialogue that could bridge gaps. On the day of the event, she stood nervously on stage, her heart throbbing with excitement and fear. “I’ve learned that racism isn’t just a battle fought on the grand stage; it’s a personal struggle that lives in our everyday interactions. Empathy is the key to understanding, to recognizing that we aren’t so different. When we listen, we begin to see through their eyes.” As she shared Amir’s story, Ella’s heart-wrenching struggle, and Julio’s brave journey, the audience’s silence was a testament to their collective realization. It was a moment where discomfort sparked change, igniting conversations that many had avoided but desperately needed. Changing Perspectives In the weeks that followed, an unexpected wave washed over Maplewood. Inspired by the event, students began forming clubs dedicated to fostering understanding and inclusion. Discussions about race and identity, once hushed or avoided, began to bloom into enlightening forums, literature nights, and multicultural fairs that celebrated diversity. Amir became a vocal advocate for awareness efforts in the school, speaking openly about his experiences. He often shared, “It’s about breaking the cycles. If we don’t talk about our experiences, how can we hope for anything to change?” Olivia continued to collect stories, each voice adding another dimension to the issues they faced. She realized that empathy, more than merely a fleeting sensation, was a conscious decision to understand and connect. The Role of Storytelling Months later, Olivia’s project was showcased at a regional conference, where representatives from various schools attended discussions on diversity and acceptance. As she stood before a crowd, recounting her journey through the stories of her peers, she believed she was fulfilling her calling as a journalist. “My journey started by asking a question, but it evolved into recognizing the gravity of listening. Through our stories—through our experiences—understanding emerges. We learn that empathy is not an innate trait but a skill developed through conversation.” Reflection and Growth As the years passed, the seeds planted during her senior project blossomed into a community more aware of itself. New initiatives cropped up aimed at not just tackling racism but fostering a culture of exploration and openness. Olivia found joy in following her peers’ journeys, realizing that the roots of her own empathy also grew deeply intertwined with theirs. On the cusp of her graduation, she stood on her porch, watching fireflies dance against the backdrop of dusk. With a heart full of gratitude and purpose, she understood that her journey to understand racism through the eyes of others had not just altered her perspective but had sown the beginnings of change in her community. “Understanding begins with a conversation,” she thought, “and empathy is the bridge that leads us to healing.” Olivia made a silent vow that night: she would continue to listen, amplify voices, and stand against the injustices that fragmented their humanity. In doing so, she knew she was not just documenting stories; she was actively participating in the narrative of change—a story that belonged to all, united through the profound lens of empathy.
