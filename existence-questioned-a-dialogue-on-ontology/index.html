Existence Questioned: A Dialogue on Ontology


The room was dimly lit, the soft hum of the fluorescent light in the ceiling creating a rhythmic buzz that filled the otherwise silent space. A long, wooden table stretched across the middle of the room, its surface worn and scarred by years of use. The air was thick with the weight of unspoken thoughts, the kind that linger like smoke after a fire. Seated across from each other were two figures, each with a different perspective on the nature of existence.

Jonathan, an old philosopher, had seen the world shift in ways he had once only dreamed of. His hair, now white and thinning, contrasted sharply with his sharp, penetrating gaze. His mind, a labyrinth of theories, had been forged in the fires of countless debates and books. He had spent his life trying to make sense of the abstract nature of being, but it seemed that with each answer, ten new questions arose.

Across from him sat Mara, a young student of philosophy, whose face bore the curiosity of someone just beginning to understand the vastness of the universe. She was eager, bright-eyed, and full of questions that had yet to find their answers. Her gaze was unclouded by the years of pondering that had shaped Jonathan’s perspective. She sought understanding, not just knowledge, and was driven by a thirst to uncover the truth of existence itself.

“Do you ever wonder,” Mara began, her voice soft yet filled with the weight of her inquiry, “what it all means? The world, life, everything. Are we just here, or is there something more to it?”

Jonathan leaned back in his chair, folding his arms. The question was not new to him, but it always had the ability to spark something deep within. He took a moment, his mind wandering through the corridors of his many thoughts before responding.

“I’ve spent a lifetime pondering such questions, Mara,” he said, his voice slow and deliberate. “In some ways, they’re the same questions that have been asked since the dawn of thought. What is existence? Why are we here? And more importantly, does it even matter?”

Mara nodded, her eyes wide with interest. “But that’s just it, isn’t it? If existence doesn’t matter, then why do we care? Why do we search for meaning? If nothing has inherent purpose, aren’t we left with nothing but the randomness of life?”

Jonathan’s gaze shifted to the window, his eyes tracing the lines of the trees outside, their branches swaying gently in the breeze. He took a long breath before replying.

“Perhaps,” he mused, “meaning is not something we find, but something we create. In the vast expanse of the universe, we may be but a fleeting moment, a speck in the grand scheme of things. Yet, within our limited lives, we seek to impose meaning, to create purpose where none may inherently exist. We build worlds of significance out of the raw materials of experience, thought, and emotion. And in doing so, we define what it means to be.”

Mara frowned, unsure of the direction the conversation was taking. “But that’s just it, isn’t it? We define meaning, but that doesn’t answer the fundamental question. Does existence have any intrinsic value? If everything is subjective, can we trust our understanding of it? Or are we just trapped in our own minds, endlessly questioning without ever reaching the truth?”

Jonathan smiled softly, the corners of his mouth lifting in an expression of fondness, as if he were speaking to a child trying to understand the complexity of a world much larger than themselves. “Ah, that is the great dilemma of ontology, isn’t it? The study of being, of existence. If everything is subjective, if our perception is the only reality we can truly know, then we are bound by the limits of our own minds. But perhaps, in accepting that limitation, we also embrace the possibility that meaning does exist, even if it is only real in the context we create for ourselves.”

Mara’s eyes narrowed as she processed his words. “But doesn’t that just make everything arbitrary? If meaning is subjective, then what stops us from claiming anything to be true? Doesn’t that lead to chaos, to the complete breakdown of understanding? Without a common foundation, how can we have any true knowledge at all?”

Jonathan’s gaze softened. “That is a valid concern, one that many philosophers have grappled with. But let me ask you this, Mara: do you believe that two people, despite seeing the world through different lenses, can ever agree on a shared truth?”

Mara hesitated, her fingers tapping lightly on the table. “I suppose… yes, they could. But that truth would be a compromise, not an absolute. It would be a fusion of perspectives, shaped by the limitations of each individual.”

Jonathan nodded slowly, his eyes thoughtful. “Precisely. Perhaps that’s all we can hope for—an understanding forged in the crucible of our collective experience. In a sense, truth becomes a dialogue, not a final answer, but a continuous process of questioning and refining. The quest for truth may never end, but it is through this quest that we come to understand the nature of our own existence.”

Mara leaned forward, her eyes alight with the fire of realization. “So, existence itself is not a fixed state, but something that evolves through our experiences and interpretations. It’s not just about discovering what is, but about shaping what could be?”

“Exactly,” Jonathan replied, his voice steady. “In the same way that a sculptor molds a block of marble into a form that reflects their vision, we, too, mold our understanding of existence. We shape it with each thought, each action, each experience. But that does not mean it is arbitrary. It means that existence is dynamic, fluid, and ever-changing.”

“But what about the external world?” Mara pressed. “If we are the ones shaping meaning, what of the things that exist outside of us? Are they any less real because we interpret them through our own subjective lens?”

Jonathan paused, his expression becoming more serious. “Ah, now we venture into the heart of the matter. The world beyond our perceptions—what is its relationship to our subjective understanding? Many philosophers, from Descartes to Kant, have wrestled with this question. Descartes famously said, ‘I think, therefore I am,’ suggesting that our own consciousness is the only thing we can be sure of. But does that mean the world outside of us has no objective reality?”

Mara’s brow furrowed as she thought about this. “If we can’t know the world as it truly is, how can we trust anything about it? How can we know that what we experience is not a mere illusion?”

“That is the crux of the problem,” Jonathan said softly. “We can never know with absolute certainty. But that does not mean we are trapped in a prison of doubt. What we experience is real to us, and that reality shapes the way we interact with the world. Even if our perceptions are flawed, they are the only means through which we engage with existence. And in that sense, existence is both a personal and shared experience—a convergence of individual realities into a collective understanding.”

Mara sat back in her chair, her mind swirling with the implications of their conversation. “So, existence is both subjective and objective at the same time. We shape meaning, but we also interact with a reality that exists independently of us.”

“Yes,” Jonathan said, his voice calm and measured. “It is a delicate balance, Mara. We are both creators and interpreters of meaning. And in that balance, we find the essence of what it means to exist.”

Mara was quiet for a long moment, the weight of the conversation settling in her mind. She had always been searching for answers, but now, she understood that the answers were not fixed, not static. They were questions in motion, ever-evolving, always open to new interpretations.

“I think I understand,” she said finally, her voice quiet but resolute. “Existence is not something we find; it’s something we create. And in the act of creation, we discover ourselves.”

Jonathan smiled, a deep, knowing smile. “Exactly. And that is the most profound discovery of all.”

The two sat in silence, the hum of the light above them now seeming almost harmonious with the quiet thoughts that filled the room. In that moment, Mara realized that the search for meaning was not a journey to find something that already existed, but a journey to create something new with each step.

And in that creation, she had found her answer.
