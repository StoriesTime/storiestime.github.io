Confronting Bias: Powerful Narratives on Racism


Marissa paused at the intersection, her brow furrowing as she scanned the faces around her. She couldn’t shake the unease brewing in the pit of her stomach. The tension of the past few weeks had left her hypersensitive to any signs of prejudice.

Marissa, a young black woman, had recently started a new job at a prestigious tech company in the heart of the city. The job had been a dream come true, one she’d worked tirelessly to achieve. But the first few weeks had been a whirlwind of microaggressions and continuous subtle racism. Her coworkers seemed oblivious to the impact of their words and actions, blithely forging on, leaving her reeling and disheartened.

“I can’t believe you made partner already,” Marissa’s friend Hadley had exclaimed when she shared her new job. “You’re going to be so successful!” It was a well-meaning compliment, but it belied deeper, insidious thoughts about who Marissa was perceived to be.

Marissa checked her phone, her boss’s email staring back at her: Reminder to be mindful of “cultural differences” as we move forward. It wasn’t the first time she’d received such a message. An implication that her presence and her achievements were somehow unfamiliar or unexpected.

Lost in thought, Marissa failed to notice the red light at first. As cars honked behind her, she jumped, startled, realizing that she wasn’t just lost in thought but lost in the moment. Did the honks reflect annoyance or something more sinister?

As she stepped off the curb to cross the street, Marissa recognized Mr. Johnson, an older gentleman with a kind face. He always smiled and offered a friendly greeting on his way to his bus stop. Marissa waved, thinking about the countless times he’d opened doors for her or held elevators with a warm, “Good morning!”

But this time, Mr. Johnson’s gaze lingered on Marissa, his smile fading slightly. She felt her heart quicken. Was he merely curious, or was there more to his stare? She couldn’t tell. Mr. Johnson had always been polite. Was his change in behavior a product of her own heightened emotions, or had there been a shift in how he saw her?

Marissa forced a smile, hoping to break the tension. “Good morning, Mr. Johnson. How are you?”

“I’m doing well, dear,” he said, his smile returning. “You seem lost in thought. Is everything alright?”

Taken aback by his perceptiveness, Marissa hesitated before answering. “I’m just trying to process some things.”

“Ah,” Mr. Johnson nodded, his smile turning sagacious. “Trust takes time to develop, especially in this world.”

His statement hit Marissa hard. She realized then that Mr. Johnson had his own experiences, his own battles with prejudice. The burden of their complexity weighed on them both.

“You’re right, Mr. Johnson,” Marissa sighed, “I’m still figuring out how to navigate it all.”

“Remember, there’s wisdom in patience,” he imparted, his gaze lingering on her face before finally drifting away. “Good luck on your journey.”

She watched Mr. Johnson board the bus, his figure disappearing beyond the window. Her mind was abuzz, analyzing their exchange and its implications as her boss’s email flitted back into her thoughts.

Confronting Bias, her boss’s email sujet, seemed to be more than just an empty platitude. It was a refrain Marissa was beginning to wholeheartedly embrace—she had to confront her own ignorance about Mr. Johnson and others, just as she had to confront her employer’s stereotypical assumptions.

Marissa’s eyes trace the patterns on the sidewalk before her, contemplating what, if anything, she’d revealed to Mr. Johnson about her own experiences. She understood the stakes now; it wasn’t just about her, it was about making the world a little kinder and a bit more equitable. Marissa felt a renewed resolve, but she knew the path wouldn’t be an easy one.

Taking a deep breath, she stepped off the curb and continued her commute, hope glimmering within. Murmuring under her breath, Marissa repeated wisdom in patience as she continued her journey, eyes firmly on the road ahead. She would confront the intersection of prejudice and power, one step at a time.
