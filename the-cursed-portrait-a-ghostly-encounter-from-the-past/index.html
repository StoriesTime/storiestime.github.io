The Cursed Portrait: A Ghostly Encounter from the Past


The first time Lydia saw the portrait in her aunt’s attic, a shiver snaked up her back even as the late afternoon sun baked heat into the dust-mottled air. She had come to inherit the sprawling, crumbling estate only days before, following her aunt’s passing. The house, a hulking relic tucked among willow groves at the edge of Bellemare, whispered with the noises of old things settling—or else, perhaps, not so settled. Lydia, city-born and city-honed, had scoffed at the idea that ghosts lingered where sunlight still fell, but when she pulled aside the faded tarp and gazed at the face staring back from centuries past, defiance curdled into something harder.

The woman’s eyes in the painting were pinched by sorrow, and her dark hair was knotted beneath a tattered lace bonnet. A garnet necklace glimmered at her throat, painted so vividly Lydia felt she could almost touch its facets. From the gilded frame, a name: Constance Morland, 1797.

Aunt Rosemary had rarely spoken about family history. Lydia had never heard of Constance. But as she leaned closer, lantern in hand, the silence of the attic thickened. The air seemed charged—a resonance, like the lingering note of a struck bell. Lydia reached out. Her fingertips hovered barely an inch from the canvas.

At once, the attic temperature plummeted. The portrait’s eyes seemed to focus, their painted irises shining with feline precision. Lydia recoiled, blinking. Was it a trick of fading paint, a stray sunbeam? She hunched her shoulders and scolded herself for flinching. Yet the unsettling sensation clung as evening crept further over the windows.

She carried the portrait from the attic that night, intent on cleaning it—a practical move, or so she reasoned. She planned to sell most of her aunt’s antiques before returning to New York; surely, an eighteenth-century portrait could fetch a fair price.

She set the portrait in the parlor, its frame dwarfed by the ornate marble mantel. The woman’s face loomed, half in shadow as twilight seeped through stained glass. Lydia fetched a damp cloth and began to tidy the frame, humming faintly to dispel the hush. As she worked, her reflection shivered and fractured in the gilt, replaced by a fleeting flash of red—a devotion to garnet that echoed the painted jewel.

Her phone buzzed. She answered, glad of the trivial distraction. It was Samuel Weller, her attorney, checking in on the estate inventory. Lydia wound the conversation briskly, promising to sign some forms by the week’s end. When she glanced back, for the briefest heartbeat, she thought she saw lips part on the painted woman’s face.

Night brought restless sleep.

She dreamed she was back in the attic, where dust and shadow merged. She heard the scuffle of fabric on floorboards. Constance Morland stood in the tense gloom, raising one hand—pale, delicate, trembling with urgency. “You see me,” the woman whispered, each syllable a conspiracy. A hissing wind pressed against Lydia’s ears. In the dream, Constance’s garnet necklace split and tumbled to the attic floor, beads scattering like drops of blood. Lydia tried to speak, but no sound emerged.

She jerked awake to silence, her body rigid beneath moth-eaten linens. The parlor clock ticked downstairs. Lydia lay staring up at the fissured ceiling, heart staccato—a brittle, jumping rhythm.

Morning light did little to clear the malaise. Over coffee, Lydia found herself repeatedly glancing at the portrait. The expression seemed changed: the mouth pinched as if stifling a cry, the garnet necklace almost pulsing crimson. Determined not to be rattled, Lydia opened her laptop. She typed in “Constance Morland Bellemare history.”

The digital rabbit hole yawned open. Records from 1798 mentioned a Constance Morland—young widow, rumored to be mad. Newspapers yellowed by time spun gossip about a disappearance: Constance vanished one October night, her jewelry gone with her. The house, inherited by her estranged brother, was plagued ever after by tragic luck. There were reports, Lydia noted uneasily, of children waking from nightmares, claiming to see “a lady in red stones” lingering by their beds.

Lydia rolled her chair back, eyes fixed on the painted woman. “Did you die here?” she muttered, feeling faintly foolish.

That night, as Lydia tidied up after a meager dinner, a strange noise cut through the house: the chime of glass, faint yet unmistakable. She followed the sound, its echo guiding her to the parlor. She paused on the threshold.

The portrait’s garnet necklace—so bright, so arresting—was gone. In its place, on the rug below the painting, lay a heap of rough red beads, scattered in chaotic arcs.

Her fingers trembled as she knelt, palm hovering over the necklace. The beads felt warm, almost alive, as they slid across her skin. Lydia counted them—seven, the number whispered in her dream. Cold crept deep into her bones. She snatched up the beads, hurried to a small box, and locked them away in a desk drawer. The portrait remained impassive, but an uncanny hush flooded the parlor.

Sleep, once again, proved elusive.

The next afternoon, the house seemed more alive than before. Ceiling timbers groaned; doors shuddered softly in invisible wind. In her aunt’s old office, Lydia pored over receipts and inventories, but concentration slipped away. She found herself returning to the drawer, fingers brushing the box’s smooth lid. Each time, her mind wandered: What had happened to Constance? Why did everyone believe she was mad? Had she, perhaps, seen too much?

Lydia resolved to ask for help. She called Bellemare’s Historical Society and requested an appointment. The following day brought Mrs. Vera Kitt, gray-haired and spry, arms loaded with folders. Vera’s eyes brightened at the mention of Constance Morland.

“Tragic figure,” the historian mused, flipping delicately through records. “Disappeared at twenty-six. Some say her ghost lingers for her missing gems.”

Lydia recounted her own experiences—the vivid dreams, the beads on the floor. She showed Vera the painting and then the garnet necklace, boxed and glimmering in the window light.

Vera’s demeanor shifted, weathered face pinched with disquiet. “I’d be careful, dear. Artefacts tied to injustice tend to… persist.”

When Vera left, Lydia felt no comfort. The beads weighed heavier now, red as accusation. That evening, as dusk buried the house, she lit a fire in the parlor and sat facing the portrait.

“I want to help,” she told the silent figure, feeling ridiculous and yet earnest. “What do you want?”

An hour passed, and another. Flames danced low, shadows pooling in corners. The silence thickened, until Lydia almost thought she’d heard a voice: “Return… what was taken…”

An idea formed. She took the garnet beads up to the attic, where she’d first found the portrait. There, in the musty dim, she uncovered a crumbling jewelry case among other forgotten relics. Lid open, velvet bitten to shreds by age, she placed the garnets inside. She set the case on the battered trunk beneath the window, heart thudding with uncertainty.

The air beneath the eaves shimmered. Lydia watched, rigid. The attic grew cold, colder than she’d ever felt. The beams groaned under ancient weight. At the edge of her vision, movement flickered; not shadow, not light, but a suggestion of presence.

A woman’s figure. Constance—brittle, beautiful, gaunt with sorrow. Garbed in lace, she bent over the jewelry case, fingers trailing over the garnets. Her eyes—those eyes from the portrait—met Lydia’s. A sadness deep as night pooled there.

“Thank you,” murmured Constance, her voice honeyed with grief.

Wind rose, rattling the annular windows. The vision wavered and waned. The scent of crushed violets filled Lydia’s nose—a note of perfume, echoing from centuries past. Then Constance was gone, the attic lighter, emptier somehow, as if a weight centuries deep had finally been lifted.

Lydia closed the box. She sat by the dormer window, staring out at the wavering willow branches. For the first time since her aunt’s death, she didn’t feel alone.

A week passed; the house felt different. Lighter, unburdened. Lydia finished cataloguing the antiques—she kept the portrait, now rendered gentle by peace, and hung it above the mantel. The garnet necklace remained in the attic box.

That autumn, when she left Bellemare, Lydia thought often of Constance Morland, the woman who had been called mad for seeing things others dismissed. Sometimes, alone in her city apartment, Lydia woke from dreams laced with red and the scent of violets, the echo of gratitude lingering in her ear. There was no fear in the dreams—only the hush of secrets shared, the promise of ghosts at rest.

In the end, the portrait was more than a relic; it was a story waiting to be heard. Lydia, nonetheless skeptical, never sold it. Instead, she let the painting watch over her—a silent witness, and a reluctant companion from a haunted but finally peaceful past.
