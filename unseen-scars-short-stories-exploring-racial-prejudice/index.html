Unseen Scars: Short Stories Exploring Racial Prejudice


The sun dipped low over Willow Street, casting long shadows that mingled with the faded red brick of the old neighborhood. For years, it was a place where stories were born—some joyous, some painful, and some that clawed deep beneath the skin, unseen yet undeniably real.

Maya’s Morning

Maya adjusted her camera strap as she threaded through the morning crowd heading to work. Each clack of her heels against the pavement echoed louder today, not because of the early rush, but because of the tight knot in her stomach. Weeks ago, she had applied for a promotion at the local newspaper. She was one of the few people of color in the newsroom, often finding herself the sola voce for stories about the city’s black community.

“Hey, Maya,” called Marcus from across the street. “Got a minute?”

They talked about the latest assignment, but Maya’s mind drifted to last Thursday. She remembered the subtle glance when she walked into the meeting, the pause before she spoke, the slightly condescending tone when the editor asked if she understood the “complexities” of the story on urban crime.

She wondered if the promotion was ever going to come. Or if her skin color was the unspoken barrier, like a ghost that neither shielded nor wounded—but left scars nevertheless.

Silent Testimonies

Inside the otherwise bustling community center, Elder Joseph sat quietly by the window, his hands folded. Around him, younger faces gathered, listening intently as he recounted stories from decades past. Not the big headlines or victories—but the small moments that cut deeply.

“Back in the day,” he began slowly, “it wasn’t always about the laws on the books but the laws in people’s hearts. I remember walking home from work when a group of kids spat at me. Doesn’t matter what I said or did, the spit landed with a purpose.”

He paused, eyes misty but voice firm. “You learn to carry that pain with you. It doesn’t disappear. It stays hidden—the unseen scars.”

Among the listeners was Aisha, a college student studying sociology, who scribbled notes furiously. For her, these stories weren’t just history; they were blueprints to understanding the present—the cycles of prejudice, bias, and oftentimes, the quiet resilience.

Crossroads at the Diner

It was the kind of afternoon where the heat seemed to melt the edges of everything, but the old diner on Main Street remained a sanctuary of familiar clinks and murmured conversations.

Jamal sat at a booth, flipping through a dog-eared book. The bell above the door jingled, and a man in a suit entered. The two locked eyes.

“Can I help you, sir?” Jamal’s tone was cautious but polite.

“I’m here for the interview,” the man said, extending a hand. Jamal glanced at the resume, realizing the man was the new HR manager.

The conversation started smoothly enough but quickly faltered when Jamal mentioned his previous job experiences in predominantly black neighborhoods.

“You know,” the manager said carefully, “some people think those areas are… problematic. Do you think you’d be able to adjust to our corporate environment?”

Jamal forced a smile, swallowed the sting, and replied with practiced grace, “I believe diversity enriches any environment.”

As the man left, Jamal looked out the window, wondering how many others carried such memories— moments when doubt and bias shook their confidence, like invisible bruises they wore alone.

Threads of Understanding

At the city library, a workshop on racial bias was underway. Lila, a teacher and advocate, facilitated conversations between individuals from all walks of life.

“Racial prejudice is often a collection of unspoken fears and assumptions,” Lila said passionately. “But it’s also about recognizing that these assumptions cause real, lasting harm. Not just physical, but emotional—scars that shape identities quietly.”

Participants shared their stories. A white man admitted his ignorance had once led to unintended hurt. A Latina woman described subtle exclusions at work. They listened to one another, sometimes uncomfortable but willing.

The room buzzed as walls dissolved, replaced by a fragile bridge—conversations brave enough to confront the past and hopeful enough to imagine an equitable future.

Echoes of the Past, Hopes for Tomorrow

That night, Maya returned to her apartment, her mind swirling with the day’s encounters and memories. She realized the unseen scars were invisible only to those unwilling to look. They existed in the hesitation of a handshake, the avoidance in a glance, and the quiet strength of those who carried them.

She sat at her desk, began typing a new article—not just a news story, but a mosaic of voices, pain, resilience, and hope. It was a tribute to the unseen, the unspoken, and the undeniable truth: racial prejudice leaves marks that do not fade with time, but awareness and empathy could be the balm for healing.

Outside, the stars blinked awake, indifferent and eternal—witnessing the fragile, fierce beauty of humanity, scarred but unbroken.

The stories behind the faces on Willow Street remind us that racial prejudice is not always loud. Often, it is a whisper that lingers in everyday interactions, an invisible weight borne silently. By sharing these stories, listening deeply, and embracing the uncomfortable truths, society moves one step closer to healing and understanding.
