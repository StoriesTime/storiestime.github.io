Ghosted: Navigating the Unspoken Rules of Online Relationships


Evelyn poured another cup of coffee, settling into the worn armchair by the window. Rain streaked the glass in uneven lines, distorting the shapes of cars and pedestrians making their way down the street below. The weather matched her mood perfectly—gray, cold, and heavy. On her phone screen was the last text thread she’d sent to Dylan, now a week old: “Hey, hope all is well. Are we still on for Friday?” Silence.

She’d been ghosted. Again.

The unfamiliarity of it had long since worn off; this wasn’t her first encounter with the disappearing act that had become far too common in online relationships. Evelyn had met Dylan on a dating app three months ago, their conversations quickly evolving from exchanging favorite movies to sharing childhood stories over late-night video calls. And then, just like that, he was gone. No explanation. No closure. Just silence.

But Evelyn wasn’t as passive as she had once been. She had beaten herself up enough over past ghosting incidents to finally recognize a painful truth: the online world played by completely new rules, and she wasn’t navigating it correctly. This time, she decided she’d unravel the tangled web of unspoken expectations and fragile commitments. Maybe it wouldn’t bring Dylan back, but it might bring her peace.

The Anatomy of Ghosting

Evelyn spent days researching online. Ghosting was everywhere. Articles, forums, podcasts—it seemed like hardly anyone was untouched by the phenomenon. It wasn’t reserved for romantic relationships either. friendships and even professional connections fell prey to this quiet betrayal. A person could vanish as if they were plucked out of existence, erasing every trace of themselves in a digital space.

The most glaring realization? People ghosted for reasons as diverse as the bonds they abandoned. For some, it was avoidance of confrontation—they didn’t know how to express their disinterest without hurting the other person. For others, it was weariness of the growing emotional demands in a connection they couldn’t sustain. There were even those who ghosted because of unforeseen life circumstances or emerging personal struggles.

Evelyn wasn’t exactly looking to give ghosters a free pass; she knew the sting of abandonment all too well. However, understanding the motivations behind ghosting gave her perspective. These weren’t purely villains lurking in online dating pools, but people grappling with emotions they couldn’t—or wouldn’t—communicate. It didn’t excuse Dylan’s sudden disappearance, but it softened the edges of her anger.

The Illusion of Connection

Online interactions often created an illusion of closeness. Evelyn thought back to Dylan’s texts—how effortlessly his words made her feel seen and understood. It wasn’t lost on her that digital communication let people bypass awkward silences, rants that ran too long, and any signs of discomfort that often filled in-person chats. Instead, texts and DMs could be polished into perfect snippets of connection, tailored to meet emotional expectations.

What Evelyn hadn’t realized was just how fragile these virtual bonds could be. Without the nuances of body language or tone to act as glue, people’s emotional investment often didn’t run as deep as in real-world relationships. She wasn’t the only one susceptible to the dopamine rush of late-night chats—these fleeting interactions rarely guaranteed commitment or accountability. That realization hit her hard, but it framed Dylan’s ghosting in a way that made sense. Maybe he was never fully invested to begin with. Maybe, to him, their connection wasn’t as real as it had been to her.

The Art of Moving On

Weeks passed, the initial heartbreak slipping into an ache Evelyn could manage. The ghosting no longer dominated her thoughts, but it still hovered like a faint shadow. The rain had given way to crisp autumn sunlight, and Evelyn finally felt ready to tackle the conversations she’d been avoiding—those with herself.

Late one night, curled in bed with a journal in hand, Evelyn scribbled furiously:

  1. What is my worth? Evelyn realized her self-worth couldn’t hinge on someone else’s texts or validation. The online world could be fickle, but her value wasn’t.

  2. What do I truly want from an online connection? She jotted goals—not expectations rooted in fantasy, but real, manageable desires. She wanted honesty, mutual investment, and the willingness to communicate—even in difficult moments.

  3. How will I respond if ghosting happens again? This question was important. Evelyn decided she wouldn’t sink into sadness for days on end. She’d allow herself time to grieve, but she’d also leverage her newfound understanding to move forward faster.

Rewriting the Rules

In the weeks that followed, Evelyn began to approach online relationships differently. She no longer jumped at the first sign of chemistry, nor did she ignore red flags in favor of potential. Instead, she made empathy a cornerstone of her interactions, discerning whether the people she connected with genuinely aligned with what she was looking for.

Her new approach brought both disappointment and hope. Some connections fizzled out in mere days, their initial spark fading after brief exchanges. But others, like Jordan—a writer she’d met on a forum about storytelling—felt substantial. They exchanged weekly emails about their creative projects, their words laced with curiosity and shared enthusiasm. Jordan wasn’t a romantic prospect, but their bond felt grounded in genuine effort.

Little by little, Evelyn learned how to balance openness with caution. She gave herself permission to invest emotionally where she felt safe, while detaching from situations that rang hollow. It wasn’t about building walls but about exercising discernment.

The Unwritten Code

Months later, Evelyn found herself at a coffee shop scrolling through her phone. She now approached online conversations with clarity, recognizing the subtle cues of growing distance—or sudden loss of interest. She understood that not every relationship was meant to last, but that didn’t mean the transient ones were meaningless. Each connection taught her something about herself, from her boundaries to her capacity for resilience.

When Dylan’s name popped up in her notifications one day—an out-of-the-blue message that read, “Hey, sorry for disappearing. Life got really overwhelming for me…"—Evelyn didn’t feel the pang of sorrow she’d anticipated months ago. Instead, she smiled faintly, typing a response before setting her phone aside.

Her reply was simple: “I understand. Life gets overwhelming sometimes. I hope things are better for you now.” Whether Dylan wanted to rekindle the connection or simply tie up loose ends didn’t matter to Evelyn—she had long since outgrown seeking closure from others. She found it within herself.

As she walked out of the coffee shop into the bustling city streets, Evelyn realized something remarkable. The online world might be saturated with ghosting, miscommunication, and impermanence, but it wasn’t insurmountable. By honoring her own needs and embracing the reality of these transient connections, she could navigate relationships more gracefully—even when silence knocked on her door.

And, for the first time, the thought of Dylan didn’t sting. Evelyn knew her worth—and she refused to let ghosting define her story any longer.
