The Abstract Expressionists: Exploring Post-WWII American Art


Once upon a time, in the aftermath of World War II, the artistic world of Europe and America underwent a significant transformation. A new movement, known as Abstract Expressionism, emerged. This radical artistic concept primarily flourished in New York, as many European artists fled there seeking refuge from the devastation of the war.

The Abstract Expressionists were pioneers in post-WWII American art, evolving beyond the traditional artistic structures and embracing chaos, spontaneity, and individualism. With artists like Jackson Pollock, Willem de Kooning, Mark Rothko, and Barnett Newman at their forefront, the movement gained momentum and resonance, and the works produced garnered both praise and criticism.

In the dimly-lit studio on Manhattan’s Lower East Side, Jackson Pollock stood before his vast, messy canvas. Splatters of paint and long lines rendered in a frenzied, impatient manner characterized his latest creation. He had heard of a new type of art that blurred boundaries and defied traditional categories, and he felt compelled to explore this new horizon.

“I am through with sculpting,” he confided in his friend, Willem de Kooning, who had recently shown Pollock a version of his “Abstract Expressionist” sculpture. “I need something more…” Pollock trailed off, searching for the right word. “More daring,” he concluded, and it was this element of danger that intrigued Pollock about Abstract Expressionism.

As the two amigos ventured into the realm of abstract painting, their works began debunking the notion of the traditional canvas. By abandoning representational art and incorporating fragments of the real world, they breathed life into something universally human. The Abstract Expressionists sought to convey the human condition in its complex, multifaceted essence, and no Picasso or Matisse had achieved this more accurately.

Mark Rothko and Barnett Newman, both armchair philosophers, took a slightly different approach in comparison to Pollock and de Kooning. While Pollock and de Kooning shared glimpses of the cosmos on huge canvases that draped the viewer in raw emotion, Rothko and Newman adopted a more introverted stance. Their profound, color-blocked works appeared to symbolize cosmic humanity, the innate yearning that fascinates humans.

Rothko’s color veils inspired meditation, leading one to reflect upon the significance of existence amidst cold, indifferent cosmos. On the other hand, Newman’s “STARS” and " onDestroy" painted black canvases with enigmatic words, evoking sentiments beyond the mundane.

Critics scoffed at the “drip painting” of Pollock and the large, enigmatic paintings of Rothko and Newman. They labeled it a passing fad — meaningless chaos — and ridiculed its defiance of the conventional aesthetic experience. Yet, as the months rolled by, these critics found themselves perplexed by the growing popularity of Abstract Expressionism.

As more and more people streamed into the galleries to gaze upon Pollock’s splattered creations, Rothko’s contemplative block paintings, and Newman’s mysterious word-collapses, the critics eventually had to admit that a new era in art was dawning.

The Abstract Expressionists had opened the door to a new world. They sought to express the metaphysical and psychological realities of human existence through art — a specific perspective explored through a broad range of forms: meditative color-fields; frenetic, raw-edged abstractions; and enigmatic, deeply personal explorations of ontology. The world had never seen anything like it.

Truly, the post-WWII era in America bore witness to a singular transformation: the evolution of art.

As we pause and contemplate the artistic legacy of Pollock, de Kooning, Rothko, and Newman, let us not forget the truly impressive feat they pulled off: they blurred the distinct lines between reality and dreams, chaos and order, art world and outsiders. The Abstract Expressionists revolutionized the world of art, pushing the boundaries of expression, thought, and possibilities. Their compelling works continue to stir emotions, challenge consciousness, and rewrite the course of artistic history.
