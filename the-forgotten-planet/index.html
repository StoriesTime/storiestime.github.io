The Forgotten Planet


In the tangled afterthoughts of mapped cosmos, there lies a planet that never registered on galactic charts, a restless sphere slipping behind the veils of nebulae and the static chatter of dying stars. Astronomers, hungry for the next big discovery, swept their broad nets through the Milky Way, but none ever snagged even the whisper of this lost world—none, until the day Leira stumbled upon the drift.

She was a salvager, not an explorer, running a battered freighter named Truant Mercy through sectors no reputable ship would dare cross. The edges here sang with static storms and old silence, but Leira ran lean, and so she followed whispers of derelicts loitering in blind spots, drones half-devoured by radiation, ghost signals fluttering like moths. In these forgotten spaces, she thrived.

Her discovery began when the onboard AI, affectionately named Moss, ticked to life with a soft green pulse.
“Spectroscopic anomaly, vector C-117,” Moss said, voice as calm as dew.
Leira squinted at the readout, expecting the usual—dead beacon, cryptic signature, a whisper of salvage. Only this time, the spectra looped back on itself, echoing in ways that physics protested.

“Run it again,” she ordered, slapping the console.

Across the main viewscreen, the anomaly blossomed from darkness: a planet, shrouded in turbulent clouds, illuminated only by a star so wan it seemed to apologize for existing. The surface gleamed with punctures of metallic blue and rusty glacier scars, its axis canted obscenely, as if some unkind impulse had kicked it off kilter eons before.

She named it The Forgotten Planet, for it asserted itself shyly, as though trying not to demand notice.

The Descent

Setting a course, Leira felt the Mercy shudder—this was not the inertia of normal flight, nor the predictable hiccups of entering orbit. It was as though the planet itself objected to being visited, repelling her with waves of static that danced along the hull.

Yet, as she punched through the last layers of exosphere, Leira saw a patchwork of ruined structures at the terminator, dim lines betraying the existence of ancient cities. Years of junk-lit salvage gave her a nose for derelict civilizations. This planet, unnamed and abandoned, was a treasure trove drowned in oblivion.

A safe landing was out of the question; the best she could hope for was stable ground and a path back to orbit if things soured. Leira picked a flat plateau near the ruins, masked in permanent dusk.

Stepping onto the surface, she found herself pressed by a fatigue so heavy it was almost mournful. It was not just her suit’s readouts warring with the cold, but a deeper sense of desolation—the awareness of a world that nobody had wanted.

The air was tenuous, not quite toxic, faintly laced with ozone. All around the plateau, pale bushes rippled in erratic breezes. Beyond them, stone causeways disappeared into broken amphitheaters, where sculptures of metal and mineral leaned toward the black clouds above.

City of Echoes

The city’s gates, once towering, had collapsed inward, their spans eaten by centuries of frost and salt wind. Inside, shadows pooled in the colonnades. It was a metropolis built for a species long gone, perhaps never known, its architecture pushed to the limits of gravity and dreams—curving towers etched with spirals, plazas of carved obsidian, ancient mechanisms frozen mid-gesture.

Her headlamp flickered over murals, the pigment strangely vivid, telling stories she could not read. There were figures with elongated limbs, clustered around spinning orbs and patterned disks. In one mural, a multitude faced a blazing sky, arms upraised, while unseen winds seemed to tilt them off their feet.

Her every step echoed louder than expected, as if voices were waiting for the chance to return.

Leira wrestled a translator drone from her pack, sending it hovering through the nearest archway. It spun data back in real-time: symbols in gold and umber, worn almost smooth. Moss parsed the glyphs, offering hesitant translations.

“These record-keepers… feared a darkness,” Moss relayed. “They believed their planet would be hidden, on orders of their High Minds, until such time as the stars sang again.”

Leira scoffed, half in awe. “They tried to make their world vanish. Did it work?”

Moss hesitated. “For a time, perhaps. But something else happened. Not concealment, but… erasure.”

The Sculptures’ Song

Wandering through the bones of the city, Leira noticed that many statues bore chips and scars, but always at the head, the chest; as if someone had tried to erase memory itself. In the central square, a monument dominated: a ring of figures, all kneeling, gazing into the void where a central statue must have once stood.

She skirted the perimeter, catching a harmony—notes of a song, wind threading through hollow metal pipes concealed in the statues’ throats. It was dissonant, unsettling, the melody unfinished. She knelt, brushing frost from a plaque.

“To those who wait and those who hide;
To those who remember and those denied;
When the stars once more awake,
Let us greet the dawn together.”

She recorded everything. The salvager’s code: nothing is too small to be valuable.

The Archives and the Machine

West of the plaza, at the outskirts of the city, she found what had once been a library. It sprawled a kilometer on each side, domed with iridescent glass now crazed and shattered. Inside, shelves spiraled toward the ceiling, each cradling cylinders of crystal—data archives, written in optical pulses rather than ink.

Leira activated her sampling kit, sliding a cylinder into a reader. Blue static filled her visor; Moss labored.

“These are… personal logbooks, I think. Dreams, stories—some scientific records. The council debated the planet’s disappearance. Many believed hiding was salvation. Others feared oblivion per se.”

She played a record at random. A voice, nothing like human, intoned:

“They told us the hiding was for safety. But what is safety when we are alone, when the night falls and none come looking? Will anyone find us, when we are fog and figment? We built the Forgetting Machine—they say it will cloak us. But my dreams are full of silence. I fear that even memory cannot find us here.”

Leira’s heart thudded. She searched the library, following cables and frozen ducts to what had once been a control chamber, now rotten with the leavings of time—a console, fused and cracked, beneath a stained-glass map of the system.

She studied the map, fingers trailing broken circuits. Moss chimed, “There—see the axis? The engine they built to fold space around their world.”

“They tried to erase themselves from the star charts entirely,” she mused. “Not hide, not defend—forget.”

Pieces snapped together. The planet was not missing; it had been deliberately subtracted from galactic memory. And with that subtraction, something else had been lost.

What Remained

Outside, the day darkened as the feeble sun spun into eclipse. The statues’ song swelled, more insistent, and across the ruins, Leira felt the vibration as if the planet’s own bones were asking her for something.

She made her way back to the main plaza, relics clattering in her bag. She stopped before the empty heart of the monument.

Moss piped up: “I detect faint activations below. Residual power readings. A machine, deep under the surface, is still running. Minimal. Like a last heartbeat.”

She descended through tumbled stairways, guided by Moss’s whispers. The undercity was close and cold. Here, the architecture turned in on itself—pipes and circuits knotted as if to keep the secret safe.

At last, in the lowest vault, she found the core of the Forgetting Machine. It was a vast lens, fractured but still glowing faintly—projecting a field that twisted the space around it into impossible angles, bleeding memory from every passing data probe.

In the broken surface, she saw herself reflected—a lone scavenger, suit streaked with frost, ringed in sullen light. She understood, viscerally, that to preserve secrecy for so long, the cost had been everything: no rescue, no return, no even being missed.

Decision in the Silence

She sat on a battered crate, voice low. “Do I shut it off, Moss?”

“To do so would end the field. The planet would be seen again.”

By whom? she wondered. A galactic government greedy for rare metals? Other salvagers? Or scholars who could mourn the lost? The people here had vanished, populations martyred on the altar of safety.

Leira weighed her choices. Was it her right to reverse the decision of a vanished race? Or was it a necessary kindness to let them be rediscovered, even if only as memories?

At her feet, a panel flickered with a simple message in the old script—she translated it slowly:
“Look again. Please, look again.”

She placed her palm on the core’s surface and, with deliberate care, cut the last feedback loop. The machine dimmed. A slow groan, as of tectonic plates shifting, echoed through the undercity. Far above, the wind changed, the statues’ pipes now singing in major chords.

Outside, the sky seemed to brighten. Somewhere, she knew, ship sensors would ping with a new planet—astronomers would scramble, ships would launch.

Epilogue: The Awakening

As she launched Truant Mercy from the surface, the sensors cleared, no longer fouled by the planet’s field. Stars blinked cheerily. The forgotten world was now a newborn, a blank to be annotated by future explorers.

Leira recorded her logs and archived her data. Scholars would weep for the tragedy; perhaps someone would decipher those lost stories, mourn the statues, rebuild the city for virtual tours. The galaxy would know the world had once existed.

She thought about the song that drifted upward—haunting, hopeful. Maybe memory was heavier than hiding, but it was the only way to keep loss from devouring everything. She marked the coordinates in her own charts, for old times’ sake, and sent a beacon spinning across the void.

And somewhere, through the forgotten planet’s silent halls, the wind carried a melody no longer lost, its echoes layering hope on the bones of oblivion.

The Forgotten Planet spun on, no longer hidden, now adrift in the sharp gaze of discovery. What it had lost, it reclaimed, one story at a time.
