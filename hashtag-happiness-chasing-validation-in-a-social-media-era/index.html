Hashtag Happiness: Chasing Validation in a Social Media Era


It was a sunny morning when Lara sat in her favorite café, staring at her phone screen. She swirled her coffee absentmindedly, waiting for the little red notification badge on her Instagram app to change. It was the small things, like that single notification, that fueled her day. She didn’t need much—just a few more likes, a couple of comments, and maybe a repost. That was all it took for Lara to feel alive.

Her feed was filled with perfectly curated moments: a trip to Paris, a candid picture of her morning routine, an impromptu photoshoot with friends at sunset. Each image was meticulously crafted, polished, and, most importantly, it was designed for validation. Lara had learned long ago that happiness in the digital world didn’t come from genuine connection but from the illusion of it. Each like and comment was a temporary high, an affirmation that she existed in the way that mattered most—online.

But as she scrolled through her feed, Lara’s phone buzzed again. A notification. A like. A comment. But it wasn’t from who she had hoped. It wasn’t from James, her college friend who had always been her best support system. It wasn’t from Megan, her old roommate who always had the best emojis for her posts. This time, it was from a stranger. Someone who had liked a photo she had posted weeks ago, someone with no mutual friends. It should’ve felt flattering, but all Lara could think was, Why didn’t James like my post?

The thought gnawed at her. She refreshed her notifications, checking for something, anything that would fill the emptiness inside her. There was nothing new. Just a few scattered likes. She sighed, her chest tightening with a mix of frustration and longing. The feeling wasn’t new—she had felt this same emptiness more times than she cared to admit. But today, it felt particularly heavy.

Lara had always been a people person. Growing up in a small town, she was known for her friendly demeanor, her ability to talk to anyone, to make connections. But when she moved to the city for college, things changed. It wasn’t that the people here were any different, but the world around her was. Social media was the new stage for relationships, where the lines between authenticity and performance blurred. It didn’t matter if you were popular in real life if you didn’t have followers or likes to back it up.

At first, Lara had resisted it. She had resisted the filters, the staged selfies, the constant need to update her status. She longed for the days of face-to-face conversations, where the quality of relationships mattered more than the quantity. But as the months wore on, she couldn’t ignore it. She couldn’t escape the pressure to perform. Her feed was like a competition she hadn’t signed up for but felt compelled to join.

Soon, Lara started to curate her life for the ‘Gram. She bought new clothes, invested in a professional photographer for her travel photos, and spent hours editing every picture until it was perfect. It wasn’t enough to just live anymore. She had to prove it. And the proof came in the form of likes and shares.

But the validation never lasted. It was fleeting. After each post, the high would fade, and she would be left wondering if it was enough. Was it enough to make people see her as she wanted to be seen? Was it enough to prove her worth in a world that increasingly felt like it only acknowledged value when it was visible online?

That was when she started noticing something strange: the more she chased validation, the more isolated she became. At first, she didn’t recognize it. She still had friends, still had connections. But as time went on, the connections that felt real started to wither. Conversations became superficial, centered around likes and shares. Even her time with James and Megan felt different. Their conversations were often interrupted by someone’s post, a new trend, or an update on someone else’s life.

And that’s when it hit her. The world she had created online was a house of cards, fragile and insubstantial. It was all smoke and mirrors. No matter how many followers she gained or how many compliments she received, there was a deep well of loneliness she couldn’t escape.

One night, after an argument with Megan about an Instagram post that had gone wrong—too many filters, too much editing—Lara decided to take a break. She needed space, not just from Megan but from the digital world that had consumed her. She put her phone in the drawer, turned off her laptop, and for the first time in months, just sat in silence.

The quiet was uncomfortable at first. It felt like something was missing, like a part of her identity had been stripped away. But as the hours passed, she started to notice something. Without the constant need to check her phone, the world around her seemed clearer. The air felt fresher. The people around her—Megan, James, even strangers passing by—felt more present. She started listening in a way she hadn’t in ages.

The next morning, Lara went for a walk in the park, just to clear her head. She passed by a group of children playing on the swings, laughing and running without a care in the world. She smiled. It had been so long since she had felt that carefree, that alive without a screen in her hand.

She picked up a coffee at her favorite café again, but this time, she didn’t reach for her phone. Instead, she watched the barista prepare her drink, savoring the small interactions, the conversations with the people around her. For the first time in months, Lara didn’t feel like she was performing for anyone. She was simply living.

Over the next few weeks, Lara’s relationship with social media changed. She didn’t abandon it entirely, but she set boundaries. She stopped worrying about every like and comment. Instead, she shared photos of things that made her happy—real moments. She didn’t edit them, didn’t obsess over them. She simply shared her life, not for validation, but for herself.

She found herself reconnecting with James and Megan in a way she hadn’t in years. Their conversations were no longer about what they had posted, but about how they had felt, what had truly mattered. They laughed about old memories, about things that had nothing to do with the digital world. And for once, Lara felt like she was truly seen—not through a screen, but as she was.

The road to rediscovery wasn’t easy. There were still moments when Lara reached for her phone, when she craved that familiar rush of validation. But now, when she opened Instagram, it wasn’t to compare herself to others or to measure her worth. It was to share a moment, a feeling, a piece of herself with others who might find it meaningful.

Lara had learned a valuable lesson: validation doesn’t come from numbers, from likes, or from comments. It comes from being present, from being authentic, and from embracing the moments that truly matter—those that cannot be captured in a post or a hashtag.

In a world that often measured happiness in followers and likes, Lara had found a different kind of joy—a joy that came from the simple, fleeting moments of life that no filter could capture.

And for the first time in a long while, Lara felt truly happy, with no need for a hashtag to prove it.
