Wounds of Bias: Short Stories Revealing Racism's Impact


In a small neighborhood that nestled between the bustling city and the quiet outskirts, Evelyn Williams had always believed she had found her place. It was a simple life. She taught history at the local high school, lived in a small cottage, and spent her weekends tending to the garden she had grown from scratch. But nothing ever truly stays simple.

Evelyn had lived through the ups and downs of racial tension in her community. It had always been there, a shadow lurking in the background. But recently, it had come to the surface in ways that made it impossible to ignore.

One evening, as she was walking home from the local bookstore, a place she often visited to buy the latest historical biographies, she passed an alleyway she had always avoided. The streetlights flickered as the cool evening breeze carried the sound of distant chatter. As she reached the corner, she heard a voice call her name.

“Evelyn?”

She froze. The voice was familiar, but there was something off about it. Turning slowly, she saw an unfamiliar man stepping out from the shadows of the alley. He was tall, with dark hair and a worn jacket, but his eyes were sharp—too sharp for someone who had just appeared out of nowhere.

“Do I know you?” she asked, instinctively stepping back.

The man smiled, but the smile never reached his eyes. “You don’t, but I know you. I know what you’ve been doing.”

Evelyn’s heart skipped a beat. She had no idea what he was referring to. She had kept to herself, focused on her work, and kept her opinions about the recent surge of racial injustice in the city quiet, not wanting to stir the pot.

“I don’t know what you mean,” she said cautiously.

The man chuckled softly, as if he found her denial amusing. “Oh, I think you do. You’ve been teaching them—telling them they are just like you. Just as worthy, just as human. But you and I both know that’s not true, don’t we?”

Evelyn’s chest tightened. She had heard whispers before. The racism that had always been an undertone in the community had now become a steady drumbeat, and people were starting to lash out at anyone who dared to challenge it. But she had never thought she would be confronted so directly.

The man stepped closer. “You can’t change what people are, Evelyn. You can’t make them something they’re not. They’ll always be different from you.”

She tried to steady her breath, but the words burned through her, settling deep within her chest. He was speaking about the students in her classroom—the ones who came from different backgrounds, some of whom had fled conflict in distant lands, others who carried the weight of generations of oppression.

“No,” she said, her voice firm, “They are not different. They are people. People who deserve the same respect as anyone else.”

The man’s expression darkened. “You can say whatever you want, but you won’t be able to protect them from what’s coming. Just like you can’t protect yourself.”

Evelyn felt a chill crawl down her spine. She knew the man wasn’t just speaking metaphorically. There had been whispers of violence, of riots, of people rising up against the “outsiders.” The racial divide was no longer a quiet undercurrent; it was a raging storm.

Before she could respond, the man turned and disappeared back into the alley. Evelyn stood frozen for a moment, her mind racing, trying to piece together what had just happened. Her thoughts swirled with a mix of anger and fear. She had always known there were those who would never accept change, who would never see past their biases. But now, it seemed like the battle was no longer about ideas or words—it was about survival.

In the Mirror’s Reflection

Maya Johnson’s morning routine had always been predictable. She woke up to the sound of her alarm, made coffee, and went through her emails before heading out to the law firm where she worked as an associate attorney. It was a fast-paced job, one that required precision, focus, and the ability to navigate the often invisible biases that lingered in her workplace.

Maya had always known that the legal world was a tough place, but she had never quite anticipated the toll it would take on her spirit. As one of the few Black attorneys at the firm, she had been forced to prove herself over and over again. She had earned her spot, but the challenge was ongoing. It wasn’t just about being good at her job—it was about convincing everyone that she belonged.

One day, as she sat in a meeting with several partners, she noticed a subtle shift in the room when she spoke. Her suggestions were met with polite nods, but no real consideration. The room felt colder, more distant, as if her voice wasn’t really being heard. She had been in enough meetings to know when people were only half-listening.

After the meeting, one of the partners, Richard, came up to her. “You did well today, Maya,” he said with a forced smile, but there was something in his tone that felt off. “Just remember, it’s all about knowing your place, you know?”

Maya’s stomach tightened. “I’m not sure I understand.”

Richard’s smile faltered, but he quickly recovered. “It’s just that you have to be careful how you present yourself. Some people in this world aren’t as open-minded as you think. There are some who will never see you as their equal, no matter how hard you work.”

Maya’s mind raced. She had heard this before—this coded language that tried to mask the truth. What Richard was really saying was that she had to work harder, be better, and even then, there would always be those who would see her as an outsider. It wasn’t just about skill or competence; it was about a deep-seated prejudice that no amount of effort could erase.

She nodded, masking the hurt beneath a calm expression. “I’ll keep that in mind.”

But the words stuck with her throughout the day, gnawing at her like an open wound. The realization hit her hard—no matter how successful she became, no matter how many cases she won or accolades she received, there would always be someone, somewhere, who would reduce her to nothing more than the color of her skin.

That night, as she stared at her reflection in the mirror, she found herself questioning everything. The woman staring back at her was successful, strong, and confident, but she was also weary. Weary of having to fight for every inch of respect, weary of having to prove that she belonged.

The Invisible Walls

Jamal Turner had never thought of himself as a revolutionary. He had always kept to his own, preferring the comfort of his small circle of friends over the chaos of public protest. But after witnessing the treatment of a close friend during a routine traffic stop—a stop that ended in violence—he found himself questioning the very fabric of the society he had grown up in.

It had happened in a quiet neighborhood, one not too far from the heart of the city. His friend, Marcus, a well-spoken, well-dressed man, had been pulled over for no apparent reason. The officers were rude, dismissive, and aggressive. They accused Marcus of being “suspicious” without cause, and when Marcus asked for clarification, things escalated.

Jamal had watched in disbelief as his friend was thrown to the ground, his body slammed against the pavement, as the officers shouted commands that were never explained. His heart had raced as he stood by, unable to help, paralyzed by the harsh reality unfolding before him.

It was the first time Jamal had truly felt the weight of racial bias in such an intimate way. It wasn’t just an abstract concept or something he had read about in books. It was real, and it was happening to people he cared about. The invisible walls of prejudice had closed in around them, walls that no amount of success, education, or good behavior could ever tear down.

After the incident, Jamal couldn’t sleep. He couldn’t shake the image of Marcus’s face, the fear and confusion in his eyes. He knew he couldn’t stay silent anymore, couldn’t pretend that everything would be okay if he just ignored it. The system was broken, and it was up to people like him to expose the cracks.

Breaking the Chains

The wounds of bias were deep, and they stretched far beyond the personal stories of individuals. They were embedded in the very structures of society, in the policies, the attitudes, and the assumptions that shaped every interaction. Evelyn, Maya, and Jamal all understood this truth, each in their own way.

Evelyn had been called naive for believing in the power of education to change minds. But she had never wavered in her belief that teaching her students about the realities of history—about the struggles that had shaped the world they lived in—was the first step toward breaking the cycle of ignorance and hate.

Maya, despite the constant hurdles she faced in her career, refused to back down. She knew that her success was not just for herself—it was for the future of other young Black attorneys who would follow in her footsteps, who would one day walk into that same boardroom and be treated with the respect they deserved.

And Jamal, though he had once been content to stay in the background, had now found his voice. He was ready to fight, not with violence, but with the power of truth, the power of unity, and the power of a community that would no longer accept the status quo.

Together, they understood that the wounds of bias could only begin to heal when people were willing to face the truth, confront the uncomfortable, and work toward a future where no one had to fight for their humanity.

But the journey would not be easy. The chains of prejudice had been forged over centuries, and breaking them would take time. But they were no longer alone. Each step, no matter how small, was a step toward a world where the shadows of bias could no longer hold power.

And that was a fight worth joining.
