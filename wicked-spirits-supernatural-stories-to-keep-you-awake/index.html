Wicked Spirits: Supernatural Stories to Keep You Awake


The village of Thistledown had always been a place where the wind whispered secrets, and shadows lingered a little too long after dusk. It was the kind of town where children were warned never to stray near the Hollowed Woods when night fell and where the elders spoke in hushed tones about spirits that walked the earth, bound neither to time nor tide. But legends were stories told to coax little ones back inside, weren’t they? That’s what Marianne thought, until the night she discovered the truth.

The Whispering Fog

Marianne Rourke was a young journalist with an appetite for the unusual, and Thistledown’s air of mystery was just the spark she needed. She arrived late one autumn afternoon, her satchel heavy with notebooks and recording devices—her tools for uncovering stories most people were too afraid to tell. On her first night, a fog crept into the village, curling through the crooked lanes like fingers searching for something unseen.

Drawn by a strange pull, Marianne stepped out onto the cobblestone street. Her breath came out in small clouds as the thick mist wrapped around her. A peculiar whisper greeted her—a series of disjointed syllables slipping through the haze, like a voice half-remembered from another dream.

“Who’s there?” Marianne called, her voice snapping against the eerie silence.

No answer came, only more of the soft murmurs carried on the cold wind. It wasn’t the wind itself—it was words disguised beneath it, layered with meaning gleaned from voices long gone.

The House at the Edge of Hollowed Woods

Determined to find the source of the whispers, Marianne followed the drifting fog to the edge of the Hollowed Woods. There stood an old house—its windows like dark, watchful eyes. The locals called it “The Marrow House,” a place no one had lived in for decades but which was said to be haunted by “wicked spirits.”

Legend said the house belonged to the Marrow family, who vanished one winter without a trace. Since then, strange happenings had plagued the area—phantom lights flickered through the trees, mournful cries echoed just beyond hearing, and shadows moved against logic and light.

Marianne felt a chill climb her spine as she pushed the creaking door open. Inside, the air was thick with dust, but beneath it lay a lingering scent, bitter and metallic—like sorrow and old blood.

Bound by Grudge

As she explored, Marianne uncovered a diary tucked beneath rotting floorboards. Its pages were brittle but legible, written by Eliza Marrow, the youngest daughter.

“The spirits come at night. They do not want to leave. They are bound by grudge, by pain, by something darker than death itself,” Eliza had written.

The last entry was a scrawl of desperation:

“If anyone finds this, know the truth. The Marrow curse is not just in us, but in the land. Wickedness does not die with the flesh. It festers. Beware the spirit that wears a human face.”

Marianne shivered, suddenly aware of the heavy silence that fell like a weight in the room. Then, footsteps—soft but deliberate—echoed from upstairs.

The Spirit’s Truth

On the second floor, Marianne found the source: a translucent figure, an apparition with hollow eyes and a sorrowful gaze. The spirit spoke, its voice a brittle whisper:

“You seek answers, but not all are meant for the living. We are the cursed, trapped by betrayal. The wickedness was born here—love twisted into wrath. To break the bonds, the curse must be faced. But beware, for to see the truth is to invite darkness into your soul.”

Marianne listened, heart pounding. The spirit revealed a conflict—between the Marrows themselves and a vengeful outsider who had stolen their peace. That outsider’s hatred had bound the family and their descendants to a loop of torment.

A Ritual of Shadows

Determined to end the hauntings, Marianne researched the old rituals mentioned in obscure texts left in the village archives. She learned that only by acknowledging the pain, speaking aloud the names of the wronged, and inviting forgiveness could the spirits be released.

On a moonless night, with the villagers gathered hesitantly around the Marrow House, Marianne recited the names, the grievances, the sadness. As the last word left her lips, a gale swept through the trees, a stifled scream dissolved into a sigh, and the oppressive weight lifted.

Rest at Last?

Morning saw the fog dissipate and the air clear of sorrow. The villagers, cautious but hopeful, thanked Marianne for bringing peace to their home. The whispers ceased, the shadows retreated, and the Marrow House, once a symbol of dread, seemed to breathe easier.

Yet, in the fading dusk, when the wind shifted just right, a chill remained—a reminder that the border between the living and the spirits is a thin, fragile veil. Wicked spirits may rest, but stories like these keep the night alive, waiting to be told. And sometimes, if you listen closely, you can still hear the faintest whisper carrying on the wind.

Epilogue: A Cautionary Tale

Marianne stayed in Thistledown for several months after, collecting tales from locals. She published Wicked Spirits: Supernatural Stories to Keep You Awake, a collection of real hauntings and legends that refused to be forgotten.

Her favorite line came from an old woodsman:

“It’s not the spirits we mind, but the stories they leave behind. They keep us awake, not with fear, but with the truth that some shadows walk with us always.”

Would you dare to hear the whispers and look beyond the veil? The night is waiting.
