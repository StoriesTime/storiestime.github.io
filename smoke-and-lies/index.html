Smoke and Lies


In the heart of the city, amidst the towering buildings and neon lights, there was a small, inconspicuous café called The Ashen Ember. It was a place that seemed untouched by time, a sanctuary where the outside world’s chaos couldn’t reach. The café’s brick walls were often cloaked in a haze of thick smoke from the countless cigarettes people smoked there, lending an almost mystical atmosphere. It was in this very café that two lives would collide in a way neither could have ever imagined.

Lana sat by the window, staring out into the foggy night. The city streets were drenched in rain, and the occasional car passed by, its headlights slicing through the darkness like slow-moving specters. Lana was used to the quiet, the solitude of her small corner. She preferred it this way, away from the world, away from people. It wasn’t that she hated company; it was simply that she had never been able to trust anyone. People lied. And lies were like smoke—easy to inhale, but impossible to hold onto.

It had been a month since she’d left everything behind: her family, her job, her life. She had packed up her apartment in a rush and moved without telling anyone. No goodbyes, no explanations. She just disappeared, hoping that maybe, just maybe, she could find something more honest in the world.

The café was the one place she felt somewhat at ease. The barista, a middle-aged man with greying hair, knew her order by heart. A cappuccino, strong but with just enough milk to make it smooth. She came here every evening after the sun had set, sat in the same corner, and watched the rain.

As the hours stretched on, she leaned back in her chair, her fingers wrapped around the warm ceramic cup. The door to the café opened, letting in the chill from the night. Lana didn’t look up at first; she was used to the sound of the bell above the door and the occasional footsteps of customers entering. But then she heard the unmistakable sound of a soft cough, followed by a deep voice saying, “One black coffee, please.”

Her curiosity piqued, she glanced up.

The man standing at the counter wasn’t particularly tall, but there was something about his presence that made him stand out. His coat was long, dark, and wet from the rain, and his face was obscured by the shadow of a wide-brimmed hat. But it was his eyes that caught Lana’s attention. They were piercing, dark, and had an intensity that made her want to look away. For a moment, she felt a strange jolt of recognition, as though she knew him from somewhere, though she was sure she’d never seen him before.

He took his coffee and walked toward the seating area, and to her surprise, he chose the table right next to hers. Lana wasn’t the type to welcome interruptions, especially from strangers, but she couldn’t shake the feeling that she was somehow drawn to this man. Her instincts screamed at her to leave, to pack up and go, but she stayed.

The man sat down, his coat dripping water onto the floor. He didn’t acknowledge her at first, which suited her just fine. She returned her attention to the window, though she couldn’t stop glancing at him. Something about him unsettled her—maybe it was the way he sat, rigid and watchful, or the subtle tension in his posture, as if he were waiting for something.

A few minutes passed, and then, almost as if reading her thoughts, the man spoke.

“Are you running from something?” His voice was low, almost a whisper, as if he didn’t want to disturb the quiet around them.

Lana blinked, surprised. How could he possibly know? She didn’t answer immediately, unsure of how to respond.

“I’m not running,” she said, her voice steady but guarded. “I’m just… waiting.”

“Waiting?” he repeated, taking a slow sip of his coffee. “For what?”

The question hung in the air between them, and Lana found herself reluctant to answer. Who was this man? Why did it feel like he was peeling back the layers of her carefully constructed walls? But instead of backing away, she felt an unexpected compulsion to speak, to share something she’d been keeping buried for far too long.

“I’m waiting for the truth,” she said, her words more fragile than she intended.

The man’s eyes softened, though there was a glimmer of something else in them, something she couldn’t quite place. He leaned forward, the shadows under his eyes deepening, and for the first time, Lana noticed how worn and tired he seemed, as though he, too, had been searching for something.

“The truth,” he said quietly. “Is that what you’re after? The truth about…?”

She hesitated. Could she trust him with the things she had locked away? Could she share her story without it turning into a lie? Her fingers tightened around her cup, the warmth of it doing little to calm her.

“The truth about everything,” she said finally. “About why people lie, why they hide behind masks, why they think they can keep secrets. I used to believe in honesty, you know? But now… now I can’t tell the difference between what’s real and what’s not.”

The man’s lips curled into a wry smile. “You’re not the first to feel that way. It’s easy to get lost in a world where everyone is spinning their own version of the truth.”

He paused, as if weighing something in his mind, before adding, “Maybe the problem isn’t with the lies themselves, but with the people who keep telling them.”

Lana frowned. She wanted to argue, to tell him that lies were the problem. That it was the deceit that had ruined her life. But something about his words resonated with her, a small seed of doubt planting itself in her mind.

“I don’t understand,” she said, her voice barely above a whisper.

The man leaned back in his chair, crossing his arms. “Lies are like smoke,” he said, staring at the swirling patterns of steam rising from his coffee. “They’re insubstantial, but they linger. They spread and fill the air, clouding everything around them. But no matter how much you try to hold onto them, they slip away. They never last.”

Lana listened, intrigued. “So, what are you saying? That we should just accept the lies?”

“No,” he replied. “What I’m saying is that sometimes, you have to let the smoke clear before you can see what’s left behind.”

A silence fell between them, thick with unspoken words. Lana wasn’t sure what to make of his cryptic response. What was he trying to tell her? That the lies she’d been told weren’t worth holding onto? That maybe, just maybe, she had to let go of the past in order to move forward?

The man stood up suddenly, breaking the silence. He placed his empty coffee cup on the table and straightened his coat. “I don’t know if you’ll find what you’re looking for,” he said, his voice more serious now. “But sometimes, the truth isn’t what you expect. It’s not always the answer you want. It’s not even always something you can trust.”

With that, he turned and walked out of the café, leaving Lana sitting alone in the soft glow of the dim lights. She stared at the door for a long moment, her mind racing with his words. Was he right? Could it be that the truth wasn’t something she could find, but something she had to make peace with?

The door to The Ashen Ember swung open again, this time letting in the scent of fresh rain. Lana glanced up, expecting to see the man return, but he was gone. The rain continued to fall, and for the first time in weeks, Lana felt the weight of her doubts shift. She didn’t have all the answers. But maybe, just maybe, she could stop looking for them.

And perhaps, in time, she would learn to live with the smoke and the lies, knowing that the truth would eventually reveal itself, in its own time, in its own way.

But for now, she sat in the quiet, letting the warmth of her cappuccino settle in her hands, and watched the rain wash away the remnants of the night.
