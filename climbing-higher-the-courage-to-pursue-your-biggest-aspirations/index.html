Climbing Higher: The Courage to Pursue Your Biggest Aspirations


Vega stared up at the rust-red mountain, her backpack digging into her shoulders, the crisp dawn air nipping under her coat. She was the first to arrive at the base, as she always was—a running joke among the local climbers in Daly’s Outfitter downtown: Vega, the Over-Early Challenger.

The great sweep of granite before her was not just earth and stone. For seven years, she’d circled the mountain, training on its smaller ridges, clocking her best times, trialing new routes that stampeded like veins toward the snowy summit. None of it was the real challenge. That was reserved for today: the ascent of Stilling Peak’s west face, a sheer and uncharted climb that locals only ever pointed at and shook their heads, chuckling into their coffee cups.

Still, Vega was not chasing bravado. She came for the skin-prickling terror, the trembling in her calves, the undeniable wildness that came with confronting something so immense it swallowed her thoughts. She knew every scar and stripe along the path above—the one no one could guide her through but herself. When people asked why she wanted to do it, she could offer no tidy answer. Was it pride? A need to prove something to her late father who’d been a climber himself? Some… thing, though. The mountain called to her like a song only she could hear.

Her phone buzzed. Alex’s face flickered on the screen: “Ready for your sunrise selfie at the top yet?”

Vega smiled. Alex always knew how to take the edge off a harrowing morning. She snapped a photo of the looming wall above her, colored by golden light, and sent it back with a single line: “Race you to the clouds.”

She clipped the phone onto her harness. In her head, she rehearsed what she’d gone over so many nights in her apartment, with topo maps spread on the floor and thermos mugs bracketing the edges to keep the corners from curling. The pitch would begin at the narrow ledge by Fisherman’s Crack, then up into an unmarked chute of frozen water. If the conditions were good, the crampons would hold. If not… She didn’t allow herself to finish the thought.

The first dozen feet felt familiar—rope secure, feet braced, hands exploring the rock. The wind, once gentle, stiffened as she climbed, whispering through the carabiners at her waist. She pressed on. For every careful step upward, there was a cascade of memory—the patient years spent gathering skill, facing failure, the cost paid in scraped skin and humility.

Around her, the mountain’s vast silence folded in tight. Above, the world narrowed to the next handhold, the rhythm of breath and muscle. The courage to continue did not come in shouts, she realized. It crept in softly, surfacing in little decisions—where to dig toes, how to shift weight, when to trust the ice’s glassy surface.

She reached the halfway shelf and struggled out of her harness for a moment, sitting with her knees pulled to her chest and the world spinning far below. Until that moment, her motivation had seemed sturdy as iron; now the enormity pressed down, making her grip the ledge with numb hands. She gazed up, seeing the wild sprawl of blue and stone above.

What was she doing, really? Who was she competing against?

“Climbing higher,” her dad once told her when she was twelve and clinging to a boulder the size of a cow, refusing to go up or down, “isn’t about beating the mountain. It’s about seeing what’s up there, and figuring out what changes in you when you get there.”

She set her jaw. She pulled out a single bar of chocolate, broke it in two, and let the taste linger. A slow resolve—less lightning, more ember—warmed her core. She fastened her gloves, reset her harness, and continued.

The route grew more treacherous. The sun was now a pale coin, pressing through ragged clouds. Vega’s world shrank to the humming drone of focused breath, icy air slicing under her collarbone, each placement of hand and boot planned five seconds ahead.

Twice she nearly faltered. The first slip caught her heart in a fist; she slammed into the wall with a gasping laugh and a rush of adrenaline. The second was subtler: a moment where she questioned, just long enough, her own right to be here. These doubts were harder to brace against than vertical stone. They clung with cold fingers: What if you don’t make it? What if you are simply an invader on this silent rock, trespassing out of pride?

At a narrow chimney high above the treeline, she caught the far-off sound of voices. Two hikers on the rim below, their laughter chipping across the miles, unaware of the climber tracing her way up the mountain’s lonely flank. Vega felt a sudden kinship—not just with them, or with her father, but with anyone who had ever dragged themselves toward something vast, pleading with the world for some sign it was worth it.

On the final pitch, the rock turned crumbly, half-melted in the early summer thaw. She worked slowly, methodically, exploring every crack with the careful hands of someone who knows she could fall. A memory invaded; her father’s battered training journal, the note he’d scribbled: If you reach the point where you almost turn back, pay attention. You’re close to real discovery.

Her breathing sounded wet and sharp in the thin air. She was close—so close her fingers trembled.

One last push. One last desperate reach. She swung her body upward in a final burst, muscle and grit and the silent cheering of every failed attempt. There was a scrape, a loud curse, and then—level ground.

She was standing on the wind-razored summit.

Vega looked around, heart leaping. All at once, the struggle funnelled into a dizzying, exultant silent victory. The clouds spidered out below her feet, the world a tangle of pine ridges and snowfields, the horizon burnt gold and violet. She screamed—a sound that tore from her gut and was torn away by the wind, but it didn’t matter. It was enough that the mountain heard.

It dawned on her, in that wild, translucent joy, that what she’d wanted all these years wasn’t just a summit. It was the memory of the climb—the uncertain gears and pauses, the nights spent scrawling dreams on crumpled coffee receipts, the stubborn refusal to let go even when everything seemed stacked against her.

Up there, surrounded by air so thin she tasted iron on her tongue, Vega found her answer. She gazed back down at the valley where her journey had started, feeling not invincible, but wonderfully vulnerable. She’d carried her doubts and hope up every inch. She let them scatter on the icy wind.

A vibration in her pocket. She pulled out her phone, covered in scratches; Alex’s message blinked again. This time she took a picture: not expertly framed, but arms outstretched, hair a mess, grin splitting her face. “Made it,” she wrote. “Let’s climb higher next time.”

Hours later, as she rappelled slowly down, every muscle baked and exhausted, Vega understood something simple and profound: The courage to ascend wasn’t a magic bolt or a famous quote—it was the morning she woke up and decided to try, again and again, for no one but herself. It was having the nerve to meet the rock half-way, to listen for the whisper of intuition when every other instinct screamed retreat.

She reached the bottom, almost dazed by the effort. She looked back up at the silent peak, sunlight now burning bright behind its crest. Tomorrow, she knew, the mountain would still be there—its edges softened by mist, daring someone else to try their luck. And Vega would walk away, battered but smiling, already planning the next impossible climb.

The lesson never shouted; it moved in quiet footsteps: Any aspiration—if you let it—will teach you how to be afraid, and then how to carry that fear upward, until it turns into something unbreakable.

And somewhere, beyond frost and fatigue, something shifts. The world is never quite so daunting after you’ve learned to climb higher.
