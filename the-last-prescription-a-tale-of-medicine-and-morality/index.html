The Last Prescription: A Tale of Medicine and Morality


Dr. Lucas Hayward had seen a lot in his medical career. With over twenty years of experience, he had treated countless patients, faced the limits of science, and witnessed the subtle dance between life and death. Yet, nothing had prepared him for the weight of the last prescription he was about to write.

The sterile white walls of St. Mary’s Hospital were a comforting backdrop to his daily routine. The constant hum of machines, the soft footsteps of nurses, and the occasional distant beeping formed a rhythm that he had long become accustomed to. Today, however, everything felt different. It was as if an invisible cloud loomed over him, one he couldn’t shake no matter how hard he tried.

Lucas sat at his desk, scrolling through patient charts, his fingers idly tracing the edge of a pen. His thoughts were elsewhere—drifting to the patient he had seen earlier that morning: Miriam Hayes, a woman in her early thirties who had come in for what seemed to be a routine check-up.

But Miriam’s case was anything but routine. She had been diagnosed with terminal cancer, a rare and aggressive form that had spread throughout her body in mere months. The prognosis was grim. Lucas had explained to her that there were no viable treatments left—only palliative care to ease her suffering.

It was a conversation he had had countless times before, but something about Miriam’s reaction haunted him. She wasn’t angry, as many others had been. She wasn’t desperate or pleading for a miracle. She was calm, almost eerily so. Her eyes held a quiet resignation, and as she shook his hand in farewell, she whispered, “Dr. Hayward, if you could help me end this… with dignity… would you?”

Those words had stayed with him all day. Lucas had always believed in the sanctity of life, in the promise of healing and hope. But Miriam’s request forced him to confront something deeper—his own ethical boundaries.

As the day wore on, Lucas found himself pacing the halls of the hospital, wrestling with the decision that gnawed at him. It was a topic few doctors ever dared to discuss openly: euthanasia. Assisted suicide. The deliberate end of a life, not for medical reasons, but out of mercy. Was it wrong? Could he, in good conscience, help someone like Miriam, whose suffering seemed beyond what any drug could alleviate?

He had seen enough suffering in his career to know that there were times when medical intervention only prolonged the agony. But to actively end a life—was that truly the ethical path? Was he not sworn to save lives, not take them?

The more he thought about it, the more the lines blurred. Miriam was not asking for a quick fix or a miracle. She was asking for a release from a pain that no amount of morphine could numb. She had lived her life, and now, she wanted to die on her own terms. But was that within his power to grant?

In the quiet of his office, Lucas sat down and reviewed Miriam’s file once again. There were no more treatments left for her. No surgeries, no medications that could offer any hope. Her cancer had spread too far. She had been brave, facing each round of chemotherapy with an unwavering spirit, but now, there was only one thing left to offer her—peace.

Lucas’ fingers hovered over the keyboard, uncertain. He had always believed in the Hippocratic Oath, which called for the preservation of life. But where did the oath stand when the preservation of life only meant prolonging suffering?

With a heavy heart, Lucas began to type. His hands shook as he drafted the prescription, each word a weight on his conscience. He could feel the gravity of the decision pressing down on him.

The prescription was simple. It contained a powerful sedative, enough to put Miriam into a deep, painless sleep. The dosage was just enough to ensure that she would never wake up, slipping away in peace.

Lucas stared at the screen for a long time, his mind racing with doubts. He knew that once he handed Miriam this prescription, there would be no going back. It wasn’t just about the law, though that was certainly a factor. It wasn’t even about his career or reputation. It was about something much deeper—the ethical and moral question that had no clear answer.

Was he right to do this? Would it be an act of mercy, or would it make him no better than the very forces he had spent his career fighting against?

In the end, Lucas made his decision. He printed the prescription, signed it, and sealed it in an envelope. The weight of the paper in his hands felt like the weight of the world. He stood up and walked down the quiet hallway, his footsteps echoing in the empty corridor.

When he reached Miriam’s room, he found her sitting by the window, staring out at the setting sun. She turned to him with a soft smile, as though she had been expecting him.

“I thought you might come,” she said, her voice calm but tinged with a sense of finality. “I’m ready, Dr. Hayward.”

Lucas walked over to her bedside and placed the envelope in her hands. “This is for you,” he said, his voice barely a whisper. “It’s your choice now. I can’t make it for you, but I can give you the option.”

Miriam looked down at the envelope, her fingers trembling slightly as she opened it. She read the prescription quietly, and when she looked up, there was a new light in her eyes—relief, maybe, or peace.

“Thank you,” she whispered. “For everything.”

Lucas nodded, though he didn’t trust himself to speak. He turned to leave, but Miriam’s voice stopped him.

“Dr. Hayward, can I ask you something?”

He turned back to her, waiting.

“Do you think I’m selfish for wanting this?” she asked. “For asking you to help me end my life?”

Lucas shook his head slowly. “No,” he said, his voice firm. “You’re not selfish. You’re asking for something that no one else can give you—control over your own fate. And that’s not selfish. That’s courage.”

Miriam smiled softly. “Then I’m ready.”

As Lucas stepped out of the room, he felt a deep sense of emptiness wash over him. He had granted Miriam her final wish, but in doing so, he had crossed a line he could never uncross. He had taken part in the end of a life, and no matter how much he tried to justify it, it would forever haunt him.

The hospital resumed its usual rhythm, the beeping of machines, the murmurs of doctors and nurses. But for Lucas, everything had changed. He had made his decision, and though he didn’t regret it, he knew that it would stay with him forever. The weight of the last prescription would always be there, a reminder of the fine line between medicine and morality, between healing and harm.

And as the sun set over St. Mary’s, Dr. Lucas Hayward stood alone in the hallway, knowing that he had just made one of the hardest choices a doctor could ever face.
