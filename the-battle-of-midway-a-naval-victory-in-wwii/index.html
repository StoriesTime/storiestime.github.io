The Battle of Midway: A Naval Victory in WWII


June 3, 1942. 0423 Hours. The Pacific heaves with anticipation, its surface ink-black, as if the ocean itself is holding its breath. Four men crouch on the deck of the USS Enterprise, the carrier’s deck-lighting dimmed, their voices hushed. Each clutches a battered mug, the bitter perfume of navy coffee mingling with salt air.

“Old man Fletcher’s gonna send us straight into hell,” mutters Radioman Second Class Jack McCulloch, striking a match for his Lucky Strike.

Lieutenant John Somerset, knuckles white around his mug, doesn’t smile. “Least this time we know hell’s out there, waiting.”

A flight of SBD Dauntless dive bombers looms behind them like sleeping birds, vapor still coiled beneath their wings. In a few hours, those birds will scream skyward, searching for a fleet they couldn’t see but were desperate to destroy.

Over the last two months, the sea had been a graveyard. The Japanese navy’s onslaught after Pearl Harbor had been nothing short of unstoppable, leaving charred wreckage and haunted survivors in their wake. Singapore, the Dutch East Indies, the Philippines—conquests that had seemed inevitable, preordained by strategy and the thunder of steel. But the men aboard the Enterprise—along with their counterparts on USS Yorktown and USS Hornet—were not in the business of bowing to inevitability.

Admiral Chester Nimitz, the architect of the U.S. Pacific Fleet’s resistance, had chosen Midway Island as his line in the sand. Intelligence officers, deciphering the arcane language of Japanese transmissions, had discerned a bold plan: the Japanese intended to sweep across the Pacific, take Midway, and finish off the American carriers they hadn’t sunk six months before at Pearl Harbor.

For Lieutenant Somerset and Radioman McCulloch, those transmissions meant looks over the shoulder, every day, every hour, for the sky might crack open and rain fire at any moment.

The night quietly slides toward dawn. Belowdecks, pilots scrawl letters home. Petty officers polish gun barrels and check the magazines of their Grumman F4F Wildcat fighters. The mess cooks keep the coffee coming, as black and unpalatable as the sea. Above, the command is transmitted: “Pilots, man your planes.”

Aboard the Japanese carriers Akagi, Kaga, Sōryū, and Hiryū, preparations unfold with clockwork precision. The Japanese admiral, Isoroku Yamamoto, has staked his entire strategy on surprise. His pilots have already gained a fearsome reputation: from the rampaging Zeros to the torpedo-armed Kates.

But today, the gods are fickle. As the sun climbs, Lieutenant Somerset scrambles into the Dauntless cockpit behind his pilot, Ensign Bob Clark, who grips the stick, face pale beneath the brim of his cap.

“We get through this,” Bob says, “you’re buying drinks in Honolulu.”

“Yeah?” John cracks, “What’ll we toast?”

Bob grins. “The Empire’s hubris.”

Fat chance, John thinks, but he manages a crooked smile.

At 0600, Midway’s radar pings: unidentified aircraft approaching. Every soul on the tiny atoll braces for annihilation. Defenders scramble their battered Brewster Buffaloes and Marine pilots man anti-aircraft guns. The Japanese bombers roar in, their bombs detonating fuel tanks and incinerating hangars in fireballs that paint the horizon.

Admiral Fletcher, from his command post aboard Yorktown, waits. His scout planes have ranged for hours, hungry for a glimpse of the enemy. By 0740, the first group spots the Japanese: reflections of sunlight across a carrier flight deck, a telltale forest of ant-like specks.

The catapult launches shake the American carriers. Wildcats arch into the sky, already straining to intercept. Meanwhile, the torpedo squadrons—VT-8 from Hornet, VT-6 from Enterprise, VT-3 from Yorktown—form up at wave-top height. Their mission is simple: get close enough, drop torpedoes, hope for luck and survival.

But fate, today, looks at them with hard, pitiless eyes.

Ensign Lloyd “Red” Brown leads VT-8’s torpedo runs. The squadron is green, flying obsolete Devastators against agile Zeros and bristling anti-aircraft fire. Red directs his pilots in by the book, never wavering as City of New York, his plane, shudders with incoming fire. One by one, the torpedo planes disintegrate. None score a hit. Only Ensign George Gay, wounded, survives the massacre, drifting in the waves as fire roils above.

For hours it seems the battle will go Japan’s way. Zeros scupper most torpedo attacks, and their pilots peel back into the sun to hunt for more prey. But timing—luck or destiny—intervenes.

At 1020, high above, Dauntless bombers from Enterprise and Yorktown arrive. Lieutenant Somerset’s formation, led by Lieutenant Commander Wade McClusky, has searched for hours, running low on fuel and hope. Then a glint: the Japanese destroyer Arashi, racing back to rejoin the main fleet after pursuing a stray American sub. McClusky takes the hint, steering his SBDs directly toward the heart of the Japanese formation.

The carriers Kaga, Akagi, and Sōryū lie exposed, their decks crowded with planes rearming for a second strike—ordinance and fuel scattered dangerously close to open flame.

Bob Clark lines up their Dauntless. “This is it, John.”

With the canopy rattling beneath enemy flak, they release their 1,000-pound bomb. Time slows, then snaps; an eruption of fire splits a carrier deck apart. Kaga is mortally wounded, flames climbing into the sky.

Elsewhere above the fray, Lieutenant Dick Best leads a pair of SBDs onto Akagi. His bomb crashes through the flight deck, igniting aviation fuel and torpedoes. Within minutes, Akagi is doomed.

The third carrier, Sōryū, is struck by three bombs, its hangars transformed into firestorms. The air above becomes an apocalyptic landscape: planes spiraling down, ships wreathed with burning fuel, and the screams of men fighting to survive.

Somerset can only glimpse it in flashes—columns of smoke, water boiling as bombs detonate, strafing fire raking decks. He hears Bob yelling, “We did it! We finally got the bastards!” John’s knuckles ache from gripping the radio.

The Dauntless climbs, battered but alive. All around, the shrieks of exploding steel, the drone of engines, and the dull thumps of distant bombs compose a savage symphony.

But it’s not over yet. Of the four Japanese carriers, Hiryū survives, launching two counter-attacks. Her bombers locate Yorktown, striking twice. The carrier staggers but stays afloat thanks to miraculous damage control. Yet fate is cruel; Hiryū’s second wave finds its mark, ultimately dooming Yorktown.

As American Dauntless squadrons refuel and rearm, they press their advantage. By 1700, SBDs from Enterprise and Yorktown bear down on Hiryū. Dive-bombers, undeterred by intense flak, pepper her decks. In moments, Hiryū is mortally wounded, her fate sealed as flames devour the wooden planks.

Night falls hard, the burning hulls flicker on the edge of the horizon. The American task force limps away, battered and bloodied, their losses keenly felt. More than a hundred aviators will never return, their bodies entombed within the coral wastes or ash left smoldering on the waves.

Lieutenant Somerset, swaddled in a borrowed jacket, watches the sunset from the deck of Enterprise. McCulloch finds him, silent but for the soft clink of dog tags.

“You think it’ll matter?” McCulloch asks. “What we did today?”

John studies the flames dancing on the clouds. “It has to,” he says, voice hoarse. “And if it doesn’t… we’ll make sure it does.”

Somewhere in the night, rescuers pluck George Gay from the sea. He floats alone for hours, witnessing the burning wreckage of the very squadron he led—a living sentinel to sacrifice and survival.

News of the victory trickles back to Hawaii, then races along cables to Washington. The mood in the high command snaps from despair to cautious hope. For the Navy, the Battle of Midway marks a turning point—not just in strategy, but in spirit. Four Japanese carriers, a seasoned cadre of pilots, and the illusion of invincibility have been lost in a single day.

But to men like Somerset, Clark, McCulloch, and the silent ghosts of torpedo squadrons, Midway will always be more than maps and graphs and shifting momentum. It is the memory of fire and steel, of fear overcome, of ordinary men bearing the unbearable and reshaping history one desperate gamble at a time.

The sea is quiet again. The wrecks slip beneath the waves, claimed by the Pacific’s depths. Over Midway Atoll, the battered but unbroken American flag flutters, and a new chapter in the war—hard, uncertain, but winnable—begins with the first uncertain rays of dawn.
