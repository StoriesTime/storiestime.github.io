Lost in Likes: The Price of Validation in a Virtual World


It was a quiet afternoon when Lena sat down with her phone, scrolling through the endless stream of posts that filled her screen. The bright sunlight streamed through her bedroom window, but it felt distant, as if the warmth from the outside world had no power over her. Her eyes were locked onto the little numbers beneath the photos of smiling faces, happy moments, and carefully curated lives. The likes—always the likes.

Every tap of her thumb on the screen, every refresh of her feed, was a gamble. Would she receive a like on her latest post? Would anyone comment? Lena was starting to feel the familiar rush of anticipation. The post was hers—her moment of sharing, of opening up her life to the world. But, in that moment, the anxiety began to creep in. What if no one cared?

She had been here before, staring at a post she had shared hours ago, watching the number of likes slowly trickle in. But it never seemed enough. It never felt like validation. The more likes she saw, the more she craved. The deeper she sank into this cycle of validation, the more she felt disconnected from the real world.

Her phone buzzed, pulling her out of her thoughts. A new notification. She opened it eagerly, half-expecting it to be the new comment she had been waiting for. But no, it was just another notification from a virtual reality game. She sighed, then clicked on her profile page to check the statistics on her latest post.

“Only 58 likes so far?” she muttered, feeling the familiar pang of disappointment. “I thought this one would be different.”

It wasn’t the first time Lena had felt this way. Over the past few months, social media had slowly taken over her life, growing from a casual pastime to a full-blown obsession. She couldn’t pinpoint when it had started, but it had become all-consuming. Every waking moment seemed to be tied to the glow of her screen, where she tried desperately to craft the perfect image of herself, to show the world that she was interesting, that she was worthy.

But what was the cost of all this validation? What was the price of the likes she so desperately sought?

Lena didn’t have answers. All she knew was that the more she tried to prove her worth through the virtual lens of social media, the less fulfilled she felt in her real life.

It was late that evening when Lena found herself at the coffee shop with her friends. Her phone had been buzzing nonstop, and she had barely noticed the world around her. Her friends chatted excitedly, catching up on life, but Lena’s mind was elsewhere. She was scrolling through Instagram again, her thumb moving almost automatically, her gaze fixed on the screen. She felt disconnected from the conversation, but she didn’t want to admit it. After all, how could she when everyone else seemed so content?

“Lena,” one of her friends, Sarah, said, snapping her fingers in front of Lena’s face. “Are you even listening?”

Lena blinked and put the phone down, feeling a slight blush creep onto her cheeks. “Sorry, I got distracted.”

“You’re always distracted by that thing,” Sarah said, her tone a mix of concern and exasperation. “You’re not even present anymore. You’ve been glued to that screen for hours.”

Lena opened her mouth to protest, but she couldn’t. The truth stung. She had become addicted to the approval of strangers online, and now, even in the company of friends, she was unable to let go of the need for constant validation.

“Maybe you need a break from all that,” another friend, Emily, added softly. “You’re missing out on real-life moments.”

Lena didn’t want to admit it, but they were right. The more she chased the likes and comments, the more distant she felt from everything else. The virtual world had taken on a new dimension, where reality seemed distant and unreal. She was losing herself in the pursuit of something intangible, something that couldn’t be measured in likes or followers.

But the pull was too strong. That night, as she lay in bed, her phone beside her, Lena found herself scrolling again. This time, she posted a picture from her evening at the coffee shop. She edited it meticulously—lighting, angles, filters—until it was “perfect.” As she hit ‘Share,’ she waited. The moment her post went live, a wave of anticipation hit her.

Would this be the post that finally gave her the validation she was craving?

The likes began to trickle in, slowly at first, but then they started coming faster. 10 likes. 20 likes. 50. It was working. She felt the familiar rush, the warmth of approval that spread through her like a drug. She watched the number climb higher, and a small smile crept onto her face.

But even as the likes kept coming, something felt off. The happiness, the rush—it was fleeting. The smile on her face faded as quickly as it had appeared. She glanced at the clock. It was 2 AM.

The virtual world had taken over her real life, and she was beginning to realize the true cost. She was losing herself in the chase for validation, for a sense of worth that came from strangers on the internet. The more she posted, the more she felt empty inside. The more likes she got, the less real she felt.

Lena closed her eyes and let out a deep sigh. The next morning, she woke up to her phone buzzing again, more notifications flooding her screen. But this time, it felt different. The excitement had faded. The validation felt hollow.

Her phone pinged again. Another like. Another comment. But this time, Lena wasn’t paying attention. She turned the screen face down and sat in silence, staring at the ceiling. It was time to take a step back. Time to disconnect from the world that had become so consumed by appearances, by likes, by fleeting approval.

A few weeks passed before Lena made the decision to deactivate her social media accounts. It wasn’t easy. She had spent so much of her life shaping her online persona, and letting it go felt like cutting off a part of herself. But slowly, the weight on her chest began to lift.

She spent more time with her friends in real life, listening to their stories, sharing her own. She started taking long walks in the park, enjoying the simple pleasure of breathing in the fresh air, feeling the sun on her skin. She rediscovered the joy of reading a book without checking her phone every few minutes, of having a conversation without wondering how it would look online.

Lena realized that, in her pursuit of validation, she had neglected the things that truly mattered—the real connections, the moments that didn’t need to be shared for approval, the experiences that couldn’t be captured in a photo or a post.

She was learning, slowly but surely, that self-worth could never be measured by likes or followers. It was an internal compass, one that didn’t require validation from the outside world to exist.

As the days passed, Lena felt more grounded. She wasn’t lost in the endless cycle of likes anymore. She had found herself again, and she was beginning to appreciate life in its raw, unfiltered beauty.

The virtual world would always be there, waiting with its promises of instant approval and empty validation. But Lena had learned that the real world, with its imperfect moments and messy emotions, was where true connection and worth were found.

And for the first time in a long time, she felt free.
