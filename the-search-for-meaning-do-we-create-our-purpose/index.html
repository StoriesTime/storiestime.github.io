The Search for Meaning: Do We Create Our Purpose?


Marek once thought that life’s meaning would come to him like a beam of sunlight piercing the fog. He had waited thirty-six years, through three continents and five changes of address, for such a sudden, irrefutable revelation. But spring arrived again in the little Belgian town of Gierle, and he was still draping towels over yet another leaky old radiator, still setting his tea to steep in silence by the kitchen window. The question nagged him constantly: What, exactly, was he living for?

His job—statistical analysis for a multinational logistics company—consumed most hours of his week, but felt as hollow as a chocolate bunny left over from Easter. On Fridays, he walked laps around the town square, examining the cobbled streets, the skeletal trees, and the chalked bicycle lanes. Only once had he attempted to talk about “purpose” with his manager. She’d laughed in a kindly way and handed him a new spreadsheet. “Look, Marek, purpose is what you do every day, right?” She tilted her cup to sip as she returned to her computer.

Was it? Marek wondered again on a drizzly April evening, as he traced a snail’s path along his garden wall. He’d read enough to know theories: the wisdom of ancient Greeks, the wild optimism of 20th-century existentialists who claimed you shape purpose yourself, piece by piece, act by act. There were philosophies, TED talks, and bestsellers, all clamoring: purpose is something you build, not something you find. But these declarations felt as distant as distant cousins—vaguely familiar, ultimately unreachable.

Marek decided he needed a hypothesis. If people truly created their own purpose, he reasoned, then those who did so would—surely—demonstrate certain patterns. They would be passionate, driven, perhaps even unshakably optimistic. He longed to interview such a person, to study the contours of a life assembled with clear intention.

His neighbor, Maud, seemed an unlikely subject, yet intriguing. Seventy-nine, clad year-round in wooly socks and faded blue jeans, she kept a ramshackle garden that burst with color: poppies, violets, even sunflowers brazenly out of season. On Sundays, laughter wafted from Maud’s house, where her family—three generations’ worth—gathered for loud, sprawling meals.

Marek, with typical awkwardness, contrived to help her one Saturday morning, offering to haul bags of soil. She pressed him into tea instead.

“Why do you keep so many flowers?” he asked, truthfully curious.

She looked both embarrassed and pleased. “Because I can,” she grinned, “and because the world is bleak without them.”

“Is that your purpose?”

She inhaled sharply, examining him as if he’d sprouted marigolds from his ears. “Purpose? Ah, you’re one of those,” she teased. “Purpose is an onion. Sometimes you only cry when you peel it.”

He sipped his tea and let her words rattle around in his chest. After tea, she handed him a small packet of wildflower seeds. “You’ll plant these in your garden,” she instructed. Then, for good measure: “And you’ll invite yourself over for soup next week.”

He smiled, thinking she was joking, until he realized she was not.

So that week, Marek planted. He dug up a patch of weed-choked earth behind his apartment and sprinkled seeds as Maud had shown him—swift, generous gestures, no second-guessing over the perfect spot. Sun or shade, she said, it hardly matters. He doubted her, but obeyed. Each morning before logging onto endless data streams, he knelt by the soil and pressed his palm flat to the dirt.

Soup night arrived, and he entered her home to a riot of scent and sound. The table was crowded: Maud’s daughter, son-in-law, three curly-haired grandkids. He was introduced as “the new gardener, watch out, he’s clever but can’t tell a daisy from a dock leaf.” They teased him gently, foisted bread into his hands, asked about his country, his family, his favorite season.

He noticed something during the meal—not a revelation, but a soft, steady hum of connection. Each person at the table had their own patchwork of troubles, but together there was a buoyancy—a flavor of something intentional, even if accidental.

After dessert, Maud asked, “Well? Did you find what you were looking for?” Her eyes twinkled.

“I’m not sure,” Marek admitted. “Maybe I expected something grand.”

She snorted. “We don’t all need to be grand. Sometimes, we’re meant to make soup. Or plant flowers. Or say yes to soup with someone new.”

He walked home that night past the square, the baker’s window gleaming gold, and thought about the wildflower seeds curling in the earth behind his flat. Each was just a seed, not a destiny. If Maud was contented, it wasn’t because she believed she had a singular, unshifting purpose, but because she relished what was small and mutable—color in a gray world, company on a night that would otherwise be silent.

The days grew longer, the mornings brighter. His flowers sprouted, an unruly patchwork that made him laugh for its lack of symmetry. When his office work ended, he stopped by Maud’s, often with leftover pastry or cheese, and stayed for stories. They spoke of loss, of their late spouses, and of recipes handed down or reconstructed from memory. Marek realized he wasn’t so much searching for his purpose as he was building it, unintentionally and brick by brick—sometimes with wildflowers, sometimes with soup, always in the company of others.

One June evening, calculation gave way to contentment. The old garden wall was ablaze with poppies and he’d been invited, again, to Sunday dinner. Marek stood outside Maud’s door, listening to the chorus of laughter leaking through the window, and recognized the fragile but persistent warmth rising in his chest.

Perhaps meaning is not something one finds, but something one stitches together, thread by thread, from small encounters and acts: the agreement to help, the new recipe attempted, the garden patiently weeded. Each action a quiet defiance against absurdity. Each connection another root sunk deep into shared soil.

Maybe the philosophers were right in their own way, he mused, as he pressed the doorbell and heard the shouts of welcome, purpose is not a fixed destination. It is the path itself—sometimes dazzling, sometimes ordinary, but always uniquely our own, made richer by every messy effort to connect, create, and care.

And as the summer twilight settled over Gierle, Marek thought for the first time in years that he finally belonged—if not to some grand universal story, then to a patch of earth, to a table set for many, to a life quietly, courageously invented, day by imperfect day.
