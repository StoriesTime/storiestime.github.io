Through the Rift: Time Travel and Parallel Worlds


Her notes—scraps of receipt paper, yellowed post-its, a bus map sharpied with spirals—lay splayed across the table as thunder rattled the windows of her studio apartment. A storm always seemed to tighten the cracks, as if moisture gave the seams of reality a brief grip. Calla wasn’t sure about the science; she hadn’t told anyone what she could do, or that she saw the rifts when she glimpsed the right angle, like shards of bent light.

Tonight’s rift yawned above the derelict clock tower on North Farrow Street.

She tucked a chipped blue key into her boot, slung her best-knitted scarf around her neck—threadbare yet defiant—and left for the storm-drowned city. If the rift closed by sunrise, she knew she might not make it back.

The Clock Tower: A monstrosity of stone and rusted iron, its hands frozen at 2:15 for half a century. Lightning branched cerulean over its jagged spires as Calla approached, rain clinging to her curls and shoulders.

The rift revealed itself as a fissure thirty feet above the base, right above the shattered stained glass. Not a scar of destruction, more a geometry of absence—like a mirror with mercury worn away, showing something else through the reflection: another city, another clock tower, all askew, all wrong, saturated in impossible colors like indigo shadows and vermilion sky.

Calla pressed her palm to the cool stone. The world thinned. She let herself fall forward, trusting the geometry—her mind humming as the air thickened, prickled, bent around her.

The instant between worlds was a peeled heartbeat, a taste of burnt sugar, pain and delight indistinguishable. Then: gray cobbles, tall cypress trees lined in neat rows, a dry breeze scented with cinnamon and engine oil.

World Two.

By habit, Calla blended into twilight. This parallel city was almost hers, but not: shutters a coral hue, electric lamplight casting roseate shadows, strange characters sprawling across neon store-fronts. People in gleaming coats strode beneath lamplit awnings, faces masked in polished porcelain with delicate brass hinges.

She walked. She learned.

Three nights in, Calla found the clockmaker—an old woman with silvered bones and eyes of lapis glass. Her shop, a closet of ticking chaos, gleamed with miniature universes ensnared in crystal orbs.

“You walk between layers,” said the clockmaker, not as a question. “We here call it slendering—a life always balanced on now and not now.”

Calla nodded, unspooling her fingers to reveal the blue key. “I need to map my way back. And I need to know why the cracks open.”

“Gravity of memory,” replied the clockmaker. “Worlds close together tug at each other. Sometimes, a memory is so weighty, its echo cracks the space between. Travelers like you are drawn to those fragile seams.”

“Why me?” Calla asked, voice finding its edge.

“You remember too fiercely,” the clockmaker said. “That can bend the world.”

She pressed a cog-and-crystal token into Calla’s hand—a circuitous invitation: leave, or lose the trail home forever.

Between Worlds

Calla learned, over hundreds of crossings, that time was not a rope but a braid: strands of possibility wound in infinite order. No journey was ever only forward or back. Some worlds held harsher versions of herself: Calla the cynic, Calla the revolutionary, Calla the architect who built towers so high they pierced the clouds, breaking whole weather systems.

She saw herself in a world on the brink of riot, shrouded in heavy iron, breathing soot. She scrawled hopes on windows with her finger, leaving routes for other selves to find—breadcrumbs scattered across realities, always shaped like memories, always tinged with fear.

Sometimes she encountered others: travelers with haunted glances, each seeking a world where the edge felt a little softer, something in their past or their future pulling them through.

But no world was ever truly home. Each had variations—a difference in the song of birds, the taste of rain, the lilt of city horns at dusk. Each version of herself, when cornered and honest, looked in the mirror with the same longing. What, after all, was she seeking?

The Collapse

The rifts began to widen. Cities flickered like film caught in a projector, sky warping, buildings half-phased and submerged in alien geometry. In some places, time folded entirely: Calla met a version of her mother she thought she’d lost, lips mouthing the words to a lullaby Calla hadn’t heard in decades.

Desperation grew. Risk of collapse, the merging—if too many rifts stayed open, worlds would bleed dry, each devouring the other’s possibility.

Calla, now a veteran walker, gathered allies as old as the cracks. The clockmaker led them, a council crossing time and probability alike. They mapped the rifts—now hundreds—chasing rumors of the Anchor, the point where Calla’s memory first burned through the world.

It was, as she already guessed, the abandoned apartment on Ashfield Lane, the echo of laughter and grief still hanging between chipped yellow walls.

The Anchor

When Calla re-entered her home-world, the storm had broken. The streetlights flickered uncertainly. The rift above the clock tower pulsed, grid-like, drawing loose realities in on themselves.

She sat on the kitchen floor, the blue key sweating in her palm. The clockmaker joined her—an uninvited ghost, or a projection, or maybe both.

“If you let go,” the clockmaker murmured, “the worlds will heal. But you must not cleave yourself in two. You need to decide what memory you will keep.”

“But every world is built of what I’ve lost,” Calla whispered, tears prickling sudden and bright.

“It’s not about forgetting,” said the clockmaker gently. “It’s about not carrying them all at once.”

Calla closed her eyes. She let the memories come—not just the pain, but the laughter, the way sunlight fractured through the window on laundry days, the way her mother hummed out of tune, the burn of first love, all the versions of herself who’d pressed forward, skin alive to interconnected possibility.

She held them, and then, very carefully, she let them melt—not discard, not erase, but lay aside, preserved in the braid of time, hers but not hers alone to bear.

The rift narrowed. The rift closed.

Reconciliation

Afterwards, Calla waited, breath shallow, afraid of emptiness.

But the worlds did not vanish. Instead, she found herself walking her city anew, the colors sharper, the sounds more precise. There were dreams where she glimpsed parallel towers, other Callas waving from far-off balconies, a tapestry of choices and memories, all singing the same quiet note: Continuance. Difference. Wonder.

She kept the blue key on her dresser, tarnished and still. Some nights she half-suspected another rift would open—but when she caught that old glint of bent light, she only nodded, and let it pass. She had found her world.

Every so often, she penned a letter to her other selves, sent through a route only walkers would know, addressed: From one who remembers. To one who lets go.

And always, woven beneath her words, was a promise that spanned universes:

Not gone. Just changed. The moment, the memory, the world—you’ll find your way through the rift, if only for one heartbeat more.
