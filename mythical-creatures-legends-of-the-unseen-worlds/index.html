Mythical Creatures: Legends of the Unseen Worlds


Hildra was only a child when she first glimpsed the rolling mists of the Hinterlands, that place her father called “the seam of all things.” In the valley, human folk whispered of the old, strange realms that lay behind the world—layers folded atop each other like pages in a book no hand had written. Yet in the shadow of the mountain’s root, where light and silence became indistinguishable at dusk, Hildra’s sharp eyes watched for what others only imagined.

On evenings filled with the scent of heather, she moved alone along the crumbled stone wall that separated her village from the wild. There, beneath ribbons of cloud, she saw the distant shimmer—like heat rising in a forge—marking an unseen boundary. Even the crows, black ordinaries against gray skies, refused to cross it. Hildra’s heart thudded in her chest, curiosity prickling at her bones.

The Arrival

It began, as such things do, with a rumor. First, Old Mina claimed to have heard the bell of a stag on two legs, calling through the midnight fog. Then the smith’s son reported finding hoofprints that danced through his wheat, the pattern a looping, impossible script. Wild fears flickered through the village—the return of the Night Court, the hoary fey who once stole milk and children’s laughter. But for Hildra, the rumors only deepened the pulse she felt through earth and air, the ancient promise that something watched from beyond.

One night, drawn by compulsion deeper than sense, Hildra slipped from her bed, wrapping a woolen shawl about her shoulders. She crept through the hush, boots soft on leaf-litter, the silver moon guiding her path. The mist thickened, swallowing the world whole. Still she pressed on, loyal to some invisible thread that stretched between her chest and the darkness.

A rustle, a low chuff of breath. Hildra froze, fingers digging into cold stone. Eyes wide, she watched a form melt from the ether—a creature antlered and bright-eyed, its hide rippling from obsidian to mottled gold. The stag had the face of a man and the wisdom of a thousand winters etched into its gaze. When he spoke, his words clapped in air like thunder wrapped in velvet.

“You seek the Unseen, small one?”

She nodded, voice lost to awe. He knelt on forelegs, green fire sparking from his crown.

“Then, learn. Come.”

Hildra hesitated less than a heartbeat before stepping forward. Grass parted as they walked, the world unfolding new and strange: trees shimmered with blue-green leaves, flowers exhaled shimmering motes, and stones whispered stories beneath her boots.

In the Realm of the Unseen

They passed beneath a gate of living silverwood. The stag—who called himself Ruthar—brought her to a lake where the water reflected stars she’d never seen. On its shore perched a gryphon, tail curled like a lazy question mark, eyes sharp as flint.

“Another human?” the gryphon rumbled. “They grow bolder.”

Ruthar dipped his head in deference. “She comes open-eyed. She listens.”

The gryphon considered Hildra. After a moment, he welcomed her with a flick of his lion’s tail.

So began her apprenticeship. Ruthar showed her the logic of unseen worlds: the way every choice branched rivers of possibility, each echoing through shadow and light. At dawn, she met the dryads—treewomen veiled in bark and laughter—who taught her to read the pulse beneath granite and root. At dusk, she listened to salamanders born of living flame, darting through the ash and flicker of tiny volcanoes sprouting at the water’s edge.

There were rules in this place, older than words:

  1. Never Name Your Heart’s Deepest Desire. Voice gives shape, and shape calls predators.

  2. Always Share Your Story with a Stranger. Tales bind you to the fabric of the Unseen; without stories, travelers fade into mist.

  3. Speak with the Wind Before Asking Directions. The winds gossip and lie, but sometimes truth tumbles accidentally.

Hildra absorbed it all, heart swelling with secret knowledge and wonder. She learned to slip between mists, to trade riddles with the selkies who drifted out of the mirror-lake, to comfort will-o’-the-wisps when their lights flickered low.

Legends and Lament

Days—or was it years?—slipped by in the Unseen world, unmarked by human calendars. Time flowed differently here, soaking through memory and dream. Yet pieces of Hildra’s old life tugged at her like distant bells: the warmth of her mother’s bread, the rumble of friends at harvest, the certainty of sunrise chasing away uncertainty.

One evening, Ruthar found her sitting by a pool ringed with opals and moss. “You miss your world,” he observed, gentle and sure.

“I do. But I do not belong fully here or there.”

“You are between. That is its own power—and its own sorrow.”

As Hildra pondered his words, the pool darkened with shadow. Across the water’s mirrored surface, a creature moved—vast and ancient, scaled in fragments of lost moonlight. The Lindwyrm, keeper of prophecy, slid free from water to speak.

“You have seen much, Child of Two Worlds,” the Lindwyrm hissed. “But legends demand more than sightseeing.” He unfurled a claw like a length of black glass. “Every bridge must be paid for. Every tale, earned.”

“Then tell me why I was brought here,” Hildra answered, voice threadbare but steady.

The Lindwyrm’s laugh was a stone splitting in ice. “Because the boundaries thin, and old wounds bleed again. Our world leaks into yours—unseen, but not unbidden. Your kind forget us, grow hungry for power over land, beast, and time. We would have a voice remembered, not erased.”

Ruthar’s eyes shone dark with warning, but Hildra stood. “Then teach me how to weave your story back into the world. Let us haunt memory not as shadow but as legend.”

The Bargain

A summit was called, beneath the roots of the World-Ash. Here gathered beings Hildra could never have imagined: naiads with hair like kelp, shaggadai spirits in waistcoats of spider-silk, gryphons and hags and river-horses, each with tales older than empire. All agreed on one thing: if the old ways were to endure, they needed new chroniclers and witnesses.

Hildra swore the first oath: to carry true stories back, to mask nothing in honey or shame.

The gryphon struck the second bargain: any human who heard the tales and believed, if only for a heartbeat, would see the world with new eyes—never quite blind to the Unseen again.

Ruthar pledged the third: so long as at least one soul gathered stories and risked the crossing, the boundary would last, thin but unbroken.

The Lindwyrm sealed the pact, his scales humming chordless music. A fragment of his own memory—older than the first storm—was spun into Hildra’s bones.

Homecoming and Loss

Hildra crossed the threshold again, stepping into her village’s dawn as if pulled from dream. To others, she’d vanished three days—enough for panic but not for mourning. Yet to Hildra, decades pressed behind her eyes, experience folded into impossible corridors.

She told no one at first, words given shape only at the hearthfire with Mina, whose mind was patchwork but whose heart was sharp as knife-edge. Gradually, she spun her stories, daring listeners to imagine the Unseen beside every furrow and fen.

Some scoffed—talk of gryphons and selkies? Nonsense for children. But some, usually the youngest or oldest, watched her with new suspicion, their eyes sparking with possibility.

Whenever the mists grew thick and crows called at the edge of twilight, Hildra pressed her palm to the old stone wall. Beneath her skin, she felt the hum—the promise of passage, the heartbeat of the Unseen.

Legends Endure

Time passed. The seasons shifted in their ancient circuit. Frost iced the windows, babies were born, fields were tilled and harvested. Yet Hildra’s stories lingered, growing at the edges of conversation, leaping from tongue to tongue when the wind moaned just right.

One evening, a child at her knee—her granddaughter, bright as beech leaves in spring—whispered, “Tell me again of the antlered man. Tell me of the lake of stars.”

Hildra smiled, her heart a river carrying ancestor and descendant alike.

So the Unseen endured. Not as memory, not as shadow. But as legend, coiled in every story told on the edge of the world: a reassurance that just beyond the senses, creatures strange and miraculous continued to wait for the bold, the foolish, and the wise.

Beyond the gray-misted Hinterlands, in places yet unnamed, there are still those who listen for hoofbeats at midnight. There are stone walls that hum with old promise, trees whose roots curl around shining secrets. If you step carefully, with eyes open and heart honest, you might feel the pull—a gentle thread, leading you toward the seam of all things.

What waits there are not just monsters or miracles, but reminders: the world’s magic lies not in what can be seen, but in what can be believed, remembered, and told anew.

If you ever find that place, remember Hildra’s story. Let it guide your steps, or at least remind you that the boundary between seen and Unseen is a thing both fragile and fiercely kept. And you—yes, you—are always close enough to cross.

So listen when the mists rise. The legends are not finished with us yet.
