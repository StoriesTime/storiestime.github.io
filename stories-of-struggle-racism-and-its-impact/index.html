Stories of Struggle: Racism and Its Impact


In a small town nestled on the outskirts of the city, there was a street that many referred to as “the dividing line.” It was the kind of street that divided two worlds, one of privilege and opportunity, the other of hardship and struggle. It wasn’t a literal boundary, but for the people who lived there, it felt like one. On the north side, the houses were bigger, the lawns well-manicured, and the people looked at each other with smiles, even if those smiles sometimes felt more like an obligation than a true connection. On the south side, the buildings were old, the streets littered with trash, and the faces were tired—worn by the weight of generations that had lived in poverty, oppression, and the sharp sting of being different.

This was where Maya lived.

Maya had always known she was different. Her skin was darker than most of the children in her school, and her hair didn’t fall in straight, perfect strands like the girls in her class. She was one of the few Black children in a predominantly white school, and as much as her parents tried to shield her from the world outside their front door, Maya felt the harshness of the world’s gaze every time she walked into the classroom.

Her earliest memories were filled with whispers and sideways glances. The teasing had started in elementary school, subtle at first. At first, they called her “Maya the Moocher,” referencing an old stereotype about Black people being lazy, and though it was just a joke to some of the kids, it stung her more than they could have ever known. She would smile and laugh along, but inside, a seed of doubt began to grow.

By middle school, the words became sharper. “Go back to where you came from,” a boy named Steven had told her one afternoon as they walked out of the school building. Maya had never seen the boy before, but it didn’t matter. His words landed with the force of a slap.

“Why do you talk so white?” another student, Liz, had asked during lunch one day. Maya didn’t know what that meant. She spoke the same way she always had, the way her parents spoke at home, the way the teachers spoke in class. But to Liz, the way Maya spoke didn’t fit into the narrow definitions of what was considered “normal.”

Maya began to ask herself questions she’d never considered before. Was there something wrong with her? Was her skin really a burden? Were the years of well-intentioned advice from her parents—about being proud of who she was, about standing tall—enough to withstand the weight of what the world wanted her to be?

Her mother, an elementary school teacher, was the first to recognize the shift in Maya’s behavior. She had seen it before in her own life, as a young woman growing up in a world that wasn’t always kind to Black girls like her. She knew the fight Maya would face in the years to come, but she also knew the fight within Maya could carry her through.

“Don’t let them dim your light, baby,” her mother would tell her in the quiet moments before bed. “You are just as beautiful as anyone else, and you have something that no one else does: your story.”

But Maya’s story wasn’t always enough to keep the pain at bay. There were times when she came home with tears in her eyes, shoulders slumped, and a sadness that seemed to consume her. The loneliness of being the only Black girl in a classroom full of children who couldn’t, or wouldn’t, understand what it meant to be her was sometimes too much to bear. Even in her own community, people looked at her differently. Some saw her as a success story, someone who had made it out of the struggles of her neighborhood and into a world of opportunities. But there were others who saw her as an outsider—a reminder of the unfairness of a system that still held Black people back, even in ways that weren’t always visible.

When Maya started high school, the weight of racism began to feel like an unbearable burden. Her classmates began to make assumptions about her that weren’t rooted in who she was as an individual, but in the color of her skin. At first, it was the microaggressions—comments like “You’re so articulate” or “I don’t see color.” They seemed harmless on the surface, but Maya had come to learn that these were coded ways of saying, “You’re not what we expect you to be.” The world didn’t just want her to fit in; it wanted her to disappear into a version of herself that was acceptable to the people around her.

And it wasn’t just the students. Maya’s teachers, too, often underestimated her. She would raise her hand, eager to contribute, but there were moments when it felt like her voice was drowned out by the louder, more confident voices of her peers. Her work was scrutinized more closely than that of her classmates, and her accomplishments were often dismissed. When she received an award for her writing, the first question from a teacher was, “Are you sure this is your work?”

Her best friend, Sarah, who had grown up with Maya on the south side, could see the change in her. Sarah was white, and though she understood the difference between them, she didn’t always understand the struggles that Maya faced. Sarah did her best to be supportive, but she was limited in what she could do.

“I don’t get it, Maya. You’re so smart. Why do they treat you like this?” Sarah asked one afternoon after a particularly bad encounter with a teacher. Maya wasn’t sure how to answer. She wanted to scream that it wasn’t fair, that the world had made her feel less than she was, that she was tired of fighting to be seen as equal. But instead, she just shook her head.

“I don’t know, Sarah. I just don’t know.”

As Maya navigated her high school years, the tension between who she was and who the world expected her to be grew stronger. But she was determined to prove that her worth wasn’t measured by the color of her skin or the limited lens through which others saw her. She began to speak up more, to challenge her teachers when they treated her like she didn’t belong. She began to surround herself with other students who understood her struggle, who didn’t see her as a token or an anomaly, but as a person with a unique story and perspective.

One of those students was Daniel, a young Black activist who had grown up in the same town. He’d been fighting for racial equality long before Maya had even thought to question the system. Daniel had an intensity about him that was hard to ignore, and his passion for social justice was contagious. He wasn’t afraid to speak out against the injustices that plagued their school and their community, and his bravery inspired Maya to do the same. They worked together on several projects aimed at raising awareness about racial inequality and creating a more inclusive environment at school.

Maya’s voice began to resonate, not just with her peers, but with the teachers and administrators who had once overlooked her. She gave speeches about her experiences, sharing the pain of discrimination and the deep-seated impact it had on her and others like her. Slowly, the walls began to come down. Students, once unaware of the struggles Black students faced, began to listen. Teachers who had dismissed her started to acknowledge her intelligence and contributions.

But even as Maya’s influence grew, the fight was far from over. Racism wasn’t something that could be eradicated overnight, and she knew that. The real challenge lay in confronting the subtle ways that racism lived in the fabric of their daily lives—in the way schools were structured, in the way people treated one another, in the hidden biases that shaped society’s expectations.

Maya had learned that the struggle against racism wasn’t just about fighting the overt acts of hatred—it was about challenging the systemic inequalities that kept people of color in positions of disadvantage. It was about making space for people like her to exist authentically, without having to shrink or conform to fit into a world that hadn’t been designed for them.

By the time Maya graduated, she had changed. She was stronger, more confident, and more determined than ever to continue the work of dismantling racism, not just in her community, but in the world at large. She had come to understand that her journey was not just hers to bear, but that of countless others who had walked the same path before her.

She still faced challenges, of course. There were still days when the world felt heavy, when the pain of racism felt like too much to bear. But Maya had learned that the fight was worth it. The voices of those who had struggled before her, who had faced the same battles and endured the same pain, carried her forward. And in the end, she knew that the story of struggle was not one of defeat, but of resilience, hope, and the relentless pursuit of equality.

For Maya, and for so many others, the journey was just beginning.
