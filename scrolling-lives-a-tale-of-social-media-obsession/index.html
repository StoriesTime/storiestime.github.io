Scrolling Lives: A Tale of Social Media Obsession


Ana woke up to the glow of her phone rather than sunlight. The digital alarm she relied on had rung fifteen minutes earlier, but she hadn’t heard it. What did wake her was the subtle buzz of notifications jolting her mind into activity. Her fingers moved instinctively, swiping across the screen before her eyelids fully opened.

A quick dive into her preferred social media platform showed several likes trickling in from the photo she had posted late last night—a carefully staged picture of her untouched cup of chamomile tea framed against the golden hues of a vintage lamp. Ana never actually drank chamomile tea; in truth, it tasted like stale flowers drenched in water. But its aesthetic appeal won over practicality, an unspoken rule in the kingdom of curated feeds.

Her morning ritual had become predictable: bed, phone, scroll. She would roll over, prop herself up lazily against the pillows, and plunge headfirst into the endless labyrinth of stories, reels, and posts. Breakfast could wait, brushing her teeth could wait, but catching up with the lives of both strangers and acquaintances was non-negotiable. It wasn’t just about being entertained anymore—it was about staying relevant, connected, and, above all, informed. Social media didn’t sleep, so Ana couldn’t afford to slip either.

This morning was no exception. The “For You” page pulled her in first. It was algorithmic magic—the perfect mix of low-effort comedy skits and inspirational quotes designed to jostle the lethargic brain into action. She felt a hollow laugh escape her lips when she scrolled past a video of someone mimicking the clichés of morning influencers—the faux-zen positivity and syrupy smiles. Ana had tried to film a similar routine once but gave up halfway. Smiling that long for the camera felt awkward, like forcing herself into a mold she didn’t quite fit.

Next came the feed tailored to her obsessions. On good days, it felt like a best friend who intuitively knew her every interest; on worse days, it resembled a mirror reflecting society’s expectations back at her. Pretty faces stared at her, flaunting their spotless skin and perfectly toned physiques. Travel bloggers waved from sparkling beaches with captioned promises of escapism for the price of a plane ticket. DIY enthusiasts transformed cracked pots into works of art while Ana struggled to stop chips of paint flaking off her apartment walls. Every post seemed to whisper a silent question: What was Ana doing with her life?

Hours melted away. It was only after Ana heard the impatient chiming of her neighbor’s cat outside her window that her trance broke. She groaned audibly, tossing her phone down. Another morning lost to the algorithm.

The day marched forward. Ana stumbled toward her kitchen and stared blankly at the pile of dishes in the sink, noting the growing crust of oatmeal on the bowls. It wasn’t that her days lacked time; no, time flowed abundantly and generously. What eluded her was focus—attention continuously siphoned away by the lure of screens.

Work was little more than an inconvenient obstacle between Ana and her phone. She had made the grievous mistake of leaving notifications on during Zoom meetings once, leading to whispered reprimands from her manager who spotted Ana glancing at her phone in the reflection of her glasses. She apologized profusely, but her thumbs still took their habitual stroll over the keyboard every chance they could.

Ironically, her job as a junior researcher had glimpses of excitement that always escaped notice. She had the opportunity to draft compelling reports and uncover fascinating trends, but her interest fizzled against the pressure of deadlines and the never-ending pull of digital distraction. While spreadsheets filled her official hours, hashtags kept her captivated.

It wasn’t until her best friend, Kayla, pushed through Ana’s digital barricade that change began to creep in.

Kayla worked as a counselor and ran a health-focused blog. Unlike Ana’s feed full of filters and complex poses, Kayla’s posts showcased honesty—unedited captions recounting struggles with anxiety, gray mornings filled with self-doubt, and small victories measured in cups of tea. She wasn’t afraid to admit when she failed, and Ana admired her for that.

One Friday evening, Ana sat scrolling aimlessly while the stale echo of sitcom laughter filled her apartment. Kayla’s call interrupted her mid-swipe. Reluctantly, Ana reached for the green button.

“I haven’t seen you in forever,” Kayla said before Ana could even greet her. “It feels like you’re always offline… Or rather, online but unreachable.”

Ana laughed hollowly. “I’ve just been busy, you know. Work’s crazy.”

Kayla didn’t buy the excuse. “It’s not work. I know it isn’t. I’ve seen your stories. You post regularly, but you never reply to my texts. Whenever I call, you check in briefly and disappear again.”

“I… I don’t mean to, Kayla,” Ana said, fumbling for words.

Her friend paused, softening her tone. “Ana, I’m telling you this because I care about you. Social media’s great in doses, but you’ve been letting it consume you. You’re living vicariously through photos of strangers, and it’s robbing you of your own life. Don’t let the black mirrors dictate your existence.”

The words stung more than Ana expected. She wanted to argue, but she couldn’t find the footing to stand on. Instead, she mumbled promises of change she wasn’t sure she believed in. They said their goodbyes, and the call ended, leaving Ana alone with her phone again.

But the seed had been planted. The following days were uneasy. Her fingers still reached for her phone the moment boredom struck, but guilt now followed each swipe. The time spent scanning feeds seemed heavier, burdened with questions Kayla’s words had raised: What am I scrolling for? What am I hoping to find?

By the end of the week, Ana decided to test herself. She set a timer for two hours of complete disconnection. Phones were cast aside, laptops untouched, even the television remained silent. She felt uneasy, unsure how to fill the void. For months, she had been accustomed to diving into screens without thinking twice. Now, that reflex felt disrupted.

She started small. She’d forgotten how satisfying brewing coffee could be—the slow gurgle of hot liquid cascading from the machine. She sat by her window, watching life pass by outside, texting Kayla about the peculiar antics of the neighbor’s cat.

When the two hours passed, Ana hesitated before reaching for her phone. It buzzed impatiently: comments to respond to, likes to acknowledge, and reels to catch up on. But as her hand brushed against it, she realized she felt something foreign. For the first time in months, the screen wasn’t pulling her back—it was merely waiting.

Weeks turned into months, and Ana gradually began reclaiming pieces of her life. Social media didn’t vanish from her routines, but its dominance faded. Her mornings were now spent walking among trees in a nearby park, savoring the stillness and listening to birdsong that had always been drowned out by headphones. She dug out her old sketchbook, filling page after page with pencil-drawn landscapes. She texted Kayla weekly, meeting her at cafes to talk about life beyond captions.

Ana hadn’t abandoned her accounts entirely, but her posts changed—they became reflections of a life lived with intention rather than performances for unseen audiences. Her followers dwindled slightly, but she didn’t mind; the ones who stayed mattered, and for once, Ana mattered to herself.

The labyrinth had been deep, and climbing out hadn’t been easy. But as Ana stared into the afternoon sunlight on her balcony, the tempting glow of a forgotten phone silent behind her, she felt free.
