The Dreamer's Guide to Achieving the Impossible


No one ever accused Morgan of dreaming small. In the narrow streets of Larchmere, where the houses leaned together as if sharing secrets, Morgan dreamed louder than city bells. On summer nights, he’d lie spread-eagled on the knobby grass behind his apartment, eyes wide to the starry explosions above, fingers tracing invisible shapes that shifted into castles and machines and distant, friendly worlds.

He once told his mother that every dream was a question meant for tomorrow, and that the answers waited just beyond restlessness. She’d smiled, brushing his hair from his forehead, saying gently, “Dreams are for sleep, Mo.” But Morgan was twelve, and the world hadn’t yet dulled his sense of infinite probability.

Every night pressed another impossible notion into his chest. And every morning, those notions scattered at the onslaught of reality’s monotony: school, chores, the city’s gray drone. Only his dream journal tethered the fantastic to the real, each page scribbled with wild inventions and impossible visions. Secretly, he believed these ideas could leak into daylight, one echo at a time.

2. The Invisible Rules

On Morgan’s thirteenth birthday, he received a book—a battered, leather-bound thing—found on a neighbor’s stoop, its cover blank and slightly sticky, smelling faintly of ozone. Tucked inside, a handwritten note: “To Those Who Refuse the World’s Limits.”

Curiosity overcame suspicion. Morgan flipped a page and found, in rich blue ink:

“There are only three rules to achieving the impossible. First: Name what the world says cannot be done. Second: Prove them wrong, without permission. Third: Tell the story, so others remember how.”

There was more, diagrams and anecdotes, sketches of wings and ladders to nowhere. The book, The Dreamer’s Guide to Achieving the Impossible, became a new kind of companion—neither advice nor commandment, but an invitation.

3. The List of Impossibilities

Morgan did exactly what the Guide suggested: made a list. Not of chores or homework, but of the things everyone—including the voice in his own head—deemed impossible.

  1. Invent an umbrella that protects from sadness.
  2. Build a bridge from his rooftop to the stars.
  3. Learn to speak the language of birds.
  4. Grow a tree that remembers people’s names.

“Impractical,” his science teacher would have scoffed. “Charming,” his grandmother might have said. But the Guide insisted: “The world’s laughter means you’re close.”

He taped the list to the inside of his closet. Not hidden, but not obvious either. Over the months, Morgan changed. He didn’t abandon his dreams for logic, but wove practicality through imagination. Each idea began to shimmer with potential instead of impossibility.

4. The First Attempt: Weathering Sadness

Autumn approached, cold and pensive. On a day thick with drizzle, Morgan watched classmates huddle under dull umbrellas. What if, he wondered, gloom could be shed as easily as water?

That evening, he scoured junk drawers. Buttons, wires, a discarded teddy bear, a strip of rainbow fabric, and two dime-sized audio chips—remnants of cheap talking cards.

He sketched: an umbrella whose canopy unfurled in splashy, iridescent colors. Inside, speakers would whisper old inside jokes or bright compliments at the push of a button. Morgan worked until dawn, sewing, soldering, and giggling quietly.

The next rainy morning, he unfurled his creation. Pink and turquoise panels glowed, laughter bubbled near his ear, and passersby grinned at the sight. He felt the first tickle of achievement, absurd yet exhilarating.

Someone from his block—a stoic, silent older man—asked, “Did you make that?” His voice held skepticism, but also hope.

Morgan only smiled, and for the first time, the impossible didn’t feel so distant.

5. Speaking with Birds

The Guide urged: “Listen longer, speak softer. Impossible is often only untried.” Morgan began feeding crows, leaving shiny trinkets on his windowsill. At first, they ignored him. Days passed, then a week. Then one morning, black shapes clustered in the dawn.

Morgan mimicked a series of caws he’d practiced (somewhat comically) in his room. A larger crow cocked its head, ruffled its wings, and dropped a purloined candy wrapper at his feet.

It wasn’t conversation—but it was connection. The birds visited every morning, trailing snippets of city lore in their beaks: rubber bands, keys, a charm from his lost bracelet. Morgan suspected they understood more than anyone credited.

He chronicled the experiment in his Guide, adding: “Progress isn’t always translation. Sometimes it’s attention, and a willingness to be odd.”

6. Building a Ladder to the Stars

For months, idea number two taunted him. “Build a bridge from your rooftop to the stars.”

“A metaphor, surely,” his practical side whispered. Morgan disagreed.

He scavenged the neighborhood for wood scraps, broken ladders, rope, and discarded holiday lights. Each evening, he lashed another rung onto his growing ladder, anchoring it to the fire escape. Neighbors peeked through curtains, unsure whether to be amused or concerned.

When finished, the ladder soared above his rooftop and into imagined possibility. Morgan timed its twinkling lights to sync with the rise of Orion. Laying back each night, he felt the stars press a little closer, just enough.

Kids on his block asked for stories about his ‘star ladder.’ He told them it worked only for those who believed both in risk and in wonder.

7. A Tree that Remembers Names

The Guide’s third rule returned: “Tell the story, so others remember how.”

At school, Morgan joined the gardening club. He convinced them to plant a small grove, tying name-etched tin tags to saplings. As each tree grew, the tags clicked gently in the wind, whispering their memories to anyone who paused beneath them.

More came: notes tucked in branches, children reading their names aloud to the trees. The grove became a living guestbook. The impossible—memory as root and leaf—became neighborhood reality.

8. The Unintended Miracle

Years blurred, things changed—the city, his family, even his dreams. Yet the umbrella, the crow visits, the stories told about the star ladder, and the grove of name-tagged trees remained.

Morgan, now older, published his collected stories. By then, others had adopted his quirks: umbrellas with laughter, public groves with names, bird feeders in windows. He received a letter from a stranger who’d never met him: “Your book changed my city.”

Morgan smiled. The impossible had shifted shape, yet persisted. The Guide’s three rules—Name, Prove, Tell—had carried him further than he’d ever gone alone.

9. The Legacy of Impossible Things

In a generation, Larchmere became famous for its tinkering dreamers. Children built contraptions overhead while adults consulted the local ‘Impossibility Library’ for blueprints and inspiration. Tourists came, not for monuments, but for glimpses of the possible hidden inside the peculiar endeavors on every street corner.

Morgan’s Guide, already well worn, gained thousands of anonymous footnotes in the margins—new names for old impossibilities. No list stayed static for long; every entry begged to be outdone.

No one could map the scope of the change. All anyone could say was that someone, somewhere, was always trying to answer a dream’s question, carrying beliefs across the impossible like bridges built in the dark.

10. Letters to the Future Dreamers

Decades later, Morgan wrote:

“To the next dreamer:

Impossible is a closing door. Your task is simply to find the lock and pick it, whether with science, kindness, or sheer tenacity. If the world laughs at you, you’re almost surely onto something. The impossible bends—but only if you tug hard enough and dare tell others what you saw on the other side.

Name your impossible. Build against the grain. Leave a door behind you; someone else will find it useful.”

He closed the battered Guide, watching a new child climb the old ladder to the stars, umbrella spinning laughter, crows swirling overhead, trees rustling with the remembered names of dreamers past.

The impossible, he decided, was simply another way of saying, “not yet.”

End
