Behind Closed Doors: Personal Pandemic Experiences


The air was thick with tension as Maria stood in the hallway, the dim light from the hallway lamp spilling through the cracks of her apartment door. She had lost track of time—weeks, maybe months—since the world outside had crumbled under the weight of a global pandemic. The news reports were a constant drone, an ever-present reminder of the chaos unfolding in every corner of the earth. But inside her apartment, time seemed to move differently. It was as if the walls had warped the passage of moments, trapping her in a reality that was both familiar and unnervingly surreal.

Maria had always considered herself a bit of a loner. She had never needed the bustling social scene, the constant interactions with friends and colleagues. But when the pandemic arrived, and the government declared strict lockdowns, she realized how deeply loneliness could cut. It wasn’t just the absence of social interactions, but the crushing silence that filled her home—the empty streets below, the absence of familiar faces on her daily walks, the sound of her own thoughts echoing in her head.

In the early days of isolation, Maria tried to make the best of it. She set up a small makeshift office in her living room and threw herself into her work. At first, it was productive, even somewhat fulfilling. The quiet allowed her to focus in ways that the distractions of her office never could. But soon, the weight of being alone for hours on end began to press on her mind. Her work became an escape, but it wasn’t enough to stave off the creeping feeling of isolation.

Her apartment, once a sanctuary, had become a cage.

As the weeks dragged on, Maria’s routine shifted. She’d wake up to the sound of the news—new lockdown orders, rising case numbers, death tolls. The world outside seemed to be disintegrating, yet the walls of her apartment remained unchanged. She would make herself breakfast, often a bowl of oatmeal or scrambled eggs, and sit by the window, watching the empty streets below. Occasionally, she’d catch a glimpse of a neighbor, but the silence was deafening. No children playing, no dogs barking, no cars honking. Just the occasional pedestrian walking briskly, their heads down, trying to avoid contact with anyone.

Maria had tried to keep in touch with friends and family over video calls. At first, they had been a lifeline—virtual hugs, virtual smiles. But the technology that had once seemed like a marvel now felt like a barrier, a reminder that she couldn’t actually touch anyone. The conversations became more strained as time went on, and she started to sense the growing fatigue in the voices of those on the other end of the call. They were all coping, all trying to adjust to the new normal, but it was clear that no one was really okay.

One afternoon, a message from her childhood friend, Leah, popped up on her phone: “I can’t do this anymore. I feel like I’m suffocating.”

Maria paused, staring at the message. She knew Leah had been struggling. The pressure of homeschooling her children, the constant worry about her husband’s job, the isolation—everything was building up. Maria didn’t know how to respond. She had no advice, no wisdom to offer. All she had was her own experience of the same suffocating feeling. And the truth was, she wasn’t sure how she was coping, either.

The isolation had started to take its toll in ways Maria hadn’t anticipated. Her mental health was slipping. The anxiety that had always simmered just below the surface now boiled over, making it hard to focus or relax. She had trouble sleeping, plagued by restless nights filled with worries about the future. What would the world look like after this? Would it ever return to normal? Or was this the beginning of something irreversible?

Days blended into one another, a blur of anxious thoughts and numbing routines. Maria had once been someone who could find peace in solitude, but now, it felt like a constant battle just to get through the day.

Her apartment, which had once felt cozy and comforting, had become a symbol of everything that was wrong in the world. Every inch of it seemed to remind her of what was missing—human connection, fresh air, the simple joy of a warm hug. The walls closed in around her, and she began to wonder how much longer she could endure this.

One evening, as the sun dipped below the horizon, Maria decided to take a walk. The streets were still eerily quiet, and the cool air felt like a slap to her skin. It had been so long since she’d ventured out, and she realized just how much she had missed the outside world, even in its new, muted form. The smell of rain in the air, the distant hum of traffic, the rustling of trees in the wind—all of it felt like a glimpse of something real, something she had almost forgotten.

As she walked, her thoughts turned to the future. What would happen when the pandemic finally ended? What would the world look like? Would it ever feel safe again? These questions had been lingering in her mind for months, but tonight they felt more urgent, more pressing. She wasn’t sure if she wanted things to go back to how they were. There were things she had learned about herself during this time, things she wasn’t sure she could go back from. The pandemic had forced her to confront her own vulnerabilities in ways she hadn’t anticipated. It had made her realize how deeply she craved human connection, how fragile her sense of security really was.

As the days turned into weeks, and then into months, Maria’s perspective slowly began to shift. She began to see the small changes in herself—how she had learned to appreciate the little things, like the warmth of a cup of coffee in the morning or the way the sunlight filtered through the blinds. She started to find moments of peace in the silence, moments where she could simply breathe without the weight of the world pressing down on her. She also became more aware of the people around her—the neighbors she had never spoken to before, the friends who were also struggling, the family members who, despite the distance, still cared.

In the midst of the pandemic’s chaos, Maria discovered something she hadn’t expected—strength. The strength to face her fears, to accept the uncertainty, and to find hope in a world that seemed to have lost its way. Behind closed doors, in the silence of her apartment, she had faced herself and come out on the other side.

The pandemic wasn’t over, not by a long shot. But as Maria sat by her window one evening, watching the world outside slowly begin to stir again, she realized that she was no longer the same person who had once felt trapped behind these walls. She had changed. The world had changed. And perhaps, in the end, that was the only thing she could hold onto—change, the constant reminder that life, in all its uncertainty, could still move forward.

In the end, Maria understood that while the pandemic had forced her to retreat behind closed doors, it had also given her the space to rebuild—both the world around her and the world within herself.
