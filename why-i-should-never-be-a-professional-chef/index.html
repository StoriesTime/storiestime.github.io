Why I Should Never Be a Professional Chef


It started with a simple thought—perhaps, for once, I could try something new, something beyond my regular nine-to-five grind. I had always loved cooking, experimenting with ingredients, trying my hand at new recipes, and occasionally impressing friends with a dish or two. But there’s a huge difference between cooking for friends at home and cooking professionally in a high-pressure kitchen.

That realization hit me hard on my first day in culinary school. At first, it all seemed like fun and games: chopping vegetables with precision, mastering the art of sauces, and learning the science behind perfecting meats. But I soon realized that professional cooking wasn’t just about good food; it was about stamina, mental fortitude, and an unwavering commitment to perfection. And in my case, it was clear that I didn’t have what it took to thrive in that environment.

The First Day: Reality Check

I walked into the culinary school on my first day feeling confident. After all, I had cooked plenty at home, and my friends always praised my culinary skills. What could go wrong? The school was pristine, with shiny stainless steel appliances, the smell of freshly baked bread in the air, and an eager group of future chefs surrounding me.

I was assigned to a station with several other students. Our instructor, Chef Pierre, had the energy of a drill sergeant and a sharpness in his eyes that suggested he’d seen it all. He was the kind of chef who would never let you forget the importance of technique. As we began to prepare our first meal—a simple vegetable stir-fry—I quickly learned that every detail mattered.

The knife I used was a bit too dull for the delicate precision Chef Pierre demanded. The vegetables didn’t slice perfectly, and my attempt to sautée them resulted in an unevenly cooked mess. Chef Pierre didn’t hesitate. “This is not home cooking, this is professional cooking!” he shouted. “If you can’t get the basics right, you’ll never make it here!”

I froze. My confidence was shattered. Home cooking, I realized, had made me lazy. At home, I had the luxury of time, the ability to fix mistakes as I went, and no one standing over my shoulder pointing out every flaw. Here, it was different. I was expected to do everything in a flash and with flawless technique.

The Pressure Builds

As the days passed, the pressure mounted. Culinary school wasn’t just about cooking; it was about managing time, multitasking, and thriving under immense stress. I found myself racing against the clock to complete dishes while balancing multiple tasks at once. I was constantly worried about my knife skills, timing, and whether my dishes would even be edible.

One day, we were assigned to prepare a three-course meal for a mock competition. I was tasked with preparing a delicate soufflé, a dish I had never made before. I spent hours practicing at home, watching videos, and reading up on the science behind soufflés. I thought I was ready.

When the big day came, everything fell apart. I misread the recipe, added the wrong amount of flour, and ended up with a flat, sad attempt at a soufflé. Chef Pierre was unforgiving. “A soufflé is about precision! If you can’t handle the delicate nature of such dishes, you’ll never succeed in this industry.”

I wanted to quit. It felt like every step I took was another misstep. But quitting wasn’t an option; I had made a commitment. So, I pushed on, enduring the endless critiques, the harsh comments, and the feeling of always being just a step behind.

The Reality of Kitchen Life

Culinary school was only the beginning. After graduation, I found myself working in a small, fast-paced restaurant kitchen as an intern. I soon learned that the world of professional cooking was not only grueling but exhausting. Long shifts, constant pressure, and the never-ending need for perfection were overwhelming.

The first time I worked a dinner rush, I understood why professional chefs often looked like they were about to break. The kitchen was hot, crowded, and filled with noise—sizzling pans, orders being shouted, timers beeping. I was expected to keep up with the rhythm of the kitchen, but every time I tried to move quickly, I stumbled. My hands shook under the pressure. I couldn’t concentrate, and the little mistakes started adding up.

One night, I was tasked with preparing the garnishes for a dozen plates of beef Wellington. It was a simple task, or so I thought. But in the frenzy of the kitchen, I forgot to finish the garnish for one plate. When the chef caught me, he yelled, “Details matter! Every plate, every garnish. Do you understand?”

That’s when I realized—I wasn’t cut out for the high-pressure world of professional kitchens. The constant intensity, the relentless pace, the perfectionism—none of it came naturally to me. It wasn’t just about cooking anymore; it was about surviving the chaos.

The Physical Toll

Aside from the mental strain, the physical toll of working as a chef was something I hadn’t anticipated. I thought the occasional backache from standing too long in the kitchen was a small price to pay for following my passion. But I quickly learned that professional chefs endure serious physical strain every day.

My feet were constantly sore from standing on hard tile floors for hours on end. My wrists ached from repetitive chopping and stirring motions. The heat from the stove made me sweat, and the constant lifting of heavy pans caused my back to hurt.

One day, after an exhausting 12-hour shift, I could barely walk. I had strained a muscle in my lower back from lifting a pot that was too heavy for me. I was sent home early, but the injury lingered for weeks, a constant reminder that the physical demands of the job were no joke.

The Emotional Exhaustion

What really drained me, though, was the emotional toll. As much as I loved food, as much as I wanted to succeed, I didn’t have the resilience to bounce back from failure after failure. In professional kitchens, you’re not just cooking food; you’re managing stress, handling difficult personalities, and dealing with long hours away from family and friends.

The fast-paced environment took its toll on my social life. I missed birthdays, family events, and even my own relaxation time because I was stuck in the kitchen, perfecting dishes or staying late to clean up. I couldn’t handle the loneliness that came with the hours and the constant pressure to be perfect.

The Decision

One day, after yet another stressful shift, I sat down in the staff room, exhausted, both physically and emotionally. I looked at the chef’s knives on the counter, the uniform that was now stained with grease and sweat, and the clock ticking away the minutes until my next shift. I realized something: I was miserable.

The passion I had for food was still there, but the reality of being a professional chef had crushed it. The joy I once found in cooking was gone, replaced by stress and fatigue. I couldn’t bear the thought of continuing down this path, not at the cost of my health, my happiness, and my relationships.

I decided that day that I wasn’t meant to be a professional chef. I needed to find a way to enjoy cooking again, without the pressure, the exhaustion, and the constant fear of failure. Maybe I’d return to cooking at home, where I could experiment freely and enjoy the process without worrying about timelines or perfection.

Moving On

Now, I look back on those days with a sense of relief. I have no regrets about trying to pursue a career in the kitchen. It taught me valuable lessons—about resilience, time management, and the importance of pursuing what truly makes you happy.

But being a professional chef was never the right path for me. I realized that sometimes, it’s okay to let go of a dream if it’s not the right fit. I still love cooking, but I do it on my terms, in my own kitchen, at my own pace. That’s where the true joy lies—for me, at least.

In the end, I learned an important lesson: just because you love something doesn’t mean you’re meant to do it professionally.
