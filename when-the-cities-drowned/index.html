When the Cities Drowned


The horizon, once a sharp line of gleaming steel and concrete, now lay smothered under a thick mist of saltwater. It had started as a whisper, an inevitable murmur of change that no one really wanted to hear, and then it had become a roar—too loud, too sudden, for anyone to ignore. Now, as the last remnants of the great cities crumbled into the ocean, their monuments, once towering symbols of human triumph, were nothing more than the ghosts of a forgotten past.

Amidst the rising tides, Mara stood at the edge of what used to be her apartment building. The water lapped gently at her ankles, a constant reminder of the world that had been and the world that was to come. She didn’t know why she had stayed. Perhaps it was the stubbornness of youth, the unwillingness to admit that everything was slipping away, or maybe it was just fear. Fear of what lay beyond the drowned cities, of a future unrecognizable, where the very idea of civilization as they had known it was slipping through their fingers like the sand beneath the waves.

She squinted into the haze, watching the water rise steadily. It wasn’t just the sea level that had increased, but the people’s will to fight against the tide had evaporated long ago. Governments had argued over causes, scientists had warned, and the richest among them had retreated to islands and floating estates long before the rest of the world could even fathom how bad things would get.

The initial signs had been subtle, almost unnoticed. Coastal towns swallowed by creeping floods, droughts that shriveled crops, storms that seemed fiercer with each passing year. But it was when the glaciers began to melt faster than anyone predicted that the true scale of the disaster became apparent. The melting ice triggered a cascade of consequences—rising oceans, shifting weather patterns, and a migration of species that humans could not predict nor control.

The cities had been built to last, to stand proud and tall against the passage of time. Steel and glass had once seemed like unshakable emblems of human mastery over nature. But nature had its own ideas, and in the end, it was nature that triumphed. Those who hadn’t already left in search of higher ground were now scrambling for anything that would float.

It had been three years since the last great flood. Mara had heard the stories of how the cities had drowned. How the towering skyscrapers, once symbols of progress and prosperity, had crumbled into the waves as if they had been nothing more than paper buildings. There had been a time when they had felt invincible, like nothing could touch them, nothing could break their foundations. But the water had broken everything.

Her memories of the last days in the city were fragmented: the panic, the shouting in the streets, the endless stream of desperate faces trying to escape the rising tides. People had tried to leave, but there had been nowhere to go. The roads had been flooded. The airports were overwhelmed. And the ships—those massive, floating behemoths—had been taken by the sea, too, like so many other things that once had been part of the system.

Mara remembered the day she left. The apartment she had once called home had been on the second floor, safe, at least for the moment, from the waves that had already begun to lap at the first floor. The floodwater had risen so quickly that she hadn’t even had time to pack a bag. There had been no time to think, no time to wonder what it all meant. She had simply grabbed what she could—her phone, a few photos, some old books—and fled.

That was the day she had seen the first massive storm cloud in the distance. The sky had darkened in the span of a few minutes, and then the rain had come, pouring down like a sheet of glass. It was then that she realized that the end was no longer a question of if, but when.

Now, three years later, she had returned to what remained of the city, though she had no intention of staying. She had thought that perhaps seeing it all again would bring some kind of closure, some finality to the chaos that had unfolded. But standing here, watching the ocean slowly reclaim what had once been home, Mara felt nothing but emptiness.

The boat had been waiting for her at the docks when she arrived. It had been a small, scrappy thing, far from the grand cruise liners that used to dock at the port. There was no crew; only a few people had managed to survive the floods, and they had learned to rely on themselves. The boat itself seemed like a relic of a time long gone, a simple vessel meant for travel, not for luxury.

Mara had spent the past few days on the boat, drifting along the coast, watching as the cities disappeared behind her. They had all fallen, every last one. The flooding had begun slowly but gained momentum, overtaking the cities in a way that no one had ever expected. In the end, it wasn’t one great event that had led to the cities drowning, but a series of smaller disasters that compounded over time—storms, floods, the collapse of the global supply chain, the social and political upheaval that followed.

She had been in contact with others who had left, people she had met along the way. Most of them were heading inland, to higher ground, to places that had been less affected by the flooding. They talked about rebuilding, about starting anew. But Mara wasn’t sure if she believed in that anymore.

What was left to rebuild? What was left to save?

It was on the sixth day of her journey that Mara spotted the first signs of land. At first, it had been a dark shape on the horizon, barely visible through the mist. But as the boat drew closer, the outline of the landmass became clearer. It was an island, jagged and uneven, with steep cliffs rising out of the water. It was unlike any place she had seen before—wild, untamed, and untouched by the modern world.

The boat docked near a rocky shore, and Mara stepped onto the land, feeling the solid ground beneath her feet for the first time in days. She took a deep breath, the air here thick with the scent of saltwater and earth. The island was barren, but there was a strange sense of peace that came with it. She wasn’t sure why, but she felt something stir within her—something that told her this place could be the beginning of something new.

As the days passed, Mara explored the island. It wasn’t large, but it was enough for her to lose herself in its forests, its beaches, its rocky outcrops. There were no people here—at least, none that she had seen. But she felt a sense of quiet in the emptiness, as though the island itself was waiting for something to happen.

One evening, as the sun dipped below the horizon, Mara stood on a cliff overlooking the ocean. She could still see the faint shapes of the drowned cities in the distance, their lights barely visible through the mist. And yet, for the first time since the flood had begun, she didn’t feel the weight of despair. She felt… something else.

Perhaps this was what they had forgotten when they built their cities. That life didn’t always need to be a struggle to dominate, to conquer. That maybe the cities, in all their glory, had been built in the wrong place, at the wrong time. The earth had tried to remind them, over and over, that nothing could last forever.

And now, as the ocean quietly claimed what had been built on the shores, Mara realized that the drowning of the cities had not been the end. It had been the beginning of something else entirely.
