Voices of Freedom: A Journey Through Human Rights


Rain pattered softly against the glass, drawing faint rivers over a map of countries Makeda had traced with eager fingers since her childhood. The old library in Addis Ababa was as familiar to her as the scent of papyrus and cedar in the air, and as quiet as the unspoken longing she nursed in her heart.

Makeda slotted a brittle volume from the shelf, the leather cracked and golden with age. “A Journey Through Human Rights: Testimonies and Triumphs,” read the inscription. She rolled the words around in her mind, feeling their weight. Freedom had always meant something simple before—a bike ride to school, an evening stroll, the ability to speak her mind in the square. But she knew, deep down, that freedom was far greater, and far, far rarer.

As she read, Makeda drifted into stories that she felt called out to her from places miles—and ages—apart. She saw, in these tales, the faces of people who had stretched the very boundaries of what might be possible for all.

Chapter 1: The Song of the River

When Makeda followed the thrum of narrative, she found herself beside a roaring river in the South, under a bruised sky of 1961. The world was all colors: the bright red of banners, the deep brown of the river, the crisp black and white of the photographs in her book.

There, Afewerk stood on the riverbank, his voice trembling yet clear. The villagers had suffered, forbidden to draw water as freely as their ancestors had for generations. Armed men patrolled the banks, enforcing laws made by people who had never carried a single water jug.

One day, Afewerk’s mother fell sick, unable to fetch water for herself. Still a boy, Afewerk walked to the river as the gunmetal sky threatened rain. He walked past the men with rifles. “Why do you come?” one barked. Afewerk swallowed his fear, his voice no louder than the river’s whisper.

“For my mother,” he said. “Is it not my right?” The question, carried on the wind, became a chant. Villagers stepped from their huts, echoing, “Is it not our right?”

The guards, uncertain in their newness, retreated. The people seized their victory and drew water with trembling hands. Days later, a ripple surged through the village—news of the peaceful protest reached the capital. The restriction crumbled, first to a trickle, and then in a tidal wave of small changes.

Makeda felt Afewerk’s voice in her own: a simple boy with a demand so unyielding that it shifted the world.

Chapter 2: Letters Across the Divides

Makeda lifted her eyes from the page, pulse quick. The dust caught the light, and she was suddenly across continents, inside a quiet apartment in Berlin, 1989. Here, she met Lena, whose hair caught the sun in a halo as she hunched over letters scattered across her kitchen table.

Lena’s city was split. Families carved in two by a wall of stone and suspicion. She was a student, bright and sharp, and she cherished simple acts: posting letters, poems and pleas, under assumed names, over the boundary to the West.

Each envelope a ripple in the wide, cold sea. She described, with the careful art of one forever watched, her longing for open windows, for books unscreened by censors. Some weeks brought silent disappointment—no answer, as if the wall had gobbled her words. Others brought clandestine hope—a book wrapped in brown paper, notes of solidarity tucked between covers.

It was Lena, and those like her, who made the fissures in the great wall wider each year. She organized secret readings in cellars and scribbled verses of banned poets in the margins of even the dullest textbooks.

On November 9, while autumn fog pressed close against windowpanes, Lena was crammed among thousands by the Brandenburg Gate. She felt the wall thrum like a living thing as shouts and laughter built to a crescendo. She pressed her hands against the rough surface, fingers trembling.

That night, boundaries fell—not simply of stone, but of fear. Lena crossed over the line, breathing a new air, and for the first time spoke loudly in the open, words of her own choosing. The right to speak, she thought, was as vital as bread, or breath itself.

Makeda saw, in Lena’s nervous hope, the architecture of human rights as something ordinary women could build and breach.

Chapter 3: The Marketplace of Change

The air in Delhi was thick with the scents of jasmine and exhaust as Makeda’s vision swept forward. Here, beyond the pages, she stood in a bustling marketplace, following the story of Arjun.

Arjun limped slightly on his bad leg, overlooked by most. He was a chaiwala, serving tea to hurried businessmen and hungry laborers, but his voice was louder than the clang of silver spoons and the endless bus horns.

In the heart of the city’s midday, Arjun stood on a box and decried the new law that forbade street children from selling wares, the policy meant to “beautify” the central district. Children clustered at his feet, some with wide, hungry eyes. “We are not garbage,” Arjun proclaimed, raising a battered copy of the constitution high for all to see. “We are citizens.”

Passersby slowed to listen. Journalists scribbled in notepads; vendors nodded. Petition after petition flowed, inked out on worn paper with trembling hands. The movement grew, spilling beyond the narrow streets and into the columns of every legal review and the halls of the supreme court.

A year later, the law was repealed, replaced instead with a network of schools and health stations meant to lift children from the street, not sweep them aside.

Makeda pressed her palm to her cheek, hearing Arjun’s voice in every crowded bus and marketplace. Human rights, she realized, are not silent treaties. They bloom where demands are made, sometimes with anger, sometimes with courage, always with hope.

Chapter 4: Footsteps Across Sand

The next narrative swept Makeda into a world of dry wind and shimmering heat—the Maghreb, 2012. Here was Amal, eyes shining bright as the morning, feet already dusty from walking miles to reach her school on the outskirts of a small village.

Amal’s journey was not merely about distance. Girls in her village were discouraged from studying once they turned twelve; marriage, work, and tradition stood as stone gates on their path. But Amal loved numbers, loved how the logic of mathematics always obeyed her—unlike the adult world, with its tangled rules.

Each day, her mother combed her hair tightly, tucking away her defiance. “You’re too old,” whispered neighbors. “Girls aren’t meant to learn geometry.” But Amal pressed on, her resolve gathering force.

A visiting teacher from Rabat, noticing Amal’s hunger for learning, wrote about her to education advocates. The story wove through government halls and into nonprofits, who sent buses to ferry girls to school, delivered textbooks wrapped in blue twine.

By 2016, girls outnumbered boys in her school’s highest class. Amal returned from her studies in the capital, teaching younger girls the equations she once learned by candlelight. As she watched her students solving for x, Amal knew each number whispered a tiny promise of freedom.

Makeda realized that for many, the right to education was the first key to every locked door.

Chapter 5: The Right to Breathe

Makeda’s mind ached with story and longing. One more tale tugged, drawing her to São Paulo, with its ceaseless hum of human possibility and need.

There, in a favela pressed against the city’s roar, Joana grew up with lungs filled too often with smoke and chemical tang. Her brother, Pedro, was asthmatic, locked indoors on windless days when the air thickened gold with smog.

Ten years old and furious, Joana drew posters—thick lines declaring the “Right to Clean Air!” She led marches that snaked up and down steep alleys, her little army chanting. Journalists snapped photos; city councilors promised policies.

It was only the beginning. Joana demanded data, published articles, wrote furious op-eds in community papers. Environmental officials conducted studies, finding toxins at levels that shocked not just Joana, but the city’s elite as well.

Years passed, and policies finally changed—factories cleaned their stacks, buses switched to cleaner fuels, trees were planted in parks that had once been dumping grounds.

Joana’s brother, grown tall and strong, ran alongside her one morning, breathless with laughter. He grinned, gulping the clean air that had once been a distant dream.

Chapter 6: The Long Arc

Makeda closed the book slowly, heart racing with the echo of voices: testimony after testimony, defiance for the right to speak, to drink, to breathe, to learn. She thought of her own future—one where she might step beyond the library walls, carrying these stories as torches.

She drifted across the plaza that evening, the humid air promising rain. Everywhere she looked, voices mingled—arguing, bargaining, teaching—and each seemed to add another note to the ancient, unfinished song of human dignity.

Makeda stood at the edge of the crowd, watching children play in the dusk. One fell, scraping her knee on the rough stone, but rose again, determined. All at once, Makeda understood: the journey for human rights would never be finished in one lifetime. Each generation carried, in its stories, a bright thread woven from the longing, pain, and promise of those who came before.

She walked slowly toward home, every step in sync with those voices—Afewerk’s courage, Lena’s hope, Arjun’s boldness, Amal’s persistence, Joana’s fire—each a note carried forward by the restless wind. These were the roots of freedom, stretching wide and deep, refusing to be silenced.

And so, beneath a sky brimming with the hope of rain, Makeda joined her voice to theirs, stepping boldly into the ongoing journey—one built on the stories and sacrifices of millions, and the tomorrows yet to come.
