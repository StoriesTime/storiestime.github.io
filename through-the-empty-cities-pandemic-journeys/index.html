Through the Empty Cities: Pandemic Journeys


The day the last train ran, the city became an echo of its own memory. Above the surface, towers of glass and concrete stared at one another, their reflections splitting in puddles rain had forgotten to dissipate. Down below, in the early blue of morning, Julian threw the duffel bag over his shoulder and slipped out of his apartment with a silence learned through solitude.

He walked streets once bursting with shouts and music, their emptiness pressed in on all sides. There were no cars. Sometimes the wind dabbled with a discarded newspaper, looping headlines over cracked pavement. “CONTAGION SPREADS FASTER THAN EXPECTED.” “LOCKDOWN EXTENDED.” “ESSENTIAL TRAVEL ONLY.” Words as brittle as leaves, trod underfoot and dissolved by rain.

Julian didn’t look up at the windows; behind each one might linger eyes desperate for news, hope, or accusation. He’d memorized the city’s arteries in better days: the coffee shop on the corner where he’d spent lazy Sundays, the bakery with soft croissants and hard opinions, the bookstore that closed two years before the world did. They were all closed now—shut behind sheets of plywood, spray-painted instructions or slogans, locks that no longer felt necessary.

Yet, here and there, the city resisted erasure. Ivy dared the bricks, a stubborn cat carved a route between yawning trash bins, and human traces survived against the odds: chalk rainbows on a driveway, a faded teddy bear in a window, a string of Christmas lights left burning long after December.

The journey wasn’t just his. Word had spread—quietly, online, through hush-hush neighborly exchanges—that out beyond the city’s core, some smaller communities had managed to fight back the spread. There were stories of communal farms, amateur nurses, solar-powered neighborhoods raising greenhouses and singing together on their porches. Survival, maybe, but of a different flavor. This promise hung before Julian, a light in the fog.

He walked. His phone lay unused, battery full; no new texts, no one left to wait for. Just compass maps downloaded six months ago, and a list in his head of what he needed to find.

Checkpoint One: The Library Square

The first checkpoint sat at Library Square. Once the heart of debate, its steps were deserted but for a hand-painted sign: “Free Books, Take or Leave.” Someone had left paperbacks in a box under the stone columns—a battered copy of The Stand, a slim volume of poems, an algebra textbook, receipt still marking chapter five.

Julian took the poems, tucking them in his bag. The scent of must and dust felt like someone whispering, “You’re not alone.” Around the corner, a mural depicted masked nurses with capes flying behind them, fists raised to the sky. Someone had sharpied “Thank You” in every accent the neighborhood had ever spoken.

He moved on, bypassing the barricades. Past the old subway entrance, where graffiti blossomed unchecked: “WHEN WE RETURN, WE REMEMBER.” The unmistakable scrawl of someone who needed the city to know they had lived.

Checkpoint Two: The Riverfront

Wind scoured the riverfront, flipping detritus with icy precision. He stopped to watch the water slide by, slow and silty, the far banks barely visible in spring haze. There had been floating parties here once, a fleet of kayaks joined by ropes bearing garlands of red cups, laughter mingling with the calls of seagulls. Now the docks creaked under their own weight.

Yet, amid the silence, life claimed its inch. A fisherman in makeshift waders stood nearly motionless, casting lines into uncertain depths. As Julian approached, the fisherman turned, face half-masked.

“You caught much?” Julian asked, voice scratchy from disuse.

The man shrugged, holding up a string with two small fish shining like coins. “Enough for me. Heading out of town?”

Julian nodded. “Or trying, anyway. Heard there’s places still making it work.”

The fisherman looked him up and down, measuring the risk. “If you’re crossing River Street, stick to the alley behind the tea shop. Military checkpoint at the main intersection—the last sweep was two nights ago, but you never know.”

He thanked the fisherman and pressed a protein bar into the man’s hand. The gesture said more than words ever could. As Julian turned, he glanced back; the fisherman saluted with two fingers, wordlessly wishing him luck.

Checkpoint Three: The Old Rail Tracks

Getting out required crossing the freight lines north of the stadium. Here the city had stilled to post-industrial reverence: weeds cracked the ties, a traffic light cycled through red, yellow, green with no vehicles to heed its routines. A raccoon sauntered across the rails, unconcerned by human affairs.

Julian’s path crunched along gravel, boots straining against the uneven ground. He followed the rail for half a mile, ducking beneath a bridge whose parapet was decorated with hundreds of fluttering ribbons. Each represented a loved one lost. He paused, caught between prayer and memory. His father had worked these rails once, driving engines that whistled midnight signals to children tucked in bed.

“Hang in there, Pop,” Julian whispered, pressing his palm to the cold iron before moving on. The journey didn’t promise healing, but each step felt like gathering pieces of a shattered world, tucking them into a pocket until they made enough of a whole to hope again.

Checkpoint Four: The Suburban Edge

Entering the suburbs felt like wading into shallow water: the noise softened, space grew, lawns gone wild with dandelions and sagging fences. A tire swing hung as if waiting for a child returned from a vanished recess. Houses carried scars—some spray-painted with warnings, others with ribbons or handprints, symbols that meant “safe” or “quarantined” or “help needed.”

He passed a community garden, its gate ajar. Inside, pairs of hands worked silently—planting, weeding, watering, all masked and wary, brow creased from months of watching headlines. He stood at the entrance and raised a hand. After a moment, a woman approached, keeping her distance.

“You lost?” she asked, voice muffled by her mask.

“Not lost. Moving through. Heard there are safer places farther out—Carlsdon, maybe. Know the way?”

She considered. “No buses. Roads watched sometimes. Cut through the woodlots along the old highway. Avoid the overpass—too exposed.”

“Rough going?”

“Depends how healthy you are. Or how desperate.”

He smiled, hollow but grateful. “Both, I guess.”

She dipped her head, eyes soft above her mask. “Stay to the trees. If you hit the barn with the blue roof, you’ll find signs. And… be careful.”

He nodded, feeling the unseen boundary between caution and kindness.

Checkpoint Five: The Outskirts

The sun fell, tossing gold and rose against a sky already bruised by fading clouds. Julian’s legs ached, the duffel now heavier from donations picked up along the route—dried beans, a bottle of hand sanitizer, two fresh apples wrapped in cloth. His sense of time stretched thin as a rubber band; days and hours formed a continuous loop, the only way he marked them by worn-down milestones and the hunger gnawing at midnight.

The trees thickened, leaves unfurling in the year’s urgent growth. Birds strafed the underbrush, a rabbit bolted for cover. Here, the city became rumor instead of reality, its sounds muffled to a memory under layer upon layer of living green.

The barn with the blue roof gleamed out of the trees, stubbornly vivid even in the creeping dusk. On its side, someone had painted a bright sunflower, petals sprawling around the words “We are still here.”

He stopped, heart surging. He crept to the door, pausing at the edge of the porch, and called out softly: “Hello?”

Lights flicked on inside—real lights, soft incandescent, humming with a promise of comfort the city had long since forgotten. A woman stepped out, masked but smiling beneath her eyes.

“We heard you were coming. You’re safe now,” she said.

Arrival

Inside the barn, warmth reached Julian first: the scent of bread, the murmur of voices, the presence of living, breathing people. Not a crowd—far from it—but enough hands to nurse a town, enough laughter strained and tentative, yet resilient.

They offered him soup and water, a seat beside the stove. He heard stories exchanged, each one stitched with heartache and hope—families separated by quarantine, friendships tested in the fires of necessity, new loves sparked by the pressure of isolation.

A young teacher explained how they’d set up a radio to keep in touch with outlying farms. A nurse described makeshift masks and homebrew sanitizer. An old man played guitar, the notes so fragile and sweet that Julian blinked back tears.

For the first time in months, he slept without dreaming of sirens, crowds, and loss. The next morning, he woke early, stepping out to see a sheet of dew veiling the grass. Blue sky promised a day untouched by the city’s relentless gloom.

He wandered to the edge of the yard. The others joined him as the sun cleared the treetops, spilling light across fields where new crops stretched toward the day. They stood together, momentarily silent—a new tribe formed not from geography or old alliances, but from survival, empathy, and the willingness to hope for something not yet found.

Julian’s journey was over. Or perhaps, standing among newly kindred souls, it had only just begun.

The world, reduced and rewritten by crisis, built itself from fragments: a mural here, a shared apple there, a mended quilt of songs and stories and small generosities. Through the empty cities, old roads led to new beginnings. Julian and his companions would map the future, their footprints stitching promise through the quiet ruins. As cities slumbered, these small communities nursed the world’s embers, waiting for the morning when the windows would open again, and the great hum of life—startling, vibrant, and whole—would return.
