The Flooded Cities: Chronicles of a Drowned World


It had been years since the world had begun to drown, and still, no one could say for sure what had truly caused it. The oceans, once distant and tame, had crept inland, turning entire metropolises into sunken ruins. The floodwaters, inexorable and merciless, had ravaged the cities of old. And in their wake, the survivors were left to navigate a changed world, a world where the horizon was dominated by the endless expanse of water.

Among the scattered remnants of humanity, one place still held meaning: the city of Marivana.

Marivana had once been a bustling hub, its skyline a testament to the triumph of human ingenuity. Skyscrapers soared like steel titans, and the streets hummed with the sounds of life. But as the sea level began to rise, the city slowly succumbed. The first to go were the lower districts, the ones closest to the shore, where the ocean surged up with frightening intensity. Buildings were swallowed whole, entire neighborhoods submerged, and the streets became rivers. The sound of rushing water became a constant, like the breath of a beast preparing to devour everything in its path.

At first, people had tried to fight. They built sea walls, raised levees, and engineered pump systems to drain the floodwaters. But nature was relentless. The oceans rose higher, faster, and soon, the idea of holding back the tide became a fantasy. By the time the waters had engulfed half the city, the survivors were left with no choice but to flee.

Among them was Kora, a former marine biologist who had once studied the oceans and their ecosystems. She had spent her entire career studying the sea, but nothing could have prepared her for the tragedy that had unfolded. Her home, her lab, and everything she had once known had been swept away in the deluge.

Now, Kora roamed the ruins of Marivana, a place where life had once thrived but had now become a drowned wasteland. She navigated through flooded streets, searching for survivors, for anything that might provide hope. The waters had risen so much that entire districts were completely submerged, their once-grand buildings now skeletal structures poking out of the waves. Every now and then, she would pass an old monument or statue, a forgotten piece of history, half-submerged in the ocean’s embrace.

Kora had joined a group of survivors who had taken refuge in the highlands, away from the water’s reach. They called themselves the Arkites, survivors of the cities who had built their new homes in the mountains, away from the flood. But even in the highlands, the threat of the rising waters was never far away. The flood had changed everything, and no one knew how long they would be safe.

As she trudged through the murky waters, Kora heard the faint sound of voices. She turned toward the source, hoping to find some sign of life. She waded through the flooded alleyways, her footsteps muffled by the water, until she reached a small gathering of people huddled together on a raised platform.

The group was led by an elderly man, whose face bore the marks of a life lived through countless hardships. His name was Elias, and he had been one of the first to witness the collapse of Marivana. He was a man of few words, but his presence commanded respect.

“We were hoping you’d come back, Kora,” Elias said, his voice low and steady. “You were always the one who understood the water. Perhaps you can still make sense of what’s happening.”

Kora nodded, though uncertainty filled her heart. She had always been a scientist, a woman of logic and reason, but in this new world, the rules seemed to have changed. The oceans, once predictable and governed by natural laws, now seemed to have a mind of their own. There were no answers, only questions.

“I’ll try,” she said, more to herself than to Elias. “But I don’t know if there’s anything left to understand.”

Chapter Two: The Last Witness

Weeks passed, and Kora’s return to the Arkites was marked by the kind of quiet acceptance that had become the norm in a world where survival was a constant struggle. The Arkites had their routines, their small hopes and dreams, but there was always the underlying fear that the rising tide would one day reach them, even in their highland refuge.

Kora continued her research, gathering samples from the flooded cities and studying the strange patterns of marine life that had begun to emerge in the wake of the flood. New species, mutated by the changing conditions, were thriving in the brackish waters. Kora had always known that the ocean was a force of immense power, but never had she seen it so alive, so unpredictable.

One day, as she combed through the wreckage of an old library that had once belonged to the Marivana Institute, she stumbled upon something unexpected: a journal. Its pages were yellowed and brittle, the ink faded by time and water, but it was still legible. The author, a man named Dr. Arthur Vance, had written about the early days of the flood. He spoke of his theories regarding the cause of the deluge, his attempts to warn the government, and his growing fear that the flood was not just a natural disaster, but something far more sinister.

As Kora read the journal, she found herself drawn into Vance’s story. He had been a climatologist, a man who had dedicated his life to studying the Earth’s changing climate. According to his notes, the flood had not been a simple consequence of global warming. No, Vance believed that the oceans were rising in response to something far more mysterious: an ancient force that had been awakened from the depths of the planet.

Kora shuddered as she read Vance’s final entry: “The waters are not simply rising. They are reclaiming what was once theirs. The oceans have begun to remember.”

Chapter Three: The Hidden Depths

The words haunted Kora as she shared them with Elias and the other Arkites. They had hoped that the flood had been nothing more than a tragic consequence of human neglect, but now, there was something else to consider. Something deeper.

“We’ve been looking at this all wrong,” Kora said, her voice filled with urgency. “This isn’t just about climate change. It’s about something much older, something that has been awakened beneath the earth. The oceans are responding to something, and we need to figure out what it is before it’s too late.”

Elias frowned, his hands gripping the railing of the platform as he stared out over the horizon, where the ocean stretched endlessly. “You think it’s something we can stop?”

“I don’t know,” Kora admitted. “But if we don’t understand what’s happening, we’ll never be able to survive it.”

As the days wore on, Kora and Elias began to explore the remnants of the drowned cities, looking for any clues that might shed light on the cause of the flood. What they discovered shocked them. There were signs of an ancient civilization buried beneath the waters, a civilization that had known the oceans in a way that no one in the modern world had.

The ruins were old, older than anything Kora had ever studied. There were symbols carved into stone, depicting strange sea creatures and patterns that seemed to follow the ebb and flow of the tides. As they ventured deeper into the flooded ruins, they uncovered a series of underground chambers, where strange devices, half-corroded by time, still hummed with a faint energy.

It was here that Kora made a discovery that would change everything. Among the ruins, she found a piece of technology unlike anything she had ever seen. It was a small, crystalline object, pulsing with an energy that seemed almost alive. She reached out to touch it, and as her fingers brushed against its surface, the water around her began to churn.

The sea had responded.

Chapter Four: The Awakening

Kora returned to the Arkites, carrying the strange crystalline object with her. It was clear now that whatever force had been awakened beneath the waves was connected to this ancient technology. The ocean was not just rising—it was alive. And it was calling to something, or someone.

The Arkites gathered around Kora, waiting for her to explain what had happened. “I think I’ve found the key,” she said, her voice filled with both fear and awe. “This artifact… it’s not just a relic. It’s part of something much bigger, something that controls the tides. If we can understand it, we might be able to change the course of the flood.”

Elias studied the object in Kora’s hands, his brow furrowed. “And if we don’t? What happens then?”

Kora looked out at the ocean, the endless expanse of water that had become both enemy and home. “If we don’t, the oceans will continue to rise. And one day, they’ll claim everything.”

The flood had only just begun. But now, for the first time in years, Kora felt a flicker of hope. The ocean had not just drowned their cities—it had given them a chance to fight back.

But what would they uncover as they dug deeper into the mysteries of the drowned world? Only time would tell, and the tides were still turning.
