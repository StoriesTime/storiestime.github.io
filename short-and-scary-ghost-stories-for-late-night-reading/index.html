Short and Scary Ghost Stories for Late Night Reading


In the dead of night, when the world outside fell silent and the moon cast eerie shadows over the sleeping town of Graymoor, a cluster of short and scary ghost stories began to take on a life of their own. These tales were not for the faint of heart, and they thrived in the late hours when the veil between the living and the dead felt thinnest.

The Hollow’s Curse

Nestled on the outskirts of Graymoor was a place locals whispered about with a mixture of fear and fascination—The Hollow. An old, abandoned cemetery surrounded by twisted oaks and sagging iron gates. It was said the Hollow was cursed, that those who dared to stay past midnight often heard the whispers of restless spirits, their voices rising on the cold wind like a mournful chant.

Story One: The Lantern Keeper

One such story told of a lantern keeper who once tended the graves in the Hollow. His lantern was said to glow with a light that never faltered, a beacon to those who were lost. But the keeper’s spirit was trapped, doomed to wander the cemetery forever, searching for a soul brave enough to take his place.

Jacob, a skeptical teenager from Graymoor, decided to test the legend. One foggy night, he slipped through the rusted gate at exactly midnight, lantern in hand—an old oil lamp he’d swiped from his grandfather’s attic.

As he ventured deeper into the Hollow, the air grew icy, and the trees seemed to lean closer, their branches gripping at his clothes. Then came the whisper: a soft, almost pleading voice, echoing from the shadows.

“Give me your light… guide me home…” it spoke.

Jacob’s heart pounded. The oil lamp flickered violently as if in response. Suddenly, an apparition in tattered clothes appeared, eyes hollow and desperate. It was the lantern keeper, eternal and unchanged by time. Jacob dropped the lamp, but it floated back to the ghost’s hand, reigniting with an unearthly glow.

Frozen, Jacob realized the curse was true—the keeper could never rest, and neither could he.

Story Two: The Silent Room

Back in town, a young woman named Elsie had recently moved into an old Victorian house. The realtor dismissed the house’s history as mere ghost stories, but Elsie quickly discovered there was a room in the attic that never felt right.

The “Silent Room,” she called it.

No matter the hour, no matter the noise elsewhere, that room was eerily quiet. No creaks, no rustles, no signs of life. And yet, every night just past 2 a.m., Elsie would hear a soft tapping on the door, like fingernails lightly drumming.

Summoning her courage, one night she opened the door. The room was empty, save for a single rocking chair, gently swaying despite the lack of breeze.

Elsie stepped in and felt a chill wrap around her. Then she saw it—the shadow of a child, pale and flickering, peering at her from the corner. The child’s eyes whispered a story of a lost soul, trapped and longing for freedom, warning her silently to leave.

Story Three: The Watcher in the Woods

Outside Graymoor, a dense forest known as Raven’s Grove was infamous for strange disappearances. Legend spoke of a spectral figure known simply as “The Watcher,” who guarded the woods and ensured no one disturbed its haunted secrets.

One autumn evening, a group of friends dared each other to spend the night within the Grove. As darkness stripped the forest of color, an oppressive feeling settled. Branches cracked unnaturally, shadows darted just beyond sight, and a cold presence seemed to stalk their every step.

Hours deep into the night, the Watcher made itself known—a towering figure engulfed in mist, with burning eyes that pierced the soul. The friends scattered, frantic and terrified, but one by one, the whispered lament of the woods claimed them.

By dawn, only one returned, pale and breathless, with eyes haunted by what she had seen—a warning to all who heard the tale.

The Thin Veil of Night

Graymoor’s ghost stories, passed down through generations, painted a portrait of a town forever entwined with spirits and mystery. The Hollow’s restless dead, the Silent Room’s unseen child, and the Watcher lurking in the woods became warnings whispered late at night, never far from the flicker of a campfire or the glow of a bedside lamp.

To read these short and scary ghost stories before sleep was to invite a brush with the supernatural, to feel the chill of something unseen watching from just beyond the edge of the world. They reminded everyone that, in the quiet of late night, the shadows are never truly empty—and the stories never truly end.
