The Silent Struggle: Understanding Invisible Illnesses


Jenna pulled her coat tighter around her shoulders as the wind bit against her face. The autumn chill was sharper today, a reminder that winter was just around the corner. She hated this time of year—not for the pumpkin spice lattes or the crimson leaves but for how it made her body feel. Every drop in temperature exaggerated her pain, turning simple tasks like getting out of bed into monumental challenges. She pushed open the door to her favorite café, the warmth inside rushing to meet her like a long-lost friend.

The barista greeted her with a familiar smile. “Hey, Jenna! The usual today?”

Jenna nodded, managing a faint smile in return. She moved to her usual corner table near the window and carefully lowered herself into the chair. Her body protested with a throbbing ache that radiated from her joints, creeping up her back and settling deep into her muscles. It wasn’t always this bad, but today was one of those days. It was difficult for anyone to notice—it always was. From the outside, she looked like any other woman in her early thirties. Inside, though, was a battle no one else could see.

Her coffee arrived, and she wrapped her fingers around the warm ceramic cup, letting the heat seep into her hands. Sometimes, she imagined explaining what it was like to a stranger, someone who might notice the tension in her shoulders or the weariness in her eyes.

“It’s like walking through life with a backpack full of bricks,” she’d say. “Except no one else can see it, so they don’t know you’re carrying the extra weight.”

But she never did explain, not really. It was hard to talk about something you couldn’t prove. Her doctors had been kind but mostly puzzled. The tests always came back normal—her blood work, scans, everything. “Maybe it’s stress,” one doctor had suggested, tilting his head sympathetically as though offering her a consolation prize. Jenna had stopped trying to explain after that.

Her phone buzzed on the table, jolting her from her thoughts. It was a text from her friend Mara.

Lunch next week? I miss you!

Jenna stared at the screen, her thumb hovering over the keyboard. She loved Mara; they had been friends since college, but the thought of committing to plans made her stomach twist. What if next week was another bad week? What if she had to cancel again, like last time? The guilt of disappointing people was nearly as heavy as the pain.

Jenna sighed and replied, Let’s aim for Thursday!

Setting her phone down, she leaned back in her chair, letting her eyes drift to the street outside. People bustled past, bundled in coats and scarves, their breath visible in the crisp air. Jenna often wondered how many of them were like her—smiling on the surface but harboring their own silent struggles. It wasn’t just her pain that went unnoticed. It was the exhaustion, the mental fog, the days when brushing her teeth felt like climbing a mountain.

She took a sip of her coffee, the warmth spreading through her chest. She had tried therapy once, thinking it might help to share her frustrations, but even that had left her feeling hollow. “Invisible illnesses can be incredibly isolating,” the therapist had said, her pen scratching across the notepad. “Finding a support system is key.”

Support system. That was a nice idea, but talking to people about something they couldn’t physically see or understand wasn’t easy. Even her closest friends sometimes looked confused when she tried to explain. “But you don’t look sick,” they would say, their brows knit in concern. Jenna knew they meant well, but those words cut deep, a reminder of how unseen her battles truly were.

Leaving the café, she decided to take the long way home. The brisk air was sharp but refreshing, and the movement helped loosen her stiff joints, if only slightly. Ahead, a park came into view. A group of teenagers were throwing a frisbee, their laughter ringing out like wind chimes. Watching them, Jenna couldn’t help but feel a pang of envy. She couldn’t remember the last time her body had allowed her the freedom to run, to leap, to laugh without restraint.

As she rounded a corner, she noticed a small bookstore she hadn’t seen before. Curious, she pushed open the door, a soft bell announcing her arrival. The scent of old paper and ink filled the air, and she felt an odd sense of comfort. Shelves lined the walls, packed tightly with books on everything from history to self-help. One title in particular caught her eye: Living with What You Can’t See. She pulled it from the shelf, noting the author’s name. It was a memoir.

The woman behind the counter smiled warmly as Jenna approached. “That’s a good one,” she said. “Hard to find people willing to talk about those things openly.”

Jenna nodded silently, clutching the book to her chest as though it was some kind of lifeline. At home, she settled onto the couch, propped up by pillows, and began to read. The author, a woman named Elise, described her journey living with an invisible illness. The uncertainty, the skepticism from others, the longing for validation—it was all there, as though Elise had pulled the words straight from Jenna’s own mind.

When she turned the final page, Jenna felt an unfamiliar emotion stir within her. It wasn’t quite hope, but something adjacent to it—a quiet determination to stop shrinking away from her reality. If Elise could write, could share her truth with the world, maybe Jenna could find a way to speak hers, even if only to a few.

The next day, as she sat once again in her corner of the café, Jenna opened her laptop. She hesitated only briefly before her fingers began to move across the keyboard. The words came slowly at first but then with increasing ease. She wrote about the bad days, the guilt, the frustration of not being believed. She wrote for herself, but also for people like her—those who carried their struggles in silence, longing for someone to say, I see you. I understand.

Her reflection in the window caught her eye, and for the first time in months, Jenna saw something other than exhaustion staring back. She saw courage, perhaps small and fragile but growing nonetheless.

When she hit “post,” Jenna didn’t expect miracles. She didn’t expect her life to suddenly change or her pain to vanish. But she did hope, quietly and fiercely, that her words might remind someone else that they were not alone—that their silent struggle, invisible as it might be, was still valid, still worth fighting.

And in that moment, for the first time in what felt like forever, the weight of her invisible burden felt just a little bit lighter.
