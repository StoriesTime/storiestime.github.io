How Not to Be a Superhero


In the quiet town of Evergreen Hills, there was an extraordinary man who was perfectly average. His name was Daniel Miller, and he worked as a software engineer for a small, forgettable tech company. He spent his days coding, drinking coffee, and avoiding social interactions. Despite his seemingly ordinary life, Daniel was convinced that he was destined for something more, something greater.

It all started one rainy evening when Daniel discovered a small package outside his apartment. It was an unmarked box, wrapped in weathered brown paper, and left precariously on his doorstep. Intrigued, Daniel brought it inside, unopened, and set it down on his kitchen counter. He stared at it for a while, wondering if it was some kind of sign. Perhaps it was destiny knocking at his door.

After a few minutes of overthinking, he opened the box. Inside, wrapped in old velvet cloth, was a strange, glowing object that resembled a cross between a crystal and a piece of ancient technology. It pulsed faintly with an eerie light, and as Daniel touched it, something inexplicable happened. His body jerked, as if jolted by electricity, and a flood of thoughts rushed through his mind. In that moment, he knew: he was meant to be a superhero.

Daniel stood there, unsure of what to do next. Superhero. The word echoed in his head. He had always dreamed of being a hero — who hadn’t? He imagined wearing a cape, saving lives, and becoming a symbol of hope for the world. But there was one tiny problem: Daniel had absolutely no idea what to do.

The object in his hand continued to pulse as if waiting for him to make the first move, but Daniel, ever the practical person, felt a wave of doubt. Could he actually be a superhero? Was this some kind of elaborate prank? Maybe the object was a mistake.

He considered throwing it away. After all, maybe the universe hadn’t meant for him to be a hero. But something deep down told him that this was his moment. He couldn’t let it slip away.

With trembling hands, Daniel made his decision. He would try.

Chapter Two: The Costume Dilemma

The first thing Daniel realized was that becoming a superhero required a costume. And not just any costume. No, this was serious business. A real superhero needed a costume that stood out. It had to be bold, striking, something that commanded attention. But Daniel had no fashion sense. None at all.

He spent hours on the internet researching superhero costumes, which only made the situation worse. Every option seemed either impractical or ridiculously flashy. He was a man of practicality, after all. He could just wear a plain outfit, right? Maybe a hoodie and jeans? But no, superheroes didn’t wear jeans. They wore capes or tights, and that was non-negotiable.

After weeks of indecision, Daniel finally took the plunge. He ordered a custom suit online — dark blue, with gold trim, and a minimalist emblem of a stylized “D” on the chest. It arrived two weeks later, and when he tried it on, it fit perfectly. He looked like a cross between a superhero and a confused professional wrestler, but he didn’t care. He was ready to make his debut.

With a deep breath, he stood in front of the mirror, flexing awkwardly. “This is it,” he whispered to himself. “I’m going to save the world.”

But then, reality set in.

Chapter Three: The First Test

Daniel’s first official act as a superhero came when he witnessed a robbery outside a convenience store. He had been walking home after a long day of coding when he saw two men with masks and guns threatening the clerk behind the counter.

In a split second, Daniel knew what he had to do. He took off running towards the store, his new superhero suit flapping in the wind. His cape — which he hadn’t really tested out before — got caught on a street sign and nearly tripped him. He was sure he looked ridiculous, but there was no turning back.

He reached the door, threw it open, and stood there in all his superhero glory. “Stop right there, villains!” he shouted, trying to sound authoritative.

The robbers froze, clearly confused.

Daniel, feeling a surge of excitement, repeated, “You heard me. Stop right there!” He raised his arms in what he hoped was an intimidating gesture but looked more like someone trying to hold back a sneeze.

The robbers burst out laughing. “Who the heck are you supposed to be?” one of them asked, eyeing his outfit.

Daniel paused, unsure how to respond. “I’m… I’m the Defender of Evergreen Hills!” he said, his voice gaining strength.

For a moment, it looked like the robbers might take him seriously. Then one of them pointed at his suit. “Buddy, that looks like something from a kid’s cartoon. Are you really trying to stop us with… that?”

Daniel glanced down at himself. His suit was a little too tight, and the golden emblem seemed to shimmer in a way that made it look more comical than heroic. The robbers took another step toward him, clearly not scared. This was not how things were supposed to go.

In a panic, Daniel launched himself toward them, attempting some sort of high-flying move he’d seen in action movies. He jumped into the air, but his cape got caught on the doorframe, and he crashed to the floor. The robbers burst into laughter again.

“Yeah, no. We’re out of here,” one of them said, waving his gun toward Daniel before bolting out the door.

Daniel sat there for a moment, dazed, his pride shattered. His first act as a superhero had ended in complete failure. He slowly got up, dusted himself off, and walked out of the store, trying to act like nothing had happened. The clerk gave him a sympathetic look, but Daniel avoided eye contact and left as quickly as possible.

Chapter Four: The Identity Crisis

After that disastrous first attempt, Daniel couldn’t shake the feeling that maybe being a superhero wasn’t as glamorous as he had imagined. He spent the next few weeks brooding over his failure. He had tried, and he had failed miserably. And the more he thought about it, the more he realized that he wasn’t cut out for this.

He still wore the suit, though. It was in his closet, and he couldn’t bring himself to get rid of it. Every time he looked at it, he remembered the thrill of thinking he could make a difference. But deep down, Daniel knew he wasn’t ready. And that terrified him.

One night, as he walked through the park in his superhero attire, contemplating what to do next, he encountered a group of teenagers who noticed his outfit. They laughed, pointing at him and calling out sarcastic remarks.

“Hey, are you here to save us from boredom?” one of them yelled.

Daniel froze. His heart raced as the teens gathered around him, mocking his every move. He wanted to defend himself, to tell them that he was the real deal. But the truth was, he wasn’t sure if he was. The doubts crept in.

“You’re not a superhero,” another teen said. “You’re just a guy in a silly costume. Go home.”

That was when Daniel realized something: Being a superhero wasn’t just about wearing the suit. It wasn’t about the flashy powers, the cape, or the emblem. It was about being ready — not just physically, but mentally. And he wasn’t ready.

Chapter Five: The Realization

Days passed, and Daniel couldn’t bring himself to wear the costume again. He thought about the things he had learned from his brief and somewhat humiliating stint as a wannabe superhero. It wasn’t about trying to live up to an impossible ideal. It wasn’t about fighting villains or saving the day. It was about finding the courage to face life’s challenges head-on.

He had tried to be someone he wasn’t. The truth was, he didn’t need to be a superhero to make a difference. He just needed to be himself. So he put the suit away, stuffed it in the back of his closet, and vowed to live a life of quiet integrity instead of chasing the impossible dream of being someone else.

From that moment on, Daniel focused on what truly mattered: being a good person, a reliable friend, and a hardworking professional. He may not have had superpowers, but he had the power of choice, the power of kindness, and the power to make the world a little bit better, one small act at a time.

In the end, Daniel realized that you didn’t need to wear a cape or have superhuman abilities to be a hero. Sometimes, the greatest heroism lies in simply being who you are, flaws and all.

And so, Daniel Miller became something far more remarkable than a superhero — he became someone who embraced his ordinary life and found extraordinary purpose in the small moments that made up each day.

The End.
