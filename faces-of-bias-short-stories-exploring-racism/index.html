Faces of Bias: Short Stories Exploring Racism


Monday mornings at the Eastwood Public Library were usually quiet, the soft rustle of pages turning filling the air. Maya, a young Black woman and an aspiring writer, loved the calm space to craft her stories. Today, however, the space felt weighted.

She noticed it when a man at the table across from her closed his laptop as she walked by, his eyes lingering a bit too long. He grabbed his bag and left with a tight smile, his body stiff. It wasn’t the first time people’s gazes trailed her in ways that felt heavier than curiosity.

Maya’s friend Sam, who was white, joined her later. When Maya described the man’s uncomfortable behavior, Sam dismissed it. “Maybe he was just having a bad day?” he offered.

But Maya knew. There was a pattern, invisible yet persistent, linking these moments. The silent judgments, the wary looks, the space reserved for suspicion or fear just because of the color of her skin. It wasn’t often explicit; more a quiet undercurrent that seeped into everyday interactions.

She shared this with Sam, who listened but struggled to grasp the subtlety. To him, it seemed like paranoia—until he watched the man’s body language when Maya approached the checkout desk. Her heart thudded as she expected him to object to her using the library card.

Instead, Sam intervened without a word, standing beside her with a friendly smile. The man’s demeanor shifted immediately, welcoming.

“See?” Sam said, a flicker of realization dawning. “My presence changed his reaction.”

Maya nodded. “That’s the face of bias: invisible, calibrated to react differently based on who you are. And sometimes, the only way to pierce it is through unexpected solidarity.”

2. The Interview

Jared had always dreamed of working in tech, but the interview for a promising startup in Silicon Valley felt different.

He walked into the room, white shirt crisp and polished résumé in hand. The panel was all white men, their questions sharp and delivered with controlled politeness. When they asked about his experience, Jared answered confidently but noticed a subtle shift in the tone when he mentioned his previous role at a smaller, minority-owned company.

Halfway through, an interviewer said, “Your background is… diverse. How do you think you’ll fit into our culture?”

The question stung—not for the word “diverse,” but for the subtle implication that he might not belong.

After the interview, Jared called his mentor, Lisa. “It felt like they were seeing more my skin than my skills,” he said.

Lisa nodded, her voice steady. “Barriers like these are part of systemic bias—prejudice embedded in structures and attitudes long before we get to this room.”

Jared pondered over it. The discrimination wasn’t always blatant. Sometimes it came in the form of coded language, cultural gatekeeping, or questions that weren’t really about qualifications but about “fit,” a euphemism for exclusion.

Instead of retreating, Jared decided to write a blog post about his experience. It spread online, resonating with many who had faced similar, unspoken barriers. Suddenly his story wasn’t just about one interview—it was about the larger fabric of bias in professional spaces.

3. The Playground Conflict

On a sunny Saturday, a group of kids played soccer in the small park near their homes. Lina, a Latina girl with quick feet and bright laughter, kicked the ball with joy.

A group of boys approached, one of them sneering: “Hey, this is our field. You don’t belong here.”

Lina was stunned but refused to give the ball away. The boys pushed harder, teasing her with slurs and mockery tied to her heritage.

Nearby, a woman named Mrs. Chen watched from a bench. She was an immigrant who had faced her own share of bias but understood the importance of standing up.

She walked over and said calmly, “This park is for everyone. And those words? They make no one proud.”

The boys grudgingly backed off, and Lina’s smile returned. Mrs. Chen sat down and shared stories of her struggles with discrimination and resilience.

Lina felt a connection and strength she hadn’t before. She realized that racism, even in small places like a neighborhood game, had real effects, shaping how young children saw themselves and others.

4. A Family Divided

In a modest home in Atlanta, family dinners were a shared ritual. But tonight was different. Marcus’s cousin, Allison, had brought her new boyfriend, a Black man named Elijah.

The room shifted as some family members’ smiles tightened, others exchanged glances that spoke volumes.

Over the meal, a comment slipped: “Do you really think he’s right for you? We worry about how people might see things.”

Elijah caught the tone. Nobody said these things directly to him, but the invisible walls were clear.

Allison defended Elijah, but Marcus sat quietly, feeling the discomfort of tradition clashing with change.

Later, Marcus talked with Elijah. “I’m sorry you had to sit through that,” Marcus said.

Elijah smiled softly. “I’m used to it. But I see hope in your family—it’s not easy to challenge these old biases.”

Marcus understood then that racism didn’t just divide strangers; it could fracture families too, creating tensions between love and ingrained prejudice.

5. The Mirror

Shana stood before the mirror, adjusting the scarf that covered her hair. As a Muslim woman of Middle Eastern descent living in a small rural town, she often felt the weight of others’ assumptions before they spoke a single word.

At work, coworkers whispered when she wasn’t around, making jokes based on stereotypes she knew all too well. Strangers on the street sometimes crossed the road to avoid her, eyes filled with suspicion.

One day, a new employee, Jenna, approached her. “I want to understand,” she said quietly. “Sometimes people say things around me that I don’t know how to handle.”

They started sharing stories over lunch, slowly peeling away layers of misunderstanding.

Shana realized that bias was not always rooted in malice but often in ignorance and fear. Through dialogue, she could chip away at some of the barriers.

Reflection in the mirror became not just about appearance, but the strength she carried to face the world’s prejudices and still stand tall.

What These Stories Reveal

Each story reflects a face of bias that manifests subtly or overtly in daily life. The librarian’s wary glance, the coded interview question, the guarded playground, the cautious family meal, the whispered workplace doubts — all show how racism permeates spaces we inhabit, shaping experiences and identities.

These narratives remind us that racism is not a relic of the past but an ongoing reality for many. Understanding the nuances—how bias can be invisible yet deeply felt—is crucial to recognizing its impact.

Importantly, these stories illustrate ways people confront, navigate, and sometimes dismantle these biases through solidarity, storytelling, courage, and empathy.

Continuing the Conversation

Conversations about racism can feel uncomfortable because they challenge deeply held beliefs and societal norms. Yet storytelling remains a powerful tool to reveal hidden biases and humanize experiences that headlines or statistics alone cannot convey.

If discrimination or exclusion has touched your life or those you care about, sharing your story contributes to a wider dialogue that fosters awareness and change. The faces of bias are many, but so are the faces of resilience and hope.

Would you like to explore more stories or discuss ways to address these challenges constructively? Sometimes the best step forward comes from understanding the many layers beneath the surface.
