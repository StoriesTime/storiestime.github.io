From Lost to Found: A Story of Personal Growth


The day Priya Bhattacharya forgot her own birthday, the universe folded in on itself and let her float sideways for a while. She was sitting in the break room of the unremarkable office where she worked, chewing woodenly on a gluten-free granola bar and scrolling through emails, when her phone vibrated with a message from her mother: “Don’t forget to give yourself a treat today. Happy birthday, Priya!”

Her thumb hovered over the screen. For one chimerical moment, she wanted to set the phone down and see if maybe the universe would rewind to the top of the calendar page and let her start again. Instead, she walked to the vending machine, inserted a crumpled dollar, and pressed the button for a candy bar she hadn’t eaten since high school.

The Snickers came crashing down with a hollow thunk. Priya held the bar in her palm, trying to summon a rush of nostalgia or the faintest hint of sweetness. Instead, she felt a blankness, a silvery-gray wash that stretched from her fingers to her feet. Nobody in the office noticed that it was her birthday, and Priya didn’t tell them. She nibbled the candy bar at her desk, flavorless as sawdust, and deleted her mother’s message.

That night, she rummaged through her closet’s bottom drawer and found the journal she started at twenty-two, pages full of wild ink and sketches of imagined cities. Underneath the warm, yellow puddle of her reading lamp, Priya flipped through the entries: stories about friends who’d vanished, lovers who’d left, poems about cities she told herself she’d visit at thirty. Instead, at thirty-three, her life was less a bright adventure and more a sequence of recurring decimals—a loop she couldn’t even name.

Somewhere outside, streetlights hummed and cars rolled by, orange on black. Priya closed her eyes and let her head fall against the pillow, the journal pressed to her chest. She slept fitfully, dreaming of a mezzanine garden over a city she’d never seen.

When morning arrived, chastened and bone-pale, Priya’s body felt heavy, as though she were wearing a suit made of sand. She made an omelet, burned it, and ended up eating an apple and instant coffee. At the office, her desk overflowed with invoices and urgent requests. She slogged through, letting herself dissolve into the numbers, ignoring the tightening knot at her sternum.

Later that week, as she was leaving for lunch, she passed the corkboard in the lobby where someone had left a little flyer. MOSTLY SILENT MEDITATION RETREAT—Fiber Arts and Walking Trails included. It sounded partly like punishment, partly like escape. There was a phone number. She almost walked past.

On impulse, she tore off the tab and tucked it inside her wallet, between a faded train pass and a fortune-cookie slip: You will find adventure where you least expect it.

Priya was not the kind of person who went on meditation retreats. She was the person who scoffed at expensive mindfulness podcasts, who could barely make it through a two-minute gratitude meditation before her skin itched. And yet, one Friday evening she emailed the organizer, a woman named Sheila who replied with gentle cheer and a promise that Priya could leave at any time.

Her friends—those she still met for drinks twice a year—sent her texts like, “You? Silent retreat? Are you running from or towards something?” Priya texted back, “A bit of both.”

The retreat center turned out to be an eccentric farmhouse bristling with wild mint and dandelions, about forty miles out of the city. She arrived late, apologetic, worrying about looking unkempt in her sneakers and old college T-shirt. The other attendees were a chorus of strangers: middle-aged couples who looked like they did yoga on Tuesdays, a tattooed woman with an air of watchfulness, and a pale, fretful man furtively scrolling through his phone outside.

“Welcome,” said Sheila, a plump, balding woman in a bright purple sari. “You’re right on time for the welcome tea.”

Priya followed her out back, where mismatched chairs circled a table under low, gold lanterns. She sipped chamomile and let Sheila’s instructions dissolve over her like warm rain: no phones, no talking except during check-ins, and plenty of mindful walks or quiet crafts. Priya’s mind buzzed, anxious and hungry for distraction; she missed her phone even as she was grateful for its enforced absence.

That night, as she settled into a musty bed and lay under a patchwork quilt, Priya realized she’d spent years building a world where she was both indispensable and invisible. She warred with herself, careful in her thoughts. Nothing changed, and nothing moved; she wondered if it ever would.

On the second day, before dawn, she walked a dew-spangled path between brambles and half-burned birch logs. The air tasted like clean slate and copper. She forced herself to look, really look, at everything: the bruised petals of thistle, the calligraphy of snail trails in the wet grass. She tried to match the slow rhythm of her breath to her steps, but her thoughts still ran ahead. What if she quit her job? What if no one at work noticed? What if she moved to a new city and wandered parks and cafes, a different kind of ghost?

It was in the old barn—strung with abandoned looms, baskets of rag yarn, and dust motes slicing the air—that something began to shift. Priya had always been clumsy with her hands, but now, in the company of soft-spoken strangers and the hum of silence, she let herself pluck random colors of wool and knot them into awkward, childish shapes. The tattooed woman smiled at her, a flicker of camaraderie passing across a hush.

After lunch, when she retreated to the library’s window seat with a battered copy of The God of Small Things, Priya registered a tiny, unfamiliar satisfaction: she was here, she was awake, and she had nowhere to be but this.

The changes accumulated slowly, like silt in a creek bed.

Attending to the minutiae of her surroundings, Priya learned to take her emotions less seriously, as if they were just part of the landscape. When loneliness swept in, she let it trail after her out on the farm’s perimeter path; she even gave it a name—Marla—and let it walk beside her, heavy-shouldered and sad.

During one “reflection” session, Sheila invited everyone to draw blind contours of their own faces. Priya laughed—a brittle, embarrassed sound—at her lopsided portrait, but something about its imperfection made her want to save it. She tacked it to the mirror in her room, a reminder that she was shaped by crookedness, by attempts and failures, not by achievement alone.

By the end of the third day, she stopped dreading the stretches of silence. They grew thick and perfumed, like air in a glasshouse. She wondered why she’d built her life to avoid her own company so fiercely.

On her final night at the retreat, Priya couldn’t sleep. She sat under a blanket on the creaky porch, watching the milky way unravel. Somewhere in the dark a fox yipped: a sound clear and self-contained, unapologetic in its strangeness.

She thought about her job, her friendships, her love life—a string of “almosts” and “not quites.” They weren’t failures, she realized: just experiments. She remembered a professor once telling her, “What matters is not the map you drew, but that you’re willing to keep moving, even when the landscape changes.” At the time, she’d been too young, too sure of her destination. Now, she could see the wisdom in just walking.

Back in the city, on a Monday stitched with sirens and elevators and the hum of copy machines, the lessons from the retreat sifted into Priya’s routine. She set small alarms during her day—not for deadlines, but to step outside for five minutes, breathe, and notice. She started making art again on weeknights, not for an audience or a social feed, but just to track what her hands could do when her mind was allowed to meander.

Slowly, she let go of the roles she thought kept her safe. At first it was only tiny mutations: skipping Friday drinks with Amy from payroll, who always made her feel small; volunteering to lead the team brainstorm, despite her terror of public speaking; saying “yes” when a neighbor invited her to a documentary screening.

When things went badly—when she froze onstage, when she burned a new recipe, when her neighbor’s friends ignored her—she shrugged. One mistake was no longer a warning sign or a verdict. She could try again or not; either way, her worth wasn’t at stake.

The bigger changes followed months later, in the messy, halting way that becomes clear only with hindsight. Priya quit her job after a year, realizing that boredom and resentment had hollowed her out. She enrolled in a weekend printmaking course, hoping to find something that thrilled her, even if only in small, glancing ways. Her world expanded and contracted, full of unfinished notebooks, lopsided bowls, and a few new friends she didn’t have to pretend with.

Sometimes, still, the grayness returned. Sometimes, Saturday nights hurt. Everything about growing felt like a puzzle where half the pieces were sky. Yet when Priya caught herself in a shop window, she would recognize a person half-stranger, half-old friend—someone who had learned it was enough, some days, just to be awake and curious and still moving.

Years later, people who hadn’t seen her in a long time would squint and ask, “Priya—is that you? You seem so different.” She’d laugh, sometimes. The old self was a suit hanging in a forgotten closet, familiar in its cut but shapeless.

She had not found the perfect city or the perfect job. She had not figured out how to make sadness disappear, or to fill every unscripted hour. But she’d learned this: getting lost was not the end. In fact, sometimes it was the start of finding things—art, friendship, even joy—hidden between all the mapped-out destinations she’d outgrown.

And so Priya kept going: not towards a finish line, but through the grainy, unpredictable afternoons of her life. Walking, pausing, sometimes sprinting, she carried with her a map freshly drawn—the kind that reminded her exactly where she was, and how to come back to herself, again and again.
