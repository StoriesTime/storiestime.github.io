Colors of Injustice: Short Stories on Racism and Prejudice


It began with a scratchy note pinned awkwardly to the noticeboard at Wilkens High School: “Color Day—celebrate your heritage. Wear the colors that define you.” The message, meant to be festive and inclusive, burst like a dye bomb in the corridors, coloring the air with whispers, questions, and a simmering tension few could ignore. For some, it was a day to embrace with pride; for others, it rang hollow, a reminder of the fractures marked by skin deep.

Miranda flicked through her phone as the bell rang, a knot tightening in her stomach. She tugged at the edges of her beige cardigan, its color blending more into the crowd than standing out. A flash of amber sunlight caught the strands of her hair as she stared down the hall where yellow, red, and blue cliques formed their kaleidoscopic clusters. She wondered—was she meant to choose a shade that reflected her heritage, or did her existence defy the alphabet of colors assigned by others?

In the classroom, Mr. Lyons, their social studies teacher, introduced the day with a hesitant smile.

“Today, we wear colors to recognize the diverse threads in our community’s rich fabric,” he said, eyes sweeping the room, sensing the unspoken unease. “Each of you belongs to this story, painted by your cultures and shaped by history.”

But not everyone saw it that way.

Down the hall, Jamal clenched his fists. His dark skin, a canvas of stories written in the lines of his hands and the curl of his hair, felt heavier today. The chatter about “Color Day” wasn’t just chatter—it was the echo of being boxed in, labeled, a mere shade in a spectrum defined by others.

In the cafeteria line, a whispered comment sliced through the hum: “Is he even trying? Always acting tough. Typical.” The words, invisible yet palpable, stung like splatters of cold paint.

Jamal’s friend, Lila, caught his glare and nudged him gently. “Ignore it. They don’t know you.”

But ignoring it felt like erasing himself.

Meanwhile, in the art room, Sophie mixed her paints thoughtfully. She wondered: Could colors heal the divisions, or only deepen them? Her brush strokes traced images of hands reaching across barriers, hues blending at the edges. But her canvas was not just art—it was a silent protest against the assumptions clamped onto identities.

“Why can’t we just be people?” she muttered, layering blues into purples.

The story of prejudice and injustice was more than names and faces—it was felt in the textures of daily life, in the stolen glances and hesitations.

As the day unfolded, an unexpected moment changed the course of things.

In the auditorium, students gathered for a panel discussion on race and identity. It was tentative at first—a dance around discomfort—but then Jamal stood up, voice steady though his heart thundered.

“I’m tired of being colored by what you think I am, rather than who I am,” he said. The words hung in the air like a defiant banner.

One by one, students began sharing their stories—of microaggressions worn like invisible bruises, of stereotypes shattering their sense of self, of dreams deferred by judgment.

Miranda spoke of feeling unseen, her multifaceted identity compressed into a single label.

Sophie talked about art as a refuge, a language beyond words and prejudice.

The room transformed, wounds laid bare in the light.

“Colors are more than what we wear,” Mr. Lyons concluded. “They are the spectrum of our experiences—the shades of our histories, the hues of our struggles and hopes.”

The discussion did not erase the past or the pain, but it began to weave understanding, thread by thread.

That evening, as students left, the noticeboard now held a new message in vibrant calligraphy:

“Injustice is not a single color. Neither is prejudice. Together, we blend the world we want to see.”

Miranda, Jamal, Sophie, and countless others realized that fighting racism and prejudice wasn’t about choosing sides or colors—it was about breaking down walls painted by fear and ignorance.

They stepped into the dusk carrying more than colors on their clothes—they carried resolve, courage, and the promise of change.

The story of Colors of Injustice is not just about one day at Wilkens High but echoes everywhere prejudice stains the fabric of society. It’s a story about the courage it takes to confront ignorance and the power collective voices have in reshaping narratives.

Through the kaleidoscope of human experience comes understanding, and when colors blend without prejudice, a new masterpiece emerges—vivid, complex, and deeply human.
