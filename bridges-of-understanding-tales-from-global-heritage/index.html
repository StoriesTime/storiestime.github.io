Bridges of Understanding: Tales from Global Heritage


In the quaint town of Lahore, Pakistan, a young girl named Amna grew up surrounded by the rich history of the Indus Valley Civilization. The old city, with its labyrinthine streets and imposing Mughal architecture, was a testament to the region's storied past. Amna's earliest memories were of listening to her grandmother's tales of the ancient civilization, which had thrived along the banks of the Indus River over 4,000 years ago. As she grew older, Amna's fascination with history deepened, and she became particularly interested in the game of polo, which originated in ancient Persia (modern-day Iran). She was intrigued by the stories of prince-kings who used to play polo on horseback, the ball – a bag filled with wool, tied to a long handle – would fly through the air as riders chased after it. The thrill of the game seemed to transcend borders and cultures, bringing people together in a shared experience. Amna's curiosity led her to embark on a journey to explore the global heritage of polo. She packed her bags, said goodbye to her loved ones, and set out to discover the origins of the game in ancient Persia. A Marionette of History As Amna traversed the vast expanse of the Middle East, she found herself in the midst of a bustling marketplace in Isfahan, Iran. The sound of vendors calling out their wares and the scent of rosewater wafting through the air transported her to a different era. Amna's eyes widened as she spotted a group of artisans crafting intricate wooden polo mallets, adorned with engravings of horseback riders. She struck up a conversation with the elderly artisan, Hassan, who regaled her with tales of the game's history. Hassan explained how polo was played by the ancient Parsees (Zoroastrians) as a means of military training, a way to foster courage, strength, and strategic thinking. The game, however, was also a reflection of Persian culture, which prized chivalry, honor, and elegance. As Amna continued her journey, she discovered that polo was not just a game but a cultural phenomenon that connected people across continents. From the polo clubs of England to the polo grounds of Argentina, she met individuals who had been captivated by the majesty of the game. Each one had a unique story to tell, each one had a personal connection to the sport that transcended borders. A Leaping Horse Across the World From Iran, Amna traveled to India, where she visited the polo clubs of Gujarat and Rajasthan. She met a young polo player, Rohan, who explained that polo was an integral part of Indian history and culture. The travelers, riders, and the majestic horses that won battles were worshipped in temples, and the game was often depicted in ancient Indian art. As Amna explored the polo clubs of India, she realized that the game was not just a sport but a celebration of tradition and heritage. Every ride, every shot, and every goal was a recreation of a centuries-old story that pulsed through the veins of Indian people. Amna's odyssey took her across international borders, to the China, where she discovered a rapidly growing polo community. The people of Shanghai, Beijing, and Hong Kong were weaving their traditions with the game, infusing it with their unique flavors and flair.
