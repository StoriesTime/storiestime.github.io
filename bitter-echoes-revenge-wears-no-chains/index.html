Bitter Echoes: Revenge Wears No Chains


The storm had rolled in faster than anticipated. The once brilliant crimson sky, painted by the setting sun, was now consumed by a relentless sheet of charcoal clouds. Shadows stretched long and ominous over the dilapidated town of Ashvale, where whispers of the past lingered like dust in untouched corners. Rain began to streak down in uneven lines, pinning the sparse debris of an abandoned marketplace to the muddy ground. From the ruins, a single figure emerged, wrapped in a fraying coat, head bent against the bitter wind.

Elira had returned to Ashvale for the first time in seven years. Her boots sunk into the earth with each step, weighed down by more than the weather. This town, with its splintered signs and hollowed homes, would never forget what it had done to her family. And she—taught by the tutelage of betrayal—would not forgive.

Her gaze fell upon the remnants of the smithy that had once belonged to her father. The walls were blackened by fire, the forge a cold skeleton of rust. Seven years had passed since that night, yet the echoes roared in her memory as if it had been yesterday. She could still hear the voices. Angry. Accusing. Driven to a fever pitch by the lies of one man. They had called her father a traitor, her mother a conspirator. Not even the innocent had been spared. As their home burned, the mob’s faces were illuminated by the fire they’d lit, their rage brighter than the flames.

Elira clenched her fists. “No chains indeed,” she muttered.

It was the words of Marcus Drell, the man who had stood at the center of it all. He’d once called himself a friend of the family. Yet it was his voice that condemned them and ensured they would find no solace, even in screams. He had stood atop the pyre of her childhood, speaking with eloquent hatred, declaring there was no place for betrayal in Ashvale. “Revenge wears no chains,” he had told the crowd. “But neither do liars. Cut them loose before they corrupt more righteous hearts.”

At the time, Elira’s screams for mercy had been drowned out by the fury of the mob and the crackle of devouring fire. She had been just fourteen—the shadows had spared her. She hid in a crevice beneath the woodshed, watching through a narrow slit of fractured wood as her family burned for crimes they’d never committed.

Time taught her that silence was a double-edged blade. It saved her then, but every moment since had been sharpened by guilt and rage. Seven years ago, she had fled. Tonight, she returned—to face those who thought they had buried her along with her family’s name.

The door to the old inn creaked open, Ajid’s Lantern still barely standing. Inside, the air carried the aroma of damp wood and sour ale, mingled with hushed voices. Everyone paused to glance her way as she stepped over the threshold, brushing rainwater from her coat. Most turned back to their drinks, dismissing her as another drifter. Only one set of eyes lingered a moment longer. She met them with cold recognition. Some faces were hard to forget.

“Out long tonight, aren’t you, Miss?” the barkeep asked, his voice hoarse but friendly enough to mask suspicion.

Elira slid an old coin across the counter. The weight of it caused the man to hesitate. It was an Ashvale coin, minted years ago, one of the last markers of a town still clinging to a fraying identity. He looked up at her, a question hanging silently between them.

“Just cider,” Elira said.

The tension released. The barkeep grasped the tankard and poured. The sounds of liquid striking tin filled the quiet space, accompanied by mutterings from the few drunks who occupied the corners.

She sat in the far corner with her cider untouched before her. Her gaze wandered the room. She saw no faces among the patrons she recognized—save one. Leaning into the shadows by the staircase, a woman with deep-set eyes studied her. Gretta. All those years, and Gretta still carried the wolfish grin Elira remembered. She’d been one of the mob that torched the forge. She’d carried a lantern in those hands, and instead of illuminating the truth, she’d fueled destruction.

For a moment, Elira thought Gretta didn’t recognize her. Seven years in the wild had hardened her, changed the girl who had cowered in the ashes of her life into the woman sitting here, venom in her veins and a storm behind her eyes. But then recognition dawned on Gretta’s face. Her grin faltered, replaced by the twitch of unease, and then she vanished up the staircase.

Elira waited patiently. She knew where Gretta was going. A name sat ready on the end of her tongue—she’d ask for Marcus Drell. And Marcus would come.

It was close to midnight when he finally arrived. He entered through the same creaking door, and the air in the tavern seemed to tighten. He was older now, but his presence carried the same weight it had on Ashvale’s darkest night. He was dressed finely, unusual for a town so ruined, and carried himself with a confidence only those with blood on their hands could muster.

Drell’s eyes scanned the room, landing on Elira. Something between a smile and a sneer crossed his face as he approached her table. He sat without asking.

“I recognized your token,” he said evenly, gesturing toward the coin she had used to pay for her drink. “Family relics stick around even after the families themselves… do not.”

Elira’s jaw tightened, but she kept her expression neutral. “That coin is the only thing I managed to save from the wreckage. You left nothing else for me to hold onto.”

Drell leaned back in his chair, his brow arched. “The Kelyn name is a burden, not a treasure. Best you let it go.”

“You’re one to talk,” she countered, her voice sharp. “You’ve always known how to discard burdens—to distance yourself from the consequences while others pay the price.”

He chuckled, but there was no mirth in it. “Is that why you staged this reunion? Vengeance? You’ll find it to be a hollow pursuit, child, one that bends you to its will more than you master it. Better to—”

“Save your wisdom,” Elira spat. Her eyes burned with icy fury. “I learned enough from watching you destroy us. Seven years gave me all the education I needed.”

For the first time, the air of smugness left him. His sharp instincts made him glance around the room. His confidence wavered, though he tried to mask it. “You’ve made a mistake coming back here.”

“Have I?” She leaned in, her voice dropping to a dangerous whisper. “You condemned innocent people. You carved the legacy I wear today. Tell me, Marcus, did you plan how you’d wear yours?”

She rose swiftly, her hand brushing the hilt of her blade beneath her coat. Drell stood too, his voice rising as he warned the patrons. But when others in the tavern shifted to intervene, the door burst open, and a line of cloaked figures entered, their steps measured and deliberate. They were not from Ashvale—and that frightened the locals even more than the storm raging outside.

Elira had spent years enduring her grief and rage. She had built connections, allies, a network of listening ears to track Drell’s every move. She had always known he wouldn’t exist in isolation. His safety, however, had been purchased by wealth, and tonight currency was no armor against retribution.

Drell saw the trap closing, and for the first time since she’d seen him speak from atop the pyre, fear snaked its way into his expression. Elira advanced as the cloaked figures closed the circle behind him.

“You’re the one who said revenge wears no chains,” she said softly, almost serenely. Then her voice turned to steel. “I’ve had seven years to unshackle myself from mercy.”

The echoes of that night all those years ago came roaring back into Ashvale, but this time, it was a different fire that would cleanse.
