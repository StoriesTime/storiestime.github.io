Voices from the Past: Life During the Cultural Revolution


In the dim light of their cramped apartment in Old Beijing, thirteen-year-old Xu Mei tugged her threadbare quilt closer, the cold seeping through the cracked windowpanes. Outside, boots echoed against the basalt street, too loud for the hour, too insistent. She squeezed her brother’s hand beneath the quilt. At the faintest touch, their silent secret: fear. It belonged to everyone now.

Mei’s father, Xu Sheng, was a history teacher—a profession that, a year earlier, had brought him quiet pride. Now, accusations hummed around like hornets. Mei caught words snatched from neighbors’ mouths, never certain if they named her father or the grocer two doors down, but always heavy, always pointed: “reactionary,” “old thinking,” “re-education.”

Shouts burst through the night. Knock. Then banging. Red armbands flashed through the frosted glass. Her mother straightened, already knowing. Doors in their alley opened in unison, each family awake, faces pressed to the cold wood, listening as the world around them fractured.

A teenager—older than Mei, red scarf brilliant at his neck—stood in their living room, reciting righteousness as if it were poetry. Shelves stripped of books. Pictures torn, family relics hurled into the corridor for public display. Each broken vase, each torn scroll: evidence of “the past.” Mei hid in the shadows, uncertain where the past began or ended. The boy’s voice cracked when he shouted. He’d been in the class above her. By morning, her father was gone.

Spring: Before and After

Mei drifted through the days, her mind catching on things—her father’s rough laugh, the pattern of his footsteps in the hall. Her mother stopped speaking whenever the little brother appeared, as if words themselves were dangerous objects. Instead, she cleaned and cleaned, scrubbing the tiny apartment until her hands cracked and bled.

At school, slogans painted in red glared from the gray walls: “Smash the Four Olds!” “Study Chairman Mao’s teachings!” Students—children, really—became the new vanguard. Classmates once bullied for stuttering or wearing patched trousers now led parades through the streets, clutching copies of Quotations of Chairman Mao. Mei avoided meeting anyone’s gaze.

They were summoned for “struggle sessions”: days when teachers lined up in the courtyard, heads bowed as children screamed accusations. Mei’s teacher, Assistant Li, who had given her a beautiful paper fan the year before, was forced to kneel for hours, her legs shaking violently in the cold mud.

Mei learned to be silent. She pressed words down inside her chest, deep enough that even in sleep, she spoke only in whispers. She listened with the attention of an animal—each day a test in which the wrong expression might cost everything.

On the first day of Spring Festival, after sweeping ashes from the stove, Mei’s mother pressed a ragged bun into her hand and said, “We must try to remember.” Mei’s little brother, only seven, stared at her with huge eyes. “Remember what?” he whispered.

“Who we are,” her mother replied, voice thin as rice soup.

Whispers and Shadows

April brought sudden warmth, and with it, a thinning of the police presence in the alleyways. Yet the city did not relax. Rumors coiled around every market cart: so-and-so sent down to the countryside, neighbor disappeared after a denunciation letter, old couples forced into exile for “bourgeois tendencies.”

Mei wandered the empty playgrounds after school, sticky with anxiety, memorizing poems in her head, hoping her father’s favorite verses would not vanish with him. She often found herself retelling stories to herself as armor—snatches of folklore and history, the tales Xu Sheng loved. The peach blossom maiden. The wise judge Bao. She dared repeat none aloud; folklore was suspect, too, if it smacked of old beliefs.

One afternoon, hiding beneath the fence, Mei listened to Auntie Bai, who lived above the tailor’s shop. She spoke to Mei’s mother in a voice like newsprint, dry and careful: “Heard anything?” Perhaps a code for “Are you safe?” or “Did you see who left last night?” Names were never spoken. The walls, everyone agreed, had ears.

Mei’s friendship with little Zhang across the hall evaporated—her mother forbade her to visit: “Their father’s on the list.” Mei stared through the iron lattice, wondering if she was on a list, too. Were lists like stories, infinite in length, always including someone’s father?

Letters Unsent

That July, her mother found one of Xu Sheng’s old notebooks jammed beneath a loose floorboard. A relic, as dangerous and beloved as a talisman. In it: notes on Tang poetry, critiques of dynastic rulers, a scrawled recipe for Mei’s favorite sweet soup.

Mei smuggled the notebook to the rooftop at dusk, rereading the careful characters.

“To Mei—Never forget that history is not only what they say it is. It is also what we love, what we remember.”

She wrote back, in tiny print between the lines:

“I am trying, Baba.”

But she never sent it—letters were searched, and little girls with dangerous fathers were watched.

At school, her teachers assigned pages from Mao’s works, slapping wooden rulers on desks if someone failed to recite a passage. But sometimes, Assistant Li slid Mei a slip of scrap paper with a math question or a quiet smile. Small acts of kindness sowed fear and gratitude in equal measure.

Autumn Harvest, 1968

A full year since the first knock in the night. Mei’s mother grew gaunter. Rations thinned, and lines at the market twisted through the lanes for hours. The radio broadcast coarse slogans, glorifying revolution and work, warning of traitors at every level. Children sang anthems in sharp voices, but on lonely streets, grandmothers still murmured prayers into folded hands.

Mei turned fourteen that autumn, birthday unspoken—candles and cakes had become extravagances, private things. Yet a piece of soft sweet potato, carefully saved, appeared on her pillow: her mother’s secret promise.

Despite careful silence, accusations haunted them. That autumn, a neighbor scrawled a denunciation on their door: “Reactionary family. Suspect.” Mei’s mother wiped it away with wet rags, hands shaking. Mei watched the red smear dissolve, not sure if it was paint or something deeper.

Later that month, Mei witnessed a public rally in the main square—dozens of men and women made to “confess” their crimes, bowing again and again as the crowd jeered. Mei looked for her father in every bowed head, certain and terrified she would see him.

That evening, she found her little brother drawing stick figures in the dirt, lines and circles: “This one’s Baba, coming home.” Mei wanted to scold him, but her voice caught. She drew a doorway beside his figure, drawing hope.

The Heart Remembers

Winter returned in dust and ice. The alleyways were quieter now, more empty spaces where families once lived. Some had been sent to the countryside for “re-education,” others simply disappeared.

Mei’s mother, once careful, grew bold in small ways. At night she boiled millet soup and whispered stories of the past, threading tales through the darkness. “Your grandfather played the erhu so well,” she’d say, tears glinting. “When you were a baby, your father painted your name in calligraphy on the door.”

Mei pressed these stories into memory as if recording secret history. She realized that people survived by remembering—not just the slogans, but who they had been, loves and losses, laughter and music. In a world that demanded new faces every day, memory became rebellion.

On the first day of Lunar New Year, Mei woke to the smell of fried dough, a miracle in those lean times. Her mother had saved enough oil for a single twisted strip. They shared it quietly, eyes bright. Mei whispered a verse from her father’s notebook, the words tasting of honey.

New Springs

The years rolled forward. Mei’s brother was sent to the countryside in 1970, one of many “educated youths” dispatched to rebuild the land. Mei, older now, began working in a textile factory where the machinery drowned out everything but her own thoughts. There were fewer denunciations, but the scars remained. Songs returned, but always carefully, in coded melodies.

One evening, Mei found herself humming one of her father’s old folk songs. An older woman across the loom joined her on the chorus, their voices threading together, low and steady. No one stopped them. Mei realized that even after years of silence, the voice of the past had not been erased—it lingered in memory, in music, in the quiet care between survivors.

Four years later, a letter arrived from the north: her brother had survived, grown into a man. Her family, battered but intact, began slowly to reconnect.

In the gray dawn of 1976, when Mei stood in Tiananmen and watched strangers weep in the street, she sensed a tremor in the world—a shifting underfoot, the very first whisper of hope. Mei tucked her father’s last message into her coat. She had survived the silent years with her memory intact—bearing witness to a history too powerful to erase.

History, Mei realized, was not only in books. It was in voices, in faces—every story whispered in secret, carried carefully through broken years, waiting for a new spring to bloom.
