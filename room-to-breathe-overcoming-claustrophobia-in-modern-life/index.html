Room to Breathe: Overcoming Claustrophobia in Modern Life


The subway train screeched to a halt, jolting Maya out of her anxious reverie. She glanced at her watch and cursed silently. Another delay. Rush hour was in full swing, and the train car was packed wall to wall with bodies pressed together like sardines in a tin can. The air seemed heavy, almost tangible, as if it too had conspired against her. A bead of sweat slid down her temple as she tightened her grip on the overhead bar. Her chest felt constricted, her breath short. The walls of the subway car seemed to close in a little more with every shuddering stop.

Maya shifted uncomfortably, trying to make herself smaller in the sea of commuters. Claustrophobia wasn’t just a fear for her; it was a daily battle. It had started years ago, an unnoticed seed that grew quietly until it had wrapped its roots around her life. Elevators, crowded buses, even small rooms with no windows—these spaces all became arenas where she struggled to reclaim control.

She hadn’t always been like this. In her twenties, Maya had been carefree, adventurous even. She’d hiked through dense, dark forests, taken scuba dives into underwater caves, and climbed creaking fire towers that swayed in strong winds. Confinement hadn’t scared her then. But something had shifted over the years. Perhaps it was the slow drip of stress, the erosion of boundaries as her job, her relationships, and the competing demands of adulthood began to hem her in. What once felt thrilling now felt suffocating.

The train jolted forward again, snapping her back to the present. Maya fought to take deep breaths, her therapist’s voice echoing in her head. “Panic only grows when we resist it, Maya. Let it pass. It’s a wave, not a permanent state.” She tried inhaling slowly through her nose, counting to four, but her thoughts rebelled, spiraling outward. What if she fainted here, in this overcrowded car? What if she couldn’t get out in time? What if, what if, what if…

When the train finally reached her stop, she stumbled out into the station like a swimmer surfacing for air. Her chest heaved as she leaned against the grimy tiled wall, letting the commuters swarm past her. The air in the station was fresher, but only slightly. She needed to get outside, to see the open sky above her.

Her therapist had called it “spatial pressure,” that sense of being trapped by unseen walls. It was logical that environments like subway cars and elevators triggered her claustrophobia; they were literal enclosed spaces. But for Maya, the feeling sometimes came in unexpected places: a long meeting in a windowless office, a crowded street where the buildings loomed too close together, or even a packed social gathering where conversations overlapped, voices rising and falling like chaotic waves.

Maya emerged into the sunlight of the city streets, blinking against the brightness. The fresh air cut through the remnants of her panic, and she took slow, deliberate steps toward her office building. As usual, she felt drained, as though the episode on the subway had sapped her energy for the day. But she couldn’t keep living like this—fighting her fear instead of understanding it. Something had to change.

Maya’s first real breakthrough came on a Sunday afternoon. She had signed up for a mindfulness workshop at a community center downtown, largely at the insistence of her best friend, Lena. The room was small but open, with soft lighting and quiet music playing in the background. About a dozen people sat on cushions arranged in a loose circle. At first, Maya had been skeptical. Was this really going to help her? Or was it just another generic self-help fad?

The instructor, a calm, gray-haired woman named Sylvia, began by inviting the participants to take stock of the space around them. “Close your eyes, if you’re comfortable,” Sylvia said. “Notice the dimensions of the room. Notice where the walls are, where the ceiling is.” Maya tensed at the mention of walls. She pictured them closing in—but then Sylvia continued.

“Now notice the space within the room as well. Feel the expanse of the air you’re breathing. It’s there, generous and free, even when walls exist around us. You may be encased in a room, but you are not trapped.”

Maya let her breath even out as she followed Sylvia’s instructions. At first, it was awkward—her mind resisted, darting back to her stressors, her fears. But gradually, she felt a subtle shift. The room didn’t seem quite as small as it had when she walked in.

After the session, Sylvia handed out small cards with a single question written on them: “What do the walls in your life represent?”

Maya stared at the card that evening. It was a question she wasn’t entirely sure how to answer. Sylvia had spoken briefly about metaphorical walls—emotional barriers, restrictive relationships, personal fears—but it wasn’t until Maya started journaling that the truth began to emerge.

The walls in her life weren’t just physical. They were the expectations she’d internalized, the boundaries she was too afraid to set, the feelings she was too reluctant to share. They were years of pretending she had it all together when, deep down, she craved space: space to breathe, to grow, to be.

Over the next few months, Maya made deliberate changes. Her therapist introduced her to exposure therapy, starting with mildly claustrophobic situations and working up to more challenging ones. At first, simply standing in a crowded elevator for ten seconds without pressing the “door open” button felt like an impossible victory. Slowly, she grew braver, tackling public transportation, social gatherings, and even her long-neglected gym membership.

She also began saying no to things she didn’t want to do. At first, this was nearly as terrifying as facing her physical fears. She had always been a people pleaser, filling her schedule with obligations that left her no room for herself. But now, when she set boundaries, something miraculous happened: she felt lighter. Freer.

One morning, on her way to work, Maya found herself smiling at the familiar chaos of the subway station. She stepped into a crowded car and, though the space was tight, she didn’t feel trapped. She focused on her breathing, her feet stable on the floor. The walls of the subway car seemed like an afterthought today—not enemies, just structures for the journey. She even noticed a clear patch of sky shining through the window, a mercurial crack of daylight in the dull underground.

The journey, of course, wasn’t linear. There were setbacks. There were days when the walls came crashing back, when her anxiety swelled too large to ignore. But they became fewer, and each time she rose again, she became more resilient. Maya learned that freedom, true freedom, came not from avoiding enclosed spaces, but from learning to live fully within them.

On her thirty-fifth birthday, Maya rented a cabin with a group of close friends. It was tucked away in a quiet corner of the woods, miles from the buzz of the city. That evening, as the sun dipped below the horizon, she stood on the wide porch and took in the vastness around her. The air smelled of pine and earth, a crisp breeze rustling through the treetops. She felt no need to do anything or be anywhere else. She simply existed, unburdened and unafraid.

The walls in her life were no longer cages. They were just markers of where she began and ended, reminders of the space that was hers to shape. Maya took a deep breath, her hand resting on the wooden railing, and felt the vastness of the moment settle into her bones. For the first time in years, she wasn’t just surviving. She was free.
