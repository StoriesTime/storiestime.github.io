Behind the Color: Overcoming Racism in Everyday Life


The sun was setting over the bustling city of Eastbrook, painting the sky in a blend of amber and purple hues. In the heart of this urban sprawl stood the office of a prestigious law firm. Inside, the hum of conversation, the click of keyboards, and the soft rustle of paper filled the air. But amidst all this, Kiana Simmons sat at her desk, staring at her computer screen, feeling the weight of her existence.

Kiana, an ambitious young woman of African descent, had earned her place at this law firm. She was a recent graduate, eager to prove her worth in a profession dominated by individuals who did not look like her. For years, she had worked relentlessly, obtaining top grades in law school, participating in internships, and networking tirelessly. But even in this professional world where talent should have been the deciding factor, there were moments when Kiana felt as though her skin color defined her more than her skills or intellect.

It wasn’t overt. No one ever told her she didn’t belong. No one made racist remarks directly to her face. But it was in the subtle looks, the unspoken words, the moments when she was overlooked in meetings, the times her suggestions were dismissed without consideration, the awkward silences when she entered a room. These small but powerful gestures created an invisible barrier that seemed impossible to break.

The first time she noticed it was during her internship. She was sitting in a meeting with a group of seasoned attorneys, all of whom were white. As the meeting progressed, Kiana spoke up, offering her analysis on a case that was being discussed. The room grew quiet for a moment, and she saw it – the slight glances exchanged between the others. They had heard her, but they didn’t really listen. When another colleague, a white male, repeated almost the same point she had just made, everyone nodded in agreement. It wasn’t that her opinion wasn’t valid. It was that it didn’t fit the image of authority that the room had subconsciously constructed.

Years of pushing through, of fighting against an invisible tide of doubt, had shaped Kiana’s resilience. Yet, at moments, her confidence faltered. She would go home and question herself: Was I being too sensitive? Was I overthinking this? But deep down, she knew that what she experienced was real. It wasn’t just in her mind. It was in the way people interacted with her, how they chose not to see her as an equal, and how her contributions were often overshadowed by others who didn’t share her background.

One evening, after a particularly exhausting day, Kiana found herself in the park, walking slowly along a path lined with trees. The evening air was crisp, and the city noise felt distant, muffled by the natural surroundings. It was here, in the quiet, that she could finally breathe. But even in the solitude, her thoughts were relentless. The weight of racism in everyday life was heavy, and it was getting harder to carry.

As she walked, she noticed a bench under a tree, and on that bench sat an older woman with a kind smile. She looked to be in her late 60s, her graying hair tucked neatly under a scarf. The woman patted the space next to her, inviting Kiana to sit. Without thinking, Kiana took the seat.

“You look like you have the world on your shoulders,” the woman said gently, her eyes warm.

Kiana hesitated, unsure whether to share her frustrations with a stranger, but there was something about the woman’s presence that made her feel safe.

“It feels like no matter how hard I work, people just don’t take me seriously,” Kiana confessed, her voice tinged with frustration. “It’s like I’m invisible, but at the same time, not. I’m not seen for who I truly am, just for the color of my skin.”

The older woman nodded knowingly, her expression thoughtful. “I’ve seen that look on many faces before. But you know, my dear, you’re not invisible. You’re just not seen by the right people.”

Kiana turned to her, confused.

“People who are trapped in their own biases, their own fears,” the woman continued. “They see the world through a lens that doesn’t allow them to truly see you. But that doesn’t mean you stop being real, and it doesn’t mean your worth is any less.”

Kiana sat in silence, taking in the woman’s words. She had heard similar sentiments before, but this time, something clicked. The idea that her worth wasn’t determined by others’ perceptions felt like a revelation.

The woman smiled softly. “You’ve got to live for yourself, not for anyone else’s approval. Break free from the weight of their judgments. You are the author of your own story, and you decide who gets to be a part of it.”

For the first time in a long while, Kiana felt a surge of hope. She realized that she didn’t need to wait for others to see her value. She could affirm it herself.

The next day, Kiana arrived at work with a renewed sense of purpose. She was still navigating the difficult path of professional life in a world where racial disparities were still very real, but she was determined to forge ahead. She had something to prove, not to the world, but to herself.

Her moment of clarity came unexpectedly during a critical case review meeting. This time, when she spoke, she was not just offering an analysis. She spoke with conviction, with the understanding that her voice mattered, that her perspective was important. It wasn’t just about fitting into the mold of what others expected from a lawyer. It was about defining what a lawyer could be, in all of her complexity and uniqueness.

To her surprise, her words were met with more than just polite nods. Her colleagues listened intently, asked insightful questions, and even commended her insights. The sense of being overlooked, of being invisible, started to fade. It wasn’t that racism had disappeared in her workplace, but Kiana realized that she could stand firm in her truth, regardless of how others chose to see her.

In the weeks that followed, Kiana made a conscious effort to build connections, not just with people who looked like her, but with everyone. She worked hard to challenge the assumptions that had been built around her. She wasn’t going to let the silent biases and microaggressions dictate her future. Slowly, the environment began to change. The people around her started to understand that her skin color wasn’t a limitation—it was a part of her, a part of what made her perspective unique.

As time went on, Kiana became not only a respected lawyer but a mentor to others who faced the same challenges. She led initiatives to promote diversity in the workplace, encouraging others to speak out against discrimination and to actively dismantle the biases that existed within the firm.

Kiana’s journey was far from over. She knew that racism in everyday life wasn’t something that could be eliminated overnight. But she also understood that change begins within, and sometimes, the most profound transformation comes when individuals refuse to accept the limitations placed upon them by others. It was not just about surviving in a world shaped by prejudice. It was about thriving, about showing the world that behind the color of her skin, there was a person with dreams, talents, and a unique voice.

And that voice? It was one that would no longer be ignored.
