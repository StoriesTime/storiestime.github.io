Unseen Forces: A Supernatural Thriller


The skies over Meridian darkened just past dusk, a bruised purple swallowing the last warmth of daylight. By the time Adelaide saw the man standing beneath the iron archway of the old Everlane cemetery, clouds had coalesced like bruises across the moon. She wasn’t looking for ghosts—she never did—but sometimes ghosts found her all the same.

It was August, the air as heavy as grief. Adelaide turned off her camera’s flash and slid it into her bag, glancing at her wristwatch purely for comfort. She’d been cataloging the gravestones after work, her summer side project, the act of recording names a balm against the ache left by her brother’s disappearance two years prior. She didn’t expect anyone else to haunt these grounds at night, much less a living man.

Except was he? He stood too still. Motionless, except for the flutter of his coat. In the distance, thunder grumbled across the low hills. Adelaide narrowed her eyes. Every sensible instinct told her to leave, but she did what she always did with trouble—she faced it.

She called out. “Are you all right?”

The man didn’t answer. He tilted his head, as though listening to something Adelaide could not hear. The hair on her arms prickled. Wind keened through the cemetery, tugging her brown hair free of its ponytail. She stared back. She would not be the first to look away.

Lightning illuminated the graveyard and, for a split second, washed the stranger in hard silver. He had no shadow.

Adelaide sucked in a breath and crossed herself out of habit. She pressed her hand to the cool granite of a nearby headstone; she always thought of her brother, Evan, when she was afraid. What would he have done? He would have gotten closer.

“You don’t have to be scared,” the man said, his voice like wind through a keyhole. “But you should listen tonight.”

Unsettled, Adelaide drew herself up, summoning the edge she’d honed working years in the city. “Listen to what?”

The wind dropped immediately. In the hush, she heard something else—a susurrus, like a breath exhaled from the earth itself. Graves shifting? The idea was laughable, except she wasn’t laughing.

“Have you lost someone?” the man asked.

Adelaide blinked. Her mouth went dry. “Who are you?”

But the man ignored her. He stared beyond her, to the tangled yew hedges. “Sometimes the borders wear thin. Sometimes the world grows fragile. Some people are pulled away—or trapped inside.”

She shivered, remembering the way her brother’s belongings had sat untouched for months, as if he’d dash back into his room the moment she turned away. “Are you saying…have you seen him? My brother?”

He let himself smile—an odd, solemn upturn. “That depends. Can you see what’s really here?”

Thunder cracked above. In the stuttering light, Adelaide thought she saw movement—a ripple of faces beyond the graves, just outside the graveyard rails. The air tasted metallic, sharp as cut grass.

She stepped back. “Are you a ghost?”

He laughed, but it sounded hollow. “Worse,” he said, “I’m a keeper.”

The word meant nothing to her, but she understood all at once that she was standing at the threshold of something vast, terrifying, and hungry.

The Forgotten Gate

Adelaide didn’t sleep that night. Instead, she poured over the photographs she’d taken in the graveyard, zooming in on corner shadows, on odd distortions where grass seemed to lean away from certain headstones. Every local legend about Everlane Cemetery swarmed her thoughts: whispered stories of flickering lights, voices in the fog, and cold hands reaching in the dark.

She called her friend Juniper, who’d always been braver. When Juniper answered, her voice was muffled, sleepy. “Did you see the ghost again?” she asked.

“It was—different,” Adelaide said, describing the man at the gate, his words, the strange shift in air. Juniper listened, the line fizzing with mutual unease.

“So, what? You think the border between worlds is…thin out there?”

Adelaide stared at the windowpane, at her own faint reflection dissolving into the storm outside. “I think something’s bleeding through. I think he was warning me.”

Juniper sighed. “You don’t have to go back, you know.”

But that wasn’t how Adelaide was built. She hung up, crawled into bed, and dreamt of her brother’s voice calling from the other side of a thick, glassy wall. In her dream, she pressed her hand to it, feeling heat pulse through the chill.

The Unseen Forces Stir

By the following evening, the storm had passed. Low cloud cover hovered, bruised and expectant, as Adelaide returned to the cemetery. She braced herself with practicalities—heavy boots, flashlight, her camera slung across her chest. The world was too quiet; every cicada, every night bird, had gone silent.

Tonight, the keeper waited beneath the same archway.

She approached carefully. “You said you’re a keeper. Keeper of what?”

He didn’t answer directly. “There are places where the world grows thin and worn. Gates. Most never notice. You have.”

Adelaide’s grip on her flashlight tightened. “I just want my brother back. If there’s a way—”

“Desire is dangerous, Adelaide. To call someone back, you summon others. Someone must guide the lost back into shadow. Someone must keep the gate.”

She swallowed fear. “Are you trapped here?”

He smiled with a kind of pity. “Mostly. Sometimes I can step out, but not for long. My job is to keep the hungry ones from crossing. Not every ghost is made equal.”

A cold gust swept through the grass, carrying with it scents of moss and wet stone. Adelaide’s skin prickled. “Who was the last keeper?”

His eyes flickered, glassy in the dark. “You’d know his name.”

Adelaide’s heart hammered. Evan. She shook her head in denial, but memory bloomed—her brother’s obsession with the cemetery, the way he’d drawn wards she thought were doodles, the late-night walks, the exhausted mutterings about “holding something back.”

She exhaled, each breath rippling in the wind. “Is that why he vanished?”

“He stepped through,” the keeper said. “Just as I did. Just as you might, if you look too long.”

Suddenly, movement by the mausoleum caught Adelaide’s eye—a flicker of white cloth, a glimpse of a face she half-remembered from family photographs. Shivers spidered up her arms. “Evan?”

But the keeper seized her shoulder. “No. Memory breeds hunger. The more you want, the stronger they become.”

Adelaide tore free, sprinting toward the mausoleum. The door, usually rusted shut, stood slightly ajar. Inside, marble glimmered in her flashlight beam, names she recognized: ancestral family, neighbors, all dead for decades. Her brother’s voice echoed faintly: Addie, you can’t let it out. Promise me.

Wind whipped inside with unnatural force. In its heart, shadows thickened, coalescing into the shapes of cowering children, mothers shielding infants, men weeping silently. None were Evan.

Yet something else pressed out of the darkness: a smear of formless black, its eyes two burning holes. It seethed not with grief, but with need.

The keeper entered behind her. “You have to leave. Now.”

But Adelaide held her ground, tears hot against her cheeks. “If Evan’s here, I have to save him.”

The shadow lunged. Bone-chill locked her in place. She tried to scream, but the keeper’s voice cut through:

“There’s only one way to shut the gate—someone always takes the watch.”

The Price of Seeing

Later, Adelaide could never say how she found herself outside, gasping on the dew-wet grass, the mausoleum door sealed tight once more. Rain hammered the world, the storm returned full force. When she tried to recall the creature’s face, it evaporated like breath on glass.

Had she pulled Evan’s spirit back? Had the keeper protected her from possession? She was too exhausted to untangle memory from miracle.

Days stumbled onward. Every time she blinked, she saw afterimages—flashes of pale faces peering through cracks, hands clutching at the world. Everlane Cemetery hummed at the center of her thoughts. Sometimes, when dusk gathered, she heard her brother’s voice just out of reach, pleading: Not yet, Addie. Don’t open the gate.

Juniper tried to coax her away, taking her to diners and movie nights, but the pull strengthened.

One night, as Adelaide locked her apartment door, she felt a weight in her pocket—a key, old and iron, not belonging to any door she knew. She pressed it tight in her fist, hearing the keeper’s voice: “Someone always takes the watch.”

She could ignore the call. She could let the storm rage behind the mausoleum’s stone, trusting another would step forward when the time came. But duty and love twisted together, urging her back.

Everlane’s gates awaited, their hinges sighing at her approach. As Adelaide stepped through, she realized she didn’t fear the dark anymore. In the boundaries between worlds, she had finally found her brother—not in the flesh, but in the task of guarding what must not pass, in the purpose of keeping what must stay lost.

She became the watcher at dusk and dawn, the eyes that stared down hunger and memory. Maybe she’d save others, someday. Most wouldn’t know a thing about the dangers slipping close to their beds at midnight, about the thin places where longing could devour you whole.

But as long as someone held the line, Meridian stayed safe. And so, every night, Adelaide wound the cold iron key around her neck, stepped into the hush of the cemetery, and faced the unseen forces that pressed, eager and eternal, against the thinning door.

The Gate Remains

No one remembers the night things nearly crossed, not the neighbors watering their lawns or the children trailing sticks along fences. Only the wind remembers the price. Only Adelaide and those called to watch stand guard.

Everlane Cemetery gleams with dew. Adelaide waits on the threshold, the keeper’s duty etched into her soul, her brother’s laughter echoing in her memories. When you pass by, you might glimpse her—silent, vigilant, holding the darkness back.

And perhaps, if you look too long into the shadows as dusk deepens and the wind sings through the yew, you’ll wonder what else waits in the spaces between. The answer is simple: the gate remains, but for now, the unseen forces sleep.

Because someone, somewhere, still keeps the watch.
