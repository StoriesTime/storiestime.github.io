Mysterious Creatures in Folklore and Fairy Tales


In the lush green belt of Old Galloway, where the mists weave their ancient dances across the moors and the lochs slumber like glass eyes beneath centuries of rumor, people breathe in stories as easily as air. In this strange and saturated land, folklore grows wild and untamed, springing from every gnarled stump and hedgerow, and crawling into the lives of those who claim not to believe in it at all.

It is said—though, in Galloway, every living soul denies this—that for one night every decade, the borders between worlds retreat from their usual posts. When the moon glows coppery and strange atop Balgrennan Hill, the Midnight Market stirs awake, summoning both the weary and the wild.

Most in Galloway would rather not recall the old songs and stories, but there is always someone who is restless enough, foolish enough, or unfortunate enough to stumble upon the secrets that the land works so hard to hide.

One Moonlit Night…

The year was one that slipped between eras. Carts still jostled on cobblestones, and telegraph wires trembled in the wind above. In a squat, ivy-throttled croft, a girl named Marjorie MacTavish pushed aside the old curtain of lace. She watched the moon, fat and burnished, straining past a bank of ragged clouds.

Her grandmother shuffled into the lamplight, face a roadmap of every fable she’d ever muttered. “Don’t let the moon tempt you, girl,” she croaked, her voice like dry peat. “Copper moon’s the gate. Keep the latch fast tonight.”

But Marjorie was fourteen: old enough for secrets, young enough for dares. Her heart drummed hard with an urge she couldn’t name. So when the clock snorted midnight and all the house snored but her, she slipped on her boots and wandered out, guided by the thick pull of curiosity and the rich scent of loch water.

She followed the boundary where wild thyme and bog myrtle tang a person’s breath, where any sensible soul would turn back. But sense has ever been the enemy of adventure, and so Marjorie pressed onward until the ground trembled beneath her, rippling with a life that was wholly unnatural.

The Market Reveals Itself

From behind a mound of foxgloves, the world twisted. A shiver in the air: not cold, but expectant. In the hollow stood a market both luminous and shrouded, candles hovering midair, stalls woven from willow switches singing a silent tune. Lantern-light struck iron and bone, cloth and feather, all arrayed along crooked counters.

Figures moved behind the trestles: tall, horned things whose shadows shifted grotesquely; diminutive creatures with beetle-shell eyes; women with hair like bramble-flames and foxes’ tails swaying as if in an unseen breeze. Everywhere, a perfume of moss and sorrow and ancient laughter.

The first to notice Marjorie was a boy her age, pale-eyed and barefoot, dressed in clothes that might have been cut from a stormcloud. “First-timer,” he remarked with a grin sharp enough to cut the mist. “Careful where you stare. Not everyone likes being seen.”

Trying to steel herself, Marjorie swung her gaze through the crowd.

A great hound with eyes of burning peat—Black Shuck, whispered about in more than a few neighboring villages—guarded a cart full of moonberries, the shimmering fruit shifting colors with the pulse of the night. A merchant with antlers crowned by thistledown peddled wolves’ dreams in glass bottles. Old Nan Kelpie, famously beautiful when the rivers flooded, paraded a necklace of fishbones, her skin rippling with the light of drowned secrets. Overhead, a flock of will-o’-wisps traded pouches of shadow for bird bones and tiny iron nails.

Stories from every fireside tale were made flesh around her. She realized, with a terror as giddy as it was grim, that at the Midnight Market, folk tales bought, traded, and bartered themselves. Myths here were not contained to old books but breathing, brooding, and hungry.

The Trade of Curiosities

“Would you rather bargain or browse?” asked the barefoot boy, who introduced himself as Lew.

Marjorie, uncertain, stuck close to Lew as they passed a stall where a red-capped goblin haggled over pockets of silent time with a harried-looking tinker. Nearby, a selkie sold shed sealskins (no returns, strictly forbidden) for locketed promises.

Each creature had its story. Some stories reeked of sorrow, some shimmered with delight. Every transaction glittered with danger.

“Can I buy anything?” Marjorie whispered.

Lew’s answer: “You can offer. But be wary of what you’re willing to give. Everything here has a price, even what you didn’t know you owned.”

Marjorie wandered up to a stall covered in black velvet, run by a woman whose face flickered between that of a withered crone and a child. Arrayed before her were trinkets no human smith could forge: a spindle of spun dusk, a feather from a heron-king, tears cupped in crystal, and a stone that whispered endlessly.

Marjorie’s heart leapt at the spindle. She reached for it, but the stall-keeper’s eyes pinned her thumb.

“And what would you give, little rootling?” the woman croaked.

“I… I have nothing,” Marjorie faltered, cheeks stinging.

The woman leaned forward, her breath cold and sweet. “You have plenty: a secret never told, a memory you cherish, a year of sleep, the taste of your favorite fruit—”

Marjorie drew back, suddenly afraid she’d offer something she wouldn’t miss until years later. “I’ll just look,” she managed.

Lew’s smile was approving, if wry. “First-timers always fancy the spindle. But it spins things… not always what you expect.”

Whispers of the Past

Drawn on by curiosity, Marjorie watched as an ancient piper, nothing but bones inside a kilt, handed over three withered lilies in exchange for a new tune from a sprightly, bird-legged youth. The melody, once played, rippled through the market, making flowers bloom in hands and coins weep with laughter.

Further along, a trio of children—pale, barefoot, and solemn—bartered a lock of cloud, procured from the highest dawn, for a satchel stitched from starlight. On asking, Lew said quietly, “Lost folk. They’ll leave at sunrise, but won’t ever quite return home.”

A soft, delicate voice lured them next to a booth festooned with woven charms against the unseelie. Here sat the Cat Sith, mirth in its silver eyes, tail as silky as dusk. The Cat sieved milk between its teeth, offering luck for a gift of music.

Marjorie whistled the tune her father used to play late at night—half-forgotten, yet honest. The Cat purred, winding thrice around her legs and depositing a silver coin in her palm. “Luck for the journey home,” Lew translated.

Marjorie fingered the coin, aware that luck, like every magic at this market, was a matter of perspective.

Temptation and Danger

Through the damp glow and shadowed magic, the stalls pressed close. A russet-haired woman with webbed fingers waved jars of dreamless sleep; an imp traded in borrowed names—“Give me a name you never use, I’ll trade you a face no one knows.” Each seller carried a story like a sword, wielding their offerings against the unwary.

Lew pointed out the Fairy Queen herself, draped in a gown of mist and nettles, her teeth tiny but hungry. She watched the mortals with a gaze old as stone.

“Sometimes she trades in wishes,” Lew murmured. “But she never gives quite what you ask, and always exactly what you desire.”

Marjorie observed as a stooped farmer bartered the memory of his mother’s smile for a harvest that would never fail—a bargain that left him weeping with empty eyes, clutching golden grain to a hollow breast.

“Feeling lucky still?” Lew asked.

Marjorie’s skin prickled. She clutched her coin harder.

An Unexpected Bargain

Behind a stall overgrown with bindweed sat the oldest creature in the market: Old Agnes, half-troll, half-sorrow, who never forgot a name, and never repeated a secret. Her eyes were clouded like loch water, her hands enormous and kind.

“Not everything needs trading, love,” Old Agnes rasped. “Sometimes a story’s worth more than all the coin in Faerie.”

Marjorie, feeling unaccountably brave, told Old Agnes her story—the loss of her father to the sea, her grandmother’s warnings, the ache that never left her when people turned away from old stories.

Old Agnes nodded, tears glimmering in the crags of her cheeks. “That was a fair gift. Child, take this.”

She pressed into Marjorie’s hand a seed the color of winter dusk. “Plant this at sunrise. Let it remember the old songs. The land needs more storytellers.”

A warmth spread along Marjorie’s palm—a gentle, forgiving heat.

From somewhere behind, Lew took her elbow. “Dawn’s coming. If you’re still here when the cock crows, you’ll never quite belong in your own world again.”

Exodus at Dawn

Even as the first threads of daylight crept over Balgrennan Hill, the market shivered and began to fade. Stalls packed up their wares with motions practiced over millennia; the stallholders’ features blurred into the mist, their laughter a memory already dissolving.

“Come on!” Lew urged.

They hurried through bracken, the taste of loch air sharp, until Galloway’s familiar bogs and scattered birch trees welcomed them once more. Marjorie looked down—the coin from the Cat and the dusk-colored seed were all she had to show for the wildest night of her life.

She turned to thank Lew, but he was gone—maybe never hers to thank at all.

The Story Grows

Back home, Marjorie planted the seed. She sang the old tune, shyly at first, then with all her longing and hope. The plant took root in the croft’s richest soil and by summer, strange blue flowers blossomed—each one humming with a tune only Marjorie could hear.

Her grandmother asked nothing, but watched her with eyes that twinkled like old magic.

In time, neighbors noticed that something had changed in the MacTavish croft. Children came to listen to Marjorie’s stories—tales sifted from the Midnight Market, stories that kept the darkness at bay and coaxed the buried lore back into the land’s pulse. The blue flowers multiplied each year, humming a gentle defiance to forgetfulness.

The Midnight Market never returned in Marjorie’s youth. Yet, sometimes, in the deepest part of night, a silver coin would jingle on her windowsill, and the fragrance of wild thyme and shadow would float through her dreams, whispering a promise:

Every story comes home, and those who remember to listen can always find their way back.

And so, deep in the heart of Galloway, among foxgloves and forgotten songs, Marjorie’s tiny garden bloomed—an unlikely beacon for the wondrously strange. For in a world that insists on reason, a flicker of magic—like an untamed story—may yet find root, if only one soul dares to wander where the old boundaries break.
