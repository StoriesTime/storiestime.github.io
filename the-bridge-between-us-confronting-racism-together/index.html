The Bridge Between Us: Confronting Racism Together


Rhett had lived in Brookston his whole life. It was the kind of town that moved slower than the rest of the world, balancing precariously between nostalgia and stubbornness. Rhett thought he understood it all—the limestone sidewalks, the century-old clock tower that never quite told the right time, the town’s creaky, trained pleasantries. It was safe, predictable, and entirely his world.

Then came Aliyah.

She moved to Brookston on the third Tuesday of June. A moving truck rattled down Elm Street just as the church bells rang noon. By the end of the day, Rhett had pieced together that Aliyah Parker, a young Black woman in her late 20s, had just taken the history teacher position at Brookston High. What he couldn’t piece together was why anyone would want to move to a place as colorless—metaphorically and literally—as Brookston.

Rhett saw her for the first time that Thursday when she walked into the hardware store where he worked. Her hair was coiled into twists that gleamed under the store’s fluorescent lighting, and she wore a vivid green scarf that reminded him of spring leaves. She stepped past the brass-handled axes and shelving nails, looking around like she was trying to make sense of something.

He hesitated before calling out, “You need help finding something?”

Her eyes flicked to him—almond-brown and steady. “I’m looking for primer.”

“Painting something specific?” he asked, already guiding her through the narrow aisles.

“Just the walls,” she replied. “New home. They’re… drab.”

The hesitancy in her voice surprised him, but he nodded. “Yeah, primer helps if you’re painting over anything old or dingy. We’ve got Kilz over here. It’s good for tough spots.”

The exchange should’ve ended once the primer landed in her hands, but something about her lingered—her quiet composure, the curiosity in her glance.

As she reached for her wallet, she added, “I get the feeling this town doesn’t get many new folks.”

Rhett shrugged. “Not lately. Most young people move into the city nearby. What brought you here?”

“Opportunity,” she said, slipping cash over the counter. “And, honestly, I thought it might be… quieter here.”

The way she said it made Rhett wonder if she already regretted the choice.

Two weeks later, Rhett found himself at a community meeting in the basement of the town hall. These meetings were usually humdrum affairs—talks about potholes, festival planning, or who’d take over as chair of the garden committee. But this evening was different.

The room was packed, a low buzz of tension swirling between mismatched chairs and foldable tables. Rhett spotted her immediately. Aliyah stood near the back, arms crossed, her face unreadable.

The talk was about the high school—specifically, a proposed unit Aliyah wanted to introduce for her sophomore history class. “The Reality of Reconstruction,” she had called it. She explained the curriculum centered on the aftermath of the Civil War, the rise of Jim Crow laws, and how systemic racism shaped the twentieth century. Rhett hadn’t seen such a fuss over a school curriculum in all his 29 years.

“I just don’t think it’s appropriate for our kids,” said Debra Schmidt, a mother of two. Her tone was laced with venom thinly disguised as concern. “It’s important to learn history, but we shouldn’t be making it divisive.”

“Divisive?” Aliyah’s voice broke through the air, crisp but calm. “Telling the truth about history isn’t divisive; it’s necessary. Our students deserve to learn the full picture of the past. Ignorance doesn’t protect them—it handicaps them.”

Debra flushed but pressed on, “How are we supposed to teach kids to love their town and country if we’re just pointing out the bad parts?”

Aliyah stood her ground. “By showing them that love and criticism aren’t opposites. True patriotism means understanding the good and the bad. When we confront the hard truths of our past, we create the opportunity for a better future.”

Rhett found himself fascinated, watching the room split: some leaning in thoughtfully, others recoiling. He felt unease deep in his gut, unsure where he stood.

When the meeting ended, Rhett lingered on the steps outside, ignoring the way a cool breeze coiled around his wrists. Aliyah walked out not long after, her expression tired but unbowed.

He surprised himself by speaking. “They’re not used to new ideas here.”

“Small towns rarely are,” she replied, meeting his gaze. There was no anger in her tone, just a muted sort of exhaustion.

He gestured to the bench nearby. “What made you take the job here, of all places? There has to be easier places to teach what you just talked about.”

Aliyah let out a short laugh. “Funny, I didn’t think you’d be the kind of guy to ask that.”

“What kind of guy am I?”

“The kind that blends in here,” she said, gently, though he wasn’t sure whether to feel defensive or flattered.

She sat down first, and after a beat, he followed. “I taught at a big city school before this,” she said. “But I realized that smaller places like this shouldn’t get to stay out of the conversation. Talking about racism isn’t just a city issue. It’s everywhere. And if no one tries here… what’s stopping the system from repeating itself?”

Rhett didn’t have an answer, so he offered the one thing that felt honest. “Must be exhausting.”

“It is. But so is silence.”

They didn’t talk long after that; the conversation ended when she thanked him for listening and walked home. Yet something shifted in Rhett. That night, he read everything he could find online about Reconstruction, Jim Crow laws, and redlining. He’d always thought of racism as something historical or distant—it embarrassed him to realize how shallow his understanding had been.

Over the coming months, something strange began to happen in Brookston. Conversations started sparking—not just at PTA meetings or within the sterile walls of Town Hall, but in cafes and barber shops. Words like “privilege” made some people bristle, but others leaned in, willing to learn. Aliyah led workshops in the evenings, guiding discussions with unflinching patience.

Rhett, almost unintentionally, found himself attending. Sometimes he’d sit in the back, just listening. Other times, he’d contribute, stumbling over his words but recognizing the importance nonetheless.

Once, during a particularly heated debate about whether the high school library should carry books discussing racial inequality, Rhett stood up and said something he hadn’t planned: “If we’re too scared to let kids read the truth, what does that say about us?”

Aliyah caught his eye afterward, quietly smiling, though she said nothing.

By the end of the year, Brookston hadn’t transformed overnight. Prejudices didn’t vanish with the flick of a switch, and change remained a painstakingly slow process. But something indisputable had shifted. Walls Rhett never even realized existed had started to crack.

One day in early spring, while repainting the chipped porch of the hardware store, Rhett saw Aliyah walking toward the library, her green scarf fluttering behind her. He raised his hand in greeting, and she waved back, her grin warm and knowing. It was one small moment, one piece of something larger.

Brookston wasn’t healed, not by a long shot. But the bridge between them—between people who had once stood worlds apart—wasn’t just theoretical anymore. It was real. For the first time, Rhett started to believe that maybe, just maybe, it could hold.
