Leonardo Da Vinci: The Man Behind the Masterpieces


The scent of linseed oil and wet plaster lingered long after sunset, curling in the dim candlelight of a cramped Florentine workshop. In a dusty corner, a boy knelt with a single burning imagination. His name was Leonardo, and at thirteen, every surface within reach had already surrendered to sketches: anatomical studies, machines that grew out of fantasy, birds captured in mid-flight, a series of wild-eyed cats. Even Lucia, the housekeeper, scolded him daily for leaving detailed doodles on her cooking bowls.

Leonardo watched his master—Andrea del Verrocchio—work a slab of marble, muscle and artistry entwined, chiseling rhythm echoing the church bells beyond. Something stirred in the young apprentice. He wanted, craved, that same control over creation, but found the stones too silent for his questions.

“What do you see when you close your eyes, maestro?” Leonardo once asked, eyes bright as noon sun.

Verrocchio considered. “I see whatever I wish. That is why I am a sculptor.” But his brow furrowed when he saw the sketches Leonardo brought him—mechanical lions, flying machines, studies of hands, and unsettling dissections of lilies and frogs.

“Why so many things, Leonardo?” the master mused aloud, leafing through the drawings.

“Because the world itself is made of many things. I wish to understand all of them,” the apprentice replied.

The Yearning for Discovery

Leonardo would walk the length of the Arno River at dawn, watching water eddy against rocks, mapping the motion to diagrams in his mind. He saw science where others saw only scenery. He plucked reeds to study their hollows, watched the way light splintered on the surface, once even pulling apart a dragonfly just to see how its wings were stitched.

At night, he scribbled notes in mirrored writing—a habit equal parts secrecy and discomfort with signature. His first experiments stumbled between disaster and marvel: a mechanical automaton, a failed glider launched from Fiesole’s hill, an oil painting of an angel in “The Baptism of Christ” that left Verrocchio swearing never to paint again.

Young Leonardo’s restless genius blossomed into legend. Subjects ranging from sculpting and painting to anatomy and architecture seized his interest. Churches hired, then dismissed, him for his unpredictable methods—a planned fresco for the Palazzo Vecchio was abandoned mid-wall when he decided the paint wasn’t luminous enough, an “improvement” nobody understood.

The Anatomy of Obsession

Fascination eclipsed fear when he first entered the Ospedale of Santa Maria Nuova, bargaining for cadavers. He cut, drew, measured undisturbed by the presence of death—methodical as a scholar, reverent as a courtier.

His drawings documented the exquisite mechanics beneath the skin: sinews, hearts, vessels, the roots of teeth. He believed understanding the body would unlock the secret architecture of beauty—a geometry that governed everything from the twist of a dancer’s hand to the flight of a swallow.

Codices filled with studies accumulated, some pages spattered with ink, others blotched by midnight candle wax. He wrote, in that peculiar mirrored scribe, “Nature is the mistress of all masters.”

For Leonardo, art and science became inseparable. Each supported the other—the painter’s shade teaching the scientist shadow, the botanist’s scrutiny guiding the sculptor’s curve of marble. His notebooks bore whispers of invention: flying machines inspired by kites and bats, armored vehicles like beetles, war machines, mechanical bridges, and even fantastical underwater breathing apparatus.

The Pursuit of the Impossible

Florence gave way to Milan, where Ludovico Sforza was eager for anything modern, miraculous, or martial. Leonardo flourished as court engineer, mounting stage spectacles, composing lutesongs, guiding the construction of canals, promising miraculous machines to outwit French invaders. Yet, he always returned to his two passions: the measureless curiosity of invention and the endless quest for perfect beauty.

Sometime during those years, he began a portrait—a merchant’s wife, her hands folded, lips curled in a secret. “La Gioconda,” they called her. He carried her likeness from city to city, never satisfied, adding a stroke here, a glimmer there, until the painting seemed to breathe.

He built grand illusions for Sforza’s festival banquets—swans emerging from clocks, mechanical lions padding across marble floors, blossoms unfolding from gilded shells. Wherever he walked, he watched, learning—from the chunk of a blacksmith’s hammer to the play of wind against cathedral buttresses.

His observations were relentless, sometimes maddening: Leonardo spent hours staring at ripples on puddles or the way smoke rose and fragmented. He climbed clocktowers to study storms, dissected birds, observed starling flocks over the fields and painstakingly drew their patterns. His curiosity was notorious; even Michelangelo—a rival—once teased him, “You start many things but finish only a few.”

Leonardo shrugged: “Details matter to those who desire mastery.”

The Cast of Chaos

War swept Milan; Sforza fled; Leonardo found himself caught between rival armies, a refugee with only trunks of paintings and manuscripts. He drifted to Mantua, then Venice, sketching city plans and defense systems—canals, barricades, inventions both impossible and ahead of their time.

In a Venice racked by plague, he turned his eye to architecture and urban drainage, drawing up plans for hygienic canals, circular cities ventilated by wind, hospitals better suited to healing than horror.

Everywhere he went, he absorbed local craftsmen, thieves, musicians, actors, doctors, and soldiers into his orbit. He quizzed them on floods, fire, flight, and the anatomy of horses. Children chased him for sketches; nobility commissioned portraits and mechanical birds. He took in an orphan apprentice—Gian Giacomo Caprotti, nicknamed “Salai”—whose antics alternately delighted and infuriated him, but whose presence was long-lasting, both muse and mischief.

Leonardo’s capacity for affection was as wide as his genius, embracing beauty wherever he found it—in men and women, beggars’ faces, wildflowers by the roadside. His love affairs—rumored, probable, secretive—were never the point; what mattered was discovery: the soul behind the eyes, the reason hands move as they do, the solution to the riddles of shadow and light.

Masterpieces from Mystery

His paintings drew pilgrims—first Florentines, then all of Italy, finally the world. “The Last Supper,” in the refectory of Santa Maria delle Grazie, captured apostles in a moment of stunned revelation, their grief and shock breaking like scattered light, hands splayed, eyes wide, anguish and faith painted in a single, fleeting instant.

His “Virgin of the Rocks” cast mysticism in the hidden recesses of a cave, fingers poised in gestures of mystic significance, all smudged with the chiaroscuro of genius. “Lady with an Ermine” seemed alive; the animal nestled in the woman’s arms, rendered with such intensity only an anatomist could manage.

And always, Mona Lisa lingered by his side, her expression a cipher, half-smile shifting as the painter himself changed. More than one observer whispered that she was the master’s private obsession, a puzzle whose solution remained always out of reach.

But masterpiece was not all. He built, disassembled, started and abandoned. Sforza’s massive equestrian statue collapsed into legend when invading armies melted its bronze for cannon. Leonardo shrugged and set to work on yet another treatise—this one on water movement, or flight, or perhaps the secret ratios of the universe hidden in the petals of a sunflower.

The Mind’s Restlessness

Leonardo’s personal life was a mosaic of contradictions: elegant but careless, charming but forgetful, known for his failings as much as his triumphs. He dressed in vibrant silks, neglected debts, gave last coins to beggars. He abhorred war but designed weapons. He was lionized, envied, accused of heresy, at times ostracized—for his independence, his interests, his refusal to conform.

His handwriting was infuriating to his assistants—a tangle of Latin, Greek, and Florentine Tuscan, inverted left to right. He maintained that writing this way trained his brain and guarded his secrets. Some joked that only Da Vinci could read Da Vinci.

He never married, never settled. At fifty, he wrote in his notebook: “I have offended God and mankind because my work didn’t reach the quality it should have.” If his contemporaries had seen this confession, they would have laughed—no one else matched the mastery of his line, the clarity of his vision, the ambition of his questions.

A Legacy Carried By Wind

Summoned finally to France by King Francis I, he crossed the Alps with Salai, his loyal notebooks, unfinished Mona Lisa, and vast rolls of paper full of dreams. He lived his final years in Amboise, given a manor and a pension, regarded as a marvel, visited by artists, scientists, and philosophers from across Europe.

He spent his last days roaming the Loire valley, observing geese at the riverbank, designing palaces for imaginary cities, tinkering with hydraulics, debating astronomy and music at royal banquets. He died, they say, in the arms of the king, who later wept for the loss of “the greatest mind of the Italian Renaissance.”

The Invisible Thread

Long after his death, his notebooks traveled—a trunkful in Salai’s inheritance, others scattered among collectors, some lost for centuries. They would inspire new generations: Galileo, Newton, even modern aeronautical engineers, all tracing glimpses of flight first imagined by Leonardo. His approach—relentless, holistic, experimental—became a model for innovators everywhere.

His paintings, too, underwent journeys: Mona Lisa, smuggled across battlefields, now quiet beneath bulletproof glass; The Last Supper, restored, admired, weathered by time and admiration; his anatomical sketches, long outpaced by modern medicine, but still teaching humility before the mysteries of nature.

What made Leonardo not just great, but unique, was this: he saw the world not as a collection of isolated disciplines, but as a tapestry with every thread connected. For him, every question was a door—every answer simply another question, richer and more bewildering.

“The painter with a mind of fire, the engineer haunted by angels,” wrote a later admirer. Leonardo himself might simply have said that he never took the world at face value—that its secrets were infinite, and every masterpiece was simply a map for those who dared to look.

Deep in the heart of the world’s museums, hiding in codices and mosaics of light and shadow, Leonardo’s spirit still wanders—unraveling nature’s riddles, chasing the elusive marriage of beauty and truth, always beginning anew, like a child with his first piece of chalk in a Florentine workshop, daring to say: Let us see what is possible.
