The Folklore Keeper: Tales Passed Down Through Generations


The stone house at the edge of Drummond’s deep forest had stood for centuries, gray but strong, its walls humming quietly with a thousand stories. In that house lived old Órlaith Byrne, the Folklore Keeper, whose age no one could quite place. Children speculated—from whispers of her sleeping in a birch crib sewn by fairies, to tales she’d traded her years for an extra hundred nights to finish her stories. Adults, less fanciful, simply called her “wise-old-Órlaith,” nodding respectfully in her presence but rarely asking about her past.

Each year on the eve of Midsummer, villagers followed winding cow paths through the thickets, lanterns bobbing, drawn by the promise of what Órlaith might tell. Her tales threaded the living to their ancestors, weaving meaning into the mossy stones, the old oak at the crossroads, the silent lake that blinked in the moonlight.

Tonight, the hour was near midnight, and the Byrne house flickered with warmth. The fire spat shadows on stuffed chairs and embroidered pillows. Cups of peat-brown tea steamed, mingling with the smell of damp earth that clung to boots. The small sitting room overflowed with faces: the village blacksmith, the schoolmistress with her inquisitive twin boys, the soft-spoken apothecary who still believed in cures brewed beneath certain moons. There were a few outsiders too—visitors with notebooks and arching eyebrows, lured by rumors of magic and tenacity.

Órlaith, stick-thin and serene, settled herself into the wide cedar chair by the fireplace. Her white hair, woven into an intricate braid, gleamed in the light. She folded her hands over the battered leather book resting in her lap—her “book of keeping,” as she called it, though she’d never once been seen to write in it. Stories, she claimed, lived between the heartbeats of the listeners.

Once everyone had settled and the hush thickened, she began. Her voice, smokier than the peat fire, still erupted with unexpected clarity.

“Tonight’s tale is older than the house, older than these woods, and perhaps as old as memory itself. Before roads, before stone fences, when deer drank freely from the river and the kingfisher’s cry echoed unmuffled, there was a boy named Fiachra.

Fiachra lived with his grandmother in a cottage so small it barely held their laughter. He had no mother nor father—only the grandmother, and her songs, and the tangle of trees that fed and sheltered them. Each night, she spun him legends of the ones who came before: of warring clans who battled not with blades, but with riddles sharpened on moonlit stones; of animals who donned human skins; of a time when the boundary between the living and the shadowfolk was as porous as a foggy dawn.

Fiachra’s grandmother was a keeper, too. It was she who taught him that every cottage, every field, had a root deep in some story—often forgotten, never truly lost.

One evening, as dusk turned the world lavender, Fiachra wandered farther than he had ever gone. Hunger for adventure pushed his feet while his mind replayed tales of the forest spirits, those ancient listeners guarding forgotten corners. Crossing a low stone bridge, he heard a peculiar melody: thin, reedy, winding through the branches like a lost father calling home.

Drawn by the song, he stumbled upon a clearing. At its center stood a tree—massive and hollow, its limbs tangled with streamers cut from star-stitched cloth. At the heart of the trunk sat a figure, pale as birch bark, with hair shivering like spider silk.

‘You’ve come for a story,’ the figure greeted. ‘Or a gift? Here, they are much the same.’

Fiachra swallowed his fear. ‘I seek nothing. Only, I followed your music.’

The figure’s lips curled in a smile. ‘You know of keeping, little one. Do you know the price?’

Fiachra remembered his grandmother’s lessons: ‘Every story keeps you, too. Share it, or be lost.’

The figure nodded. ‘Then take this.’ In Fiachra’s palm, she placed a seed—gleaming gold, warm as bread. ‘Planted at need, it grows into the story you need most.’

He returned home, the seed heavy in his pocket. His grandmother, eyes shining, said, ‘You met Her. We all do, someday. But not everyone brings back a gift.’

Years hurried on, as they do. Fiachra’s cottage survived hard frosts and generous summers. But drought came, and the river fell silent; the harvest spoiled. Fiachra remembered his gift. He dug a hollow near the river’s throat, planting the seed with care. He watered it with his own tears, whispered to it memories of laughter, told it every story he could recall.

Under the full moon, the earth split. From the hollow rose a tree—vast, bearing fruit like glass lanterns and leaves inked with writing. Fiachra gathered the fruit and shared it among his neighbors. Those who ate remembered tales their mothers had hush-whispered, or songs their fathers had drummed on tabletops.

Stories returned. With them, hope grew, wild and untamed as clover. The drought ended, but more important: no one forgot who they’d been.”

Órlaith closed her eyes, as if listening to something distant.

A hush, soft as down, followed in the room. Then a child—a bold one named Eamonn—piped up. “Is that true? The story tree? Did it really save the village?”

Órlaith cracked one eye open. “I tell you this: in Drummond, when the crops grow weak, neighbors gather at the old stone bridge and share their tales anew. Maybe that’s all a story tree ever was—each voice another branch making the old roots stronger.”

A gentle laughter rippled; several adults exchanged glances, some skeptical, others yearning.

Before the moment could fade, Mrs. Collins, the blacksmith’s wife, asked, “How did you become the keeper, Órlaith? Who did you meet in your woods?”

Órlaith turned her gaze to the fire. “That’s another tale, and a longer one.” The clock chimed midnight. “Why not share a story of your own? Tonight, we keep what matters.”

Others picked up the thread seamlessly. The apothecary shared a memory of her great-uncle teaching her the recipe for nettle salve by candlelight. A fretful man from the next village recited a half-forgotten myth about a puca who’d tricked his grandfather into dancing all night. The twins, barely managing to keep their eyes open, took turns remembering the first time they heard a curlew’s cry echo through the hawthorn thicket.

As tales spiraled, bouncing across the lamplight and the circle of faces, something subtle shifted. Listeners straightened, caught glimpses of themselves in the oldest narratives. They traded doubts for curiosity, letting their problems shrink beneath the dome of shared remembering.

As always, in the early hours before dawn, Órlaith saw her guests off—some in twos and threes, others alone, their lanterns trailing sparks on the mist. Some years, a fresh tale would enter her “book,” whispered back to her as she sat by the dying logs. Other years, simply the laughter and sorrow of her neighbors would enrich the soil of herself.

Long after everyone was gone, the fire guttered. Órlaith sat alone. Outside, wind whistled in the yew branches. She let her thoughts drift backward, tracing the chain: her own grandmother’s gentle hand, the silver-haired woman who led her along the forest path, the night she’d first heard music unlike any she could play. Stories, she mused, did not belong to keepers—they only asked to be carried, to be tended, to be given as gifts.

At sunrise, Órlaith opened her battered window. Birds called over muddy fields where villagers trudged to chores. At the distant edge of the garden, wild clover tangled with foxglove, and the air buzzed with the possibility of more stories.

In Drummond, no day was ordinary. Each stone hummed, each tree whispered. For as long as a single voice dared to remember and share, the threads that bound them all would hold—ancient, mutable, ever-growing.

And in the keeper’s cottage, generation upon generation collected quietly, safe as seeds, ready to bloom wherever a tale was needed most.

And so the legacy of the Folklore Keeper thrived—written not in ink or stone, but in voices, in memory, and in the quiet magic of stories kept alive through generations.
