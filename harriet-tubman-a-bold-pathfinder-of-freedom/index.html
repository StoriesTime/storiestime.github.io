Harriet Tubman: A Bold Pathfinder of Freedom


In the gloom and obscurity of the 19th century, Harriet Tubman, boldly and imperatively, took up the call for freedom. While many would have been intimidated by the official System and its cruel demands for servitude, Harriet Tubman was not like many. Her resolute determination made her a pathfinder of freedom, planting the seeds of hope in the hearts of those preyed upon by the endless march of this dishonorable age.

You see, in a world where the color of one’s skin hampered the path to freedom, Tubman fearlessly threaded her way through the maze of oppression. She was born into hardship, a task not easily shouldered, but Harriet seized each challenge with the fervor of the desperate and the ingenuity of the indomitable. She routinized smuggling texts like the Book of Songs and the New Testament in the Bible, sacred repositories of solace and inspiration for the downtrodden.

When the call to escape to freedom rang out, the men and women bound in servitude leaned on Tubman’s legendary resolve. She had the courage to lead them, map out each step and reveal safe havens so they could live free from their master’s cruel purview. Tubman was nothing short of a guide in the wilderness of tyranny.

The path, however, was fraught with danger. Harriet Tubman was no novice to peril. As a conductor on the Underground Railroad, she bravely confronted any obstacle that dared to impede the course to freedom. Slave-catchers, bounty hunters, and even her master’s hired thugs followed her every move. But with a mind prepared for adversity, Harriet always found an escape. Physical courage was but one weapon in her arsenal; cunning and swiftness were her ever-present companions.

One of the most daring exploits of Harriet Tubman was when she single-handedly led 70 slaves to freedom in one extraordinary mission. This was accomplished through a series of intricate arrangements - from trading where they would secretly gather, to the construction of hiding places, each step finely choreographed.

Escape from the serfdom was no easy feat - just slipping away meant leaving behind a life deliberately stripped of one’s dignity and self-worth. However, Harriet Tubman’s magnetic personality made her a beacon of hope in despair. She was known to be the ‘Moses’ of her people, guiding them from slavery’s merciless precincts to the pulsating, vibrant wilderness of freedom.

In 1860, Harriet Tubman turned her gaze toward the slave-holding Confederacy. The Civil War was still a few years away, and she had an idea - to free her people from bondage by any means necessary. And, as always, Harriet marched fearlessly in her cause. With her brother and his 18-year-old son, she embarked on a daring journey to scout out Confederate strongholds and gather intelligence to aid the Union army.

However, her days of semi-freedom were numbered. Scarcely six years into her newfound respectability, a call came from the nation’s capital for Harriet to serve on a critical sanitary project. This translated to her taking up the role of a nurse, a tireless caregiver tending to the physical and emotional wounds of the soldiers. Not many realize that she even medically assisted in the first battles of the Civil War. Despite this enormous role, she never let this burden weigh her down.

In the end, the world would remember her not just as someone who led scores to freedom, but also for her selfless service at the height of a brutal war, as an unbidden soldier and caregiver. Harriet Tubman passed away on March 10, 1913, at the age of 70. However, her legacy continues to linger; ringing in the ears of every downtrodden, inspiring each heart brave enough to fight against the imminent purview of oppression.

Life’s challenges and oppressions did not break Harriet Tubman, and neither can they break the spirit of countless others imbibed with her valor. She escaped the chains of iniquity and fear not once, but time and time again - a testament to her indomitable spirit unyielding to the powers that sought to break her, urging all to fearlessly forge onward in the path less trodden.
