Living With Racism: A Short Story of Struggle and Hope


The first time Amir realized that something was different, he was six years old. His grandmother pressed a gentle kiss to his forehead, whispered, Always hold your head high, baby, and handed him a backpack already heavy for his small frame. He wondered why she always had that look—the one halfway between worry and pride—whenever she waved him off for school.

His neighborhood was a quilt of voices—Cantonese laced with gossip, chatter in Vietnamese and Spanish, the scent of garlic and cumin rising from apartments veined with potholes and hope. At school, though, Amir learned that each patch in the quilt was carefully inspected, labeled, and sometimes dismissed. Mrs. Hansen taught math in the mornings, her hands always clutching the marker like a lifeline. She smiled at Amir, but reserved her stories—the ones about her grandchildren and vacation trips—for the children with lighter skin and crisp, bright sneakers.

At recess, Amir liked to play soccer. Sometimes, when his foot found the ball, he forgot about the outside world and the comparisons. Other times, when his team lost, the other kids would groan, “If Amir hadn’t slowed us down, maybe we would’ve won.” No one said it directly, but Amir saw in their eyes the secret arithmetic—points subtracted for skin, for hair, for the languages that sometimes slipped out with his laughter.

His best friend in primary school was Minh, who lived two buildings away, shared his gummy smile and his knack for comic book sketches. Minh’s parents owned a corner store, and sometimes the kids would sneak candy, pretending they were pirates. Minh’s mother watched them play, her gaze sharp, as if waiting for the world to show who was safe and who was not.

One bleak Monday, Amir’s father was late to pick him up. The school office, all white tile and echoing shoes, felt more like a clinic than a safe haven. The secretary—a hawk with bifocals—looked at Amir as if he were an unclaimed package. When his father finally arrived, breathless and apologetic, the secretary asked, “Is that your real father, Amir?” She said it in that way, like a joke, but Amir flinched. He hadn’t done anything except exist.

As Amir grew, the incidents grew with him—transformed, sharpened, sometimes so subtle he questioned if they really happened. Teachers demanded, “Speak up! Don’t mumble!” when he answered questions softly, yet praised others’ quiet confidence. He was told to check his attitude if he protested a lower grade on a group project, though everyone knew who had done the work.

One winter, his high school history teacher divided the class for a debate on immigration. “Take the side against it, Amir,” the teacher said, not bothering to ask for volunteers. Laughter fluttered through the room. Amir’s cheeks burned but he obeyed. After class, he lingered to ask why. The teacher shrugged. “Just wanted to see how you’d handle it. You should get used to defending your place here.”

When Amir was fifteen, his father lost his job. The factory, once fat with orders, shuttered in a midwinter downsizing. Amir spent late nights at the kitchen table translating forms, writing emails for his father, whose English was steady but accented. On weekends, Amir helped his mother clean offices—corridors of polished glass, computer screens like dull moons.

In these offices, people didn’t always see them. They walked past Amir and his mother with brisk nods, sometimes shivering as if the cold was their fault. Once, while wiping down the sink, Amir overheard two men laughing by the water cooler.

"They hire anyone these days," one said.  
"Can you even understand a damn word?" said the other.

Amir pressed the cloth tightly in his fist.

Resilience became Amir’s armor. He learned how to turn embarrassment into silence, anger into ambition. He watched news reports about protests and violence, seeing faces that looked like his own attached to stories laced with suspicion. He spent afternoons at the library, feeding himself histories of struggle and resistance, the words of Baldwin, Angelou, Morrison.

In the gym, basketball provided a brief escape. His coach, Mr. Walker, encouraged him to lead drills, to offer opinions. Yet when the team went to compete in districts, Amir was the only one checked at the entrance, his bag carefully searched. Someone muttered, “Just procedure.”

At a party after the finals, Amir watched as his classmates paired off, music pounding, laughter bouncing off the walls. As he reached over for a soda, one boy blocked his way. “Heard your dad finally got a job—the gas station, right?” he said, voice slick with mockery.

A girl beside him giggled behind her hand. “I like your accent,” she said, though Amir hadn’t spoken.

Amir wanted to vanish, but he stubbornly met their gaze. “Not everyone gets to choose their start,” he said. The conversation died, replaced by uneasy silence.

The spring of Amir’s senior year, his history teacher assigned the class a “personal heritage project.” Amir gathered old photographs—his grandmother in Lahore, his father at twenty leaning against a rickety balcony, his mother’s wedding day shrouded in marigolds. He chose to write his family’s story in verse.

When Amir read his poem aloud, his voice steady, his classmates listened. For once, the room did not crackle with snickers or disbelief. When he finished, nobody clapped; a gentle hush lingered.

Later, one student found him at his locker. “I never knew you went through that,” she said. “Sometimes, I think it’s not real, you know? That people just exaggerate.” She paused, then, “I’m sorry.”

Amir shrugged. “It’s not your fault.”

That night, Amir told his story again, this time to himself, this time without shame.

In college, Amir found kinship—a cluster of students named Mohammad, Li, Anita, Kwame, all hungry to belong. They talked about code-switching, about the exhaustion of having to explain and defend, about angry fathers and tender grandmothers. Together, they marched, chanted, scribbled slogans on signs, sat in campus offices organizing events.

For the first time, Amir felt both invisible and powerful. At a rally, someone grabbed the microphone and screamed until her voice cracked. Amir wept, and did not care who saw.

He interned at a community center, working with younger students—kids like Minh’s younger brother, who now looked up at Amir as if he was already a hero. Sometimes, the teens whispered stories about racist teachers, about being followed in stores. Amir gave them space to talk, to process, to breathe. He watched as their faces shifted from despair to hope.

Once, he invited his mother to visit the community center. She fussed over him, complained about the unfamiliar smells. “You’re doing good work,” she said softly, adjusting his collar. “You’re making things better.”

Years later, Amir sat at a park bench, watching as his own daughter—stubborn, bright-eyed—chased pigeons across the path. The city’s skyline glowed in the dusk, sharp as a promise. An older woman sat beside him, hands gnarled with age, knitting quietly. She glanced at his daughter, at Amir, and nodded.

“She’s got your spirit,” the woman said.

Amir smiled. “I hope so.”

The woman’s eyes were wise, and sad. “The world is not always kind. But maybe it learns. Maybe your daughter won’t have to fight as you did.”

Amir looked at the city, saw the river ripple with light, heard his daughter’s laughter. He thought about his father’s rough hands, his grandmother’s kitchen songs, Minh’s still-familiar smile. He remembered the slights, the suspicion, the desire to disappear—and the choice, again and again, to stay visible, to assert his right to joy.

“I’ll teach her,” Amir said quietly, “to hold her head high.”

Beside him, the woman nodded, her eyes bright with shared memory.

In the distance, Amir’s daughter ran, arms outstretched, face turned toward the open sky.
