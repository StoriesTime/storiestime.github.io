Ashes in the Wind: A Volcano's Fury


The dawn came pale and uncertain over the village of San Lucía, draped along the skirts of the sleeping giant, Volcán de la Niebla. Mist curled in luminous bands around the black flanks of the mountain, gentle as a lover’s hand, almost hiding the patchwork of green terraces and the scattered adobe homes below. For centuries, the people had woven their lives along this deliberate slope, tending coffee in the high fields and corn in the valley. Yet in every heart there flickered a secret unease, a memory as old as the stones themselves—the knowledge that their mountain was not only guardian, but also destroyer.

Miguel remembered the last time the earth shivered, once in his mother’s arms, the mud walls trembling and the air humming with a sound that was not quite thunder. He had been five, and now, at twenty-six, he often rose before the sun and watched the horizon, as if the dark silhouette could offer answers.

He was at the crest of the ridge, the air sharp with scent of pine and damp soil, when he heard the first dull roar. It came from deep within, a low migraine of sound that vibrated through his bones. The birds fell silent—a different silence, heavy and pressed with foreboding.

Miguel dropped his hoe and ran, his feet sliding on gravel, breath turning ragged. The sky behind the volcano was different now—a bruise-colored ribbon growing wider, pulsing with a sickly orange. A distant boom rumbled through the earth, and the ground shuddered again, this time like the stomach of some monstrous beast awakening.

He stumbled into the center of San Lucía, where neighbors gathered in clumps, their faces upturned, mouths open in silent prayers and curses. The old priest, Padre Gabriel, tottered near the cracked bell tower, clutching his rosary as if he could lasso salvation from the sky.

“It’s waking,” someone whispered, words carried down the line like the beginnings of an incantation. “She’s waking.”

Above them, the world changed. Clouds swirled and parted, unveiling an angry boil at the summit—molten rock glowing, spewing flickers of fire, while a thick column of ash stormed upward. In minutes, the day faded to twilight, the strange dusk of disaster settling over San Lucía.

Miguel found his mother Maria, standing in their small garden, her hands muddy and trembling as she pulled onions from the soft earth. She looked at him, face streaked with tears and soil.

“We must leave,” she said, quietly, as if speaking louder might solidify her terror.

He nodded, numb confidence lifting him above fear. “Get what you can.”

An eerie procession began, with villagers filling burlap sacks—food, photographs, saints carved from cheap wood. The youngest children clung to their parents, older ones clutching battered books or pets. Miguel steered Maria by the elbow toward the road leading out, past the church, toward the last bridge over the Rio Calma.

As they left, ash began to fall—soft at first, like snow, then heavier, grey rain that coated hair and skin. It filled the furrows of the fields, smothered the thatch on roofs, turned the world to monochrome.

They joined the flow of refugees, eyes stinging, lungs burning, moving away from their homes but not their memories. Behind them, Volcán de la Niebla thundered and spat. A red-hot fissure opened near the summit, vomiting a river of fire that tumbled down, devouring everything in its path.

In the confusion, Don Ernesto, the oldest man in the village, paused after reaching the safety of a neighboring hill, staring back at the plume billowing into the sky.

“Did I not tell you?” he muttered to anyone who would listen, voice carrying above the wind. “The old tales warn us—when she calls, you must listen.”

Miguel watched the mountain in silence. He remembered the stories his grandfather told—of a time when ancestors thought the volcano a sleeping goddess, quick to anger if disturbed by the proud or the careless.

He wondered if they had offended her, these past years, with their small ambitions—new roads cut through forest, plastic bottles tossed carelessly, laughter that was too loud or prayers that were too few.

Darkness thickened. The bridge ahead trembled, and someone began to cry. The line of villagers shuffled forward, out of the ashfall and into siren-washed night, as officials in dust masks guided the frightened procession.

They were taken in by an evacuee center in the town of San Jerónimo—a gymnasium whose high windows leaked pale light onto rows of cots. The air crackled with radio broadcasts, lists of missing people, warnings about inhaled ash. Out there—across the valley—the inferno raged, and the mountain’s fury became a myth re-written in real time.

Miguel claimed a patch near a far wall for himself and Maria, tucking his mother’s blanket tighter around her shoulders. People whispered their fears in three languages, sleepless and raw. Reports came: the lava had split the old road; the school was gone; someone had seen the church’s cross listing against a black sky.

In the days that followed, the world shrank to the rhythm of government updates and the cold taste of fear. Telephone lines were down, but news traveled anyhow—a cousin’s house had vanished beneath ash, a neighbor’s car was spotted half-melted at the crossroads. And always, the slow, drifting flakes, filtering into lungs and settling in hair, barely visible but utterly pervasive.

Still, there were moments of kindness. Volunteers handed out food, doctors soothed the panic of children, engineers arrived with masks and bottled water. Once, a truck playing salsa music trundled up to the gym and someone danced, crazy and wild, and briefly too alive for disaster.

Each night, Miguel lay awake beside his mother, staring at the spiderweb cracks in the basketball backboard, afraid of closing his eyes. His heart pounded with a hundred unanswered questions: Would the mountain stop? Would they return? How many memories could he carry without a home?

Weeks passed before the eruption slowed. The endless coughing clouds gave way to ragged sun, and preliminary teams ventured back along the old slopes. Miguel returned with a handful of volunteers, winding a slow and agonizing path through a landscape he barely recognized.

San Lucía was gone, buried beneath drifts of grey. Only the tallest trees marked the spot where the plaza must have been; the bell tower had fallen, half-melted, like a knucklebone discarded by some careless spirit. He found his house by the shape of the rubble, now a shallow mound in ashy twilight. He poked at the debris, hoping to salvage Maria’s locket or perhaps a fragment of her wedding shawl. No luck—only grit and the memory of what was.

Some of the villagers wept; others stood motionless, stunned into silence by the scope of loss. Yet as the team pressed forward, Miguel noticed shoots of green sprouting among the devastation—ferns curling up through the dust, seeds cracked open by anomalous heat.

Padre Gabriel, shoulders stooped but voice strong, led prayers in the ruins. Scientists arrived, testing the soil—ash would enrich it, they said, in time. The people would return, replant, build anew.

Miguel stooped and ran dirt through his hands. The wind shifted, sending tiny eddies spiraling upward. The past was gone, scoured clean by the fury of nature. Yet beneath the cinders, tomorrow waited.

He closed his fist, feeling the promise inside.

For now, all that was left were ashes in the wind, but the future, as always, would grow from the ruins.
