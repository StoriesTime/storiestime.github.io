When Words Hurt: A Short Story on Racism and Prejudice


The sun dipped low behind the aging brick buildings of Maplewood High, casting long shadows across the schoolyard. Jason sat quietly on the weathered bench at the edge of the playground, his arms wrapped around his knees, his eyes fixed on the cracked pavement beneath him. Around him, the buzz of laughter and chatter was thick in the air; groups of classmates gathered in their usual spots, sharing jokes and stories. But Jason felt invisible, as if a glass wall separated him from the rest of the world.

He was the only Black student in his grade, and just this morning, the weight of that reality had pressed on him harder than usual. A group of boys in the hallway had called him names—words full of venom and ignorance. They didn’t even try to hide their contempt.

“You don’t belong here, Jason,” one had sneered, the words ringing in his ears. Another shouted, “Go back to where you came from!”

Each insult was a blow, invisible but deeply painful. Jason had tilted his head down, swallowing the lump in his throat as the taunts echoed off the lockers. His teacher’s voice faded in and out, but the cruelty in those words stayed with him, like a dark cloud following him through the day.

After school, Jason’s mother noticed his silence. At the dinner table, she nudged gently, “How was your day at school, honey?”

Jason swallowed hard. “It was okay,” he replied, forcing a faint smile. But as soon as dinner ended, he slipped away to his room, the façade crumbling as tears streamed silently down his face.

Meanwhile, across town, Lucy sat at her desk, staring at the essay prompt on her laptop screen: Write about a time you experienced or witnessed prejudice. It was an assignment from Mr. Stevens, her English teacher, designed to open students’ minds, or so he had promised.

Lucy’s mind drifted to her new neighbor—Jason. She’d seen him walking home alone most days, head down, shoulders hunched. Something about his quiet strength intrigued her. They had only exchanged awkward greetings once or twice, but she sensed there was a story beneath his silence, one that needed to be told.

Determined, Lucy started typing, weaving together what she had heard from classmates, what she had observed, and what she had learned from her own conversations with Jason. She wrote about the words that hurt, the invisible scars left by prejudice, and the resilience it took to face each day under that weight.

But Lucy worried—would her classmates understand? Would her words make a difference, or would they be met with the same judgment and silence that Jason faced?

The next morning, the classroom was buzzing with nervous energy. Mr. Stevens had announced that the class would be sharing their essays. One by one, students stood at the front, reading their personal stories about prejudice, some subtle, some stark.

When it was Lucy’s turn, she took a deep breath and began. Her voice was steady but gentle as she recounted Jason’s experience, highlighting not just the pain but his courage. She spoke of how the harsh words didn’t define him, but the way he continued to stand tall despite them did.

As she finished, a hush fell over the room. Some faces reflected surprise, others discomfort, but slowly, hands raised. Students asked questions, shared their own experiences, and expressed a desire to change the toxic atmosphere that had allowed such hurtful words to thrive.

Mr. Stevens nodded approvingly. “This is the start of something important,” he said.

After class, Jason approached Lucy, a hesitant smile breaking through the usual reserve. “Thank you,” he said quietly. “For speaking up.”

Lucy shrugged, a warm smile spreading across her face. “You’re not alone, Jason. Not anymore.”

From that day on, the words that once cut deep began to lose their power. Conversations opened, friendships formed, and the walls built by prejudice slowly started crumbling within the halls of Maplewood High. The road ahead was long, but with every shared story, the community grew stronger, more understanding.

And Jason? He still wore his scars, but now, they told a story of survival and hope.

In the end, the story of Maplewood High was a reminder that words can wound, but they can also heal—if only we choose to speak with kindness and listen with open hearts.
