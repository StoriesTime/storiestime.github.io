Ethics in Action: Short Stories Challenging Moral Philosophies


Ella sat at her desk, the warm light from her desk lamp casting a soft glow on the pages of her notebook. She had always been a rational thinker, someone who believed in the power of logic and reason to guide decisions. As a philosophy professor, she had spent countless hours lecturing her students about ethical theories: utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics, and relativism. But recently, her beliefs had been shaken to their core.

It had all started a few weeks ago with a phone call she had received late one evening. Her younger brother, Adam, had been in a car accident. Though he had survived, he was in critical condition, and the doctors had explained that his chances of survival would require an expensive, life-saving procedure. The catch? The procedure had a very high risk of failure—so high that the hospital’s ethics board had been involved to weigh the moral implications.

As she sat in the quiet of her apartment, Ella could feel the weight of the situation pressing down on her. The ethics board had suggested two possible paths: one was to proceed with the procedure, giving Adam a slim chance of survival, but potentially costing him his life if it failed. The other option was to refuse the procedure, which would guarantee Adam’s death, but the ethical reasoning behind this choice was based on the idea of minimizing harm by not subjecting him to unnecessary suffering.

Ella knew her brother. He had always been the daring one, the one who never shied away from a challenge. Would he want to live, even if it meant enduring tremendous suffering? Would he rather pass away peacefully, avoiding the possibility of a failed surgery that might leave him in a worse state? The question gnawed at her, and she felt like the philosophical concepts she had once taught so confidently were no longer enough to guide her through the murky waters of real-life ethics.

The decision weighed heavily on her, not only because of her deep love for her brother but also because of her commitment to teaching her students about the practical implications of moral philosophies. Could abstract ideas truly be applied to real-life situations?

Part II: The Philosophical Divide

The next day, Ella found herself facing her class. As she walked into the lecture hall, her mind was still preoccupied with the decision she had yet to make. She glanced at the row of eager students, their faces attentive and ready to dive into another discussion on ethical dilemmas. The topic for today’s class was supposed to be the trolley problem—an ethical thought experiment often discussed in terms of utilitarianism and deontology.

“Good morning, everyone,” Ella began, her voice steady despite the inner turmoil she was feeling. “Today we’ll be discussing the trolley problem, one of the most famous moral dilemmas in modern philosophy. I want you to think critically about the choices that are presented, and consider how different ethical frameworks might approach the situation.”

She outlined the basic premise of the trolley problem: A runaway trolley is headed toward five people tied to the tracks. You are standing next to a lever that, if pulled, will divert the trolley onto another track where one person is tied. The dilemma is whether to pull the lever, sacrificing one person to save five, or do nothing and allow the trolley to continue on its path.

Ella asked her students to discuss the dilemma in small groups, prompting them to consider different ethical philosophies. One group argued for utilitarianism, the belief that the right action is the one that maximizes overall happiness. By pulling the lever and sacrificing one life, they reasoned, five lives would be saved, creating a net positive outcome.

Another group argued from a deontological perspective, emphasizing the importance of moral rules and duties. According to this view, it would be wrong to actively intervene and cause harm, even if it meant saving more lives. The students in this group contended that sacrificing one life for the greater good violated the fundamental principle that one should never treat others merely as a means to an end.

Ella listened to the debates, her mind wandering back to her own ethical crisis. If the trolley problem was a moral puzzle that philosophers could dissect from every angle, then why did the situation with Adam feel so different? Wasn’t this dilemma also about weighing lives? Wasn’t it about minimizing suffering?

But something deep inside her told her that the philosophical answers were not enough. Ethics in the real world wasn’t just about applying theories—it was about navigating the complex, nuanced emotions and consequences that shaped our choices.

Part III: A Moment of Clarity

Later that evening, Ella sat with her parents in the hospital waiting room. Adam was undergoing the final rounds of tests before the decision was made about the surgery. Her mother, holding a small cross in her hands, whispered a prayer, while her father stared at the ground, his face creased with worry.

“Ella,” her father said, breaking the silence. “We know that you’re the one who teaches all these theories about right and wrong, but we need you to help us decide what’s best for Adam.”

Ella looked at him, her mind still spinning with ethical theories, but now clouded by something deeper—emotion, love, and fear. She had always prided herself on her ability to think through moral issues logically, but now, in the face of her brother’s life-or-death situation, those philosophical ideals seemed distant and disconnected.

She thought back to the trolley problem and how it had once seemed so clear to her. The utilitarian argument had always made sense: maximize happiness, minimize suffering. But was that truly the right approach here? Was it ethical to place such a heavy decision in the hands of one person, especially when the consequences were so personal?

“Ella,” her mother said, her voice breaking through her thoughts. “We’re asking you, as the philosopher, to tell us what’s right. What would you do?”

Ella stood up and walked toward the window, gazing out at the hospital parking lot. She could hear the distant hum of passing cars, but it felt like the world had slowed down, as if time had paused, waiting for her to speak.

In that moment, Ella realized that ethics, while important, was not always about choosing between right and wrong. It was about understanding the human experience—the uncertainty, the pain, and the love that shaped our decisions. It was about recognizing that no theory could provide a simple answer to complex, real-world dilemmas.

“I don’t have the answer,” Ella said quietly. “I wish I did. But sometimes, ethics isn’t about the perfect solution. It’s about doing the best we can with the information we have, while also considering the consequences for everyone involved. It’s about empathy, understanding that our choices affect others, even if we don’t know how.”

Her father nodded slowly, as though coming to terms with her words. “So, you’re saying that maybe there’s no perfect answer?”

Ella turned back to face them. “Exactly. Maybe we just have to trust that we’re making the right decision for Adam, based on what he would want, and what we believe is the most compassionate choice.”

Part IV: A Difficult Decision

The following day, the decision was made. Adam’s surgery was scheduled. The doctors explained the risks again, but this time, it wasn’t just about the statistics. It was about their family’s love for him, their hope that he would pull through, and their willingness to take a chance.

Ella held Adam’s hand as he was wheeled into the operating room. She couldn’t predict the outcome, but she had come to accept that sometimes, the right decision isn’t clear. The only thing that mattered now was that they had made the choice out of love, compassion, and the belief that Adam deserved a chance, even if the odds were stacked against him.

In the end, ethics wasn’t just about applying rules or theories—it was about being human. It was about the messy, complicated choices we make when the stakes are highest. And perhaps, that was the greatest lesson of all.

Epilogue: Reflections

Months later, after Adam had recovered, Ella found herself back in the classroom, discussing another ethical dilemma. She glanced at her students, now more thoughtful and engaged than ever. They had learned about theories and frameworks, but they had also learned that ethics wasn’t just an intellectual exercise—it was a guide for navigating the complexities of life.

As she spoke about moral philosophy, Ella could not help but reflect on the personal journey she had gone through. In the end, she realized that there would never be a perfect solution to every moral problem. But the process of grappling with those problems, the effort to understand and empathize, was what truly mattered.

And that, she believed, was what ethics in action was all about.
