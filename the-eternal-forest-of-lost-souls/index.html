The Eternal Forest of Lost Souls


No moonlight ever touched the moss-veiled branches at the heart of the Ildoren Wilds, where the world’s maps faded into a rootwork of old whispers and broken promises. Here, the ground ran soft underfoot with centuries of fallen leaves that never decayed, as if the forest itself refused to surrender anything to time. Men and women stood at the edge of these woods for ages, dreaming of what secrets lay inside, spinning stories of glory, madness, and mystery. Yet those who crossed the threshold seldom returned, and even the lucky few who emerged again were never quite whole.

A Desperate Entrance

Lisette Varran pressed a hand to her aching ribs, the faint echo of her pursuers’ barbed shouts still clinging to her memory. She slipped beneath a flattened arch of silver ash limbs, every instinct screaming to run in the other direction. But behind her, the torches flickered and the hounds bayed. Inside, the shadows beckoned—a strange reprieve, darker than night, older than any safety she’d ever known. She stepped inside, and the world clenched shut behind her like the snapping jaw of something alive.

The air changed immediately. It thickened, tasting of old tea and petrichor, humming with the pressure of a thousand untold stories. Tall, ancient trees rose like cathedral pillars, their boles braided with ghostly, silent wisteria. Somewhere, water burbled unseen, and luminous spores danced in the stagnant air.

Lisette panted, waiting for pursuit, but none came. At her back, only a wall of fog and shadow remained, impenetrable and cold.

She wandered forward, pushing aside ferns studded with glistening blue veins. The farther she moved, the fainter the memories of her flight became. Faces—her mother’s, her brother’s, her betrayer’s—slipped away into the hush of green silence.

Shadows with Names

Eternal night ruled here, but Lisette soon realized she was not alone. Figures drifted between the trees, pale and swift. Their eyes flashed like silver coins, unreadable and far too sad for comfort.

One form, more solid than the rest, materialized before her, blocking the path. He wore tattered black armor, tufted with lichen. His sword hung at his hip, rusted deep with long absence. Instead of flesh, his exposed hands shimmered with light, like sunlight glimpsed in a basin of wine.

“Lost?” he asked. His voice contained the hush of twilight; it was the first sound more than a whisper since Lisette had entered.

She nodded, voice raw. “I didn’t mean— They were chasing—”

He inclined his head as if the pattern were familiar. “Most who come here run from something. Others, from nothing at all.”

Lisette peered beyond him—a half-dozen other shapes clustered there, all translucent. Some wept openly; others simply faded at her glance, scattering like fog. “Are you… dead?”

He smiled, teeth as dark as jet. “If only it were simple as that. Here we are both and neither—forgotten, remembered, unmade and remade, in cycles without end.”

A New Kind of Hunger

As Lisette pressed deeper, the relentless hush began to gnaw at her mind. There were paths here, certainly: bootprints pressed deep into the mulch, twisted ladders of vine, half-forgotten symbols burned into bark. Yet, following any route seemed to draw her back to where she started, only with a bitter taste at the back of her throat and a cold need in her bones.

Once in a while, she heard voices. Not quite echoes, they slithered in her ear, murmuring of roads abandoned, names never spoken, choices that had cracked the world.

“You can go back,” hissed one, just beyond the reach of her torch, coaxing with the sweet poison of hope. “Every path can turn again. You only need to give up what remains.”

Another voice, lighter, carried on the stagnant breeze. “Or you can stay and forget. The pain fades here. Only the hunger stays.”

Lisette’s hunger sharpened. Food she carried from the outside—dried beef, hard cheese—turned to chalk in her mouth. Water boiled away on her tongue, no matter how long she drank. Sleep only brought her dreams of gnarled roots and unending, endless green.

Companions in the Gloom

On her third night, a child—a boy, perhaps ten—appeared at her fire. His eyes were infinite, reflecting a cosmos she could hardly bear.

“Have you seen my brother?” he asked.

Lisette thought, tried to answer, but her own tongue tripped over the memory. “No. What’s his name?”

The boy frowned. “I don’t remember. Only that he was kind. Can you help me look?”

Night after night, she walked the twisted trails with the lost child, calling for his brother, following a melody neither of them quite remembered. The forest seemed to mold itself to their footsteps, sending small, spectral animals—a deer with a smoky pelt, a fox whose laughter sounded like rain on dry leaves—darting alongside them. These were more curious than dangerous, always gone when looked at directly, but Lisette felt their presence shift something inside her.

Together, the pair began collecting tiny tokens in their search: a battered coin with a hole through its center, a scrap of silk caught on a branch, an iron ring so old the inscription was worn clean away. Each object promised an answer, but each melted to mist at the next dawn.

The Heart of the Forest

Eventually, the trees thinned, revealing a wide, mirror-still pond. At its center rose a great willow, gravid with sorrow, limbs dipped in silken water. On the far bank, figures in pale cloaks stood unmoving, backs turned.

The boy knelt, washing his hands in the pool. “This is where the brothers come to remember,” he said softly. “Some do not leave.”

Lisette knelt beside him, feeling her reflection flicker—a hundred faces shifting through her eyes. She saw herself as a child, dancing beside her brother in the dust of a vanished summer. She saw herself older, fleeing from war, her hands red, her heart emptied of hope. She saw all the Lisettes she had been, each one turning to greet her with silent accusation.

She looked at the boy, shivering. “Why do you stay here? Why don’t you move on?”

He shrugged, swallowing. “I’m afraid if I forget, I’ll lose everything I never had. All I ever wanted was to be remembered.”

The lure of oblivion pressed at her mind. Here was a place where loss was soft, pain was gentle, grief wrapped you in green shadows. The urge to disappear—to surrender memory, hunger, hurt—was almost overwhelming.

A Borne Decision

Yet—defiance sparked in her chest. She remembered the shouting, the running, her mother’s hands pressing a coin into her palm, whispering, “Never forget who you are, Lisette.”

She pressed her last hoarded memory into the boy’s hand—a faded, velvet ribbon, once bright crimson. “Take this. It’s all I have left. If you find your brother, give it to him.”

He smiled, warmth blossoming, though tears coursed down his cheeks. “Will you stay with me? Or will you leave?”

Lisette felt the trees tense, the ground shiver. She thought of the world outside—broken and cruel, but brightly alive. Of her family, of debts unpaid and dreams unspoken. She stood, bolstered by a sudden certainty.

“I’m going,” she said. “I will not forget.”

The pond rippled, the willow’s branches parted, unveiling a narrow path of light. Lisette embraced the boy, feeling him drift away like fog in the sun. The forest’s voices wailed once, then faded to silence.

She walked, heart racing, following the light until roots gave way to hard earth and the stifling silence was replaced by the faraway thunder of her home world.

Emergence

Lisette stumbled from the forest at dawn, blinking in the clean chill of morning. The woods behind her rustled with secrets, but the door no longer beckoned. Sunlight played across her hands, the taste of memory sweet on her tongue. She was herself again, newly made.

She carried with her the weight of all she had seen—the faces of the lost, the quiet ache of those too tired to keep remembering. She knew the world would never understand what she had left behind, or what she might have become, had she stayed.

But she walked on, one step at a time, leaving only footprints for the wild things to find.

In all the long years after, when Lisette wandered near the edge of forgotten forests or sat awake before the fire, she told no stories of what she had seen. Some secrets, like the Eternal Forest, belonged only to those who had walked among its shadows and emerged into the light, the memory of loss shimmering just beneath their skin.
