The Haunted Mirror: Reflections from Beyond


The cedar chest had always sat, unnoticed, beneath the attic window. Its metal fittings, tarnished with time, bit coldly into Jamie’s palms as she lifted the lid and exhaled a little gust of ancestral dust. Sunlight, filtered through moth-eaten drapes, colored the stagnant air, painting delicate patterns over forgotten family memorabilia. Jamie’s curiosity blazed brighter than her apprehension. Every squeak and groan of the ancient floorboards carved a thrill into her bones, a reminder that this was her late grandmother’s house now—and, by inheritance, hers.

The chest’s contents were a collection of the peculiar: a locket without a picture, velvet gloves too decayed for use, brittle letters composed in looping script, and, at the bottom, a mirror half-shrouded in threadbare cloth. Jamie paused, fingers tingling. The mirror’s gilt frame looked incongruously opulent, its flourishes an elaborate contrast to the rest of the attic’s clutter.

She tugged away the cloth and stared at her reflection. It seemed ordinary—the same ash-blonde hair, glasses smudged by attic grime, mouth twisting in a nervous smile. Yet, for a heartbeat, her eyes seemed too dark—almost sunken, as though shadowed by something behind the glass rather than her own fatigue. Then it was just her again, with nothing but dust motes dancing in the slanted beams of sunlight.

Curiosity overwhelmed her rationality. Despite the unease blooming in her chest, Jamie carted the mirror downstairs, setting it up against the faded wallpaper of the guest room. She found herself drawn to it, compelled to watch her own reflection in a way that bordered on obsession. She half-laughed at herself, chalking it up to the monotony of unpacking.

But that night, a chill crept through the house. It pressed against Jamie’s skin with the teasing fingers of a forgotten draft. Though the rest of the space remained still, the guest room felt alive with anticipation, as if something waited just beyond sight—trapped within the reflection of that ancient mirror. She told herself it was only her imagination.

Sleep eluded her. Sighing, Jamie left her bed and drifted toward the glowing rectangular door of the guest room. As she stepped inside, moonlight shimmering across the glass surface, the air thickened. Her eyes met her reflected self. Her movements were mirrored equivalently, the expression—hers, surely—familiar… until it wasn’t. The reflection smiled wider, a split second later than Jamie, eyes flickering with a smoldering, hidden knowledge.

Adrenaline surged through her veins. She blinked hard, shook her head, stepped back. The reflection followed, mirroring every gesture precisely—but the sense of lag lingered. She fled the room, heart stampeding. The mirror was just glass, wood, and old lacquer. It couldn’t mimic her—a mere second off—unless it wasn’t only a mirror.

Jamie spent the next day bustling about the house, forcefully ignoring the guest room. She wandered through the kitchen, fixed tea she didn’t drink, contemplated shelves of gothic novels, and scrolled endless feeds on her phone. Yet the mirror gnawed at her, refusing to be forgotten. Every mirror is a window, she mused, not just to ourselves but perhaps to somewhere else.

Her grandmother, wary of superstition, always said, “Don’t stare too long—mirrors are curious things. They remember what we forget.” Jamie had thought it a charming eccentricity, one of those cryptic old-lady warnings no one took seriously. Until now.

By evening, as rain skittered across the windows, Jamie found herself again before the glass. Determined to conquer her fear, she looked her reflection full in the face. The reflected Jamie raised an eyebrow before she did, jaw tightening in a silent rebuke. A jagged cold swept the room, and Jamie gasped as the glass grew silver-bright, blinding, then faded into clarity.

But now she stood face-to-face with a reflection not quite hers. The facial features were hers, but subtly wrong, as though drawn by an artist who had met Jamie only once—a slightly sharper nose, eyes glittering with intent rather than surprise. The Not-Jamie lifted an arm; Jamie did the same, experimentally; their gestures synced in practiced dance. But as Jamie dropped her hand, the reflection’s lingered, palm pressed to the inside of the glass.

Jamie’s pulse hammered. The Not-Jamie’s lips parted, and a soft, hollow voice emerged—not from the mirror, but from Jamie’s own skull.

“Hello,” it breathed. “Can you hear me?”

The air thickened, scents of lavender and dust pressing. Jamie backed away. The reflection frowned, mimicking her retreat so believably that it sent a tremor through her limbs.

“You are not real,” Jamie whispered.

“Neither are you, unless you look close enough,” the reflection replied—voice all velvet menace.

Jamie fled the room, hands clamped over her ears. Each boom of thunder seemed to echo the Not-Jamie’s hollow whisper.

Days elapsed in a distorted fog. The house’s weathered silence warped reality; doors that should have stayed closed drifted open, and mirrors fogged for no reason. Cold spots pooled in corners, and when Jamie walked by reflective surfaces, she glimpsed her double watching, patiently, from angles that didn’t match her own movements. She scoured the internet for answers: haunted mirrors, portals, spirit attachments. Folk legends blossomed: looking glasses are thresholds, unguarded gateways to dimensions still shrouded in mystery.

Compelled, Jamie dusted off the letters she’d found in the attic. In one, her grandmother confessed:
If you find the old mirror, darling, don’t be afraid. It’s older than you or me, and sometimes it hungers. But it can also offer answers, if you ask politely…

That night, Jamie returned to the guest room, lantern gripped white-knuckled. Her reflection already awaited—a version of herself, waiting, hopeful, eyes pleading for attention. Resolve braced her spine, and Jamie stood tall.

“What do you want?” she demanded.

The reflection didn’t answer directly. Instead, it gestured to something behind Jamie—a shadow unfurling across the peeling wallpaper. She spun around, found nothing but the empty room. Yet when she turned back, the room in the mirror was different. The wallpaper had repaired itself, the curtains hung fresh, and beyond the window, daylight streamed in, effervescent. Jamie, in the mirror, looked happy—content, even—while her real heart pinned tight and trembling inside her chest.

“Is that my life?” she asked, voice straining.

The reflection’s lips curled.

“It could be, if you’re willing to listen. We are not so different, you and I. This place—this house—has histories you do not understand.”

Jamie studied the glass. In the frame, faint fleurs-de-lis marked the border—French, perhaps, rivaling the gothic ornamentation. She wondered how many faces, how many generations, had studied themselves and wondered what watched them back.

A sudden cold flicker moved in the mirror’s background: a shadowy figure, just out of focus, watching Jamie’s double. She leaned closer. The figure’s hand rose, palm pressed to the opposite side of the mirror, mirroring the Not-Jamie, who nodded solemnly.

“Who are you?” Jamie asked, addressing the shadow.

The room’s air vibrated softly, like a plucked cello string. A voice—ancient, brittle—surfaced.

“I am who you left behind. I am memory, regret, the things you do not wish to see.”

The figure’s eyes were Jamie’s grandmother’s—a universe of disappointment and gentle reproach. Jamie staggered back, breath icy, yet unable to look away.

The next days turned restless, liminal. Each night—almost against her will—Jamie returned to the haunted mirror. Each time, more of her ancestor’s world seeped through: glimpses of her grandmother tending flowers never seen, laughter from children in clothing a century passé, a lonely man in Civil War regalia polishing the edges of the frame. Each vision faded with the coming dawn.

Compelled by these haunted memories, Jamie rifled through the dusty chest for more letters. She found a journal, small and green with gold-leaf initials—M.L.S.—her great-great-grandmother, Marianne Lucille Spencer. The entries rambled with slow descent into dread.

March 14, 1885:
Saw him again—the man in the glass. He speaks kindly, asks after my health. Mother says the mirror is a gift from her grandmother. I find it repulsing, yet I am drawn to it…*

June 2, 1885:
I fear the mirror’s games. My sleep is punctured by voices. Sometimes, I feel as if my reflection moves when I remain still.*

It wasn’t just Jamie. The mirror had always been more than an ornament—all generations before her had brushed the edge of its mystery. She roasted marshmallows over the crackling fireplace, reading each entry by candlelight, comforted and exposed in equal measure.

On a rain-choked evening, she risked a question aloud in the guest room:

“If you are memory and regret, if you are beyond, what do you want from me?”

Thunder blacked out the window. The Not-Jamie pressed both hands to the glass, face mournful, eyes brimming.

“Release,” she intoned.

Jamie gathered her courage, reached out, pressing her own palms to the ancient surface. A chill surged ferociously; breath puffed in the cold, but she did not let go. The house itself seemed to groan, floor to rooftop, as though its very bones resented the confrontation. Shadows bunched along the ceiling; the lantern’s glow flickered.

The Not-Jamie, tears welling, choked out:

“Speak the words.”

Jamie’s mind unfurled with fragments from the letters:
“To part what was joined, to see and be seen, to free what’s remembered from between…”

Her voice, hoarse but steadily gaining strength, finished:

“Return, reveal, release the scene.”

Moonlight stabbed through ruptured clouds, landing with icy precision on the glass. The mirror shimmered, its surface swirling, coalescing—past and present converging. Faces flickered—her grandmother, Marianne Lucille, the silent Civil War soldier, even an infant gurgling in century-old clothing. Each image pleaded for acknowledgment, for peace.

Jamie cried openly; sorrow rippled between both sides of the glass.

“Let me go,” the Not-Jamie whispered.

The mirror pulsed, a heartbeat between worlds. Jamie stepped back, hands tearing away. She watched as the reflection faded, replaced by a silvery mist that slowly ebbed, revealing only Jamie’s real face—tearstained, exhausted, profoundly free.

She never saw the Not-Jamie again. The next day, the mirror’s glass returned to its mundane reflection. Jamie’s image was her own—unmarred by temporal echoes. The room’s air brightened; the house’s oppressive atmosphere lifted.

Over time, Jamie discovered that the curse—if one could call it that—had stemmed from generations of unresolved regrets. Each ancestor had harbored fears, sorrows, forbidden love; each had projected their secrets, their unspoken truths, into the glass. The mirror had become a vessel for those unshed tears, those unsaid confessions.

Having confronted the haunted mirror, Jamie found herself altered. Echoes of her ancestors’ mistakes cautioned her not to repeat their silences. She cultivated an openness with herself and the world, refusing to let fear stifle truth.

Years later, as Jamie stood by the old attic window, she eyed the cedar chest with a smile rather than dread. The mirror, wrapped carefully, rested in the guest room—not as a relic of terror, but a gentle reminder of history, of the thin membrane between worlds, of the necessity of facing the shadows within.

The mirror never haunted her again. Its surface, once a prison, was now just glass: a testament to endurance, to release, to the courage it takes to look—truly look—without flinching.

And so, the house sighed and settled, content, its ghosts at rest at last. Jamie flourished in its shelter, the past a companion rather than a captor. The haunted mirror, having offered its final reflection, remained silent—an artifact charged with story, not sorrow, from beyond.
