Starlight Dreams: The Quest for New Worlds


Dune watched the sky dissolve into a prism of colors. Every night, the stars danced above her as if whispering secrets, enticing her to dream beyond the boundaries of her world. The village of Lomera was quiet, nestled in the shadow of Mount Veyra, and the concept of anything beyond its borders was a faint haze of impossibility. But Dune had always felt an ache deep within her chest, as though her heart beat not for this land but for the territories of the unknown.

Her fascination with the stars began in childhood when she found an ancient scroll tucked beneath an abandoned stone altar near the elder’s lodge. It spoke of distant worlds with skies that shimmered like crystal waters and landscapes dotted with towers of light. Legend had it that such worlds were only accessible through the Veil—the fragile boundary between their realm and the vast universe beyond—but no one alive had ever seen it.

Dune became obsessed with the idea of discovering new worlds. Her mother warned her that such dreams were dangerous, that no one who chased the Veil ever returned. But warnings were fragile chains, and Dune’s vision grew brighter with every passing year.

For fifteen years, the scroll sat on the corner of her desk, illuminated only by candlelight. She had studied its every line, traced its worn edges, and unraveled its cryptic riddles. The Veil, according to the text, resided deep within Mount Veyra—accessible only during the Zenith, a celestial event where three planets aligned perfectly with the central star. The next Zenith was approaching in mere weeks.

“You’re consumed by nonsense,” Bryn, her best friend, argued passionately one evening as they shared a meal of roasted root vegetables and stewed herbs. “People have searched the mountain for decades and found nothing but jagged stone and perilous falls.”

Dune wiped her mouth and set her spoon aside, her eyes sharp with determination. “That’s because they search without knowing what they’re looking for,” she shot back. “The scroll isn’t just a tale—it’s a map, Bryn. With the Zenith coming, I have to try. I’ll never forgive myself if I don’t.”

“More likely, you’ll never make it back,” Bryn muttered grimly. But he didn’t stop her. He knew better than anyone that once Dune fixed her mind on something, there stood little chance of persuading her otherwise.

The morning of her departure was surreal. Lomera’s mountains were bathed in hues of gold by the dawn, and the icy air carried the scent of pine and promises. Bryn stood at the edge of the village, his arms crossed.

“I’ll give you three days,” he said. “After that, I’m coming to drag you back."

“Three days is all I’ll need,” Dune replied, hefting the leather satchel over her shoulder. It contained basic supplies: food rations, rope, a carved whistle, and a small crystal orb said to contain protective energy. She didn’t really believe in the orb’s mysterious properties, but better to err on the side of caution.

The ascent was grueling. Mount Veyra, beautiful yet ruthless, was a labyrinth of cliffs, narrow ledges, and slivers of frozen streams. Dune followed the map etched into the scroll, noting its symbolic markers—a tree scorched black by lightning, a cluster of rocks shaped like a stag. By the second day, her muscles ached, and the cold bit at her skin despite her heavy cloak.

It was near dusk when she stumbled upon the archway.

At the summit, the arch stood silent and weathered, hidden behind a natural barricade of boulders. It was far simpler than she had imagined—a formation made of two intersecting slabs of pale stone and a third slab perched above as the header. Carvings of moons and suns adorned its surface, while the faint shimmer of energy clung to its edges like fractured sunlight.

Dune stepped closer, her breath visible in the icy air. “The Veil,” she murmured.

She pulled the scroll from her satchel and read the final instructions. The Zenith was at its apex; the alignment of the three planets glowed faintly in the horizon’s heavens, casting a silver thread of light toward the archway.

The ancient text instructed her to activate the Veil using the power of intent. “It must recognize you,” the scroll said. “Your desire to explore must outweigh your fear of what lies beyond.”

Dune placed her hand on the stone and closed her eyes. Her thoughts surged through her, memories of her endless fascination with the stars and the potent yearning to uncover secrets hidden within them. “I seek new worlds,” she whispered. “I will not falter.”

For a moment, nothing happened, and unease gripped her chest. Then, a vibration pulsed through the ground. The archway slowly shimmered, glowing until the space within its frame became a translucent curtain of light.

Heart pounding, Dune gathered her belongings and stepped forward.

The transition between worlds wasn’t a burst of color or a rush of wind—it was quiet, subtle, like sinking into an ocean of silk. When she emerged on the other side, the sight before her stole her breath.

She stood atop a field of golden grass that shifted and sparkled like starlight. Towering cliffs adorned with crystalline structures loomed in the distance, reflecting the light of twin suns hanging low in an emerald sky. Waterfalls formed sheets of liquid diamonds, and a flock of creatures that resembled glowing phoenixes soared gracefully above the horizon.

This world was everything—and more.

Dune spent hours exploring, marveling at the alien flora and fauna, mapping the terrain, and sampling the local fruits that tasted of honey and sun-warmed peaches. She knew deep down this was the discovery she had been waiting for her entire life.

But her joy was cut short.

The sky darkened unexpectedly. From the shadows emerged towering beings, their forms fluid and emanating soft light, almost translucent but menacing. Dune instinctively reached for the orb. Energy buzzed through her veins as she held it tightly.

The beings did not speak; their movements radiated intent and mistrust. This was no welcome party, but neither was it an immediate attack. Their forms swirled, as though questioning why she was here.

Then, one stepped forward. It extended what might have been an arm, motioning to the arch Dune had passed through, now faintly blinking in the distance. She suddenly understood—they wanted her to leave.

Reluctance burned within her, but Dune knew this wasn’t her realm to conquer or own. It was a gift—a lesson that life unfolded in infinite ways. She bowed her head in respect and turned back toward the passage.

As she stepped through the Veil, she felt its gentle pull once more and then the familiar bite of earth’s mountain air. Everything seemed unchanged around her, except for the star-filled ache within her soul—one neither diminished nor satisfied, but something altogether new.

When she returned to Lomera, Bryn was there waiting, arms crossed but smirk in tow. “Well, you live to tell the tale. That’s a start.”

She grinned. “Start is exactly the word for it.”

For Dune, this quest wasn’t the end of her starlight dreams—it was only their beginning.
