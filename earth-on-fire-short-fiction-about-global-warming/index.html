Earth on Fire: Short Fiction About Global Warming


Smoke hung heavy across the horizon, blurring the line where sky used to be. In the small town of Redhaven, nestled between the cracked earth and scorched forests, the air tasted of ash and forgotten summers. Mara tightened the straps on her dusty backpack, the heat prickling at her skin as she looked back at the ruins behind her—once lush fields, now charred and lifeless.

The wildfire had raged for weeks, an unstoppable beast fueled by the relentless heatwave that had gripped the continent in a fiery fist. It was the kind of summer people whispered about in disbelief, a summer where rivers ran dry, where the old oak that had shaded the town square since before anyone could remember had withered to a skeleton of blackened branches.

Mara adjusted the respirator mask on her face, the only barrier between her lungs and the choking smog. Every breath felt borrowed, and each step carried the weight of a world unraveling. She wasn’t just running from the fire; she was running from a future that lightning-fast changes denied them.

Her journey began at dawn, the sun already a blazing orb hanging low in the sky. She aimed for the old weather station on the outskirts—a relic from safer times when climate was something predictable, not a ticking bomb. The data there was crucial; if she could retrieve the archived climate models and share them with the resistance groups scattered across the country, there might still be a way to push back against the catastrophe.

Everywhere, the signs of global warming’s brutal march were evident. The ground bubbled where hot springs erupted unexpectedly; once frozen lakes had turned to mud; swarms of insects, displaced from their natural habitats, buzzed aggressively. The drought had shattered the seasons, replaced by endless waves of heat that turned people’s dreams to dust.

Mara wasn’t alone in her hope. She carried a memory of her younger brother, Noah, who had died in the first wave of floods that submerged half their town three years prior. That memory was fire too, but it burned a different kind of determination—one that refused to watch the world slip into oblivion without a fight.

The path was treacherous. Charred tree trunks hunched like sentinels of despair, and the shadows they cast flickered unnaturally against the blood-red sky. At one point, she stumbled upon a creek bed, once a gurgling thread feeding the land but now cracked like old paint, its veins dry and broken. Mara knelt to touch the cold earth, tasting the bitter dryness that seeped into her fingers.

Further along, she met a group of survivors huddled in the wreckage of what had been a community garden. Their faces were etched with exhaustion and worry, the kind carved out by years of drought and shrinking resources. They had stories — stories of shifting climates, of storms arriving months too early, and strange new diseases crawling out from the heat.

“We’ve got to head west,” an elder told her, voice broken but steady. “The coast may still have water, but it’s flooding faster than we can move. The ocean rises, and with it, our hopes sink.”

It was a cruel irony: too much water in one place, none in another. The polar ice sheets had shattered, melting into the great oceans and swallowing the lowlands, turning the world’s coastlines into perilous marshlands. Species disappeared daily; new ones mutated in the confused heat. It was a slow apocalypse, unfolding in whispers and roars alike.

Mara’s own town, once famed for peaceful rivers and fruit orchards, was now a ghost, swallowed by fire and despair. And still, she pushed onward, stepping over the skeletons of abandoned cars and snapped power lines, her path lit only by the faint green glow of emergency markers scattered along an old highway.

When she finally reached the weather station, the building was half-collapsed, its windows shattered by the fierce winds that had swept through the firestorm. Inside, the air was stale, thick with dust and forgotten ice crystals, relics of past winters. The terminals, powered by long-dead generators, flickered feebly in response to Mara’s battered solar charger.

She sifted through the digital archives, scrolling through years of climate data—temperature jumps, carbon spikes, failed policies. It was a catalog of human neglect, a harsh mirror held up to a species slow to wake.

A sudden tremor shook the ground, and debris tumbled from the ceiling. The earth was burning and shaking, a world on the brink of collapse, but Mara pressed on. She downloaded the crucial files onto her device, her fingers trembling as the room filled with the roar of distant thunder—not rain, but the crackling collapse of trees succumbing to the fire’s hunger.

Outside, the sky turned darker, curtains of smoke thickening into a suffocating shroud. But Mara had what she came for. Clutching the device, she stepped out into the inferno, determination blazing brighter than the wildfire engulfing the world.

Her steps carried the weight of a fragile hope—that by exposing the harsh truths about climate change, by rallying the people despite dwindling resources and rising seas, there might still be a chance to heal the planet before it turned completely to ash.

Because even when the Earth is on fire, human resolve can spark a wildfire of change.
