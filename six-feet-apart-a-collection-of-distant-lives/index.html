Six Feet Apart: A Collection of Distant Lives


It had been months since the city streets had first emptied. An eerie silence settled over the usual hum of cars, conversations, and bustling feet. At the height of the pandemic, the world had changed in ways that no one could have anticipated, and the people who had once been intertwined in shared spaces were now scattered, isolated, each in their own corner of existence. The distance, once a simple measurement, became a constant reminder of fragility.

In an apartment on the 23rd floor of an aging building, the windows were drawn, and the blinds fluttered slightly with the passing of a late spring breeze. A man sat alone at his kitchen table, the remnants of his breakfast—a half-finished cup of coffee and a plate with a solitary piece of toast—left forgotten. For the first time in weeks, he hadn’t thought to look at his phone. He wasn’t sure why, but today, the world outside seemed distant in a way that made him not want to engage. The noise, the notifications, the incessant chatter—everything felt like an overwhelming static.

His name was Elias, and though his apartment was small, it had become a universe of its own during the past few months. A universe defined by four walls, a window that overlooked a park now abandoned of its usual visitors, and an old leather chair where he spent hours reading books he had long meant to get to. The quiet had become both a curse and a sanctuary.

Elias wasn’t sure how to feel about the isolation. It had started off as a relief—a break from the usual chaos of life, the constant meetings, the busy work schedule, and the crowded spaces that once felt oppressive. But over time, it began to eat away at him. The isolation that had initially brought a sense of peace soon became a heavy weight that suffocated him. He missed the sound of voices, the laughter, the simple hum of life beyond his apartment.

Today was different. As Elias sat with his thoughts, the monotony was interrupted by the sound of a knock at the door. His heart skipped. He hadn’t had a visitor in weeks, and the thought of someone standing on the other side of the door filled him with both curiosity and apprehension.

He stood and walked slowly toward the door, his hand lingering on the doorknob for a moment longer than necessary. He was cautious now—always cautious—ever since the world had changed. Elias opened the door just a crack, peering out at the hallway.

A woman stood before him, wearing a mask, her eyes barely visible through the fabric, but there was something about her that struck a chord. She was holding a small, wrapped package in her hand, which she extended toward him.

“It’s for you,” she said softly, her voice muffled behind the mask.

Elias hesitated. He hadn’t been expecting anyone, but there was a part of him that recognized her, though he couldn’t quite place her.

“Who are you?” he asked, his voice cautious. He tried to read her expression, but her eyes were shadowed, unreadable.

“I’m Sarah,” she said, her eyes flicking briefly down at the package. “We used to work together at the bookstore, before… all of this.”

The mention of the bookstore hit him like a wave of nostalgia. Elias had worked at a small independent bookstore in the heart of the city, a place that had felt like home for so many years. Sarah had been one of his colleagues, a fellow introvert who always seemed to understand the comfort of being surrounded by books. They’d never been particularly close, but there was a familiarity there that comforted him.

“I… I didn’t recognize you,” he admitted, his hand reaching out cautiously to take the package. It was wrapped simply, in brown paper, with a small note tucked underneath the string.

“I understand,” Sarah said, a hint of sadness in her voice. “It’s been hard to keep track of anyone these days. We all got so used to being so close, and now, we’re so far apart.”

Elias nodded, his fingers brushing the string of the package. It was odd, this strange distance that had come to define the world. He used to walk past hundreds of people on his way to work, and now, he barely spoke to anyone at all. The six feet between them had become a symbol of something deeper—of the fear and uncertainty that gripped everyone, of the absence of connection that had once been so natural.

“Thank you,” he said, stepping back and closing the door after a brief pause. He unwrapped the package carefully, revealing a small book, the cover worn but familiar. It was one of his favorite novels, one he had lent out to someone years ago and never got back.

There was no note inside, just the title—The Distant Lands. The irony of the title wasn’t lost on him. He placed the book on his kitchen table and sat back down.

Across town, in another part of the city, Rachel sat in her living room, staring at the blank screen of her laptop. The days had begun to blend together, one indistinguishable from the next. There was no work to attend to, no meetings to prepare for, no emails to answer. She was a freelancer, a writer, but even the solace of working from home had become a trap. The world outside her window seemed like a distant memory, a world where people existed beyond screens, where human connection wasn’t mediated by technology.

Rachel was scrolling through old photographs when her phone buzzed. It was a text message from her sister, Sophie, who had been a lifeline throughout this strange time. Despite the distance, despite the isolation, they had kept in touch regularly. The messages were a small comfort, a reminder that there were still people out there, people who cared.

How are you holding up?

Rachel smiled softly as she typed back, It’s strange. I don’t know if I’m supposed to be sad or grateful. The quiet is both peaceful and terrifying.

I get that. I’ve been thinking a lot about the past—about how we used to always have people around. I miss that.

Rachel paused, her thumb hovering over the screen. She missed it too, more than she cared to admit. The noise, the laughter, the sound of voices filling the room. Her life had once been full of bustling conversations, shared meals, spontaneous gatherings, and now, the emptiness was overwhelming. She longed for those days, even as she understood that they were gone.

What do you think it’s going to be like, after all of this? Rachel sent.

There was a long pause before Sophie’s reply came. I don’t know. I guess we’ll all have to figure out how to live together, but from a distance. Maybe we’ll learn how to be closer without being close.

Rachel thought about those words long after she’d put her phone down. Maybe Sophie was right. The world had changed, and they had changed with it. The idea of closeness had shifted, and perhaps it wasn’t about being physically near someone anymore. It was about the space between, the understanding that even with six feet of distance, they could still share something real.

That evening, as the sky darkened and the city lights flickered on, Elias found himself at the window, looking out at the park below. The benches sat empty, the paths quiet, but there was something different about the way the world felt now. The distance that had once felt like a barrier between him and others had started to feel like an invitation—a challenge to redefine what it meant to be connected.

His phone buzzed again, and this time, it was Sarah. She had sent him a message with a simple question: What was it about the book?

He smiled softly, his fingers gliding over the screen as he typed back: It’s a story about people who are separated by vast distances but find a way to remain close through their shared experiences. It’s about the power of memory and the connections that transcend space.

He stared at the message for a moment before adding, Maybe that’s what we need right now. Not to be close, but to find ways to be together, even from a distance.

The response came quickly: I think you’re right. We’ll get through this. Just six feet apart, but still close.

Elias leaned back in his chair, the weight of the world still there, but somehow, lighter. The distance was real, and yet, in that moment, he felt a little less alone. Maybe it wasn’t about being close to others physically, but about keeping the connection alive, even if it meant living in separate worlds. After all, it was in the spaces between that the most meaningful connections often thrived.

As he turned the page of the book in his hands, Elias realized that the story was not just about distant lands—it was about the possibility of finding a way to belong to each other, no matter how far apart they might be.
