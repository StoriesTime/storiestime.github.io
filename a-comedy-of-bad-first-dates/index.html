A Comedy of Bad First Dates


It started with an app. Not one of those cutting-edge matchmaking darlings you see splashed across TV ads, but a clunky, lesser-known platform with more glitches than features. Lacey Dowling had signed up on a whim after a particularly painful bout of insomnia. At two in the morning, fueled by a mix of loneliness and curiosity, she created her account and swiped through potential matches with little expectation. If meeting new people meant potential calamity, at least it’d make for a good story.

The next morning, it began.

Date #1: The Over-Explainer

Her first match, Jeff, seemed promising enough—as promising as someone who opened with, “Heyo!” could be. He was tall and outdoorsy, judging by a half-dozen pictures featuring mountain climbing, fishing, and one shameless vacation shot of him sipping a margarita in an infinity pool. Their chatting flowed easily, so they arranged to meet at a cozy café downtown. Lacey appreciated a casual vibe, though nothing prepared her for what followed.

Jeff showed up ten minutes late, his tie crooked and hair sticking straight up as though escaping a wind tunnel. “Sorry,” he gasped, straightening his shirt buttons under her bemused stare. “Car died. Had to sprint here.”

“Don’t worry about it,” Lacey said, smiling sympathetically. She motioned to the menu. “What do you want to grab? Coffee’s great here.”

“I’ll take a latte,” he said, ordering briskly. Then he launched into a winding, never-ending explanation of his car trouble—from the mechanic’s vague advice last month to today’s breakdown while parallel parking. Lacey nodded politely at first, sipping her coffee, but by minute fifteen, her patience waned.

When Jeff segued into a detailed history of engine models with the enthusiasm of a used-car salesman, she suppressed a laugh. So much for meaningful connection—this was more like sitting through a TED Talk no one had signed up for. By the time he mentioned obscure tire brands, Lacey began wishing the café sold earplugs.

Date #2: The Pick-Up Line Prodigy

Three days later, Lacey matched with Marco—a self-proclaimed comedian whose opening message read: “Are you an overdue library book? Because you’ve got ‘fine’ written all over you!”

Against her better judgment, she responded. Marco’s confidence was intriguing, though his jokes bordered on absurdity. After a few exchanges, they agreed to meet at a farm-to-table restaurant that Marco promised was “life-changing.”

He arrived wearing a purple blazer so loud it rivaled the neon sign outside. When Lacey commented on his bold fashion choice, he grinned wide enough to split the earth. “Well, a queen deserves a royal escort,” he quipped, tipping an imaginary hat.

Throughout dinner, Marco’s talent for humor became both his charm and greatest flaw. Each response to every question came wrapped in wordplay or one-liners.

“So, what do you do for work?” Lacey asked carefully.

“I compile spreadsheets for a logistics firm. Sounds boring, but don’t worry—my excel-lent skills spice things up!”

When she chuckled politely, his enthusiasm doubled. “Favorite food?” he pressed.

“Probably Thai—”

“Oh! Thai food—when done right—throws a party in your mouth. When done wrong, it’s the wingman who ruins the date.”

Every reply invited an excessive punchline. By dessert, she was grinning purely out of survival instinct, exhausted from Marco’s need to perform. As the night ended, he insisted on walking her home to “protect from imaginary hooligans.” While his antics left her bewildered, at least he was harmless—and hilariously weird.

Date #3: The Hobby Enthusiast

Lacey nearly swore off dating after Marco, but the third match arrived sooner than expected. Amy, her extroverted roommate, convinced her to reconsider. “You haven’t met the right person yet, babe,” Amy said over dinner. “It’s statistical. Try this guy—Mitch. He’s hot.”

Indeed, Mitch appeared genuinely attractive: clean-cut smile, understated profile, no obvious red flags in his written responses. When he suggested meeting at an up-and-coming food truck festival, Lacey agreed, envisioning greasy tacos and fewer complications.

They met amidst wafting aromas of barbecue, fusion cooking, and fried delights. Mitch greeted her casually, dressed in faded jeans and crisp sneakers. He seemed perfect—humble yet confident, the type strangers trust instinctively. As they wandered from stall to stall, talking about travel and work, Lacey felt hopeful for the first time.

Until Mitch began dissecting food trucks.

“This truck is trying too hard,” he said, pointing to a retro-style one selling doughnuts. “Overpriced frou-frou sugar bombs. No originality.”

“Okay…” Lacey said skeptically.

“And this one”—he gestured toward a pho noodle stand—“went corporate. See how polished everything is? They’re funded by investors. Total sellouts.”

It wasn’t just food trucks. Mitch’s commentary extended to art, movies, fitness trends, and beyond, punctuated by intense grievances over everything. By the time the festival ended, Lacey knew this match was doomed. No one could survive relentless opinionated tirades, least of all her.

Date #4: The Over-Sharer

The fourth date didn’t happen immediately. Lacey temporarily deleted the app, citing emotional fatigue, yet Amy pushed her to reinstate it weeks later. After much convincing, Lacey reopened the floodgates of potential catastrophe.

Her newest match, Julian, seemed refreshingly normal—at least until their coffee shop rendezvous spun wildly off course. What started as light-hearted chit-chat quickly steamrolled into Julian oversharing his life story. He delved into every failed college exam, every ex-girlfriend’s bizarre reason for dumping him, even his aversion to cilantro (“It’s basically soap!”).

“I’ve been struggling lately,” Julian confessed barely ten minutes into the meeting. “Got evicted three months ago. My buddy lets me crash in his basement for now. Also, I chipped my tooth recently—can you see it?”

Lacey blinked, mortified as Julian leaned forward, opening his mouth wide at their table. She caught herself instinctively looking away.

To top it off, Julian ended the conversation by wondering aloud, “Do the baristas remind you of my cousin? He’s hilarious. Got arrested once. Told you about him, right?”

She fled before deciphering his cousin’s full backstory.

The Twist of Fate

After four disastrous encounters, Lacey was ready to quit dating permanently. Yet fate had other plans. One snowy evening in December, stuck waiting for an impossible bus after work, Lacey opened her weather-beaten umbrella and stood stiffly under a flickering streetlamp.

“Cold, isn’t it?” a voice remarked beside her.

Turning, she saw a man with kind eyes and an understated leather jacket smiling quietly, holding a matching umbrella. He introduced himself as Nate. They exchanged laughs over failed public transit schedules, quirky anecdotes, and shared frustrations over modern apps gone haywire.

Two hours later, standing amidst icy roads, Lacey realized she didn’t want their conversation to end. Nate felt warm—easy, genuine. Bad dates may have multiplied infinitely, but there was hope, tangled irreversibly with surprise timing.

And for the first time, she couldn’t stop smiling.
