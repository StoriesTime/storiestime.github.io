Behind the Mask: The Secrets of a Notorious Antagonist


The rain fell in unrelenting sheets over the hollow streets of Parsmont, a city caught between nostalgia and the inevitable pull of decay. Shadows flickered under pale streetlights as the cobbled alleyways echoed with the tapping rhythm of hurried footsteps. Those who walked here at night did so out of necessity, not courage. And yet, amid the biting wind and forlorn solitude, there was always a singular figure who seemed to belong to the night itself.

Korzath hadn’t always been a name that evoked fear. Decades ago, it was just the surname of a quiet watchmaker, a man whose craft was precision and whose life pivoted on the delicate rotation of gears. Alric Korzath, however, had learned young that even the subtlest mechanisms could wreak monumental havoc when manipulated. And tonight, the solitary figure behind a grotesque mask personified this philosophy.

Discovery of a Monster

It began twenty years ago, in the golden district of Parsmont. Alric was neither noble nor impoverished. If anything, he occupied a space the world quickly forgot: unobtrusive and invisible. At the age of twelve, his intrinsic ability to understand the mechanics of timepieces attracted the attention of the prestigious Bellis Observatory, where he was apprenticed for the next decade. The clever boy from the working-class neighborhood had become a celebrated craftsman.

But a machine, Alric would learn, lacked compassion. And the society that sat so reverently before his intricate clocks lacked it even more.

It was after he crafted his magnum opus—an impossibly complex chronometer gifted to wealthy merchant Dorne Hanover—that Alric’s life unraveled. When he delivered the masterpiece, Hanover refused payment, claiming minor imperfections only visible to “a trained eye.” Threatened with ruin and unable to afford recourse, Alric sank into the same anonymity from which he had risen.

Late one night, consumed by bitterness and fury, he broke into Hanover’s mansion. But robbery wasn’t his intent. He stole instead only his timepiece, replacing it with a faulty forgery he made in his workshop. By dawn, a scandal erupted: the great Dorne Hanover now found himself the humiliated subject of rumors and sneers. Alric had given his oppressor a taste of what it meant to be small.

But Hanover retaliated. His goons ransacked Alric’s workshop, burning it to cinders and leaving the watchmaker broken. In the ashes of his trade, the kernel of hatred bloomed. It crystalized in his mind that neither noble appeals nor impromptu vengeance would fix the rot embedded in the city—only calculated annihilation could.

Rise of the Mask

The first time Alric donned the mask, it was merely cloth stitched from the remains of his former life—a black scarf and coat snagged from the ruins of his workshop. But as his subversion grew, so did the mythology around him.

Korzath the Watcher, as people began calling him, was no longer the stripped-down craftsman. He morphed into the architect of terror. His mask became distinctive, a frightening visage of curled bronze and iron that resembled the face of some ancient clock-god: hollow eyes, vines of etched rivets, and sharp teeth that allowed for no smile. Through it, he was omnipotent.

Those who sat comfortably atop Parsmont’s pyramid soon became his targets. His early acts were surgical: a flood valve sabotaged to wash out Hanover’s textile warehouses, an ingenious puzzle lock encoded onto the vault of Elean Moonwick—a financier who profited from labor exploitation. Each crime bled perfection. Each was a mark not of petty discord but a declaration of war against those who thrived through the suffering of others.

But anonymity and power attracted two things Korzath had no intention of acquiring: admirers and imitators.

Shadows Beneath the Hero

A gang arose in Parsmont’s underbelly—a faction calling themselves the Gilded Shard—claiming to follow Korzath’s philosophy. They lived not by thoughtful destruction but by senseless mayhem, scrawling his insignia in blood after each act of chaos. The attention they drew was relentless, and for the first time, whispers questioned whether Korzath the Watcher was a visionary or a butcher.

The truth was far more elusive. Behind his bronze mask, Alric Korzath despised the Gilded Shard as much as he did those ruling the golden district. Brutes so easily corrupted could never understand the necessity of balance; they could only tip the scales further into entropy.

But what had begun as a crusade for justice was becoming corrupted in its own abstraction. To reignite his purpose, Alric designed the greatest act of sabotage Parsmont had ever seen. The Governor of Parsmont, Lady Jiselle Oramont, was set to unveil a sprawling theater built atop an underground aquifer. Korzath saw an opportunity—one geared to make Parsmont shudder.

The Mechanism of Fear

For months, he coveted a post on the construction team, posing as an elderly engineer with a forged résumé under the name “Theo Harrittus.” Deftly and invisibly, he recalibrated the wiring that supported the ceiling’s chandeliers, primed explosives beneath the aquifer’s access chamber, and sabotaged the flow of drinking water to ensure the ensuing drama would be prolonged.

But he hadn’t anticipated her. Petra Morant, a city official investigating unregulated labor during the theater’s construction, emerged through the scaffolding like an unspoken warning. Perched always in the corner, evaluating the crew, she rarely engaged—but her eyes lingered too long on him. He felt hunted under the weight of her scrutiny.

Petra’s relentless presence turned Korzath’s plan questionable. He began to imagine failure—or worse, success laced with unintended consequences. Could she unmask him if she became suspicious enough? Could she foil him if she chose precisely the wrong hour to review the aquifer chamber?

The Truth Betrayed

On the evening of the grand unveiling, Korzath donned his mask for what was meant to be his masterpiece. But as the crowd gathered and the governor approached the podium under lavish chandeliers, two constables led Theo Harrittus—his own persona—onto the stage.

Petra. She had trapped him. Scouring through layers of forged credentials, she had pieced together Theo Harrittus’s real identity long before tonight. A surge of disbelief ran through the spectators; who could’ve imagined a watchmaker, hidden in their forgotten boroughs, as the terrifying Watcher?

But even among ruin, Alric moved with precision. With a cunning motion, he triggered a timed detonation just beneath the aquifer. It created no havoc, but rather an orchestrated flood outside the venue—a theatrical warning. Within moments of his dramatic escape through the vents, whispers weren’t debating Theo Harrittus’s guilt. They debated Korzath’s ingenuity.

The Legacy Behind the Mask

The years wore on, but Korzath’s legend never dulled. His mask, once a weapon, took on a gripping mythos. To most, he was an avenger wielding terror with purpose, a man forgotten by society who refused to let injustice fester unnoticed. To others, he was a monster multiplying the agony of an already suffering city.

When Alric retired his mask, handing it off to no successor, Parsmont slowly layered new myths over his truth. Merchants claimed his ghost haunted their vaults. Workers whispered of “The Watcher” when an accident killed the wealthy during high-risk labor. But no one, not even Petra Morant, attempted to rewrite what Korzath had shown: just as machinery could operate without mercy, so too could the forgotten masses.

Behind the mask, Alric Korzath wasn’t a hero. He wasn’t a villain. He was merely a craftsman in a broken world—and sometimes behind every notorious name, there lies anger sharper than a blade.

Perhaps everyone owns a mask; it’s simply a matter of which injustices prompt us to use it.
