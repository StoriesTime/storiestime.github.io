Echoes of Dignity: A Story on Human Rights Abuse


Jasira awoke to the tinny murmur of a distant radio. Its static-laced tune bled through the cracked plaster and dust-worn sheets. Someone, somewhere, was listening to the morning news, perhaps in another apartment block, or even on the street below in downtown Sefali. The morning was coughed up by horns and the yelps of street vendors, but the radio was the sound that clung most stubbornly, a ghostly thread Jasira could not unravel.

Her thoughts ran laps around the same battered arena: the day she would stand again before another committee, another stern-eyed officer with a stack of files stamped and stained and riddled with lies. She pressed her palm against the windowsill, feeling the cool seep into her skin, and tried—in vain—not to think about the last Thursday of the old world.

The knock on the door came at precisely seven.

“You’re late,” Rasim said, stepping into the narrow room with a thermos in one hand and a faded dossier in the other. Rasim’s smile was frayed, like old cloth, but Jasira cherished it. It was proof that even now, some things held together.

“Late?” Jasira asked, her voice quiet. “By whose clock?”

Rasim shrugged, passing her a chipped mug. “The committee will have a new schedule, they want you at the courthouse before eight. We leave in ten.”

She sipped the tea, letting the warmth stencil shapes on her tongue. There was a time, before the regime changed hands, when Jasira was a human rights officer. She used to track missing persons, slipping their photographs under the noses of police officers who looked away, bored. Back then, Rasim was a journalist who believed stories could tilt the world in new directions. In a different country, they might have built slow, sunlit lives, humming along with the small work of protest and repair. Here, however, they’d both become something else: witnesses, survivors, professionals at the business of tending wounds that never healed.

The street outside was already feverish. Sirens pricked the morning air; a truck loaded with volunteers from the Ministry trundled past. At the far end of Gerin Avenue, a billboard flashed the regime’s emblem—a silvered lion gnawing a tattered plume. Beside it, a poster of the Supreme Director: eyes blank, mouth distant.

Jasira pulled a scarf higher over her mouth. “Do you ever get used to it?” she murmured.

Rasim followed her gaze, squinting. “I stopped looking years ago. Doesn’t do much good. Here.”

He offered her the dossier, thick with case summaries. Names, birthdates, ages. Reasons for detainment—most vague, stamped with a bureaucratic flourish: “Subversive Activity,” “Illegal Assembly,” “Suspicion of Collusion.” She already knew them by heart. These were the new catechisms, repeated in offices and courtrooms day after day.
