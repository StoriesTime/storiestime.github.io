Scrolling Through Life: How Social Media Shapes Our Reality


There was a time when the idea of reality felt grounded, tangible even, something we could touch, taste, and experience face-to-face. But as the digital age expanded, a new realm emerged, one that was not bound by physical laws, yet influenced everything from how we communicate to how we perceive ourselves and others. This new world was social media—a place where lives were curated, filtered, and presented in perfect snapshots. And as millions of people scrolled through their feeds daily, the fabric of reality began to shift.

For Lily, an aspiring artist, social media was both a gift and a curse. Every morning, she woke up with the familiar sound of her phone buzzing beside her bed. Her thumb hovered over the screen, and as she unlocked her phone, she was immediately sucked into the whirlwind of posts, tweets, snaps, and stories. The bright blue glow of the Instagram app drew her in, like a magnet.

Her feed was a blend of influencers in sleek outfits, perfect food plates at sunlit brunches, and picturesque landscapes from places she’d only dreamed of visiting. There, on her screen, life seemed more exciting, more fulfilling, more perfect. And every day, she scrolled.

But beneath the filter of filters, Lily felt a growing sense of unease. The more she scrolled, the more disconnected she felt from her own life. Each photo seemed like a standard to measure herself against, and each story a benchmark she was failing to live up to. It wasn’t that she didn’t know these images weren’t “real”—she was well aware that they were edited, rehearsed, and perfected before they ever made it to her feed. But even with that knowledge, the images seeped into her mind, shaping her perception of what success, beauty, and happiness should look like.

One evening, as Lily scrolled through a story of a friend vacationing in a remote tropical paradise, she felt a pang of jealousy. “Why haven’t I done something like that?” she thought. “Why am I still here, sitting alone in my apartment, struggling to make ends meet?”

Her phone buzzed again. This time, it was a notification from one of the many artists she followed—someone whose work was highly admired in the art community. The artist had just shared a photo of their new gallery opening, a beautifully decorated space with impressive crowds admiring their work.

Lily sighed. She had always wanted to be in that position—showing her art to the world, gaining recognition, living in a bustling city full of opportunities. Instead, she was stuck in a small, quiet town where opportunities felt few and far between.

She put down her phone, but the images and thoughts lingered. For the rest of the evening, they played on a loop in her mind. Every time she closed her eyes, she could see the curated lives of the people she followed: the success, the happiness, the beauty.

The next morning, Lily decided to break the cycle. She logged off of all her social media accounts, determined to spend a day living in the real world, not the digital one. But as soon as she opened the door, the weight of the silence hit her. Without her phone to distract her, she had nothing but the world around her, and it felt… quiet. Uncomfortably quiet.

She spent the day visiting a local art museum, trying to find inspiration in the work of others. But her thoughts kept drifting back to the lives she was missing out on. Without the constant stream of validation from likes and comments, she felt insignificant.

By the evening, she was ready to return to her digital haven, to the place where her self-worth had always been measured by the number of followers she had, the comments she received, and the number of views her work accumulated. The digital world gave her a sense of belonging, even if it wasn’t real.

As the days passed, Lily returned to her scrolling routine. But the nagging feeling of disconnection stayed with her. It was as though social media had become an uninvited guest in her life, dictating her emotions, choices, and desires.

She spent hours curating her own feed, posting pictures of her art, selfies with a carefully chosen backdrop, and inspirational quotes. But as she watched her follower count rise and the likes pour in, something felt hollow. Each new comment, each new follower gave her a fleeting sense of accomplishment, but it quickly faded, replaced by the relentless pursuit of the next post, the next high.

One afternoon, as Lily was scrolling through her feed, she came across a post that caught her attention. It was from an artist she admired—someone who had built a successful career, someone whose work was featured in galleries across the country. The artist shared a behind-the-scenes look at their studio, the long hours spent creating, and the challenges they faced along the way. They spoke openly about the struggles of maintaining a presence on social media and how the pressure to constantly share and post had affected their mental health.

The post struck a chord with Lily. For the first time, she saw someone being real. Not just showing the highlight reel, but revealing the messy, imperfect parts of the journey. It was a refreshing contrast to the perfection that flooded her feed every day.

Inspired, Lily decided to take a different approach. Instead of striving for perfection, she decided to focus on authenticity. She began sharing more than just the polished pieces of her work. She shared the process—the messy sketches, the failures, the times she struggled to find inspiration. She opened up about the challenges of being an artist in a world that demanded constant visibility and success.

At first, it was terrifying. She feared the judgment of her followers, the comments that might call her out for being “too real.” But as she shared more of herself, something unexpected happened. People responded. They resonated with her honesty, with the vulnerability she put out there. Slowly, Lily realized that her worth wasn’t tied to the perfect images or the curated posts. It was in the authenticity she shared with others.

Over time, Lily’s relationship with social media began to evolve. She still used it to share her art, but it was no longer a tool for comparison or validation. Instead, it became a platform for connection—an opportunity to engage with others who were also navigating the complexities of life in a digital world.

She no longer felt the pressure to measure her success against the perfectly curated feeds of others. Her self-worth was no longer defined by likes or followers. Instead, it was grounded in her own sense of purpose and the real connections she had made with others.

As Lily continued to scroll through life, she realized that social media would always be a part of her world. But it no longer controlled her reality. She had learned to use it as a tool for inspiration, not comparison. It was just one piece of the puzzle, a way to connect with others, share her journey, and find her place in a world that was increasingly digital but still very much human.

And in the end, Lily discovered that the truest form of connection—the one that mattered the most—was the one she had with herself.
