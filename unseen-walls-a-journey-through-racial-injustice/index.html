Unseen Walls: A Journey Through Racial Injustice


It was a crisp autumn morning when Joshua found himself staring at the walls of the old courthouse in the heart of the city. The building, standing tall and proud, had witnessed decades of history, yet its cracks seemed to tell stories of deep-seated struggles. As he approached the courthouse steps, he could feel the weight of generations past, each one burdened with a different kind of injustice, a different kind of fight.

Joshua had grown up in a world where every street corner, every interaction, seemed to remind him of the invisible barriers that had been placed in front of him simply because of the color of his skin. Yet, as a young man, he had learned to navigate life with determination, never fully understanding why the walls were there—only that they existed.

Today was different. Today, Joshua was not walking with the same casual purpose he had in the past. He had been summoned to testify in a case that involved a systemic problem he had known all too well but never had the chance to confront head-on. The case was about racial profiling, and he had witnessed it many times—both in the lives of those around him and in his own.

His footsteps echoed in the empty hallways of the courthouse as he made his way toward the courtroom. The walls, adorned with faded portraits of past judges, seemed to watch him silently, their eyes unblinking and indifferent. In a way, Joshua knew that these walls were like many others in his life—silent, but always present, always keeping track of who was allowed in and who was kept out.

As he sat in the courtroom, waiting for his turn to speak, his mind wandered back to the moments in his childhood when these walls, these barriers, first began to take shape. He thought about the times when he was a teenager, walking down the street with his friends, when a police car would slow down beside them, the officers’ eyes scanning them with suspicion. Joshua had learned early that in certain places, in certain spaces, his mere presence was a threat.

The first time he was stopped, he was only sixteen. He had been walking home from the corner store when a police car pulled up beside him. The officer, a man with a stern face, had rolled down the window and asked, “What are you doing here?” Joshua, confused, had replied, “I’m just walking home.” The officer’s eyes narrowed, and for a moment, Joshua thought the man would accuse him of something. But the officer simply drove off, leaving Joshua feeling like he had just narrowly avoided a trap that he hadn’t even known existed.

It wasn’t long before Joshua realized that the officer’s suspicion was not an isolated incident—it was part of something larger. He began to notice how the people around him were treated differently. His friends, his family, his neighbors—all of them seemed to face the same invisible wall that kept them at arm’s length from the opportunities that others took for granted.

Despite this, Joshua had managed to carve out a life for himself. He worked hard in school, earned a scholarship to college, and eventually found a job at a well-known tech company. But even with his success, the walls remained. There were times when he would enter a room and feel the cold, invisible scrutiny of others. It wasn’t always overt, but it was always there—a look of surprise when he spoke well, an unspoken question hanging in the air: “How did you get here?”

Joshua had learned to push through those moments, to act as though they didn’t exist. But today, as he sat in the courtroom waiting to testify, he realized that it was no longer enough to ignore the walls. They had to be confronted, named, and dismantled.

The case he was about to speak in was not just about him or his experience—it was about the thousands of others who had been affected by the same invisible walls. People who had been stopped, questioned, and arrested because they looked out of place. People who had been denied opportunities, simply because they didn’t fit the mold of what society deemed acceptable.

When his name was called, Joshua stood up and walked to the witness stand. The room fell silent, the weight of the moment pressing down on him. He could feel the eyes of the jury, the judge, and the spectators on him, all waiting for him to speak. It was in these moments that Joshua realized the true significance of his testimony. He was not just speaking for himself; he was speaking for all those who had been silenced by the walls.

With a steady voice, he began to recount his experiences, each word heavy with the truth of what he had lived through. “I remember the first time I was stopped by a police officer,” he said, his voice clear and unwavering. “I was sixteen years old, and I had done nothing wrong. But because of the way I looked, I was questioned, detained, and made to feel like I didn’t belong. That moment, that experience, shaped everything that came after it.”

As he spoke, he could see the faces of the jury shift. Some looked uncomfortable, others seemed to be processing the weight of his words. But Joshua knew that this was only the beginning. The walls of racial injustice were not going to fall with just one story, one testimony. They would take time to dismantle, and the work would be difficult. But today, he was standing at the frontlines, ready to do his part.

After he finished his testimony, he took his seat again, his mind still racing with thoughts of the walls he had spoken about. He knew that this moment was not the end, but rather the beginning of something much larger. It was a moment in a long struggle, a struggle that had been fought by generations before him and would continue long after he was gone.

As the trial continued, Joshua thought about his family, his friends, and all the people who had supported him along the way. They were the ones who had helped him break through the walls, even when they seemed impossible to overcome. They were the ones who had reminded him that, despite the walls, he was not alone.

In the years that followed, Joshua would continue to fight for justice, speaking out against the racial inequality that still existed in the world. He would become an advocate, using his voice to bring attention to the issues that so many had tried to ignore. But he would never forget the moment he had stood in that courtroom, facing down the invisible walls that had shaped his life, and the lives of so many others.

The journey was far from over, but with each step, Joshua knew that the walls were becoming a little less unseen. They were being recognized, understood, and eventually—one day—torn down.

And as he walked away from the courthouse that day, Joshua knew that the walls might still stand, but the fight for justice had only just begun. The journey through racial injustice was long and difficult, but with every voice raised, with every story told, the walls would eventually crumble. One story at a time.
