Cultural Crossroads: The Impact of Migration on Identity


Adira took a deep breath as she stepped off the plane, clutching her small carry-on bag like a lifeline. The air felt different here—cooler, less humid than the tropical warmth of her hometown. The morning sun splashed across her face, but the brightness wasn’t enough to drown out the swirling emotions inside her. Ahead lay London, the city her family had long dreamed of calling home.

The chaos of the airport surrounded her: announcements crackling through overhead speakers, baggage carts squeaking against linoleum floors, and passengers hurriedly moving toward various gates. Yet all Adira could focus on was an internal question that had been unrelenting ever since her parents had finalized the decision to leave Mumbai: Who am I going to be now?

It wasn’t just a question of where she was going—it was about everything she’d left behind. Back home, there were streets she could have navigated blindfolded, food stalls where the vendors knew her usual chai order, and a community that overflowed with warmth, even when it felt suffocating. Here, she would have to rebuild her identity brick by brick, but with what materials?

Adira’s father, Arun, waved eagerly from the arrivals gate. His wide grin betrayed none of the exhaustion she knew he must’ve felt after the turbulence of leaving everything familiar. Her mother, Sanya, rested a protective hand on Adira’s shoulder as they made their way to their taxi.

The ride into the heart of London presented Adira with a kaleidoscope of impressions. Red double-decker buses wove in and out of traffic, pedestrians braved crosswalks with hurried determination, and the aroma of roasted coffee beans wafted from cafés tucked between towering glass buildings. She was mesmerized, yet a kind of ache ebbed inside her chest. All this dynamism felt like an invitation to vanish into anonymity—into an overwhelming sea of strangers.

Their new apartment, nestled in the borough of Hackney, was modest but sufficient. The walls were still barren, the rooms smelled faintly of fresh paint, and sunlight streamed in through lace curtains drawn tight against the mid-morning chill. Adira’s mother directed the movers with a calm efficiency while her father leaned on the counter, already scrolling through listings for job interviews.

Adira didn’t wait for instructions. Instead, she crept out of the small apartment and wandered onto the busy street. With no real destination, she followed the rhythm of the city itself, her feet moving almost involuntarily. Her dupatta fluttered in the crisp wind, earning curious glances from passersby.

At a corner market, she collided with an elderly shopkeeper who was arranging flowers in a tin pail. “Careful there,” he warned, his voice thick with an unfamiliar accent. She muttered an apology, but the man grinned kindly. “You new around here?” Adira hesitated but nodded. “Welcome to the neighborhood,” he said, handing her a yellow chrysanthemum. “Little gesture of good luck.”

She smiled and clutched the flower, touched by the stranger’s warmth in a city where she had expected only indifference. Adira quickly discovered that Hackney wasn’t a homogeneous place—it was a living, breathing amalgamation of many lives, cultures, and traditions.

In just her first week, she encountered Turkish baklava shops sitting comfortably next to Polish delis, Jamaican jerk chicken stands parked beside Vietnamese bánh mì carts, and street signs tagged with graffiti in a dozen non-English scripts. Walking to school meant passing mosques, churches, temples, and the occasional art gallery tucked between them. In Hackney, no one seemed to entirely belong, yet everyone fit. But was that enough for Adira? Could she find herself in this cacophony?

School, as it turned out, was its own brand of bewilderment. Adira’s classmates were polite but distant at first. Her accent marked her as a newcomer, and she was acutely aware that her clothes stood out in a sea of muted sweaters and trainers. Yet the break room was where she met Esther, a Nigerian girl with an afro that glowed like a halo and a laugh that rolled out like music.

“I love your scarf,” Esther said one day, gesturing at Adira’s dupatta. “The color’s amazing.”

Adira flushed. “Thanks. My mom keeps insisting we keep… you know, some traditions.”

“Well, keep doing it,” Esther said without hesitation. “We’ve got to hold on to something, right? My mom still won’t let me leave the house without knowing exactly where I’m going. Says it’s one thing she brought with her when she left Lagos—worrying.”

That first genuine conversation blossomed quickly into a friendship that revealed more bonds than differences. They traded stories about their families—parallels wrapped in unique details. From Esther, Adira learned words in Yoruba, danced to Afrobeats blaring from a Bluetooth speaker, and tried her first plate of Nigerian jollof rice, which Esther promised was superior to any other version. From Adira, Esther absorbed the delicate rhythm of Hindi words, savored bites of homemade samosas, and followed her to a Bollywood film screening that left Esther humming along to the songs on the way home.

As the months passed, Adira grew increasingly aware of the push and pull within her. Some days, she longed to disappear fully into this new environment, to hang her dupatta at the back of the closet and adopt the city’s casual uniform of hoodies and jeans. On other days, she clung fiercely to her traditions, cooking her mother’s recipes with extra spice and practicing intricate mehndi designs out of nostalgia for Mumbai’s crowded festivals. She existed on a strange borderland, neither there nor here.

But so did everyone else, it seemed.

The elderly flower vendor from her first day in Hackney? She learned from a casual chat that he’d emigrated from Poland as a young man, fleeing political turmoil. The Turkish baklava shop owner down the street? His family had grown roots after fleeing conflict decades ago. Even Esther’s parents had their own tales of adjusting and negotiating their place in this metropolitan crossroads. Hackney wasn’t just a neighborhood. It was a mosaic, made richer by every new piece.

One afternoon, Adira came home to find her parents excitedly debating over a wedding invitation. The RSVP date was soon approaching, and her extended family in India was eagerly awaiting their reply. Adira watched in silence as her father listed the expenses they’d need to budget for airfare, while her mother fretted over whether they still had suitable clothing for such an occasion.

“Maybe we should stay,” Sanya sighed. “It’s been so long since we’ve seen everyone back home, but…”

Arun shook his head gently and said, “It’s not just about going back. It’s also about finding our footing here.”

That struck a chord in Adira. For weeks, she’d been wondering if it was possible to honor the person she was raised to be while embracing the person she was becoming. Could she sing her favorite Bollywood songs while learning slang from her classmates? Could she celebrate Diwali at home while joining Esther’s family for Christmas dinner? The answer, she was beginning to see, was yes. None of it required erasing anything. She could carry her history forward without allowing it to anchor her in the past.

The next day, Adira stood on the same street where she’d been handed her first yellow chrysanthemum. The flower vendor nodded at her in recognition as she passed, and she returned the gesture. This time, however, she wasn’t wandering aimlessly. She was headed to the local cultural center where she had volunteered to teach basic Hindi to children. It felt like a small bridge between her worlds, a step toward ensuring that language and heritage could also thrive in this patchwork city.

Looking up at the gray London sky, she didn’t feel the ache she had upon arrival. Instead, she felt a sense of possibility. Migration, she now understood, wasn’t just about relocation; it was about transformation—the constant give-and-take of identity. And here, in the midst of this colorful, bustling, sometimes chaotic city, Adira had found something like a home. Maybe not the only one, but for now, it was enough.
