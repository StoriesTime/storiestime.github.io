From Manager to Mentor: Cultivating Influence in the Workplace


Martin Kincaid was the kind of manager who always seemed to be in motion. His stride was purposeful, his emails concise, and his office door frequently closed as he tackled mountains of work. At thirty-nine, he had achieved everything he thought he wanted: a corner office at Albright Dynamics, a substantial paycheck, and the respect of his peers. But beneath the polished exterior lay a quiet frustration he rarely acknowledged. Something was missing, but he didn’t quite know what it was.

It wasn’t until an unexpected conversation with his longtime CEO, Eleanor Blackwell, that the cracks in his carefully curated professional life began to show.

“Martin,” Eleanor began as she leaned back in her chair, “you’re doing great work. Your team hits deadlines, clients love the results, and the board couldn’t be more pleased." She paused, tapping a thoughtful finger against her chin. “But tell me something. If you left here tomorrow, what would your legacy be?”

Her words hung in the air like a challenge. Legacy? The concept felt foreign to him. Sure, his team’s impressive deliverables spoke to his leadership, but he rarely thought beyond quarterly goals, profitability, and flawless execution. What did legacy have to do with anything?

Torn between diplomacy and confusion, Martin hesitated. “I’d like to think that my work speaks for itself, Eleanor.”

“That’s the problem, Martin," she replied, her tone gentle but firm. “Work does speak for itself, but people carry a more profound message forward. Teams remember how you shaped them, how you helped them grow. The influence you leave behind isn’t just in spreadsheets or reports—it’s in people. More than a manager, great leaders know how to be mentors.”

Martin wasn’t sure how to respond. He’d always prided himself on being efficient and results-oriented, but Eleanor was suggesting he aim for something deeper, more personal. Her words lingered long after the meeting ended.

The next day in the office felt strangely different. Martin watched his team from the large glass pane of his office. Ella, his top-performing account specialist, was hunched over her laptop, her face set in the familiar look of someone pushing through fatigue. Brian and Steph were brainstorming in the conference room, though Brian—usually quick with ideas—looked withdrawn today. Aiden, the youngest and newest hire, nervously flitted between tasks like someone trying to prove their worth at every step.

Martin suddenly realized he couldn’t recall the last time he’d had a conversation with any of them that didn’t revolve around deadlines or deliverables. Did he even know what motivated them or what challenges they faced? Did they see him as someone they could turn to, or just a figure who occasionally gave praise when their work hit the mark?

It was a sobering thought. If Eleanor was right, he was little more than a taskmaster. And if he left tomorrow? His absence might be noted, but not truly felt.

Later that evening, Martin did something uncharacteristic. He dug through his bookshelf for a dog-eared copy of The Servant Leader, a book someone had gifted him years ago but which he’d ignored after reading only a chapter. This time, though, the pages seemed alive with meaning. He found himself underlining passages about empathy, listening, and fostering relationships. The more he read, the more the idea of mentorship began to resonate—not as a duty, but as an opportunity to make a difference.

Change didn’t happen all at once, but Martin knew that to shift from merely managing to mentoring, he would need a radically different approach. He started small.

The first opportunity came during their Monday morning meeting. Instead of his usual rapid-fire barrage of updates and assignments, Martin paused after running through the agenda.

“Before we dive into individual tasks,” he said, “I want to ask each of you something. What’s one challenge you’re facing this week that you’d like more support on? It doesn’t have to be work—it can be anything.”

His team exchanged puzzled glances, unsure if this was a trick. Finally, Ella broke the silence.

“Well… I guess I’ve been struggling to balance the client work with the administrative side,” she confessed. “Sometimes I think I spend more energy on paperwork than on strategy.”

Martin nodded. “That’s a fair point. Let’s sit down after this meeting and figure out which processes we can streamline or delegate.”

As the others chimed in, what started as hesitation soon morphed into an honest, open dialogue. For the first time in years, Martin walked out of the meeting feeling lighter. He hadn’t just assigned tasks—he’d started a conversation.

Over the next few months, Martin’s transformation began to take root. He made an effort to connect with his team on a personal level. Coffee chats, once a rarity, became regular fixtures of his week. Listening to their stories, he discovered hidden ambitions and untapped potential in each person.

Take Brian, for example. Once withdrawn, Brian revealed a side of himself Martin hadn’t seen before. He wasn’t just a solid strategist—he was brimming with innovative ideas he had been too nervous to pitch in meetings. By assigning Brian ownership of a new campaign, Martin gave him the chance to shine. And he did.

Then there was Aiden, whose nervous energy initially masked a deep hunger for feedback and mentorship. Instead of only critiquing Aiden’s work like many managers might, Martin made a point to celebrate his growth. When Aiden presented a marketing analysis that exceeded expectations, Martin brought him up during the weekly all-hands meeting, commending his progress in front of the larger team.

“All it takes sometimes,” Martin reflected later, “is a few words to make someone feel seen.”

With time, the entire department began to shift. The once transactional atmosphere was replaced by a spirit of collaboration. People weren’t just tackling projects—they were helping one another succeed. And while Martin still doled out tasks and made tough decisions, the team looked at him differently now, as more than just “the boss.” He’d earned their trust, not through power, but through presence, patience, and a genuine commitment to their success.

Martin’s realization deepened during a year-end review. Steph, one of his longest-serving team members, arrived for her one-on-one meeting holding a thank-you card.

She smiled awkwardly, setting it on his desk. “I just wanted to say… I’ve worked for a couple of managers before, but you’ve made the biggest impact on me. You don’t just care about the work—I feel like you care about who we are as people. And that’s not something you see every day.”

The words struck a chord within him. Legacy.

Eleanor’s question from months earlier echoed in his mind. This was what it meant. Legacy wasn’t a checklist or a trophy—it was the effect you had on others, the seeds you planted in the people you touched.

Years later, when Martin moved on to a new role that would challenge him in fresh ways, the farewell party his team threw him wasn’t filled with flashy presentations or generic speeches. Instead, one by one, his colleagues shared stories of how his mentorship had shaped the trajectory of their careers.

“I got my leadership role because of you,” Ella said.

“You taught me how to believe in myself,” Aiden added.

“I still use the lessons you taught me about listening,” said Brian.

As they spoke, Martin realized that he had finally bridged the gap between manager and mentor, between professional success and personal fulfillment. For the first time in his career, he knew exactly what he’d be leaving behind—a legacy worth remembering.
