Rainforest Requiem


The rainforest groaned with ancient vitality, its melodies carried by whispers through the canopies. A gentle rain fell, collecting in wide, waxy leaves before trickling down into the loamy soil. To those unfamiliar, it might seem like a haven untouched by time, but to Smokhana, the evidence of its ongoing battles was everywhere.

Smokhana crouched beneath a sprawling kapok tree, her fingers brushing over a patch of disturbed earth. The soil, dark and rich, had been uprooted by heavy boots. The pungent scent of disturbed moss lingered in the air, mocking the serenity of the forest around it. She closed her eyes, taking in the sounds beyond the rain’s rhythm—the clash of metallic tools against bark, muted voices, and distant machines whining with mechanical hunger.

These sounds intruded like splinters beneath her skin. Her tribe, the Mukeyane, reverently referred to this forest as Pah’khweyran, the Living Heart. For generations, every chant, every celebration, every solemn prayer had been tied to this land. It nurtured them as much as they nurtured it. But now, strangers were carving away pieces of it with biting steel and greed in their hearts, blind to the balance they were tipping.

A low rustle by her side drew Smokhana’s attention. She turned, seeing Kevari emerge from the shadows. His face was carved with lines of worry, his lean frame tense with urgency.

“They’ve moved further upriver,” he whispered, his machete gleaming in the faint light filtering through the canopy. “Three more trees felled this morning.”

Smokhana stood, her jaw tightening. “Did you see their faces? Are they soldiers or wanderers?”

“They’re workers. Simple laborers, by the looks of them. But they bear the mark of the corporate scythe.”

“It always begins like this,” Smokhana muttered. “With workers and axes. But behind them are machines… and excuses.”

Kevari nodded grimly. The Mukeyane had watched this pattern unfold countless times in recent years. Logging companies, mineral prospects, or, worse, oil exploration—all veiled as progress yet sucking the life from the lungs of the earth. The treaties were nothing but ink on forgotten paper, promises that disintegrated faster than the leaves underfoot.

“Do we confront them or warn the others?” Kevari asked, though his tone suggested he already knew the answer.

“Both,” Smokhana replied, her voice steady as stone. “Mother Pah’khweyran has suffered enough losses. We won’t let this spread.”

Hours later, Smokhana and Kevari crept closer to the intruder’s camp under the cover of dusk. The air was thick with fumes tinged with the sour edge of diesel, far removed from the usual rich tapestry of the forest’s scents. Smokhana’s eyes narrowed as she spotted the source of the disturbance—an array of tents perched beside a wide clearing with torn stumps dotting the horizon.

The workers, dirt-streaked and weary, moved like shadows between the industrial machines parked at the edges of the clearing. Chainsaws rested against a makeshift rack, their metal glinting with malice under the flickering light of gas lanterns. An older man, dressed in faded overalls, sipped from a tin cup while speaking to a figure Smokhana didn’t immediately recognize—a foreman, by the confident way he carried himself.

“They’re building roads next,” Kevari murmured, pointing to orange flags driven into the soil at the clearing’s edge. “Roads bring trucks. And then… the forest becomes theirs.”

Smokhana’s fists clenched. Her people had always known that progress would eventually encroach upon their sacred land, but foreknowledge did not erase the sting of its arrival. She glanced back at Kevari, her voice low but firm. “Tomorrow, we strike. Give word to the others. Tonight, the Living Heart will remind them where they walk.”

Smokhana’s warnings reached the Mukeyane village long after the sun vanished, swallowed by the embrace of night. Around the ceremonial fire, the elders listened intently, their wrinkled faces serene but their eyes stormy. They were guardians of the sacred knowledge passed down since the first breath of Pah’khweyran, and tonight their duty came alive with vibrant intensity.

“We will not bear arms,” Elder Vayron declared, his words weighted with finality. “To respond with anger disrupts the harmony. Instead, we will honor the rainforest’s call. We confront them with its guardians.”

The tribe murmured in agreement, some uncertain but respectful of the elders’ decree. Smokhana watched as they prepared, weaving restorative chants into the air and painting their bodies with patterns drawn from nature itself. She herself joined them, murmuring ancient hymns to summon the spirits of the trees, animals, and the soil that cradled them all.

By dawn, the rain began to fall again, gentle and persistent. The workers awoke groggily, wiping moisture from their faces, oblivious to the ominous shift in the air. When they stepped from their tents, the forest around them had come alive in ways they couldn’t explain.

Roots had surfaced where there had been none the day before, curling around abandoned tools and tires. Saplings grew where fire pits had smoldered, their leaves swaying though the wind was still. The birds and monkeys, typically subdued at dawn, orchestrated a cacophony that echoed through the clearing with unnatural resonance.

And then they saw the Mukeyane.

Emerging as though summoned by the fog itself, the figures came. Smokhana stood at their helm, her long hair trailing behind her like a current. Beside her, Kevari held aloft an unlit torch, ceremonial and symbolic. A dozen others followed, each painted and clad in garments of the crimson earth and emerald vines.

The workers froze. A few glanced toward the foreman, who barked something incomprehensible into the loudness of the forest’s voice.

“I speak for Pah’khweyran,” Smokhana called, her voice cutting through the noise with clarity. “And so do my people. Here, life exists as one—river, sky, root, breath. You trespass and seek to destroy what lives in harmony.”

“Who do you think you are?” the foreman demanded, stepping forward with arms held wide. His defiant posture faltered when he noticed the serrated vines now coiled around the equipment nearest to him. “We’re contracted. Legal! You have no authority—”

“You do not listen,” Smokhana interrupted, her dark eyes gleaming with an unyielding gaze. “We are not asking for mercy. The Living Heart does not negotiate. Leave these grounds, or you will answer to more than us.”

The foreman glanced at the workers for support, finding their wide-eyed expressions anything but reassuring. Behind him, the rainforest’s avengers stood motionless—human and nonhuman alike. Shadows loomed in the gaps between trees where jaguars were known to tread. Brightly feathered eagles perched alarmingly close, claws flexing. The forest had seemingly conspired to make even the most arrogant among them feel helpless.

A long moment passed, and then, the crackle of thunder rolled overhead. Smokhana tilted her head skyward. Rain began to pour harder, soaking the intruders as though attempting to erase their existence.

One by one, the workers began to scatter, their resolve broken as if the jungle itself had commanded it. The foreman lingered, glaring furiously as if to reclaim some sense of control. But when vines crawled closer to his boots, his defiance faltered. Without another word, he turned and followed the others, muttering into his breath.

By the following night, the Mukeyane celebrated with solemn songs, each note resonating deep within the grove where the spirits dwelled. Smokhana stood amid her people. Relief fluttered in her chest for the small victory, though its weight still lingered.

“Will they return?” Kevari asked, staring at the distant trees where the loggers had vanished.

“Perhaps,” Smokhana replied. “But Pah’khweyran fights with us now. And if they return, so will we.”

The rainforest sighed, its lullabies carrying through the endless canopy, a requiem not of despair but of resilience. The fight was far from over, but for now, the Living Heart still beat.
