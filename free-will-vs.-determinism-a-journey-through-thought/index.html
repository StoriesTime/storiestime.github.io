Free Will vs. Determinism: A Journey Through Thought


Eli strode down the cobblestone streets of his town, his boots striking the earth with a rhythm that seemed to echo his growing frustration. The sun blazed overhead, indifferent, as if mocking the turmoil within his mind. He had spent the last four weeks in heated debates with professors, classmates, and even strangers at the local café about one question that refused to relent: Do we have free will, or is everything predetermined?

The question gnawed at every corner of his thoughts, inching its way into every conversation, every decision he made. That morning, Eli had found himself frozen at the counter of the bakery, staring at two loaves of bread. Should he pick the sourdough because it was his favorite, or the rye because he wanted to push himself into unfamiliar territory? Was that even a real choice? If his stepping outside of comfort was itself predetermined by his hunger for novelty, what did free will truly mean?

He opted for the sourdough—a decision he immediately regretted.

It wasn’t the bread that concerned him. It was the implications. If his preference had guided him like an invisible hand, was the rye ever truly a possibility? Or had it been eliminated before he even thought about it, the illusion of choice masking an inevitable result?

The bakery was only the latest instance in Eli’s downhill spiral into philosophic confusion. His thoughts had layers now, like a vast labyrinth leading him deeper into himself. They frightened him, yet he couldn’t deny their grip.

That evening, while the others in his boarding house played cards, Eli retreated to his room to wrestle with his thoughts in solitude. His desk was cluttered with books: expansive treatises on philosophy, thin paperbacks explaining quantum physics, and prints of essays from schools of thought he’d only recently learned existed—existentialism, determinism, libertarianism, compatibilism. He had read them all, scoured every corner for an answer, yet the truth remained elusive.

Eli’s lamp flickered as he flipped open one of the books with frustration, his eyes landing on a chapter titled The Clockwork Universe. It described the deterministic model as envisioned by thinkers like Pierre-Simon Laplace. If one knew the location and momentum of every particle in the universe at any given time, the future could, in theory, be calculated with precision—the concept terrified him and fascinated him all at once. Was he simply a cog in some eternal machine, ticking forward toward an unchangeable destiny?

But alongside it, the text presented counterarguments from philosophers who believed that the human mind operated outside these constraints. Free will wasn’t an illusion—it was the cornerstone of what it meant to truly exist. “We are not merely victims of causation,” the text read, “but rather participants in shaping our own futures.”

Placing the book aside, Eli let out a frustrated sigh. Why did neither perspective feel entirely true? If everything was determined, why did making a choice often feel agonizing, deliberate, intentional—so raw that it sometimes hurt? And if he truly possessed free will, why did so many of his actions seem to align with patterns he hadn’t even consciously noticed?

The answer wouldn’t come from a book, Eli decided. He needed something more. It was then he remembered the letter he had received weeks ago from Professor Adler. In it, the professor had invited him—not directly, but diplomatically—to join a small retreat hosted deep in the woods beyond the hills. It was a gathering for thinkers, Adler promised, an opportunity to engage with others who also wrestled with life’s deepest uncertainties.

At first, Eli had dismissed it. A retreat sounded indulgent, and his arguments with Adler in class had often left them on uneven terms. But now, faced with the wilderness of his mind, the idea of shared exploration and conversation didn’t sound so bad. Besides, what did he have to lose?

The trail leading to the retreat wound through dense forest, where sunlight streamed sparsely between the branches and the rustling of leaves formed an ever-present backdrop. By the time Eli arrived at the lodge, the air smelled of roasted coffee and pine resin, grounding him, if only slightly.

The participants were already engaged in conversation. There was Layla, a neuroscientist researching consciousness; Marco, a determinist who believed everything—from politics to personal taste—was written in the threads of existence; and Sofia, who argued passionately for human agency, maintaining that ethics would collapse if free will weren’t real.

The debates began almost immediately.

“There’s no evidence to support free will at the neural level,” Layla argued, her voice measured but firm. “Brain scans show decisions are initiated before people even become aware of them. If anything, free will is just a narrative we construct after the subconscious has already moved us toward an action.”

“And yet,” Sofia interjected sharply, “we still punish people for crimes and praise them for heroic acts. If blame or credit exists, doesn’t that imply choice? Otherwise, the justice system is a farce. Do we throw someone in prison because ‘the universe decided they had to commit murder’? That’s absurd.”

Marco leaned back in his chair, sipping his coffee slowly. “Justice isn’t about free will; it’s about maintaining order. Whether someone chooses their actions or not is irrelevant to society. Laws enforce stability, not ethical purity.”

Eli scribbled incoherent notes, unable to keep up with the swift exchange. More than anything, he felt overwhelmed. Each stance contradicted and complemented the others in strange ways. Were they debating the same subject, or were they arguing different levels of reality entirely?

As the hours turned to days, Eli realized the retreat wasn’t seeking answers—it was embracing the journey. Adler himself was often silent during the discussions but occasionally chimed in, adding cryptic observations about paradoxes. “Consider Schrödinger’s cat,” he mused one night by the fireplace. “The universe doesn’t force itself to pick one state or the other until observation occurs. Perhaps free will and determinism are similar—they coexist until you try to pin one side down. And maybe that act of ‘choosing’ is itself the observer influencing the outcome.”

Eli didn’t respond. Instead, his mind returned to his morning dilemma at the bakery. Had he ever chosen sourdough, or had the universe orchestrated the decision through a chain of causes stretching far before his arrival at the counter? Could Adler’s paradox hold the answer?

On the final day, as Eli prepared to leave the lodge, Adler approached him at the door.

“What did you discover?” Adler asked, his tone curious yet knowing.

Eli hesitated. “I don’t think I’ve found the truth. But maybe that’s not the point. Maybe we’re supposed to live as though we’re free, even if we aren’t, or as though we’re destined, even if we could choose otherwise. The question isn’t whether free will exists—it’s what happens when we act like it does.”

Adler smiled faintly, satisfied. “The journey through thought is far more illuminating than any destination you could reach,” he said. “Carry that with you.”

Eli nodded, stepping outside into the forest air. As the thin trail stretched before him, weaving into the unknown, he took a deep, deliberate step forward.
