The Sound of Music: A Summer Festival That Changed My Life


July found me sitting on the battered steps of our farmhouse porch, sweat trickling between my shoulder blades as the afternoon wind rattled the empty coffee can I’d set out for tips—a gag, mostly, for my dog Daisy, who looked as likely to toss coins as to master the banjo. Cicadas chewed at the silence. The fields buzzed with that inexplicable restlessness that comes before every big change, but I didn’t know it then.

It had been Dad’s idea. “There’s a music festival up in Haverly,” he said, returning from town with groceries and rumors. “They’re looking for volunteers. Nothing fancy—setting up tents, showing folks around. Some bands. You love music,” he’d added pointedly, glancing at the busted guitar propped by the door.

I shrugged. Seventeen, prickly, convinced life’s big stories only unfurled somewhere far from rows of corn and the relentless churn of the grain silo. But two days later, curiosity and the prospect of free concert tickets lured me into the backseat of Mom’s ancient Subaru, destination: The Summer Festival of Sound.

The festival grounds sprawled across a hillside that once grew apples, orchards returning to wild tangle. The main stage—a repurposed barn—rose like a ship’s prow, banners flapping. Stalls mushroomed across the grass, redolent of kettle corn and patchouli. The sound of tuning guitars and hammered dulcimers mixed with the sharper notes of teenagers testing cheap plastic recorders, the laughter of buskers playing for quarters.

At the check-in table, I met Mara: a freelance photographer from the city, wearing a battered straw hat, bootlace dangling rakishly from her wrist. She was in charge of us volunteers, a job she took to with the enthusiasm of someone who genuinely believed in magic.

“We’re here to make sure everyone remembers why music matters,” she said, pressing a walkie-talkie into my hand alongside a map. Mara rattled off instructions, her speech interwoven with half-finished stories: “If you run into Josie near the cider stand, ask about the mechanical owl. And if you see the old lady with green hair—trust me, you’ll know—give her one of these wristbands, she’s infamous for sneaking in.”

There was a rhythm to the chaos, like the first murmurings of a song before the melody finds its footing.

My first real task was helping set up for the string quartet workshop, under a striped tent by the lake. I carried folding chairs, cursing at jammed hinges, until my palms blistered. In the midst of this, I met Emmett: tall, balding, with fingers red and swollen from years of bass playing.

He smiled at my awkward attempts to fan myself with a program. “Music,” he said, “is the best way to survive summer. Take breaks. Drink water. In music and in life, don’t panic if you miss a note; there’s always the next phrase.”

It stuck with me all day, as I zipped between the main stage and the craft stalls. From sunup to sunfall, melodies from bluegrass bands and indie folk duos trailed after me. I watched families sway together in the grass, saw aging rockers teaching preschoolers how to clap on two and four, and met local legends with stories woven into every note.

I got lost in harmony—the sense of belonging, the tapestry of sound and humanity. It was the first time I understood music not just as escape, but as communion.

Backstage, after dusk, the festival shimmered with a different kind of energy. Lanterns cast pools of amber light. Volunteers gathered for lukewarm coffee, swapping tales of near-disaster: a sudden downpour during the jazz set, a runaway goat in the audience. People you only glimpsed during daylight became friends in darkness, their faces illuminated by the glow of stage lights and smartphones.

A hush rippled through the group when Mara announced, “Anyone who wants to join the midnight jam, follow me.” I hesitated. My battered guitar was in the car, the strings likely out of tune after months of neglect. But somehow, emboldened by the day, I ran to fetch it.

We gathered under the willow tree, a haphazard circle of music lovers: an eighty-year-old fiddler, a child pounding a cardboard box for rhythm, Mara strumming a borrowed mandolin. It didn’t matter if someone missed a chord or mumbled the lyrics. Each song swelled with unexpected harmony, mistakes leaping instantly into moments of levity or revelation.

I played quietly at first, letting others carry the melody. Then, in the hush between songs, Emmett nodded at me: “Got anything you want to try?” My fingers found the opening chords of one of Dad’s old favorites, hesitantly. The group joined in as if they’d always known the tune.

In that moment—half-strummed, urgent, imperfect—I heard my own voice joined by others, the music taking shape in ways I’d never planned.

The second morning, the festival’s rhythm shifted. I was up before dawn, standing barefoot in dew-soaked grass. A faint murmur of song drifted from the tent village: lullabies spilling from parents who couldn’t sleep, or late-night musicians spinning the final threads of a harmony begun hours before.

I watched as vendors brewed coffee, the air fragrant and sharp. The festival came alive: kite-makers flying their creations on the hill, children gathering for the “Find Your Instrument” parade, volunteers manning pancake stations with spatulas raised like conductors.

It was here I met Sofia, with her electric violin slung across her chest. She needed help fixing an amp. We worked side by side, soldering wires, our laughter blending easily. Sofia was sixteen, from three towns away, and ambitious in a way I’d never dared—she talked about touring, recording, composing scores for films not yet made.

“Music isn’t just what you hear,” she said, flipping her wild hair back from her face. “It’s what you feel in your ribs.” She dragged me to the songwriter’s workshop, where amateurs performed verses scribbled on napkins, their voices shaking but proud.

Inside that tent, the old boundaries of the world fell away. Timid dads, bold teens, grandparents with banjos—each performer made something ephemeral but real, spinning laughter and memory from thin air. Something in me cracked open, soft as a seed sprouting underground: the realization that someone like me could write, perform, be heard.

The festival rolled into its final day, a crescendo of sound. Mara wrangled us volunteers for a group photo, arms intertwined, mud smeared across shins and cheeks. The sky threatened rain, but nobody rushed. Performers gathered backstage, nerves fizzing, tuning up for the last big show.

I remember Emmett clapping me on the back, nodding toward the main stage: “You’re playing tonight.”

I laughed, swallowing nerves. “I’m not—”

He shook his head, his smile wide. “You are. You belong up there same as anyone.”

Mara grinned. “Everyone does one song. It’s tradition. Even people who think they can’t.”

My guts churned with adrenaline as twilight fell and the lights snapped on, illuminating the makeshift stage for the Grand Finale. A crowd pressed close to the barrier, faces gleaming in the half-light. There’s something about festival energy—everyone yearning for magic, believing it possible just this once.

When my turn came, I climbed the creaking steps, hearing the thud of my heart over the applause. My guitar felt awkward, my voice too thin in the crackling speakers. But then I recognized a handful of faces in the dark—Emmett, Mara, Sofia, Dad waving from the crowd, arms aloft.

I started slow, voice low and tentative, but the crowd filled in the notes I missed. By the chorus, I was nearly shouting, lost to everything but the joy that vibrated through me, the air electric with possibility.

When the song ended, the applause was thunderous. I couldn’t stop smiling. It was fierce and wild and brighter than any dream I’d ever had before.

Dawn found us dismantling tents and folding chairs, sleep-deprived and sore but reluctant to let the music end. Mara pressed postcards into my hand—photos she’d taken, moments I wouldn’t have noticed otherwise: me carrying a stack of chairs, Sofia helping a tiny child tuck a violin under her chin, Emmett’s laugh caught mid-song.

“You’ll want to remember this,” Mara said, hugging me tightly. “Festival magic doesn’t always happen twice.”

Back home, weeks later, the world seemed quieter, but something inside me hummed, a harmony I hadn’t noticed before. I found myself lingering over chords, scribbling lyrics in notebooks, searching for the community of melodies that had carried me through those days.

The festival didn’t offer answers or easy endings. Life was still hard, school came calling, and the farm’s needs kept me grounded in everyday reality. But I carried with me the rhythm of shared laughter and mistakes, the courage borrowed from friends and strangers alike, and the belief that music could crack the sky wide open—even in small towns, even for kids with nothing but a battered guitar and a pocketful of memories.

That summer changed the shape of my days, reminded me that magic is made, not found—and that once you’ve heard the sound of music rising from a field at sunset, you spend your life chasing echoes, song after song after song.
