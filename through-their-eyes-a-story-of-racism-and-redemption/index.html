Through Their Eyes: A Story of Racism and Redemption


Miguel adjusted the brim of his worn Yankees cap as he boarded the uptown train. It was a small shield against the unwelcome stares he had grown accustomed to since moving to Port Hollow two years ago. He didn’t need to glance around to confirm it; he could feel the weight of every gaze thickening the air in the cramped subway car. A life spent under observation, under suspicion—that was the reality Miguel knew all too well.

Port Hollow wasn’t like the Bronx. Nearly everyone here was white, middle-class, and comfortably numb to lives different from their own. It was a town of manicured lawns, PTA meetings, and an unspoken but glaring disconnect between the few neighborhoods like Cutter’s Alley—which Miguel called home—and the rest of the polished city. He’d come here with hope, looking for a fresh start after leaving the chaos of New York City behind. What he found was a quiet hostility dressed in polite smiles.

“Hey, bro!” A voice cut through Miguel’s thoughts. He turned to see Marcus, one of the few friends he’d made since moving here. Marcus was tall, wiry, and Black, with a permanent smirk that suggested he was two steps ahead in whatever game he was playing.

“Marcus,” Miguel greeted, offering a brief nod as his friend slid into the seat beside him.

“Bro, did you hear about what happened at Jefferson High?” Marcus leaned in, his voice low, but the energy in his words made it clear that this was juicy news.

“No.” Miguel stiffened. Jefferson High wasn’t just a school to him—it was his workplace. He worked part-time as a janitor there, cleaning hallways long after the classes had emptied and the fluorescent lights cast their tired glow over the walls.

Marcus looked around to make sure no one was listening, then whispered, “Some kid spray-painted something on the front doors last night. Racist stuff. The principals weren’t gonna say anything, but you know how social media works. Everyone’s talking about it now.”

Miguel exhaled sharply. “Racist stuff? Like what?”

Marcus grimaced. “Something about keeping Jefferson ‘clean.’ But you can guess what they meant.”

Miguel felt his stomach churn. The last few weeks at Jefferson had already been tense. A group of anonymous parents had started pushing the school board to expand its “security measures.” Miguel didn’t have to guess who they were referring to when they cited “outsiders” and “maintaining a safe environment for our kids.”

His thoughts were interrupted by the automated voice announcing their stop. Miguel stood, tugged his cap lower, and followed Marcus out onto the crowded platform.

The rest of the day at Jefferson felt like walking through quicksand. The staff was on edge, the whispers of students buzzing like invisible mosquitoes in the hallways. Miguel’s supervisor, a wiry man in his sixties named Mr. Thorne, called everyone into the faculty lounge near the end of their shift.

“This place is under enough scrutiny as it is,” Thorne began, running a hand through his thinning gray hair. “Whatever the vandals did last night, it’s just going to make the board breathe down our necks harder. So I need everyone on their toes. No mistakes.”

Miguel stood at the back of the room, arms crossed. He noticed Thorne didn’t once mention what the graffiti actually said. He suspected it wasn’t for discretion’s sake, but rather a discomfort with stating it aloud.

Later that night, Miguel found himself back in his tiny apartment. His usual routine would have dictated a quiet dinner, sorting through bills, and preparing for another day. But something about that morning’s events gnawed at him. He couldn’t just leave it alone.

He grabbed his coat and left, heading toward Jefferson High. The streets were mostly empty, the cold wind scraping against his face as he hurried along the sidewalks. When the school finally came into view, Miguel slowed his pace.

He approached the main entrance, where the custodial crew had scrubbed the graffiti earlier. The gray-painted steel doors looked pristine, but Miguel couldn’t help but picture the hateful words that had once covered them. He could almost hear the laughter of whoever did it. His hands clenched into fists at his sides.

“Miguel?”

The voice startled him, and he turned to see a girl standing at the street corner. She was petite, with wild curls tucked under a knitted beanie. It took him a second to recognize her as one of the students who hung out near the cafeteria after their shifts overlapped. Her name was Lena.

“What are you doing here?” Miguel asked, his voice harsher than he intended.

“I could ask you the same thing,” she shot back, eyeing him cautiously. Then she sighed and said, “I came to look, you know? At the doors.”

“Why?” Miguel studied her face, noticing the dark circles under her eyes. She looked exhausted.

“Because my friend Jada—a sophomore—she’s been a wreck all day. Her parents want to pull her out of Jefferson now. They said it’s clear this place isn’t safe for kids like her.” Lena lowered her gaze. “And I can’t say I blame them.”

Miguel didn’t know what to say. He leaned back against the brick wall, the weight of everything settling deeper in his chest.

“This isn’t new,” Lena continued. “It’s just that now it’s out in the open. People like me and Jada—we’ve been dealing with this since the day we stepped foot in this town.”

Miguel could relate. He’d felt the same tightrope tension every day since moving to Port Hollow. But hearing it from a teenager made it hit harder. What kind of world was this, where kids had to carry burdens they didn’t ask for?

Over the next few weeks, Miguel and Lena found themselves unlikely allies. She would report whispers and murmurs she overheard in between classes, and Miguel would relay what he picked up during his shifts. The implication was clear: whoever had defaced the school’s doors wasn’t just a lone actor. There was a quiet network in Port Hollow that harbored hostility toward those who didn’t fit their narrow mold.

What infuriated Miguel even more was how many chose to stay silent. Teachers in the breakroom made offhand comments about “keeping their heads down.” Administrators spoke of “trying not to rock the boat.” It seemed as though the status quo was the easiest path for those unaffected by its ugliness.

And yet, amidst the complacency and fear, Miguel noticed something else: resilience. He saw it in Lena, who refused to let her voice be silenced, and in kids like Jada, who still walked into Jefferson each day, shoulders squaring a little more under the weight they carried. He saw it in parents who attended town hall meetings ready to fight—not with fists or hateful words, but with the truth about what Port Hollow could and should become.

Their strength stirred something dormant in Miguel.

One evening, Lena texted him, her usual brevity laced with urgency: Come to the park. It’s starting.

Miguel didn’t need to ask what she meant. By the time he arrived, a small crowd had gathered near the fountain at the center of Oakwood Park. Parents, students, friends, neighbors—a collection of faces that Port Hollow rarely acknowledged in one gathering.

Someone handed Miguel a small candle, and soon the soft glow of flames surrounded him. They weren’t here to shout. They weren’t here to provoke. They were here to be seen, to stand together in defiance of hate.

When Miguel caught Lena’s eyes across the circle, she nodded as if to say, This is what it’s about.

He nodded back, finally understanding.

It didn’t happen overnight. Redemption rarely does. But bit by bit, Miguel saw things shift in Port Hollow. Conversations began to crack open spaces that had long been sealed by indifference. People started to listen instead of looking away. It wasn’t perfect. It wasn’t easy. But it was movement—and that, Miguel realized, was worth everything.

Sometimes, he still felt the stares. But now, he met them head-on, with the knowledge that change wasn’t just something they hoped for. It was something they were building, piece by piece, together.
