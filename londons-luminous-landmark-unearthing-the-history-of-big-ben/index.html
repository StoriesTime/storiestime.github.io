London's Luminous Landmark: Unearthing the History of Big Ben


In the heart of London, nestled between the towering structures of modernity, lies a timeless landmark, bigger than Ben. And yet, as I stand in front of it, my mind wanders, trying to unravel the mystery enclosed in its solid stone walls. This magnificent piece of architecture, now known as Big Ben, has a story far deeper and more intriguing than the grand façade it presents to the world. Unearthing its history is like tracking the heartbeat of London itself.

When I entered the grounds of the monumental structure, the first thing that strikes me is the striking silence. There is a sense of awe that emanates from each visitor here. As I stroll around the enclosure, the lone woman in the crowd, I realize that the silence is not out of respect or fear, but out of reverence. Big Ben is more than a mere tourist attraction; it is a living symbol of the city’s resilience and power, a testament to the ingenuity of its creators.

The story of Big Ben begins with the clock tower itself. There are several myths and legends that surround this structure, but the truth lies buried in the annals of history. Contrary to popular belief, the tower was not built specifically for a clock. It was initially built to house the bells for the neighboring Palace of Westminster, now more commonly known as the Houses of Parliament.

The clock tower had been a part of London’s skyline for over a century when the need for a larger and more accurate clock was felt. Theodolus Elands, a renowned horologist of the time, was appointed to the task of creating the clock. He designed a unique four-faced clock, each face being more magnificent than the last, with the largest being a whopping 23 feet in diameter.

The clock was a masterpiece, but the real challenge lay in getting it to work. The clock’s gears and machinery, after all, were forty feet in the air, making it one of the tallest mechanical structures of its time. But Elands was nothing if not patient, and he persevered, finally unveiling the magnificent creation in 1859. The tower, now retitled as Big Ben after the mythical giant who supposedly held it in place during its construction, was an instant favorite with the locals.

Over the years, the clock has undergone several renovations, with the most significant being in 1987, when a fire damaged the internal workings of the clock and threatened to halt its operation permanently. It took a decade to restore the tower to its original glory, the clock’s gears ticking in harmony once more.

Big Ben has played witness to two world wars, the structural devastation of London, and the myriad changes in the city’s political landscape. It stands as a constant reminder of London’s and mankind’s tenacity in the face of adversity.

In the temptation of laying bare the history of Big Ben, one cannot ignore the romantic legends surrounding it. According to popular lore, it was love that inspired the clock tower, with the story of the Baron who was spurned by a beautiful princess and vowed to create a clock tower as a testament to his love. While the tale is delightful, it is but a speck of dust compared to the greater story of a city’s resilience and a tower’s indomitable spirit.

As I peer up at the magnificent clock, I wonder at the stories it has witnessed - the hopes, the fears, the moments of victory, and defeat. It stands tall, an open book of the city’s past, a constant reminder of the human spirit’s enduring nature. Big Ben, the epitome of London’s luminous landmarks, shines on, undeterred by the passage of time. Its story goes beyond the stories it tells; it is a chronicle of civilization’s resilience, an ode to human creativity, and a silent sentinel that guards the city’s vibrant past.
