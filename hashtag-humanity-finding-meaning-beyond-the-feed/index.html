Hashtag Humanity: Finding Meaning Beyond the Feed


The hum of the city was sharper than usual, muted yet palpable as Erin strolled through the crowded streets of downtown Montréal. Her phone buzzed in her pocket, vying for her attention, but she let it simmer. She had been tethered to that glowing little rectangle for years—a portal to the endless digital feed that defined her existence. Social media likes, perfectly filtered snapshots, trending hashtags. All of it had consumed her, absorbed her energy and her sense of self. Today, however, was different—she was looking for something else. She was searching for meaning beyond the feed.

For years, Erin had meticulously curated her online persona—#EcoWarrior one day, #Wanderlust the next. Her Instagram following had ballooned to six figures, her Twitter threads sparked debates, and her TikTok was a carousel of engaging micro-narratives. She was celebrated, envied, recognized. Yet, a relentless emptiness thrived in the hollow spaces between her digital triumphs.

That emptiness was hard to pin down; it showed up in the quiet moments, in the silences between notifications. It whispered to her in the middle of the night, when she’d scroll for hours, chasing validation while neglecting the yearning deep in her chest. She had long ignored it, drowned it out with dopamine hits from likes and comments. But now, she was listening. That yearning had grown louder, crippling even. It was time to tune out the feed and tune into herself.

Erin ducked into a small café near the arts district, away from the main strip. The sign on the door read, Lieu Sans Wi-Fi. No Wi-Fi? That was a strange choice, almost reckless for a business in 2023. She hesitated, quickly pulling her phone out of her pocket to check for signal. Bars were weak. A low battery glared at her from the upper corner. She sighed and stepped inside.

The interior was unassuming—rustic wood tables, dull orange lamps casting a warm glow, shelves crammed with books, plants, ceramics, relics from a slower era. It wasn’t just the absence of Wi-Fi; it felt disconnected entirely from the tech-driven world. Erin noticed the patrons—reading, chatting, sketching, lost in their own spheres. No laptops open, no phones flickering under the tables. She chose the corner seat by the window, watching the drizzle outside as she slid her phone into her bag and purposefully ignored its incessant buzzing.

A waiter appeared like an apparition, tall with dark eyes, holding a pencil and notepad instead of a tablet—a rarity these days. Erin smiled half-heartedly and ordered coffee. She was about to ask for the Wi-Fi password reflexively, then stopped herself. It was absurd how ingrained the habit had become—how technology seemed to govern her movements, even when she wanted to push it away.

“Just coffee today?” the waiter asked, tilting his head slightly. Erin nodded, but then quirked an eyebrow and asked, “What’s the story with this place? No Wi-Fi?”

The man chuckled. “We’ve never had it here. Intentionally. The owner wanted a space where people could… reconnect. With themselves, with each other, with the world. It’s strange for some, liberating for others.”

Erin’s coffee arrived moments later, and she stirred it absentmindedly, unprepared for feelings she didn’t know how to process. Reconnection, huh? It sounded noble, romantic even. But it sparked a gnawing discomfort in her chest.

Once the coffee cooled, Erin took a hesitant sip, her gaze flitting between the rain outside and the people inside. A child sat in a booth with her mother, stacking colored blocks instead of watching cartoons on a tablet. A couple exchanged notes over their shared notebook, laughter escaping in bursts. And across from Erin, an older gentleman scribbled furiously into a leather-bound journal, pausing every now and then to chew the end of his pencil, lost in thought.

Erin’s fingers twitched instinctively, as if reaching for her phone, but today she left it alone. She uncrossed and recrossed her legs, then leaned forward and wondered how these strangers lived their lives away from the relentless deluge of social media. Did they feel lighter here? Freer? She was almost angry with herself for how tied she felt to the algorithm, to the digital tide that had carried her for so long.

Her gaze drifted to the little bookshelf mounted near the café’s rear wall. After finishing her coffee, she walked over, trailing her fingers along the spines—titles of philosophy, poetry, travel memoirs. Books felt alien, like relics from another era. The one that caught her attention was Digital Solitude: Finding Yourself in a Noisy World. She took it down and flipped through the pages.

The book described a quiet rebellion—a movement of people actively choosing to detach from constant connectivity in favor of a simpler life. The text was interspersed with stories of former social media stars, tech entrepreneurs, and digital influencers, all of whom had abandoned their feeds to rediscover long-lost pieces of humanity. There were anecdotes of nights spent under the stars without selfies capturing the moment, weeks spent traveling through hidden villages with no GPS app guiding the way. They were people like Erin—people who had realized their digital lives were full but their hearts were empty.

She turned to a chapter titled The Illusion of Authenticity. It explored how social media shapes modern identity—users presenting themselves as raw, real, and vulnerable but carefully staging those versions to win approval. Erin felt her stomach churn at the pages before her eyes. How much of what she posted was genuine? How many of her followers knew the truth about how hollow she often felt behind the screen?

Time slipped away unnoticed as Erin lost herself in the book until the waiter returned, asking softly if she needed anything else. She blinked, realizing an hour had passed. Her phone still sat silent in her bag—a strange and liberating absence. Erin shook her head, put the book back, and decided it was time to leave.

As she stepped outside, the rain had stopped, leaving the streets glistening in faint sunlight. Pedestrians hustled by, each of them holding onto their devices like lifelines. Erin didn’t reach for hers. Instead, she wandered aimlessly through the arts district, letting herself soak in the moment without looking for angles or hashtags to immortalize it. The world felt surprisingly tactile—bricks glistening, lavender on the breeze, muted laughter spilling from open windows.

She passed a small mural on the side of a building, vibrant strokes of paint exploding into the shape of tangled roots and blooming flowers. Beneath it, a phrase was scribbled: Grow beyond what confines you. She paused and stared at it. Confinement. It wasn’t the technology itself, was it? It was how she let it dictate her life, trap her in virtual loops while her real world remained untouched.

She smiled for the first time in weeks.

Two weeks later, Erin posted for the first time in over a month. It wasn’t a sponsored post or an edited selfie; it was a candid snapshot of the café, sunlight pooling faintly beneath the orange lamps. The caption said simply: Lieu Sans Wi-Fi. A small corner of humanity.

The likes were slower than usual—barely a trickle—and Erin didn’t care. For the first time in years, she felt free. She wasn’t chasing trending hashtags or clout anymore. She was chasing something infinitely more profound: her humanity.

As the days passed, Erin continued her digital detox, returning to the café often and finding herself savoring cafés, bookstores, parks—places where the feed couldn’t reach. Authenticity was no longer a chore; it was an everyday practice. She still posted sometimes, but not for applause. Her captions hinted at journeys of self-discovery and renewal. And her audience? It wasn’t the crowd she’d once cultivated; it was a smaller, quieter community of people who valued the story beyond the filter.

Erin was no longer bound by the scroll. She had found what she’d been searching for all along—her narrative, her truth, her meaning. And perhaps, something even greater than that.

Humanity.
