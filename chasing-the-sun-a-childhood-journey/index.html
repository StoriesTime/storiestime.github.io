Chasing the Sun: A Childhood Journey


There was something magical about the mornings in our small village, nestled at the foot of a sprawling mountain range. The sun, barely peeking over the horizon, cast a warm, golden glow across the dew-covered grass. It was a place where dreams began to stir the moment you opened your eyes, and the day promised endless possibilities. For me, and for my friends, the most exciting part of the day was the chase—a pursuit of the sun that had been ingrained in us ever since we were little.

I remember the first time I truly understood what it meant to chase the sun. I was just a child, barely seven, and I spent most of my days running across fields, climbing trees, and skipping stones across the pond. The sun was our guide, our beacon, our goal. Every morning, as it rose above the mountains, we would gather at the edge of the village, our feet restless with anticipation. We weren’t chasing the literal sun, of course. But there was something in the way it seemed to pull us, urging us to run faster, further, to reach it before it slipped out of reach.

It started innocently enough. One of the older kids, Tomás, who always had a mischievous gleam in his eyes, would suggest it. “Let’s see if we can catch it!” he’d say, his voice filled with excitement. And without a moment’s hesitation, the group of us—some of us barefoot, others with worn shoes—would begin running.

We’d race across the fields, our legs pumping, hearts pounding in sync with the rhythm of our footsteps. The sun, in all its beauty, would begin to climb higher, but we never stopped running. There was something captivating about how it stayed just out of reach. It was as though it was playing with us, toying with our youthful hearts, daring us to chase it to the ends of the earth.

It wasn’t long before we realized that the chase was more than just a race. It was a metaphor for our own lives, an allegory for our dreams, our hopes, our childhood ambitions. The sun, in its infinite rise, was the horizon of possibilities that awaited us. We were chasing something far bigger than ourselves, something that could never truly be caught, yet we tried anyway, day after day.

We ran through the changing seasons, the sun always setting a little earlier in the evening, pulling us toward the end of each day. In winter, the sun would barely rise above the horizon, but we still ran. In spring, the fields would burst with color, and the chase would take on a new energy as we leapt over puddles and avoided the mud. Summer was when the chase felt the most real, the sun shining down with a fierce brilliance that matched our energy.

Each year, as we grew older, the chase changed. We no longer ran with the reckless abandon of our youth, but we still ran. We began to notice things—the way the light shifted as the day progressed, the soft warmth on our skin, the cool breeze that would sweep across our faces. The sun, though still a distant goal, began to feel closer. We learned that the chase wasn’t about reaching it, but about how we moved along the way.

Tomás, who had always been the one to start the race, eventually became quiet. One day, he didn’t show up at the starting point. We waited for him, but he never came. The chase continued without him, but there was a hollow feeling in the air. No one mentioned it, but we all felt it—the absence of the one who had always led us.

As we grew older, life began to pull us in different directions. Some of us left the village to seek out the world beyond the mountains, others stayed behind, but the memory of the chase lingered. I often found myself thinking about those days—the carefree mornings when the sun beckoned us and we followed without hesitation. Even in my adult life, I could still feel the pull of that chase, a desire to reach for something just out of reach.

But as the years passed, I began to understand the real meaning of our childhood pursuit. The sun wasn’t a destination, but a journey. It wasn’t about catching it, but about chasing it. Life itself was a chase—chasing dreams, chasing love, chasing fulfillment. We spent our lives running, hoping to find something that would make us whole. The sun was our symbol of all those things we longed for, and though we could never truly possess it, it was there to remind us that the chase was worth it.

One summer evening, many years after Tomás had disappeared from our lives, I found myself standing at the edge of the same field where we had once started our race. The sun was setting, casting a brilliant orange glow across the sky. It felt like time had stopped, and for a moment, I was seven again, standing at the starting line, ready to run. But this time, there was no race. There was no need to reach the sun. I had already caught it.

In the stillness of that moment, I understood the lesson that had always been there. The journey had been the reward. The chase had never been about reaching the sun, but about embracing the journey, the experiences, the friendships, and the lessons learned along the way.

And as the sun dipped below the horizon, I smiled to myself. I had chased it, and in doing so, I had lived a life full of moments that would forever shine in my memory, just like the golden rays of that sun.
