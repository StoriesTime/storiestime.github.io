Travel Diaries: A Glimpse into Local Traditions and Festivals


Five faded journals and a patched cotton tote were all that remained from a decade of tracing continents. The journals began as crisp, neat Moleskines, but like their owner—me—they’d grown water-streaked and spice-scented through travel’s battering tides. They carried secrets folded into margins and, every so often, caught the scent of incense or mead wafting from ancient markets and village squares.

I didn’t start out planning to document local festivals and traditions. At first, I wanted to simply escape the humming monotony of my nine-to-five. That changed in Kerala, when I stumbled into the midst of the Thrissur Pooram.

The Thunder of Drums in Kerala

I arrived in Thrissur on a whim, following the scent of jasmine and the rumor of a celebration. Any sleep still clinging to my eyelids evaporated the moment I stepped from the train—the air vibrated with the distant pulse of chenda drums. The town was in metamorphosis; streets were draped in marigold garlands, and crowds pressed in from every alley, moving toward the Vadakkunnathan Temple, the heart of the festival.

This was not a tourist festival. This was something ancient and living. Each participant—barefoot mahouts in white dhotis, women with oil-slicked hair, children clutching sweet coconut ladoos—seemed born to this celebration, and I an uninvited observer, adopted by their mirth.

The Pooram is known for its grand elephant procession, and the elephants themselves, forty in total, paraded with gilded headdresses and peacock-feather fans. The percussive symphony of the Panchavadyam rose in waves, building toward what locals called “the moment the gods arrive.”

A part of me believed the gods might actually descend, drawn by the gravity of so much music, color, and devotion. Strangers pressed flowers into my hands; a grandmother painted my forehead with sandalwood paste. In that instant, the distance between observer and insider vanished. It became clear: every festival is less a spectacle to be witnessed, more an invitation to partake.

Lantern Light: Mid-Autumn in Hoi An

My journey next wound north, crossing the Indian Ocean and Vietnam’s lush spine, to Hoi An. Here, the Mid-Autumn Festival is known less for dragons and more for the moon’s silver gaze.

The ancient town shimmered with a lantern-lit glow. Every alley spilled light, reflecting in the lazy curves of the Thu Bon River. Vendors sold mooncakes—dense with lotus seed or salted egg yolk—wrapped in impossibly intricate paper. Local children paraded paper dragons, their faces shining with excitement and speckled with yellow lantern dust.

What I found captivating was not the revelry, but the rhythm of tradition. Grandparents doted on grandchildren, telling fairy tales of Chang’e and the moon rabbit as they peeled pomelos. Even the simplest acts, like lighting a lantern and letting it drift along the river, carried the weight of centuries past.

Midway through the night, I sat beside a family who insisted I share their mooncakes. Few words were exchanged, but between the shared sweets and soft laughter, an unspoken camaraderie grew. Through Hoi An’s festival, I learned that tradition isn’t only about performance—it’s about inviting others, even strangers, into the circle of belonging.

Fire, Salt, and Shadows in Andalucía

Spain drew me next, a tapestry of whitewashed villages and rolling olive groves. I arrived in Cádiz on the eve of Carnaval—the night air was slick with salt from the Atlantic, and anticipation seemed to ripple directly from sea to city walls.

Cádiz’s Carnaval is a storm of satire, a celebration where locals skewered politicians and sang comic coplas with ribald glee. I followed masked paraders through labyrinthine alleys, the night split with fireworks and laughter that tumbled off ancient stone balconies.

Yet what fascinated me most unfolded on the outskirts, in Alcalá de los Gazules, during the Fiesta de la Sal. Here the villagers built salt sculptures, shaped rough as the land. The salt, harvested from the old marshes, was more than mineral—it was a living memory of the Moors, of salt taxes and ancient trade routes. As bonfires dipped toward dawn, I sat with a shepherd’s family, watching salt shadows dance on the walls, tasting the region’s jamón and bitter orange zest.

They spoke of ancestors and harvest moons, of traditions that survived plagues and famine. In their stories swirled the realization that festivals persist by anchoring us—momentary lights against the wild drift of centuries.

Tulip Parades and Silent Remembrance in the Netherlands

From fierce Spanish revels, I traveled north to Lisse, the Netherlands, in the heart of tulip country. April here means the Bollenstreek Flower Parade—a kaleidoscope of color winding past sprawling fields of orange, pink, and gold.

Locals spent weeks weaving blooms into floats. I joined a team preparing a spectacular flower bike, hands sticky with sap and pollen. One farmer winked and shared that the parade began, humbly, as a post-war symbol of hope and renewal. The scent of hyacinths filled the air—floral and musky, both joy and memory intertwined.

Yet amid the jubilance, another tradition unfolded on May 4th: Dodenherdenking, the national Day of Remembrance. In Amsterdam’s Dam Square, the crowd stood two minutes in silent homage to those lost in World War II. The same hands that had woven tulips now held vigil candles—grief and celebration, all part of the cycle.

In the hush, I saw how cultural heritage isn’t just bright festival garments; it’s also the quiet, stoic moments, the holding together through darkness.

Kanda Matsuri: Tokyo’s Living Heritage

Finally, my travels brought me east, to Tokyo, for the legendary Kanda Matsuri. Every May, the Kanda Myojin Shrine erupts into a weeklong festival, as an endless train of mikoshi (portable shrines) surge through metropolitan streets.

For Tokyoites, this is more than a tribute to ancient deities—it’s a celebration of resilience, renewal and the embrace of continuous reinvention. I rented a yukata, sweating through the thick city air, and joined a local team as they hoisted the mikoshi. The crowd’s energy was contagious, punctuated by enthusiastic chants of “Wasshoi!”

Hundreds of thousands lined the route, but the excitement wasn’t limited to spectacle. Vendors hawked sweet dango and sizzling takoyaki; children blew bubbles between lines of food stalls. Old and young wore matching happi coats, their identities merging into one living mosaic.

I learned that festivals in Japan are a living bridge—connecting ancient customs to modern city life, honoring the persistence of ancestors while making space for joyful reinvention.

Tarantella in Southern Italy

Fall beckoned me south to Calabria, on Italy’s wild toe. Here, the Feast of San Rocco unfurls in August, riffs of tarantella music twisting through cobbled piazzas. Dancers, young and old, circled the church, their tarantella steps echoing the myth of the tarantula bite—a fever only the music could cure.

Families roasted peppers on street corners, garlic thick in the air. The blessing of animals, the flick of holy water, were followed by fireworks that stitched fire across the southern sky. I joined in the wild, whirling dance, strangers pulling me into their spinning circle. In that joyful chaos, I grasped the real magic of folk festivals—they welcome every heart, forging instant kinship through music and movement.

Reflections from the Road

Years passed. Each journey left me richer with secrets—recipes whispered in open-air markets, steps of a dance taught under lantern light, stories murmured by curio-sellers as rain pattered on tin roofs.

When I paged through my journals in quiet moments, I saw a different map emerge—not just of places, but of shared rituals. Each festival, from Kerala’s riotous Pooram to Tokyo’s stately Kanda Matsuri, revealed a universal need: to gather, to remember, to celebrate what makes us both unique and connected.

Traditions survive because they are retold, not as relics but as living dialogues—woven and rewoven, open to new faces, their invitations passed hand to hand through time. Festivities blend ancient roots with contemporaneous joy, showing that heritage thrives where people gather: in music, food, remembrance, and laughter.

And for every traveler, that is the richest discovery—finding, within unfamiliar rhythms, the pulse of humanity’s oldest song.
