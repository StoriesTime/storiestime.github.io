The Silent Divide: Voices Lost to Prejudice


The early morning fog had not yet lifted as Leena sat by her bedroom window, the cold glass pressing against her palm. From her small apartment on the edge of the city, she could see the faded silhouette of the tower blocks across the street. Her eyes were tired, the kind of tiredness that seeped deep into your bones, especially after years of being misunderstood, sidelined, and overlooked.

The world outside her window had never seemed more distant, yet it was all too familiar. It was the kind of place where people tried to fit into boxes—boxes that made it easier to ignore the complexities of others. Leena had spent most of her life in one of those boxes, her voice muffled by the invisible walls of prejudice.

She had always known that the world did not view her the same way it viewed others. As a young girl, the looks were subtle but unmistakable—the quick glances, the half-formed smiles that didn’t quite meet the eyes. Her family had come from a different place, a different culture. Her mother had taught her to never apologize for who she was, yet society seemed determined to make her do just that.

Leena had learned early on that silence could sometimes be the safest choice. But as she grew older, the silence became harder to endure. The world outside had been built for others—those whose names and faces didn’t make people pause and wonder. Leena had always felt like an outsider, and that feeling only deepened as she stepped into adulthood.

The divide, however, was not just between people of different backgrounds. It was also between those who had the privilege to speak, and those who had their voices stolen from them, whether by design or by accident. For Leena, this divide was not only about race or ethnicity—it was about the assumption that people like her didn’t have anything valuable to say.

Her career had begun in the most unassuming way. After graduating with a degree in urban planning, she had taken an entry-level position at a small firm. She was eager, hopeful, and determined to prove herself. But despite her qualifications and passion, she had quickly learned that her presence in meetings, her ideas and suggestions, were often overlooked.

The first time it happened, she had been sitting in a meeting with a group of senior colleagues. They had been discussing the development of a new park in a neighborhood that had been rapidly gentrifying. Leena had suggested that the park should include spaces for the long-standing community members, particularly the elderly who had lived there for decades. She had explained that their needs were often forgotten in the rush to create spaces that appealed to newer, more affluent residents.

One of the senior managers had nodded, but when he spoke, it was as if she hadn’t said anything at all. He had instead recommended a playground for children, a safe, comfortable suggestion that aligned with the needs of the newcomers.

Leena felt her heart sink, but she forced herself to sit there quietly, the words still burning on her tongue. As the meeting continued, her thoughts began to blur, overtaken by the overwhelming silence in the room—the silence that followed every time she tried to contribute.

This was not the first time. Nor would it be the last. In the years that followed, Leena had learned to navigate this silence, adapting, adjusting her tone, her ideas, her presence. It became second nature to shrink herself, to make herself smaller so as not to take up too much space in a room where her voice felt out of place.

But the years of shrinking took their toll. Leena became increasingly disillusioned with the systems that perpetuated this divide. The urban planning industry, with all its talk of inclusivity and community, had its own set of unwritten rules, rules that prioritized the voices of those in power and minimized the perspectives of those like her. It wasn’t just about race or gender—it was about the subtle, systemic silencing of those who were deemed less important, less deserving of a platform.

And yet, Leena knew something had to change. She couldn’t continue to live in a world where she was expected to remain silent. It wasn’t just about her—it was about the countless others whose voices had been erased, whose stories had been ignored. There was a quiet strength in her determination, a strength that had been building for years, even if she had never fully recognized it until now.

Her breaking point came one summer afternoon. The firm she worked for had been commissioned to design a public space in a neighborhood that had been significantly impacted by a recent wave of displacement. As usual, the focus of the discussion was on the needs of those who had moved in—young professionals, tech workers, and families who could afford the newly renovated condos. But when Leena spoke up again, this time with more conviction, it was different.

She spoke not just about the elderly, or the long-time residents, but about the deep cultural erasure that was happening in the name of progress. She spoke about the people who had lived there for generations, whose lives and histories were being erased in favor of more modern, marketable ideals.

The room grew quiet as she spoke, but this time, the silence was different. It wasn’t the usual dismissive quiet, but something else—something that suggested she had hit a nerve. The senior managers looked at her with expressions she couldn’t quite read. She felt exposed, vulnerable, but she also felt something else—something that had been buried beneath the layers of silence for so long.

For the first time, her voice had been heard.

But that moment of recognition was fleeting. The changes she had proposed were eventually dismissed, and the design was altered to suit the vision of the wealthier newcomers. Yet, Leena didn’t walk away defeated. She realized something important that day: the real work wasn’t about changing the minds of a few decision-makers—it was about amplifying the voices of those who had been silenced for so long.

In the following months, she began to meet with community groups, grassroots organizations, and activists who were working to make a real impact. Together, they started pushing for more inclusive planning practices, ones that took into account the needs of all residents, not just the privileged few.

Leena’s efforts were not without resistance. There were those who wanted to keep things as they were, to maintain the status quo where the powerful had a louder say than the marginalized. But as Leena’s network grew, so did her resolve. She was no longer willing to be a passive participant in the system—she was determined to make sure that the voices lost to prejudice would no longer remain unheard.

In the years that followed, Leena became a prominent advocate for inclusive urban planning. She worked tirelessly, but she never forgot the quiet moments—the moments when she had been dismissed, overlooked, and silenced. She used those memories as fuel, reminding herself of why her work mattered, and why the work of amplifying lost voices was so essential.

The divide was not easily bridged, but Leena’s persistence began to create ripples. More people started listening, more voices began to be heard. It wasn’t a perfect solution, and there were many battles still to fight, but Leena knew that the real power was in the stories—the untold stories of those who had lived on the margins, whose voices had been silenced for too long.

She had learned something profound along the way: silence, though it had once been her survival mechanism, was no longer an option. Her voice, and the voices of those who had been ignored, would no longer remain lost to prejudice. The divide could be healed, one voice at a time.
