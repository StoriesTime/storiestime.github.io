Living Likes: The Impact of Social Media on Daily Life


Evelyn scrolled through her phone as the morning sunlight lazily spilled into the cramped apartment. Another morning, another breakfast selfie posted, another anxious wait for the digital nod of approval—likes and comments. She was a twenty-eight-year-old freelance graphic designer in a bustling city, juggling deadlines and the urge to freeze moments of her life in filtered perfection.

She glanced at her notifications; twenty new likes on her latest post. It sparked an immediate easing of the knot inside her chest. Oddly enough, this virtual applause had become a small but vital fuel each day. It was a rhythm, a pulse, a way to measure belonging in an increasingly isolated world.

The Currency of Approval

When Evelyn first joined social media, it was a casual endeavor—a tool to connect with friends and share small victories. But over the years, likes turned into a mirror reflecting her worth in a way no mirror ever could. Her daily routine, from what she ate to how she dressed, was subconsciously influenced by what would perform well online.

This transformation was subtle. At first, she posted photos of her projects, her sketches, occasionally a candid smile. Gradually, the posts became more curated—soft lighting, carefully arranged objects, sometimes even staged scenes. The goal wasn’t just to share anymore; it was to impress, to capture fleeting moments of validation.

Her friends noticed the shift. Emma, a close companion since college, remarked over coffee one day, “You seem more like your Instagram feed than yourself sometimes.”

Evelyn laughed it off, but the comment lingered.

The Social Feed as a Lens

In the city’s crowded cafes and subway cars, screens shone brighter than the faces around them. The social feed was the new window to the world—a stream weaving personal narratives into the global fabric of emotions, achievements, and aesthetics. This digital life, however, was a double-edged sword.

Evelyn saw how people around her were caught in a delicate dance—showing the ideal, masking the real. The pressure to respond instantly to messages, the anxiety of not posting enough, the addictive pull of refreshing the feed for something new, something engaging.

Her own nights ended not with sleep but with an endless scroll, as if searching for some unstated answer buried in the curated lives of others.

Real Connections or Digital Shadows?

One afternoon, Evelyn met with Marcus, an artist she admired who had blown up on social media for his unfiltered, raw posts. His feed was starkly different—a messy studio, unpainted canvases, failures shared without apology.

“I don’t chase likes,” Marcus said, stroking his beard thoughtfully. “I post because it’s part of my process, my journey. Likes? They’re just noise.”

Evelyn envied that freedom.

That meeting planted a seed. She began experimenting—posting a photo of her chaotic workspace, sharing a poem about self-doubt, responding honestly when someone asked how she was doing instead of parroting the usual “good.” The likes dipped but the comments grew richer, deeper.

It was her own little revolution against the tyranny of the curated self.

The Psychological Weight

Psychologists have long studied the impact of social media on mental health, and Evelyn was feeling it daily. The constant comparison to idealized lives bred insecurity and envy. Her sense of self became fragmented between the real and the digital.

Some days, she felt triumphant at a post’s success; others, crushed by silence or criticism. She struggled to keep sight of her identity beyond the screen and the dopamine hit of virtual praise.

She started reading books on mindfulness, setting strict usage limits on her devices, and practicing presence in the offline world. It was hard, especially in a culture that equated online engagement with social capital.

Changing How We Live

Social media had reshaped daily life in countless ways. It affected how people communicated, how they saw themselves and others, even how culture itself evolved.

Evelyn noticed how news, trends, and movements exploded online, creating global communities but also echo chambers. Activism morphed into hashtags, and art into pixels.

Yet, the human craving for connection remained unchanged beneath the digital veneer.

She saw friends celebrating achievements online but retreating into loneliness offline. She saw parents struggling to set boundaries for children born into this always-online era. She saw relationships flourish or crumble tethered to screens.

Finding Balance in a Digital Age

For Evelyn, the journey was ongoing. Slowly, she reclaimed parts of her day from social media’s grasp—morning walks without her phone, coffee dates without scrolling, journaling instead of tweeting.

Her work flourished when she worked for the joy of creation rather than social approval. She reconnected with passions lost to obsessive posting and chasing trends.

In moments of calm, she allowed herself to just be—not a persona to be liked or shared, but a person to be lived.

A New Perspective

Living likes had taught Evelyn many things: the potency of human connection, the fragility of self-worth, and the challenge of authenticity in an amplified world.

Social media was not a villain nor a savior; it was a mirror reflecting the complexities of modern life—its pursuit of belonging, beauty, and meaning.

Evelyn understood the impact it had wielded on her daily routine and outlook, but she also saw the possibilities: to use the platform with intention, to foster real bonds rather than digital facades, and to live fully both online and unplugged.

As the sun dipped below the skyline and her notifications softly chimed, she smiled—not because of the likes—but because she was finally living on her terms.

Living likes had changed her life, but they no longer ruled it.
