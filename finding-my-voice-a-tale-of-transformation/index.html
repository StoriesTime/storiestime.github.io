Finding My Voice: A Tale of Transformation


I still remember the day I lost my voice. It was as if a switch had been flipped, and suddenly I was no longer able to express myself in the way I had always known. The silence was deafening, a constant reminder of my inability to communicate. I had been a singer, a dancer, a writer - I had found my voice in many aspects of my life. But it wasn't until I lost my ability to sing that I realized how much my voice truly meant to me. It was as if I had lost a part of myself. I tried to sing again, but my voice was hoarse and barely audible. I sounded like a wounded animal, as if I was trying to howl at the moon but couldn't quite hit the right pitch. I was devastated. My friends and family tried to be supportive, but I could tell they were struggling to understand what I was going through. They would ask me to whisper, write down what I wanted to say, or even text them. But it wasn't the same. It wasn't voice. I visited specialists, trying to figure out what was wrong. Ears, nose, and throat exams revealed nothing out of the ordinary. The doctor told me I was fine, but my body told a different story. I felt like I was living in a nightmare. Days turned into weeks, and the silence became suffocating. I couldn't bear the thought of interacting with people without my voice. I felt like a ghost, invisible and insignificant. I started questioning who I was without my voice. Was I still the same person? I decided to look inward for answers. I started journaling, pouring my emotions onto paper. Writing was a new way for me to express myself, but it felt foreign. The words didn't come as naturally as they had when I was singing. I sounded like a stranger, writing in the third person as if I was observing myself from afar. My journal became my sanctuary. I wrote about my feelings, my fears, and my hopes. I delved into the reasons why I loved singing and the emotions it stirred within me. I shed tears and laughed, knowing that my words were safe in that notebook. One day, I stumbled upon a community of people who were recovering from vocal cord injuries or damages. They had turned to alternative forms of expression, like spoken word, poetry, and acting. I realized that my loss of voice wasn't a disability, but an opportunity for growth. I began to explore the world of spoken word. I found solace in the rhythmic flow of words, the way they danced on my tongue when I spoke. I started attending open mic nights, listening to stories, thoughts, and experiences shared by strangers who understood the power of voice. I soon discovered I could find my voice in other forms. Writing expressed my thoughts beautifully, but spoken word touched my heart in ways I never thought possible. It traveled far, deep and strong ringing beyond my pages of writing and entering every soul I could touch. Embracing the idea that my loss had opened doors to a new form of expression allowed me to discover aspects of myself I had never known. I found that voice wasn't just about singing; it was about the power to convey thought and emotion, regardless of the format. It's been months since I last sang, but I've rediscovered my voice. It's in my spoken word performances, in my written words, and in the memories of my loved ones. It's in the feelings I evoke in others and the sparks that fly from my interactions. My voice is gone, but I've found my voice. It's not a replacement of the past, but an embracing of the new found voice that I was always capable of holding and speaking with a new silence and courage than ever before.
