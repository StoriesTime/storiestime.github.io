Existential Enigma: A Walk Through the Park with the Absurdist


On a dim, wind-swept evening at the old City Park, I met a man who went by the nom de plume Absurdist. You won’t find him listed in the phone book or on social media; he’s a creature of fables and word-of-mouth whispers, gaining notoriety in the hushed, bemused hum of midnight gossip. Said to brave the velvet night merely in his pajamas and slippers, he would ‘walk’ through the park, dispensing theories on the absurdity of life itself.

I found him one chilly October night under the spectral silhouettes of oaks, their branch-arms outstretched like overhead thermals. The somber sky was as bleak as a washed-out slate, the bare branches wonderfully resonant with whispers of a bygone season.

He eyed me, observing, gauging, and sighed. His eyes were pools reflecting the yellow smolder of a passing streetlamp. For an oxymoron, he was striking—as warm and as mutable as the very wind itself. His laughter echoed in the park with the twang of an antique piano, warming me like spirits of another age.

“Mister Absurdist?” I queried.

“Ah!” He raised an eyebrow, surveying me like some gastronomic critique considering a tenderloin of nouvelle cuisine. “You honor me,” he replied, inclining thoughtfully. He looked beyond my visage, into my heart, into memories and dreams I hardly recall or dared pin down.

“Now then, let’s dispense with the introduction.” He looked at me sideways, like Puck concocting mischief. “You know what life is, but do you know existence?” he asked.

“I think so,” I confessed, feeling just a tremor of unease.

“Here, I say, is where humans trip and fall! Existence—what a quandary! What a perplexing puzzle! What an… existential enigma!”

A hundred moons spun around us as his ruminations spiraled in every direction, tracking the roots of life and the labyrinthine wells of our very perceptions. At every turn, there was cause for doubt: uncertainty with a capital ‘U’. It seemed that no matter where we tread or how often we tried to find a semblance of the familiar in the unfamiliar, that mysterious chasm yawned in the background and grinned.

“But,” he mused, pausing on the edge of a slippery slope, “there is a silver lining. We must own the absurd to understand it to our own salvation.”

It was apropos that we stood on the precipice of the park’s bandstand, a rustically charming site with a painted circle, where once dances occurred and romances were spun. The once-bright paint had chipped and flaked, flecks of peeling pastel colors on which starlight played ghoulish games.

“Enough philosophizing!” Absurdist declared. And here, he revealed the essence of his peculiar charm: his infectious laughter; his child-like capacity to find whimsy nestled amongst the posies; and his headlong leap into the embrace of that most dour companion, absurdity.

We sauntered through the skeletal, silent trees, till we were greeted by the park brook. A gentle ripple in stone-bed, its burble sounded like a wistful whisper. “Watch this,” Absurdist whispered, satisfied.

The park seemed to shrink at his feet. Bending and stretching, rippling the view with his aura’s soft embrace, Absurdist slowly turned, hands raised, fingers straight. He touched the air as if plucking invisible strings of the universe in a vast performance.

I gasped as he gently nudged the air. Scenes flickered past: a ballet swathed in moonlight; children chasing imaginary fireflies; lovers sharing secrets on a rug of shadows; a solemn funeral processional passing beneath a somber sky, slick with tears yet mysteriously beautiful.

The laugh rumbled through him. “Ah, the absurdity of life! How exquisite!”

Then, he embarked another narrative on existence. This time it was richer, juicier—fuller of life and its latent mysteries. Yet every expression, every tale dissolved in the face of the next, the next dancing beside that vast, lunatic ballet of existence: absurdity.

As we left the park, I managed to sneak him a curious glance. This enigma of an individual was more than just a midnight philosopher or marauder of shadows—he was existence itself, exquisitely and uniquely absurd. I did not need to ask why he chose such a unique way of life. Then, it occurred to me, we each have our ways of navigating the labyrinth of life; each our own approach to resolve that enduring existential enigma.

In our whirlwind walk, he had taught me more than the absurdity of existence—he had reaffirmed the mystery of life. For life, in all its strange, humorous, and beautiful moments, was and always will be our walking, dancing, laughing, crying, loving, and constantly evolving interpretation of existence.

Soon, it was time to leave the park, the enigmatic Absurdist, and the hauntingly beautiful existence that swirled together within its hallowed ground. I knew as I closed the door on the park’s fading light, there was another adventure that awaited me—another matrix of absurdity that would ripple and shape us all. The park, that magical-realistic stage of melancholy and mirth, had offered one truth—that in our life-long saga, we are merely the puppeteers; existence, its perplexing dynamics, a vast, living, breathing puppet show.
