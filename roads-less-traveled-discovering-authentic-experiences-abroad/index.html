Roads Less Traveled: Discovering Authentic Experiences Abroad


Nestled against the lush backdrop of terraced hills in northern Vietnam, the tiny village of Pá Thẻn was not found on any itinerary or list of “hidden gems.” Alexandra had ended up there entirely by accident. Her planned route had included the scenic Ha Giang Loop, famed for its winding mountain roads and breathtaking vistas. But when her motorbike sputtered to an unexpected halt halfway through the journey, a dusty path leading into Pá Thẻn beckoned her toward an adventure she hadn’t planned.

The village revealed itself like a whisper. Straw-roofed houses emerged from the dense greenery. Smoke curled lazily from chimneys, mingling with the scent of burning wood and damp earth. Children, their laughter echoing like wind chimes, ran barefoot along the dirt paths. This was not the Vietnam everyone talked about—not the bustling Hanoi with its dizzying energy, nor the tourist-filled Halong Bay with its fleet of cruise ships. This was something else entirely.

Alexandra’s presence was greeted with mild curiosity. She wasn’t the first foreigner to stumble upon Pá Thẻn, but outsiders rarely arrived without intent or invitation. It was the village elder who finally walked up to her, his weathered face framed by a wide-brimmed hat. Speaking a clipped but intriguing blend of Vietnamese and the local dialect, he motioned her toward the rickety wooden bench outside his home.

Over shared tea—an earthy brew that warmed her to the core—Alexandra began piecing together fragments of the story this village offered. Pá Thẻn was home to an ethnic minority group, known for their vibrant weaving traditions and ancient shamanistic practices. The elder pointed to women just outside, sitting in the shade of an enormous, twisted banyan tree, their hands deftly working looms. Colors unfolded before Alexandra: crimson threads, indigo bands, and golden trims. The designs seemed to tell stories—of thunderous storms, mythical beasts, and forests that stretched beyond the horizon.

Alexandra left her bike in Pá Thẻn, deciding to explore the village and its outskirts on foot. The elder’s grandson, a boy of about twelve, led her to a secluded waterfall that sparkled like a hidden gem under the midday sun. Climbing higher, they reached a bamboo grove where Alexandra first came across painted stones scattered across the ground. The boy explained as best as he could—these were offerings for the spirits that watched over the forest.

That evening, the village prepared for a ritual. Though Alexandra had hoped to stay discreet, she was invited to join the ceremony. A large fire was lit in the village square, its glow chasing shadows off the ancient wooden shaman drum. Villagers danced and sang, their voices rising and falling like waves. The shaman, dressed in intricate robes, began to chant. His voice was raw and haunting, evoking feelings Alexandra could barely understand. She stood apart, her heart pounding to the rhythm of a culture she’d never known.

It was in Pá Thẻn, amidst unfamiliar faces and customs, that Alexandra felt the weight of authenticity. She had come to Vietnam seeking adventure—but here, she had found a connection.

Weeks later, Alexandra traded the misty hills of Pá Thẻn for the maze-like alleys of Fez, Morocco. With its ancient medina, bustling souks, and labyrinthine streets, it seemed the opposite of what she’d experienced in Vietnam—chaotic and surreal. Yet her intent remained: to find the heart of a place by venturing off the beaten path.

She quickly realized that every local offered their own perspective on what defined the city. A carpet merchant in a dimly lit stall swore that history lived in his handwoven tapestries. A spice seller argued that the soul of Fez was in its flavors, its saffron, cumin, and rare ras el hanout blend.

It was a kind-eyed woman selling bread who suggested otherwise. She spoke little English but scribbled an address on a scrap of paper, pressing it into Alexandra’s hand with a smile. Guided by curiosity, Alexandra found herself climbing a steep set of stairs to what appeared to be little more than a rooftop overlooking the medina.

There, under the shelter of red canvas awnings, a weather-beaten man gently strung the chords of an oud, a traditional Moroccan lute. The melody was raw and sorrowful, yet delicate, painting emotions for which no words existed. Alexandra sat in silence, captivated. Without words, the man offered her thick mint tea steeped in sugar, the air heavy with the aroma of orange blossoms.

He rarely performed for tourists, she learned later. But he’d been compelled not by money, but by a shared understanding—a traveler seeking meaning, and an artist trying to convey it.

Her next destination was deeper still into uncharted territory: the highlands of Ethiopia. She ventured into the Simien Mountains with a guide whose father had once tended goats in the same valleys they traversed. Days were spent hiking rugged trails that spun tales of geological drama, while evenings were spent in villages nestled like pearls between jagged peaks.

It was in one such village, far from the paved roads, that Alexandra found herself part of a family feast. In celebration of Meskel—a festival unknown to her but sacred to the hosts—her hands were sprinkled with water and dipped into a communal platter of injera with tibs, rich stewed meat accented by equal parts spice and hospitality. Children clung to her arms, coaxing laughter from her exhaustion. Conversations flowed in fragmented words, patched together by smiles.

The journey stretched onward, and with each step deeper into these unfamiliar worlds, Alexandra felt herself change. Her need for orchestrated experiences, for the guidebook-defined “see and do,” fell away. Here, in these seemingly forgotten corners of the globe, she was forging something rarer—a bond with the land, the people, and their unfiltered realities.

Alexandra returned home months later, her luggage lighter than she’d started with and her soul profoundly heavier. She had crossed paths with those who had never dreamed of leaving their villages, yet possessed universes to share. Her memories were marked not by selfies or souvenirs, but by woven scarves gifted in gratitude, by melodies too poignant to forget, by unspoken connections forged over shared meals.

It wasn’t always comfortable. She had navigated broken languages, circumvented cultural misunderstandings, and grown familiar with the pangs of doubt when days felt isolating. Yet, she realized, the authentic experiences—the pulse of true discovery—lay in those very moments.

In her living room, Alexandra sat down with a notebook, flipping back through the stories she had scrawled—snippets of laughter and sorrow, myths and songs, landscapes and faces. She hoped to share them with others, not as tales of exotic places, but as calls to action: to step away from the grand avenues and into the winding paths where the rhythm of life beats just a little quieter.

For it was there, in those roads less traveled, that she’d truly discovered the world.
