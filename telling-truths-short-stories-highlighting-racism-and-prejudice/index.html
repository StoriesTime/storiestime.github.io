Telling Truths: Short Stories Highlighting Racism and Prejudice


Maya stood at the entrance of the old bookstore clutching a small stack of books she had borrowed from the library. The air was thick with the scent of aged paper and dust, the kind that wrapped itself around you like a warm blanket, promising stories waiting to be discovered. The bell above the door jingled softly as she stepped inside.

“Good afternoon, Maya,” Mrs. Turner greeted her from behind the counter, eyes twinkling above her spectacles.

“Hi, Mrs. Turner. I’m here to return these,” Maya said, placing the books neatly on the counter.

Mrs. Turner smiled but then her expression shifted, subtle but unmistakable. Her eyes flicked to Maya’s dark skin, a hesitation that wasn’t there for others.

“Thank you, dear.” The words were polite but clipped.

Maya noticed and felt a familiar heaviness settle in her chest. It was as if the store, which should have been a sanctuary, had drawn invisible lines marking where she belonged and where she didn’t.

Outside, the town buzzed with the usual rhythms of small-town life, but beneath the surface, prejudices ran deep. Maya had lived here all her life yet carried the constant reminder that to some, she was always an outsider.

At school, things were no different. She’d raised her hand countless times only to be overlooked. Teachers often mispronounced names with careless apathy, but with her, it felt more pointed. Once, a classmate whispered loudly enough for others to hear, “She probably didn’t get those grades fairly.”

Each slight was like a small puncture to her spirit, invisible wounds carved from assumptions and ignorance. What hurt most wasn’t just the words but the truth they implied—that no matter how hard she worked, the world had already judged her.

One afternoon, sitting beneath the sprawling branches of the old maple in the schoolyard, she confided in her best friend, Lucas.

“Why does it feel like people see me first as ‘different’ and only then as Maya?” she asked quietly.

Lucas shrugged, looking uncomfortable. “I don’t know. Maybe because they’re scared or… don’t understand.”

Maya looked out at the playground where groups formed and reformed, alliances drawn along invisible lines.

“But isn’t it weird? We’re all just… people. Why does it have to be about color?”

Lucas nodded but didn’t reply. He was white, from the town’s majority families. To him, her experience felt distant, like a story he read but never lived.

One summer evening, Maya’s mother invited their neighbors—a mix of longtime residents and newcomers—over for a barbecue. The air was alive with laughter, clinking glasses, and the smell of grilled food.

Halfway through, a conversation erupted near the patio.

“Well, it’s just that some people don’t belong here. They don’t share our values,” said Mr. Hargrove, a man respected for his community involvement but older and stubborn in his views.

Maya’s mother exchanged a glance with her husband, then finally spoke up.

“And who decides where someone belongs?” she asked gently. “Isn’t it richer when we learn from one another? When differences are bridges, not barriers?”

Mr. Hargrove scoffed but was silent. The atmosphere thickened with unspoken tension.

Later, Maya helped collect plates and glasses, the earlier words echoing in her mind. The idea of belonging had always felt elusive to her, like chasing a shadow.

School started again, and Maya poured herself into a new project for English class—a collection of short stories inspired by the people and experiences around her. She wanted to tell truths; not the sanitized versions, but the hard, messy realities of living with prejudice.

Her first story was about Jamal, a boy in her neighborhood who stopped playing soccer because older kids called him names. Another was about Mrs. Nguyen, the quiet librarian who faced subtle exclusion because of her accent. Through these stories, Maya found a voice she hadn’t known she possessed.

When she read her collection aloud in class, some students shifted uncomfortably, while others listened in silence. For the first time, many faced the realities they had ignored or dismissed.

One afternoon, as Maya walked home, she noticed a colorful mural being painted on the side of the community center. Curious, she stopped, watching the artist—a young woman with a camera and paintbrush.

“What’s this about?” Maya asked.

“It’s a piece about our town’s true colors—celebrating diversity and confronting the prejudice,” the artist said, smiling.

Maya felt a flicker of hope.

“Can I help?” she asked.

Together, they painted images of people from all backgrounds, their faces vibrant and full of life. The mural spoke of unity, struggle, and the dreams of a community willing to see itself more clearly.

As days turned into weeks, Maya noticed subtle shifts. Mrs. Turner greeted her with more warmth. At school, a few students who once avoided her now walked alongside her. The mural became a meeting point where conversations grew, sometimes rocky but honest.

Maya understood that dismantling prejudice was not swift nor easy. It demanded that people confront uncomfortable truths and tell their stories, even when those truths challenged long-held beliefs.

And as she watched the sun set behind the painted faces on the wall, she knew that each story told was like a seed—small, potent, and with the promise to change the world, one mind at a time.

Epilogue

Maya’s collection of stories eventually reached beyond her town, inspiring other young writers and communities to share their own realities. Through words, empathy grew, and barriers started to crumble.

Truth, Maya learned, was not just about revealing pain but about lighting the path toward understanding. In telling truths about racism and prejudice, she found a way not just to survive, but to thrive—and to invite others to do the same.
