Behind the Mask: Short Stories of Complex Antagonists


In a city forgotten by time, nestled between jagged mountains and endless seas, there was a town known for its rare and precious objects: the Glass Merchant. Everyone knew of the merchant who could make mirrors that captured not only reflections but also the soul. His name was Karras.

Karras wasn’t born into wealth or privilege. His family, like so many in the town, had to fight for survival, scavenging for remnants of forgotten worlds. But Karras had always been different. From a young age, he had an uncanny ability to see through people. While others saw only the surface, Karras could peel back the layers of a person’s mind, uncovering desires, fears, regrets, and secrets. This gift, however, was a curse.

He didn’t like people, and they didn’t like him. His gift alienated him from others, and he grew to distrust the world around him. It wasn’t that he was cruel—he simply couldn’t trust anyone. Over the years, Karras developed an obsession with creating mirrors, something that could hold the essence of what he saw in others.

The first mirror he created wasn’t perfect, but it worked. He’d studied glassblowing under a master craftsman, learned the alchemy of light and shadow, and with meticulous patience, he created a mirror that would reflect not just the body but the true nature of the soul. It was a mirror that showed people what they feared most about themselves.

Soon, his creations became coveted treasures. People would come from all corners of the world, desperate to buy his mirrors. However, they were never the same after seeing themselves in his creations. Some were driven mad by what they saw; others locked themselves away, too afraid to face the truth. The town began to whisper, “The Glass Merchant sees you for who you truly are.”

But Karras wasn’t immune to the power of his own creations. The more mirrors he made, the more he saw of himself, too. In the reflection, he found the man he had become: cold, calculating, distant, and alone. Yet, unlike those who bought his mirrors, he couldn’t walk away. He was drawn deeper into the labyrinth of his own soul, seeing things that he didn’t want to face but couldn’t escape.

His most coveted mirror—the one he called “The Mask”—was unlike anything he had ever created. This mirror didn’t show a person’s soul. It showed their darkest desire, the thing they wanted most but feared most. Karras had never sold it; it was his secret. He kept it in a room locked away from the world.

One day, a man named Elian came to town. Elian was a wanderer, a poet, and a storyteller. He sought Karras out, desperate to find the man who could see into the hearts of others. Elian had heard rumors of the merchant’s extraordinary mirrors and believed that by gazing into one, he would unlock the key to his own life’s meaning.

“I’ve heard tales of your mirrors,” Elian said as he stood in the doorway of Karras’s workshop. “They say they can show the truth, the essence of a person’s soul. I must see it for myself.”

Karras studied him, noting the gleam in his eyes. This man wasn’t like the others. There was a hunger in him, a thirst for understanding that Karras found both unsettling and intriguing. He could sense Elian’s deep inner conflict, something that had been buried beneath the surface. “You think you want to see the truth,” Karras replied, his voice low. “But can you handle it?”

Elian smiled. “I must know, or I’ll never be at peace.”

Karras led him to the room with the Mask, placing the mirror in front of him. The surface shimmered, almost alive with energy. “Look into it, then. But know this: once you see, there’s no going back.”

Elian hesitated for only a moment before he gazed into the mirror. What he saw shook him to his core. He saw himself—an image he recognized, but the reflection was distorted, torn between light and shadow. In the mirror, he was not a poet, but a man of power and destruction. He saw the faces of those he had wronged, those he had abandoned in his pursuit of fame and glory. But even worse, he saw a darkness within himself, a desire for control that he had never acknowledged. The hunger for power, to mold the world into his image, was undeniable.

He recoiled from the mirror, shaking. “I—”

“You see now, don’t you?” Karras’s voice was cold, like the chill of the mountain winds. “What do you fear most about yourself?”

Elian staggered back, unable to find the words. He wanted to deny what he saw, to pretend it was a trick. But deep inside, he knew the truth. This mirror had revealed the man he might become if he let his desires consume him. It was the mask he wore, and it was suffocating him.

Karras watched silently, a flicker of something—regret, perhaps—crossing his face. He had seen that same darkness in the eyes of every person who had looked into his mirrors. What did it mean, to reveal the truth? Could anyone ever truly accept what they saw?

In the end, Elian left the town, never to return. Some say he disappeared into the mountains, others claim he found redemption. But Karras never knew for sure. What he did know, however, was that even the greatest of his mirrors could not reflect the true essence of a man’s soul. The truth was too complex, too tangled to be captured in glass. And so, the Glass Merchant of mirrors remained a man who wore his own mask, hiding behind the reflections he had created, forever searching for something he could never truly find.

Story 2: The Warden of the Ashen Gates

The Ashen Gates stood as the only barrier between the living world and the world of shadows, a realm where the forgotten lingered in eternal twilight. The gates were ancient, their iron bars twisted and worn by centuries of storms and decay. And at the heart of it all was the Warden, a figure whose true name was lost to history.

Once, the Warden had been a man, a protector of the realm. His purpose had been to guard the gates, to ensure that no evil could cross over into the world of the living. But over the centuries, the Warden had changed. The duty that had once been a noble cause had slowly become a prison of his own making.

The Warden had not always been this way. In his youth, he had been a soldier, a warrior who fought for honor and glory. He had been a man of conviction, ready to defend his land against any enemy. But during one of his battles, a great darkness had descended upon the kingdom. The enemy had been unlike any the Warden had faced before—an army of shadow, creatures born from the very fears and regrets of the people.

The Warden fought bravely, but his forces were overwhelmed. In his final moments, as the shadows closed in around him, he made a desperate bargain. He would guard the Ashen Gates for all eternity, ensuring that no more darkness could enter the world, if only he could live long enough to keep the gates closed. His soul would be bound to the gates, his fate intertwined with their endless watch.

And so, he became the Warden, trapped in the realm of shadows, a keeper of the gates, never to leave. But as the centuries wore on, the Warden grew disillusioned. He no longer saw his duty as a noble sacrifice, but as a punishment. He had been betrayed, not by the world, but by his own sense of righteousness.

The Warden began to see the shadows differently. What if the darkness wasn’t evil? What if it was merely misunderstood? He started to question the very foundations of his duty. Were the creatures beyond the gates truly a threat? Or had he been so consumed by his own sense of duty that he had never considered another perspective?

One day, a woman named Lyra came to the gates. She was a scholar, a seeker of lost knowledge, and she had heard tales of the Ashen Gates. Intrigued by the mystery, she traveled to the far reaches of the world to find them. When she arrived, she saw the Warden standing silently before her, his eyes cold and distant.

“I seek knowledge,” she said. “I’ve heard that the Ashen Gates are the key to understanding the nature of the world. Will you let me pass?”

The Warden studied her, his gaze piercing. He could sense something about her—something different from the countless others who had come before. “What is it that you seek, traveler?” he asked, his voice hollow.

“Truth,” Lyra replied. “I wish to know what lies beyond the gates.”

The Warden hesitated. He had not let anyone pass through the gates in centuries. But something in Lyra’s eyes, a quiet determination, made him wonder if perhaps she was the answer to his own internal conflict.

“You would risk your life for the truth?” the Warden asked.

Lyra nodded. “I would.”

For the first time in years, the Warden felt a flicker of something—hope, perhaps. He stepped aside, and the gates creaked open. Lyra passed through, vanishing into the darkness beyond.

As the gates closed behind her, the Warden stood alone, contemplating the decision he had made. What had driven him to release her? Was it hope for redemption, or had he simply grown tired of the endless watch? The shadows whispered in the distance, but for the first time in centuries, the Warden didn’t feel as though he had to fight them.

And so, the gates remained open, the boundary between the living and the dead no longer absolute. The Warden had made his choice, and in doing so, he had embraced the very darkness he had once sworn to protect the world from.
