Rethinking Human Nature: Perspectives from the Great Philosophers


Once upon a time, in the heart of a charming village nestled between mountains and seas, people chatted about life. Their discussions took various angles, from the trivial and mundane to the deep and philosophical. Amongst those conversations, one young man eagerly inquired about what the greatest philosophers thought about human nature.

“Stoicism teaches that the way to find peace of mind is to understand that some things are not under our control,” his father explained to him before his curiosity overwhelmed their conversation. “However, the complexities of human behavior, as argued by Nietzsche, cannot be fully understood by a single approach.”

Accordingly, word of this curiosity found its way to a wise teacher in town who reveled in the sharing wisdom. He opened up his academy and gathered various sages from across the land, each known for their expertise on the teachings of key philosophers.

The young man spent his days fervently attending their lectures, absorbing knowledge about the nature of humanity through their diverse perspectives. Among the philosophers, he found Plato particularly captivating. For Plato, human nature was an unstable force, subject to corruption, and needed control. He proposed a society designed for the equal distribution of knowledge and virtue. The young man couldn’t help but wonder if such a system could truly liberate the human spirit.

Meanwhile, Aristotle proposed the spirit of human nature was not to acquire knowledge but to live harmoniously as a member of society. To him, human beings are social creatures, and their full development could only be found through involvement in the world around them. The young man found himself pondering: How can a person be fulfilled and still give back to society?

From Confucius, he learned of the intrinsic morality inherent in human beings. He argued that if a person were to follow their innate moral compass, the vast majority of society would follow suit. Entire societies could be founded on harmony and mutual respect. But, he wondered, is it human nature to always do what is right or are we influenced by our surroundings?

Content from the lessons, the young man decided to create a society on his model - an experiment to test the various perspectives that he had absorbed from the great philosophers. He gathered a group of curious individuals, each eagerly ready to partake in a new vision of life. They planted the seeds of Bacchic enlightenment, embracing the fruitful wisdom of the philosophers.

First, they issued a system founded on Plato’s principles. All members were to be taught the same virtue and knowledge to ensure no one was left in corruption’s shadow. As the reality of the system manifested itself, some individuals rebelled against the constraints of their society. Could this be an inevitable part of human nature? The young man now wondered.

Then, they employed Aristotle’s approach. They engaged with community, put efforts into enhancing the happiness within - and the well-being of their people. Still, some individuals thrived on mediocrity, leaving a disparity in their ranks. Perhaps not everyone was destined for harmonious prosperity?

Finally, they focused on Confucius’ wisdom by creating a society promoting morality. However, as the students grew older, some succumbed to temptations and stray from the set path. Human desires are complex and varied, it seemed, leaving the young man to question if inherent morality was truly attainable.

The young man, now knowledgeable and seasoned in his pursuits, pensive and contemplative, stood on the crossroads of wisdom, wondering how the philosophers’ teachings could shine light on the diverse nature of the human spirit. While each philosopher proposed a unique perspective on human nature, it was evident that nothing could contain or explain the intricate fabrics of human desire entirely. This complexity posed a challenge to the idealistic belief of one single approach to controlling human nature.

The community carried on, embracing the wisdom of their predecessors while understanding the significance of self-discovery and self-actualization. A synthesis of these teachings shaped their lives moving forward, each individual weaving their unique path to find balance within the complexities of human existence.

And so, the legacy of their quest for the true understanding of human nature lived on, sparking discourse and teaching generations to come. The wisdom of the great philosophers became an essential part of the village’s heritage. The participants grew to understand that each person, in their own way, could bend and shape their human nature based on the wisdom they inherited.

From that day forth, the village became a repository of knowledge, an ark of philosophers’ best thinking about human nature. The wisdom they left behind became an essential part of the village’s heritage, passed down from generation to generation. And so, their journey marked not only the evolution of human understanding but also the beauty of embracing one’s true nature.

In the end, the young man reflected on the simplicity of the question and the complexity of the answer. The wisest course of action was not to force human nature into one ‘perfect’ outlook but to embrace the infinite possibilities of self-discovery and understanding.
