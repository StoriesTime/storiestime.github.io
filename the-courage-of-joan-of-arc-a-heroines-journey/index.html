The Courage of Joan of Arc: A Heroine's Journey


Joan knelt in the soft loam of the early morning, her hands trembling, chilled by dew clinging to the grass and the crisp air of her small village of Domrémy. The thatched roofs of the cottages behind her lay half-hidden in the thick fog, their chimneys puffing faint, ghostly signals into the gray sky. It was 1428, and the hundred years’ war battered the fractured French countryside like an illness that refused to heal. Yet, for this moment, the village was still, as if the world itself paused in anticipation.

Joan fixed her eyes on the horizon, lips moving in silent prayer, seeking the confidence to face the voices that had changed her fate. She remembered the day—sunlight streaming through the cedar trees, motes of dust swirling—and the bright, commanding voice that broke through the quiet. “Daughter of France, stand.” From that first call, Joan believed her life was no longer hers alone, but a thread in the vast tapestry of her homeland.

As the morning fog lifted, revealing hints of sunlight, Joan rose, brushing off her skirt with shaking hands. There was work to be done: tending fields, wrangling livestock, helping her father haul wood. But all the while, a flame grew inside her—a mixture of awe, conviction, and fear. The visions returned, saints emerging from the shadows; she could almost smell the faint scent of roses that attended their visits, hear the echo of clarion armor striking stone. “Go to the dauphin,” they urged. “Find him. Restore France.”

No one believed her at first. Her father pleaded for her to stop the stories, to remain safe at home. The village priest frowned, uncertain, torn between superstition and faith. Yet, word spread across Domrémy, weaving into the air like mist: Joan, the shepherd’s daughter, claimed voices from heaven called her to lead armies and crown a king. This was a time of desperation, after all. The English held swathes of French land, and a great blackness lay across the people, stifling hope. Perhaps the strangest thing of all was that some people chose to believe.

Joan made her way to Vaucouleurs, feet blistering, determined on the muddy road. She convinced Robert de Baudricourt, the garrison commander, not with force, but with her eyes, her words strong and unswerving. “Let me pass. I am sent to the dauphin.” She spoke as if she already wore armor, as if invisible angels flanked her as she walked. By the time she finally stood before Charles, the dauphin of France, rumors of her courage—her implacable will—had grown until they eclipsed her humble birth. But nothing had prepared her for the grandeur of Chinon, the painted ceilings, the swirl of courtiers shrouded in suspicion.

Charles tested her, hiding among his courtiers, waiting for her to falter. He had tired of omens, mystics, charlatans—each one leaving hope further battered. But Joan strode into the hall, ignoring laughter and whispers, and knelt before Charles, calling him king, swearing to save France. She revealed secrets only he would know, words exchanged behind closed doors, his own private prayers. The room fell silent.

Within days, the churchmen debated. Some eyed her with suspicion, others with awe. Was she mad, a witch, a saint? Yet, none could doubt the utter force of her conviction, or her knowledge of distant battles and hidden matters. The warehouse of faith, locked tight by years of betrayal and war, creaked open. And so, at seventeen, Joan was handed a sword, fitted with breastplate and banner, and rode out beneath the fleur-de-lis.

The army became silent when she approached. Hardened men, scarred by defeat, looked at the girl in shining armor with something approaching reverence. She did not boast or threaten. She prayed with them. She rode among them at dawn, kneeling in mud and snow, speaking softly to one and all. “O gentle France,” she murmured before battle, “let me be your shield.”

The enemy mocked her at first—‘the Maid,’ they called her, painting her as a peasant lunatic. At Orléans, besieged and battered for months, the English jeered so loudly that their taunts echoed over the ramparts. Joan stared back coolly, not with contempt, but the cold fire of someone who has weighed their fear and found it wanting.

Storming the Tourelles, the heart of the English fortifications, Joan led from the front. The arrows hissed past, slashing at her banner. She felt a sting, a sudden shock as an arrow pierced her shoulder, hot blood soaking her sleeve. She staggered, the world swirling in red and gray, almost collapsing. Yet, she pressed on, lifting her standard, shouting encouragement as the soldiers surged forward. The English lines broke. Orléans was freed.

That single moment—Joan wounded, returning to battle, refusing defeat—burned itself into legend. The soldiers did not simply see a commander; they saw hope embodied, resilience shining from ordinary flesh. Belief became a contagion; the mere sight of her banner made men run faster, fight harder. French victories followed, city after city surrendering without bloodshed. Rumors grew wild: Joan could not be killed, Joan saw the future, Joan was the sword of God.

Still, the grandeur of victory brought fresh burdens. Crowds pressed around her, blessing her, kissing the hem of her armor. The court, uneasy with her power, watched her with wary mistrust—and envy. Cardinals whispered of exorcisms; generals grumbled at orders from a peasant girl. Charles, whose throne she fought to secure, drifted apart, his advisers urging caution, diplomacy, delay.

Yet Joan pressed onward. She marched to Reims, heartland of the French soul, leading Charles through battle and celebration to the ancient cathedral, where kings were anointed amidst trumpet and incense. As the oil was poured on his head, as the gold crown settled on his brow, Joan’s eyes filled with tears. This was the vision promised, the thread she had followed across war and suffering. France was reborn.

But the tide of luck changed as quickly as it had risen. Paris, still English-held, resisted her assault, its walls bristling with archers and cannons. Joan was wounded again, this time unable to break the city’s defenses. Retreat followed, rumors darkening like storm clouds. Her visions grew clouded, filled with warnings and sorrow. Still, she pressed on, fighting for Compiègne—one last stand.

It was there, fulfilling a desperate sortie, that Joan found herself cut off, walls behind her, the gate slammed shut by fearful allies. English troops swarmed. In the muddy spring fields, covered in sweat and armor, she was seized and dragged away. There would be no rescue, no last-minute miracle. News of her capture swept through France like a shadow dropped across the land.

Imprisoned in a damp tower, chained and watched by English guards, Joan faced her trial. She was accused of heresy, witchcraft, wearing men’s clothing. Priests and lawyers questioned, threatened, cajoled. She stood alone—a peasant girl, outmatched, unlettered, all power stripped from her but one: her defiant truth. At night, prayers drifted from under her breath, quiet as wind through cathedral columns. She never recanted.

In May 1431, on the market square of Rouen, Joan climbed the scaffold with faltering steps, chains clinking. The crowd pressed in, eyes filled with awe and fear. The pyre burned. As the flames reached her, Joan cried out, “Jesus!” Her voice rang across the square, echoing long after smoke hid her from view.

Joan’s death sent ripples through France and England alike. She became myth—saint to some, traitor to others. Her courage, impossible to explain or contain, inspired generations. Decades later, her name was cleared, Venice and Rome recognizing what war and suspicion had tried to erase. Long after banners had faded and armies dispersed, Joan of Arc’s flame endured, illuminating the tangled line between faith, defiance, and the price of heroism.

From a simple village under the shadow of conflict to a firelit martyrdom and transfiguration into legend, Joan’s journey was one shaped not by destiny alone, but the unbreakable courage she carried within: the courage to stand, to speak, to fight not only for her king or her country, but for the very possibility of hope. Her life, brief yet insurmountable, remains a testament to the power of conviction—even when the world stands armed against it.

The soldiers of France marched on. The land healed slowly, as all wounds do. Yet Joan’s story, told in village hearths and royal palaces alike, lived on—whispered by fireside, repeated in sermons, celebrated in the songs of distant provinces. Each retelling sharpened the memory of her courage: the peasant girl in armor, riding at dawn, a banner streaming bright as any sun.
