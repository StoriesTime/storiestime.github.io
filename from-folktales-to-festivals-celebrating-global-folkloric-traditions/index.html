From Folktales to Festivals: Celebrating Global Folkloric Traditions


The rhythmic hum of a sitar echoed through the narrow streets of Udaipur as Mehreen Patel adjusted the flower garland strung across her market stall. The annual Mewar Festival was in full swing—an event steeped in centuries-old traditions, where folklore came to life in vibrant colors, music, and dance.

Mehreen adored this time of year. Her grandmother’s stories, which once drifted across the candlelit room of her childhood home, now danced across the lakeside city in the form of elaborate processions, puppet shows, and ornate depictions of the legend of Shiva and Parvati. As a storyteller and a historian, Mehreen found solace in the extraordinary way humanity had, across generations and continents, woven cultural narratives into community celebrations.

But Mehreen’s story was not unique to Udaipur. Similar festivals unfolded across the world, each one a tapestry of folktales, myths, and legends made fresh and alive in the hearts of their people. Mehreen had spent years traveling to understand these rich narratives and the festivals that kept them alive. For her, festivals weren’t merely events; they were archives of the human spirit, unraveling hidden truths about our ancestors and their values. Standing by her stall now, she allowed herself to recount the journeys that had shaped her.

A Fable Carved from Ice: The Harbin Ice and Snow Festival

Mehreen’s travels had taken her far from Rajasthan, to the icebound landscapes of Harbin, China. Each winter, the Songhua River transformed into a glittering metropolis of ice sculptures, lit in a kaleidoscope of colors. Beneath the frosted towers, carved palaces, and frozen dragons, she discovered the origins of the Harbin Ice and Snow Festival—rooted in an old tale of resilience.

According to the legend, the winter festival paid homage to farmers who braved endless snowstorms to save their harvests by lighting ice lanterns. These lanterns illuminated not just their surroundings but also their unwavering determination in adversity. Over time, these lanterns became symbols of hope and ingenuity.

Walking through Harbin, Mehreen felt an intimate connection between the folktale and the festival itself. She realized the festival was not simply a tourist attraction; it was a torchbearer for the region’s enduring legacy. The trails of light slicing through the dark, frigid nights, carved out from ancestral narratives, awoke a profound respect within her.

The Carnival Whispers of Venice

Not long after Harbin, Mehreen found herself wandering the labyrinthine streets of Venice during its annual Carnival. Here, whispers of folktales hid like secrets behind every mask. Traditional stories of romance, betrayal, and the illusions of grandeur played out in vibrant displays. Decadent costumes, elaborate masks, and spirited dances hid the deep truths of Venetian culture within their intricacies.

Paolo, a local craftsman, became Mehreen’s guide as she explored the history underpinning the festivities. He explained that many of the masks were inspired by Italian folktales, such as Il Dottore (the Doctor), Arlecchino (Harlequin), and other characters from Commedia dell’Arte. These masked figures carried with them not just entertainment but moral tales, exploring human folly, love, and ambition.

“The Carnival is a reminder,” Paolo once said, adjusting a sparkling mask in his shop, “that life is theater, and we wear many faces. The folktales that inspire us for Carnival are echoes of our existence, our many roles.”

As Mehreen stood on the Bridge of Sighs that evening, surrounded by music and the glimmering reflections of costumed revelers on the canal, she marveled at the profound layers of meaning that folklore gave to such festivities.

The Sacred Dances of Bali

In Bali, Indonesia, Mehreen’s quest brought her to the spirit-laden rites of Nyepi, the Day of Silence. On this day, a Hindu belief in cosmic balance was brought to life, bridging ancient myths and modern devotion. The island pulsed with energy during the days leading up to the silent observance. Giant Ogoh-Ogoh effigies, representing demons and evil spirits, paraded through the streets in an extravagant switch between chaos and calm.

As the festival unfolded, Mehreen heard an old storyteller recount the tale of Bhoma—the Balinese guardian of the earth—who is believed to cleanse the land of negativity. The Ogoh-Ogoh, towering figures of art and imagination, represented this cosmic fight between good and evil.

On the night of the parade, the air was thick with incense and drums. Children blew conch shells while dancers, adorned in vivid costumes, recreated ancient battles from folklore. And yet, as midnight struck, silence descended. Mehreen lay awake in the stillness of Bali’s starry night, understanding finally that tales of balance—passed down through generations—weren’t just heard but enacted through every movement, every performance.

Voices of the North in Sámi Joik

Deep in the Arctic Circle, Mehreen journeyed to Sápmi, the traditional territory of the Sámi people in Northern Scandinavia. Here, amidst snow-covered mountains and dancing northern lights, she encountered a festival unlike any other. It wasn’t adorned with processions or decorated streets, but with the hauntingly beautiful tradition of Joik.

A Joik is a form of traditional Sámi singing, not a retelling of a story but a living embodiment of one. Mehreen learned that through a Joik, a person, place, or even spirit was conjured in melodic form. Sámi festivals became platforms for such storytelling, where echoes of reindeer herders and the wisdom of ancestors flowed freely.

Elder Kari Nutti told Mehreen a tale about the sun goddess Beiwe, believed to have emerged from Sámi folklore. During the festivals, the Sámi people sang songs to Beiwe, thanking her for ending the long winter. As voices lifted in harmony with the wind, Mehreen felt a rare stillness in her heart, as though she herself had become part of the sacred lore.

A Celebration of Continuity

Year after year, after her journeys came to an end, Mehreen would return to Udaipur for the Mewar Festival. She often thought about the streets of Harbin, the canals of Venice, the effigies in Bali, and the songs of the Sámi. In each place, she had found fragments of a truth so universal and yet so profoundly unique. Stories passed from parent to child, preserved in song and light, had birthed festivals that became the very fabric of community.

And so, Mehreen stood at her stall, surrounded by lanterns and garlands, watching as dancers began another performance on the festival stage. She smiled, knowing that this was more than festivity—it was history, identity, and connection. It was humanity’s ongoing conversation with its myths and dreams, made immortal through the joy of celebration.

For Mehreen, every festival was an invitation to rediscover the beauty of the human story—a reminder that wherever she traveled, she need only follow the sound of the drum or the glimmer of firelight to find the universal rhythm where folklore became life itself.
