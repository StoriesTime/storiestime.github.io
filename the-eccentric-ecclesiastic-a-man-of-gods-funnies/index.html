The Eccentric Ecclesiastic: A Man of God's Funnies


In the quaint, leafy town of Pobbleville, nestled between rolling hills and whispering pines, there lived an unusual man of the cloth. Father Edmund Finley was known for his otherworldly sermons, profound wisdom, and — more importantly, for the eccentrics in his moral lessons.

Aged, leathery, with twinkling eyes like emeralds hidden in a forest, Edmund was no ordinary clergyman. His fondness for societal anomalies and peculiar musings inspired his congregation to look beyond the paint-by-numbers conceptions of life. More often than not, his congregation would leave his sermons as smiling, repentant testament to Father Edmund’s unique rallying cry for beauty and the oddities found in God’s grand puzzling; life’s great quilt.

Father Edmund’s exceptional character, however, lay in his unwavering devotion to communicate these insights as dramatically as possible. Every storytelling effort of his embraced what seemed to be an unlikely menagerie of allegories; cats chased by dogs, singin’ ships, and children flying kites in the storms.

Edmund’s exceptional narrative style often provoked confusion and bewilderment amongst his congregation.* Nonetheless, his diligent, dramatic endeavors unfailingly anchored people’s faith in God’s whimsical woodworking that seemed as unstoppable as the changing seasons.

A renowned Divine Word’s tale enveloped by vivacity and eccentric penchant: once, Father Edmund led his congregation to the river’s bank for Lent, during which he passionately raved about Jonah and the whale. In his own words, the congregation sat captivated, as Edmund’s imaginative rendition unveiled an enormous wooden orca – its gaping maw wide, jaws grinding like a stone mill – on the muddy edge of the river.

“Dear men, women, and children of Pobbleville, hold me as I dive into the terrible abyss brought to life before our very souls.” Father Edmund’s leathered hand gripped the pulpit. He launched into the river, vanishing in the beast’s gaping mouth.

Cloaked in exuberance and genuine love, the Father re-emerged, triumphant, uninspected and unharmed, as the wooden orca split open at sea – a sculpted vessel in God’s gallery of creation that bore the tale of Christ’s sacrifice – a sonorous reminder of mankind’s salvation.

Father Edmund became a local legend, fostering a lively, personalized worship experience that flowed as vividly as the river’s waters. The Eccentric Ecclesiastic, as the townspeople lovingly began to nickname him, spun sermons that were as colorful as his untidy beard. From bulls on a hill to the Girl with the Golden Ticket, Edmund always managed to leave his flock to feel the warmth of the hand prodding and guiding them through the unknown yet familiar storyline of their eternal life.

As their affectionate shepherd, Edmund preached the sacred beauty of God’s sacred puzzle – life – and encouraged his congregation to explore the bizarre, uplifting intricacies to discover their unique place within His design.

Through his lessons, Edmund challenged the people of Pobbleville to shy the trite formulas and rules that cramp individual expression or the passions they felt to be true; after all, there would be no sparks from peculiar ideas and ambition to forge stories worth unearthing.

So, nestled between the hills whispering ancient tales and the pines breathing life, Father Edmund’s legacy and legacy of The Eccentric Ecclesiastic long endured, even after his physical frame was cast into the eternity that stretched before him like the colorful quilt of life’s melodic fabric, innocence and divinity, seeking to make each of his charges players of their distinct narratives amongst the chiaroscuro of love and faith.
