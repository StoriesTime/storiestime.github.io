Faces of Prejudice: Short Stories Revealing Hidden Racism


The elegant dining room of “Le Bord de l’Étoile” was nothing short of exquisite. Crystal chandeliers cast soft light over linen-clad tables, polished silverware glinting under their glow. The air was thick with the mingled scents of herbs, roasted meats, and rich sauces. Marissa, a young graphic designer with Caribbean roots, had dreamed of this night—a celebration of her promotion. She sat poised, her fingers brushing the stem of a crystal wine glass, eager to savor the moment with her boyfriend, Thomas.

Across from her, Thomas, a man of well-established lineage and white privilege, chatted amicably with family friends. The laughter and easy smiles around the room built a warm bubble insulated from the world outside. Yet, beneath that buoyant surface, subtle currents moved unseen.

As Marissa glanced around, she caught a few furtive looks—furtive because she was the only person of color in the room. When she smiled at someone, their eyes quickly darted away, as if startled. When she offered toasting glasses, hands withdrew ever so slightly. Her laughter, vibrant and genuine, was met mostly with polite nods rather than reciprocal warmth.

During the main course, she overheard hushed conversations touching on ’the others’—always in a tone tinged with discomfort, sometimes outright disdain. Thomas seemed oblivious. He spoke about work politics, career goals, future plans, but never once acknowledged Marissa’s discomfort.

Later, outside the restaurant, Marissa’s voice wavered as she shared her feelings. “It’s as if I’m here, but not really seen. Like I’m a shadow in a bright room.”

Thomas frowned. “You’re overthinking it. They’re just formal, this is their world.”

“But don’t you see? Their world isn’t ready to embrace mine. They don’t even try.”

Marissa’s story revealed a face of hidden racism: the polite exclusion, the unspoken othering in elite social circles, where color is a silent barrier cloaked in civility.

Story 2: Overqualified

Khalil sat across the sterile, fluorescent-lit room, clutching a folder of meticulously prepared documents. He was a software engineer with ten years of experience, an advanced degree, and a portfolio bursting with successful projects. Yet, as this was his fifth interview that month, his confidence waned.

“So, Khalil…” began the interviewer, a middle-aged white man with a rehearsed smile. “Your experience is impressive, but I’m curious—why did you leave your last position after just one year?”

Khalil pressed his lips together. The real answer was complex: company politics, cultural insensitivity, microaggressions that built a daily dossier of alienation. Instead, he said, “I was looking for new challenges, a place where I could grow.”

The conversation trudged on. Subtle hints emerged. “We’re a close-knit team, so fitting in is crucial.” The phrase, innocuous on the surface, was a gentle gatekeeper. Despite his credentials, Khalil wasn’t the ‘right fit.’

After the interview, Khalil’s phone buzzed. An automated email: We thank you for your application but will not proceed further. Again.

Later, in a coffee shop, he confided to a friend, “I’m not losing jobs. I’m losing myself in the screening process.”

This story uncovered an insidious truth: systemic racism masked as cultural fit interviews and barriers that reject talent based on unspoken biases, rather than merit.

Story 3: The Classroom Question

Ms. Nguyen, an Asian American teacher, lined up her diverse group of students before the history lesson on civil rights.

“Today,” she began, “we’ll talk about the power of voices in shaping justice.”

A hand shot up from Jamal, a Black student with a quick wit. “Ms. N, why do some people still think white people are better?”

The classroom fell silent. The question lay heavy in the air, demanding attention.

Rather than brushing it aside, Ms. Nguyen leaned in, “That’s a brave question, Jamal. It’s because some believe in stereotypes rooted in fear and ignorance.”

She recounted stories of discrimination faced by her parents when they immigrated, of microaggressions in academic spaces, the everyday invisibility many endured. The students listened, some shocked, some contemplative.

A quiet girl in the back whispered, “I never knew people like you went through that.”

The lesson evolved beyond textbooks, becoming a conversation on empathy, awareness, and active confrontation of prejudice.

This vignette illuminated the educational frontlines where acknowledging hidden racism blossoms into understanding and change.

Story 4: The Neighbor’s Gaze

Eli and Amir had just moved into a quiet suburban neighborhood. Excitement bubbled as they unpacked boxes, eager to begin their new chapter. Both were Pakistani American, bringing with them stories, flavors, and dreams.

The next-door neighbor, Mrs. Wicks, approached hesitantly. “Welcome,” she said, shaking their hands stiffly. Over the following weeks, her smiles remained forced. When children played in front yards, she pulled curtains shut if she saw Amir playing with neighborhood kids.

One afternoon, Amir found a note slipped under their door: Not everyone here supports this kind of family.

They debated confronting Mrs. Wicks or leaving. However, choosing dialogue, they invited her over for tea.

Amir shared stories of their family’s journey, their love for the community, and hopes to build bridges.

Her eyes moistened. “I guess I was scared of what I didn’t understand.”

Their neighborhood slowly transformed—not overnight, but inch by inch, the mechanisms of suspicion, prejudice, and unspoken fears eroded.

This story reflects the daily, often unseen battles against prejudice in communities where difference triggers silent walls before compassion can break through.

Story 5: The Medical Appointment

Dr. Laura Mitchell, a seasoned physician, prided herself on patient care. When Jamila, an African American woman in her thirties, arrived complaining of persistent fatigue and pain, Laura carefully listened.

After basic tests, Laura suggested stress management and recommended over-the-counter remedies. Jamila left feeling unheard, her symptoms dismissed.

Weeks later, Jamila returned to a different clinic. More comprehensive evaluation revealed an autoimmune condition that required immediate treatment.

Laura reflected, troubled. Had unconscious bias led her to underestimate Jamila’s pain and concerns? Was Jamila not believed because of her race?

Such disparities in healthcare outcomes expose a face of hidden racism, translating into life-altering consequences.

Conclusion: Unmasking the Invisible

Each story peels back a layer from the façade of politeness, professionalism, or everyday normalcy, revealing hidden racism in various forms: at social dinners, job interviews, classrooms, neighborhoods, and healthcare settings. These are not acts of overt hate but subtle, systemic barriers and biases that endure through silence and discomfort.

Recognizing these faces is the first step toward genuine understanding, dialogue, and transformation. Hidden racism weaves itself into the fabric of daily interactions. Yet, in the telling, there is power—power to claim visibility, dignity, and change.

The challenge remains: to see not only the explicit but the implicit, to listen to voices muffled by prejudice, and to dismantle the invisible walls piece by piece.
