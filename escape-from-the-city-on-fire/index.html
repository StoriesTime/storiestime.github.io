Escape From the City on Fire


The city had never felt so alive—or so close to death. The skies above were streaked with orange and crimson, and the air smelled of smoke, metal, and desperation. People on the streets moved in frenzied packs, shouting over the sirens, the crackling of flames, and the rumbling tremors beneath their feet. Cars were abandoned on the roads, their engines still warm, doors hanging open, as though their owners had fled in haste.

At the heart of this chaos was Liam, a man who had spent his life in the city but had never quite felt at home. Today, that sense of not belonging would be the key to his survival.

It had started as a typical day, or as typical as a day could get in a place that never slept. Liam had awoken to the usual sounds: the hum of the city below his high-rise apartment, the distant roar of traffic, and the soft murmur of conversations spilling through thin walls. He didn’t have a set routine. He never had. A transient soul, drifting between jobs, apartments, and relationships, he was content to keep moving, to avoid attachment. It was easier that way, or so he thought.

Then came the announcement.

“This is a public safety announcement. Fires have broken out across the city in multiple locations. Emergency services are overwhelmed. Evacuations are in progress, but please proceed with caution. Seek shelter in secure areas. We advise all residents to stay indoors and away from the affected zones.”

At first, Liam had thought it was a drill. It wasn’t until he heard the first explosion, a loud, ear-shattering boom, followed by a wave of heat that slammed against the windows of his apartment, that he knew it was all too real.

His phone buzzed with a text from Mia, his only friend in the city.

“Get out. Now. The fires are spreading.”

He didn’t need to think twice. Without wasting a moment, he grabbed his jacket, shoved his phone and wallet into his pocket, and rushed out the door. He didn’t even lock it behind him.

The elevator was out, like most of the building’s systems, so he took the stairs two at a time, his heart pounding with the urgency of escape. As he reached the lobby, he was hit with a wave of smoke. The fire was close—too close. The entrance doors had been left ajar, allowing the thick, suffocating air to pour inside.

A few people huddled in the lobby, eyes wide with fear. Some were arguing, others crying. They all seemed to be waiting for someone else to take charge, to offer direction. Liam didn’t hesitate. He pushed through the doors, ignoring the screams of those who wanted to stay inside. The city had never felt more dangerous than it did now.

Liam stepped into the streets, immediately choking on the thick smoke that hung in the air. The streets, usually bustling with activity, were now empty except for a few desperate souls who ran in all directions, not knowing where to go. Sirens wailed, and in the distance, he could see the first signs of the inferno beginning to consume buildings.

He had lived through his share of disasters—small ones, isolated ones—but nothing like this. This was chaos on a scale he couldn’t comprehend.

He moved quickly, following the crowd for a while, hoping that someone knew where the evacuation zones were. But after a few blocks, he realized that no one had any clear answers. People just ran, blindly, driven by fear and panic.

It wasn’t until he saw the line of cars stretching for miles that he realized how bad things had become. Everyone was trying to flee the city. But with the roads gridlocked, trapped in a sea of metal and desperation, escape was impossible.

He needed to find another way out.

Mia’s words echoed in his mind: “The fires are spreading.”

There was no time to waste. If he stayed on the streets, he would only get caught in the inevitable collapse.

Liam scanned his surroundings, searching for an alternative route. To his right, he saw the narrow alley between two buildings. It was dark, and the air was heavy with the smell of burning wood and plastic. But it was a chance—a chance to get out of the main thoroughfare and perhaps find a way through the chaos.

With no better options, he darted into the alley, running as fast as his legs could carry him. The further he moved into the alley, the more he realized how eerily quiet it was. There were no other people, no cries for help. It was as though the alley was some sort of forgotten space, untouched by the flames that raged just a few blocks away.

But as he moved deeper, he began to hear a faint sound—a soft crackling, almost like the whisper of flames licking at wood.

He froze.

The alley had no exits. He was trapped.

Suddenly, a figure emerged from the shadows, moving swiftly and quietly. It was a woman, dressed in a dark jacket, with her hair pulled back tightly. Her face was streaked with dirt and ash, but her eyes were sharp, scanning every corner as though searching for something—or someone.

She noticed Liam immediately.

“Where are you going?” she asked, her voice low and urgent.

Liam hesitated. “I—I don’t know. I just need to get out.”

She eyed him for a moment, her gaze assessing, then nodded. “You’re not getting out the way you came in. The fire’s already on the other side.”

Liam’s heart dropped. He hadn’t even realized how close the flames had come until now.

“What do you suggest we do?”

The woman didn’t answer immediately. She turned, scanning the alley as if she were weighing her options. After a moment, she spoke again.

“There’s a tunnel,” she said, her voice barely above a whisper. “It’s an old utility access route. It leads to the river. From there, we can get out of the city.”

Liam didn’t ask questions. He followed her as she led the way, her footsteps quick and purposeful. She didn’t seem afraid—no, not afraid at all. She was like a person who had seen this kind of disaster before.

They reached a rusty door at the end of the alley, half-hidden by debris. The woman pulled out a set of keys and unlocked it, revealing a narrow staircase leading down into the dark.

Liam followed without hesitation, his mind focused only on the cold, distant promise of safety.

The further they descended, the more Liam could feel the heat of the fire creeping closer. The walls of the tunnel were damp, the air thick with the smell of mold and decay. The only light came from the faint glow of the woman’s flashlight, which illuminated the path ahead.

“You know this place well?” Liam asked, trying to break the silence.

The woman nodded. “I’ve been down here before. During the last crisis. It’s the only way out when the city burns.”

“Last crisis?” Liam repeated. “How long has this been happening?”

She didn’t answer right away. When she did, her voice was almost bitter. “Too long. Too many times. It’s always the same. The city catches fire, people panic, and the rich escape. The rest of us… we’re left to burn.”

Liam wanted to ask more, but the tunnel curved sharply ahead, and he found himself focusing all his attention on keeping up with her quick pace.

The journey down felt endless. They reached the end of the tunnel after what seemed like an eternity, and Liam could hear the roar of the river ahead. The woman pushed open a heavy metal door, and they stepped out into the cool night air.

The city was still burning. In the distance, he could see entire blocks consumed by flames, black smoke rising like a funeral pyre. The lights from the city flickered, casting strange shadows across the water. But they were safe—at least for now.

Liam took a deep breath, feeling the weight of everything settle on his shoulders. He turned to the woman, who was already pulling out a small boat tied to the dock.

“This is the only way out,” she said, her voice resolute.

Liam nodded. He didn’t need any more convincing. He climbed into the boat, feeling a sense of finality in the moment. As the woman pushed them away from the shore, he watched the city burn, the flames flickering in the distance like the dying embers of a world that had already given up on itself.

For the first time in years, Liam felt as though he might actually escape—escape not just from the city, but from the past he had always run from. The woman, who had chosen to remain anonymous, had led him to freedom. And as they drifted away from the chaos, he realized something he had never known before.

Escape wasn’t just about running from the fire. It was about escaping the need to stay.
