The Complexity of Evil: Understanding Your Antagonist


The moon hung low over the coastal village of Garthmoor, scattering pale light over rooftops layered with soot and sea spray. Among the twisted alleys curled like a serpent’s spine through the heart of the town, the night’s silence was shattered by the distant chime of the mortuary bell. The villagers knew this sound was not simply a sign of death—it was a harbinger.

Eira Cartwright, the town’s undertaker, knelt over the wide oak coffin splayed in her workshop. Her work demanded precision, care, and silence, but tonight her hands trembled. The air in the room was acrid with the scent of cedarwood shavings and her flickering oil lamp. She leaned closer to the massive figure on the wooden slab, a man whose life had ended in abrupt violence. His name was rarely spoken without the weight of disdain—Gale Darragh, the butcher of Garthmoor.

For years, tales of Gale’s savage deeds had crept through village homes like smoke threading under doors. He was the town’s greatest terror, a man whose crimes against humanity surpassed understanding. What made it worse was the absence of logic or pattern. Some villains had reasons, grievances, or traumas—they could be dissected, studied, perhaps even pitied. Gale Darragh left only questions sharpened by fear.

Eira ran her hand over the crisscrossing scars on his sinewy arms. She had no love for the man, but she was an undertaker. Her duty lay beyond judgment. Yet as the wind rattled the shutters of her home, she broke a cardinal rule and whispered her question to the darkness.

“Why? Why were you the way you were?”

What Eira didn’t notice as she bent back to her somber task was a shadow shifting unnaturally in the dim light—a whisper of something far older than Gale Darragh and far colder than death itself.

Long before he earned the name “the butcher,” Gale had been a boy in the fishing quarter, like so many others in Garthmoor. The Darragh line was riddled with hardship. Gale’s father was a drunk, his mother a tired woman weighed down by too many children, and their house barely stood against the violent storms that smashed its timber skeleton each winter. But hardship was not enough to seed the kind of wickedness Gale carried into adulthood.

Villagers often claimed he had been born broken, destined to tread a bloodier path than the rest, but reality is rarely so simple. Gale’s childhood, while harsh, offered moments of warmth. It was known that he adored his eldest sister, Mairin, a sharp-tongued girl who shielded her younger siblings from their father’s fists. By the time Gale was ten, Mairin had dragged him to the cliffs overlooking the black-capped waves and taught him how to wrest freedom from pain, even if only through defiance.

“When the world presses you, press back,” she had said, her wind-tangled hair like a storm cloud.

It was no wonder that Gale had taken Mairin’s death so hard. She had fallen from the very cliffs she had once proclaimed her dominion over, her body left broken on the rocks below. The villagers whispered of curses and ill omens, but Gale didn’t waste time on the supernatural. He blamed his father for not repairing the house’s blistering wood, for leaving nails overexposed that cracked under her weight. And when no one else would share in his hatred, he turned everything inward.

But anger has a peculiar nature—it cannot remain inward for long. Anger needs space to breathe, to grow. By the time Gale was a young man, he had become the artisan of his own downfall, lashing out at everyone and no one, feeding the dark narratives others built around him.

It wasn’t until years later that the world saw what Gale was capable of. When his father finally died, the old man’s body was discovered with wounds that couldn’t have been accidental. And despite the poorly murmured denials, the villagers knew—it was Gale. That knowledge passed silently from neighbor to neighbor as eyes turned cold and hands gripped tools tighter when he entered the marketplace. Still, no one confronted him outright, for they feared they might become the next name spoken with dread beneath the mortuary bell.

Eira’s hands rested over the ruined face of the dead man before her, looking for some semblance of humanity she could piece together. The villagers had compelled her to bury him far from the sacred grounds of the cemetery—a final indignity, even in death. They could not forgive him. She didn’t blame them for that. Nor could she.

But she couldn’t shake the thought that the man on her table—the murderer, the terror, the monster—had once also been a boy who had looked out over the cliffs. She couldn’t stop herself from wanting to understand him.

At first, it seemed like nothing but grief and frustration acting on her exhausted mind. Yet, as she worked, the thought grew: Where does such evil come from? Could it have been prevented? Eira had seen death in many forms—violent, slow, peaceful—but never had someone’s life before death pulled so sharply at her curiosity.

The stirring shadow in the room coiled tighter around her. It whispered, though its voice had no sound. It was ancient and chilling and spoke only in echoes of regret and pain.

Eira’s mother had once told her stories about the nature of the world. She swore there was a balance to all things—the sea and the sky, the fire and the water, the joy and the sorrow. But what about the line between good and evil? What happened when the weight shifted too far one way?

The villagers had convinced themselves that they understood Gale Darragh. He was a born predator, a beast wearing human skin. They refused to admit that his story was more complicated, for simple explanations are much softer to live with.

Perhaps they were right to shun complexity. It was dangerous to admit that terror might not simply live in others but grow in any of us, given the right—or wrong—conditions.

Eira, for her part, finished her work on Gale despite the murmurs she’d heard throughout the day. She carried the heavy coffin out of her home the following morning with the help of those few villagers willing to be near it. They would not speak of him as they carried him to the edge of the woods, to the unmarked grave that would swallow him whole.

He would be forgotten soon enough, becoming nothing more than a cautionary tale for children to hear on stormy nights. But for Eira, the man would linger longer in her thoughts. Though she would never know the full depth of his darkness, she knew enough to make her uneasy. He had not been born evil. He had been made—not by one moment or one person, but through layers of loss, fury, and neglect.

The shadow that had whispered in her workshop clung to her still, silent but persistent. She felt it in her dreams and wondered if it wasn’t simply tied to Gale but to all the unspoken complexities the world demanded she ignore.

Eira realized the burden of understanding did not lie in justifying Gale’s choices, nor in forgiving them. It rested in acknowledging the uncomfortable truth that monsters are rarely born whole—they are built.
