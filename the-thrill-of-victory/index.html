The Thrill of Victory


The bell rang, signaling the end of the first half. Coach Johnson had his eyes set on one individual, Johnny Deed. In his ten years of coaching football at Hanover High School, he had never seen a ferocious talent like Johnny. More than just a first-class athlete, Johnny had a flair for the dramatic. That was something that set him apart, something that could carry him to victory in the State Championship game, which was mere weeks away.

Coach Johnson was renowned for his unmatched motivational speeches, often inspiring fear in his players as he drove them to give their all. The team could feel his gaze, whether it was the starting quarterback, or the last bench-warmer. But for some reason, on this particular evening, his words fell flat. No one seemed to be listening, but that was to be expected. They were worn out. These local matches were child’s play compared to the State Championships.

The coach knew that he had to do something dramatic, something that would rekindle the fire in his team. So he headed to the locker room, to where Johnny was, nursing his bruises. The tall, lanky figure of the coach cast a dramatic shadow on the floor. His booming voice shook the air.

“Deed,” he said, his eyes boring into the half-African, half-Navajo athlete, “this is it. This is the moment we’ve worked toward, our lives leading up to this point.”

Despite his tough exterior, he knew the young man’s heart was fragile. He continued, seeking to bolster his prowess, “You have this talent, Johnny, a talent that could make or break careers, that could cement you as a legend. But it’s not just your talent; it’s your heart that makes you a true warrior on the field.”

Johnny looked up at him, the coach saw determination shining in his eyes, he had turned a corner. Coach Johnson smiled, and said softly, “Remember, my boy, when the stakes are highest, the fear of loss is second only to the thrill of victory.”

The second half was in full swing, Hanover High urgently trailing. And suddenly, it was as if Johnny had been struck by lightning. The athleticism he had become known for transformed into something supernatural. His heart was pumping with adrenaline, his muscles fueled by the spirit of the game. As the opposition neared their goal, attempting to score, Johnny launched himself into the air, a daring, lightning-fast leap. As his legs spread wide, his arms flung the opposition back. There was a gasp from the audience—they had witnessed the ultimate example of grace under pressure.

Within moments, a teammate scored. Victory! The crowd went wild. Johnny had helped lead his team to the State Championship. He had prevented the infiltration of a goal that would have depleted their spirit. As his bruised body writhed in pain, he was filled with jubilation, a vast, magnificent feeling of accomplishment. On the field, he was invincible.

Victory left an indelible mark on the team and the town of Hanover. The night was a whirlwind of celebration, of alcohol-soaked cries of triumph. One aspect haunted Johnny through the haze of a night that he would never forget.

It was the thought of Coach Johnson’s parting words, the thrill of victory weighing heavily on his mind: “The fear of loss is second only to the thrill of victory.” It was true, he mused. Victory might bring happiness, but the fear of loss could bring just as much, if not more, power.

The young athlete pondered this as he lay awake that night in bed, listening to the desolation of the Hanover night. Victory had left him weary, yet he knew then that he was strong. Victory had brought him pain, but he knew that of all things, victory had left him the mightiest.

The Northwest wind blew through the windows of the Deed family’s home that night, paying homage to that night of triumph in Hanover High School’s history. The winds seemed to whisper a question: “What will you accept in exchange for your spirit?”

Johnny knew the answer: “A wholehearted, staggering victory.” But he wondered, was true victory possible without the fear of loss? Could one exist without the other?

Moral:

Victory can be intoxicating; it can lead you to believe that nothing is far from your reach, but underneath its intoxication lies a fear as deep-rooted as your triumph. A true warrior ​​knows that victory and loss are intrinsically linked, each shaping us into greater beings.
