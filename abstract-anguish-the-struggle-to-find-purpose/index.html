Abstract Anguish: The Struggle to Find Purpose


Veiled in the grip of dawn’s unyielding gray, Arlen stared out at the city skyline, its jagged edges painted with a thousand shades of indifference. The streets below teemed with life—figures darted like clockwork ants bound by schedules, routines, and unattainable expectations. He wondered if they felt it, that gnawing void he carried every day, or if it was his alone, a cruel joke life devised for one who dared to question.

Arlen’s apartment bore the scars of his restless search: crumpled pages littered the cold oak floors, their lines scribbled with outlines of fleeting ideas, journal entries riddled with doubt, sketches of labyrinths that went nowhere. The walls were blank except for a single photograph of the ocean—a vast, unchanging horizon—that seemed to mock him. “Once, I believed I’d conquer the waves,” he muttered to no one. Once, but not now.

It hadn’t always been like this. There had been moments when purpose burned brightly, when the pursuit of meaning felt tangible and the world around him seemed charged with infinite possibilities. He recalled his younger self, eager and impulsive, clasping books about philosophy and dreaming of building bridges between the abstract and concrete worlds.

But life had been relentless. The motion of years turned into obligations—jobs taken to pay bills, friendships eroded by shared disappointments, passions swallowed whole by the crushing weight of mediocrity. Somewhere along the way, Arlen had become unmoored from himself, like a ship adrift in a featureless sea, unsure of where to sail or whether the stars above were real.

Still, today, something felt different. He couldn’t name it—a faint rebellion against the apathy that threatened to consume him, a yearning to make sense of it all. Though his mind screamed that the search for meaning was futile, that life was an endless loop without resolution, a whisper somewhere within urged him otherwise. The whisper, fragile as it was, demanded curiosity, demanded answers. And so he reached for pen and paper once more, hoping perhaps this time he’d find clarity.

As he wrote, abstract notions warred within him. Was meaning in action? Was it in creation? Was purpose tied to legacy, or could it be momentary—a fleeting fragment of peace in an otherwise chaotic existence? He poured out questions without answers, inscribed them as though writing them down would summon the truths hidden within their complexity. Hours passed unnoticed, his hand cramping from the force of his thoughts.

The afternoon sun clawed through the blinds, bathing the room with light. Arlen rose from his chair, pacing the confines of his apartment, the surroundings closing in like the pages of an unwelcome book. He stepped out onto the balcony, seeking respite in the fresh air. He inhaled deeply, closing his eyes to the sounds below—cars blaring horns, children shrieking with laughter, the cyclical cadence of life in motion.

“You’ll never crack it this way,” a voice startled him from his stillness. He turned to see Mira, his neighbor from across the hall, leaning against the wall separating their balconies. Mira often struck him as aloof, unaffected by the lingering questions Arlen carried. Yet her gaze now held interest, a perceptiveness that unsettled him.

“Crack what?” he replied.

“Purpose.” She smirked, brushing a strand of frizzy, auburn hair from her face. “Every time I see you, you look like someone trying to solve a jigsaw puzzle with no edges.”

The analogy landed uncomfortably. “What else is there but to try?” Arlen muttered, feeling exposed.

Mira tilted her head, watching him thoughtfully. “I think,” she began slowly, “we mistake purpose for something grand—a singular truth that defines us, that sets us apart. But maybe that’s not the way.”

He frowned. “Then what is?”

“Moments,” she answered. “Connections. Passion where we least expect it. There’s no map for this, Arlen. You just have to walk the path and figure it out as you go.”

Her words stirred something in him—a resistance tempered by intrigue. “That’s vague. Convenient answer for someone who doesn’t ask questions.”

She laughed softly. “The questions matter. But if you wait for the answers to act, you’ll waste the journey entirely.”

This lingered. Mira’s cryptic philosophy resonated like notes of an unfinished melody. Why had these words, spoken thoughtlessly by someone he’d always dismissed as uncomplicated, left an impression so deep it forced him to reconsider his approach?

Determined to make sense of her outlook, Arlen decided to step outside the confines of his spirals of thought. He put away the papers that had been his refuge, grabbed his coat, and ventured onto the streets he had so often avoided witnessing from above. Mira had dared him—not verbally, but implicitly—to find the threads of meaning outside himself.

The city, in its noise and chaos, threatened to swallow him as it always did. But Arlen softened to its rhythms, to the faces passing him by. He watched the hurried family crossing the street, a father adjusting his child’s tiny hat with infinite tenderness. He caught sight of the elderly shopkeeper who looked at his wares with pride, going about his work with reverence despite the lack of demand. Arlen noticed colors he’d ignored before—the faded murals painted by unseen hands; the blooming petals spilling from the cracks of the sidewalk.

Without fully understanding why, Arlen felt compelled to sit on a bench in the park that sprawled toward the city’s edges. He opened his journal, flipped past pages of unresolved questions, and began to write—to describe the simplicity of the lives weaving around him, the fragmented snapshots of humanity manifest in these quiet details. Was this purpose? Observing, appreciating? Or was it simply distraction masquerading as meaning?

These queries folded differently now, less burdensome than before. Meaning didn’t need immediate resolution, he realized. It was a nectar teased out of the living, a flicker to grasp in fragments rather than a single, unifying flame. The struggle to find purpose was, perhaps, the essence of purpose itself—the willingness to chase light despite its elusiveness.

That day unfolded in mysterious ways, and though Arlen didn’t return home as a man transformed, he did carry back with him the beginnings of a truth he might not yet comprehend. The anguish remained, but its edges were dulled by his curiosity, by the faint notion that meaning might exist in the pursuit rather than the end. As Mira had said, you had to walk—not to arrive, but simply to know the feeling of forward movement.

The night fell heavily, the city skyline again framed in its indifferent splendor. Arlen placed the pen down, gripping the journal as if it had become a lifeline. He didn’t know where the search would lead him or if the ache inside would ever fully dissolve. What he knew, however, was something he hadn’t dared admit before: the questions mattered, but not more than the act of living.

Tomorrow, he’d go back to the park, maybe speak to the shopkeeper or follow the trail of those blooming sidewalk flowers. He wouldn’t ask what it proved. He would only listen—to the moments, the connections, the passions where he least expected them. Maybe Mira was right. Maybe that was enough.
