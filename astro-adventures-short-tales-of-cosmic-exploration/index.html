Astro Adventures: Short Tales of Cosmic Exploration


It was the silence that made deep space so haunting. Captain Liora Vance often found herself pondering this as she sat hunched over the navigation console aboard the Stellar Horizon. The spaceship’s humming systems exuded the only sounds, a soft mechanical chorus of calibrated functionality, while endless darkness and pinpricks of starlight stretched infinitely beyond the reinforced viewing panels.

Their mission felt small compared to the vastness pressing in around them. A ragtag team of six, they were tasked with cataloging anomalies in Sector X5-F, a relatively uncharted quadrant of the galaxy. These zones were known to harbor all manner of phenomena—collapsed stars, wormholes slumbering like cosmic predators, and worlds barely skirting the boundaries of habitable conditions. For Vance and her crew, this wasn’t just exploration; it was survival theater on an epic stage.

“Captain, anomaly detected ahead,” said Ivonne Modrin, the ship’s chief of astrophysics, her voice laden with a mix of confidence and caution. Her lavender-hued hair was tied back, but stray strands floated weightlessly in the cabin’s artificial gravity. “Coordinates are 12.48 by -87.02. It’s… distinct.”

“What kind of distinct?” Vance asked, swiveling her chair. Modrin’s screen showed a pulse-like signature, a rhythm coming irregularly from something just beyond the ship’s current position—an object in an otherwise barren patch of space.

“It’s not a pulsar, not a cyclic emission… It’s, uh, inconsistent,” Modrin said, squinting into her readings. “But definitely artificial.”

“You think it’s a beacon?” chimed in Quince Reyes, the ship’s engineer, his hands streaked with mineral dust from repairs he’d been running on the vent systems. Unlike Modrin, skepticism was his default setting. He leaned in over Modrin’s shoulder. “Or leftovers from some old race that got bored of existing?”

“Could be,” Vance replied, rubbing her jaw. “Helmsman, how’s the trajectory looking? Are we in a good position to make a detour?”

Abasi Kwenke, the ship’s navigator, tapped the glowing screen before him with the precision of a concert pianist. He had a habit of tilting his head when focusing; his long braids caught the light from the interface with each subtle movement. “We can reach it in two hours if we adjust to 145 vectral angle. We’ll need to slow to sub-light speeds, though, given the debris.”

“Do it,” Vance ordered. “Everyone else, suit up and prep. If it’s artificial, then someone—or something—sent it out. And no one sends signals into space without a reason.”

The Stellar Horizon ghosted toward the signal source, engines quieting as it drifted closer to a strange, jagged object partially embedded in a small asteroid. The crew assembled in the observatory room, peering through reinforced glass at what appeared to be a pitch-black monolith. Six meters tall and unnervingly angular, it jutted out from the asteroid like an obsidian claw. Its pulse—bright scatters of coppery light—flashed irregularly across its edges.

“Right…” said Reyes, strapped into his exosuit. He checked the seals on his helmet before turning toward Modrin, who was affixing a scanning drone to her wrist’s utility port. “Explain to me why this thing’s got a heartbeat.”

“Hyperbole aside,” Modrin replied curtly, “likely some sort of ancient data relay. It’s broadcasting something, and if I know one thing about signal traces, it’s that artifacts like this don’t talk randomly.”

“Artifacts,” Reyes muttered. “Because sure, everything in deep space is a treasure chest waiting for us to poke it.”

Vance cut through the chatter. “Enough. Reyes, Modrin, you’re leading the EVA crew. Approach the artifact slowly. Your objective is preliminary scans only—no touching. We’ll keep telemetry locked on you. If anything feels off, you pull back immediately. Got it?”

“Aye, Captain,” they echoed, albeit with contrasting enthusiasm.

Minutes later, Reyes and Modrin were outside the ship, magnetized boots clinking steadily against the asteroid’s coarse surface. The void of space loomed ominously behind them, making Reyes mutter about how he knew better than to join exploration missions. A thin tether restrained them from floating out into nothingness, a silver thread glimmering faintly under distant starlight.

“Close enough to deploy the drone,” Modrin said, her voice calm over the comms channel. She paused and inhaled sharply. “Wait. There’s something… odd here. Do you feel that?”

Reyes stopped walking. His footfalls had been entirely silent, but he swore he felt something—a faint vibration. It pulsed through the asteroid like a dormant heartbeat. “Yeah. What is that? I thought rocks didn’t throb.”

“It’s not seismic,” Modrin whispered.

Still, she pressed forward, initiating the drone’s archscan protocol. The small device floated ahead as data streams populated her HUD. The artifact’s composition appeared as a headache-inducing amalgamation of unknown alloys and crystalline filaments. But then the readings flickered unpredictably. Its molecular structure rearranged itself, becoming dynamic and resisting every attempt at classification.

“That’s—” Modrin started, but the drone emitted a sharp whine before collapsing back to the ground, its circuits fried.

The artifact’s pulse quickened.

“Back! Fall back, now!” Vance’s voice barked through their comms.

Neither Reyes nor Modrin needed convincing. They retreated in hurried strides, magnetically locked to the asteroid’s surface until their tether reeled them back toward the Stellar Horizon. Reyes caught himself glancing over his shoulder—despite not wanting to. His instincts screamed to look away, but the artifact’s coppery light grew menacingly brighter, turning the black void behind it into an ominous stage.

“They made it back just in time,” Kwenke muttered as they reentered the ship’s airlock, the crew breathing collective sighs of relief. “That thing wasn’t happy.”

“That’s putting it lightly,” Reyes replied, shrugging off his suit awkwardly. “That pulse kicked up as soon as it realized we were scanning it. Worse than that one time we poked a sleeping gamma eel.”

Modrin, still poring over partial data extracted by the drone before its destruction, looked up with wide eyes. Her fingers tapped feverishly against the console, piecing together fragments. “Captain, listen to this.” She fed an audio stream into the room’s speakers, filtered from the artifact’s erratic transmissions.

The sound came slowly, a warped cacophony of layered tones—high-pitched squeals intertwining with bass rumbles, like a spectral orchestra tuning their instruments in some forgotten arctic dimension. Beneath the symphony, faint human-like whispers could be detected, incomprehensible yet undeniably there.

“What is that?” asked Tilly Aragon, the mission’s biologist. She had been silent until now, studying the footage from the external cameras. Her voice trembled as she turned toward the others. “That sounds alive.”

“I can’t explain it yet,” Modrin said, her voice tinged with frustration and excitement. “But it’s an encoded pattern. This isn’t just some mindless beacon—it’s communication. Or… a warning.”

The whispers crescendoed for one last dreadful moment before falling silent.

Hours passed, casting the crew in tense uncertainty. The artifact started sending rhythmic tremors into the surrounding space. Debris floated erratically, tugged inwards by invisible tendrils of unnatural gravity. Vance paced the bridge, scanning for solutions while her gut told her one undeniable truth: they had awakened something.

“Captain,” Kwenke said, breaking the silence. “I don’t think we’re alone.”

“What do you mean?” Vance asked, her voice hushed. She forced herself to stare at the observation screen.

Nestled in the shadow of a larger asteroid, another ship had appeared—sleek, alien in design, its hull resembling polished obsidian. It hovered silently, no lights or identifiable markings.

The artifact’s pulse did not cease—it increased.
