From Struggle to Success: Overcoming Learning Challenges


Jordan sat at the small desk in the corner of his bedroom, staring at the open textbook in front of him. The words danced across the page, refusing to stay still long enough for him to grasp their meaning. His fingers tightened around the pencil in his hand as frustration bubbled up inside him. It felt like everyone else at school had some secret code they’d cracked, a key to unlocking concepts that he just couldn’t figure out.

It wasn’t always this way. In the early grades, Jordan loved school. He enjoyed learning new things and spent hours building elaborate Lego structures and reading short storybooks with his mom. But as the years passed, the lessons grew harder, and his struggle to keep up became more pronounced. Teachers were patient at first, but by the time Jordan reached middle school, those patient smiles had become strained. Notes sent home painted the same picture: “Jordan has potential if only he would apply himself.” He wanted to scream every time he heard that. He was applying himself—his efforts just didn’t seem to be enough.

Tonight was no different. The looming deadline for his geography essay filled him with dread. He had reread the requirements countless times, but starting felt impossible. He tapped the pencil against the desk, closing his eyes and silently willing the inspiration to come. But the silence of the room only deepened his sense of inadequacy.

In the living room, Jordan could hear his younger sister, Mia, chattering with their mom about her third-grade science project. Mia, with her straight A’s, her shiny merit badges, and her stack of accolades. While Jordan struggled to pass his subjects, Mia breezed through school like it was a game she’d mastered before she even started. He loved her, but sometimes the comparisons cut deep.

“Jordan,” his mom called from the hall, interrupting his thoughts, “have you eaten? It’s getting late.”

“I’ll eat later,” he replied, his voice barely audible. He didn’t want her to see how far behind he was.

But his mom wasn’t one to give up on him so easily. She opened the door, her warm brown eyes immediately cataloging the scattered papers, the half-written sentences, the defeated slump of her son’s shoulders. “What’s going on?” she asked, sitting on the edge of his bed.

Jordan hesitated. “I don’t know how to do it,” he finally admitted. “It’s like everyone else knows something I don’t. I don’t know if… I don’t know if I can do this.”

She sighed gently, running a hand through his hair. It was a gesture she used when she wanted to comfort him. “You don’t have to do it alone,” she said. “Let’s figure out what’s holding you back.”

The next morning, Jordan’s mom took him to a learning specialist. He felt embarrassed and vulnerable sitting in the office, explaining how he struggled to keep track of what teachers said in class, how reading left him more confused than informed, and how his essays never came out the way he pictured them in his head. The specialist listened without judgment, asking questions that made Jordan feel like someone finally understood.

After a series of evaluations, they had an answer: Jordan had undiagnosed dyslexia and ADHD. It was a relief to have a name for what he’d been experiencing, but it was also a little scary. What did this mean for his future? Would he always feel this way—trapped between effort and results, incapable of catching up?

The specialist reassured him that this wasn’t the case. She spoke about how his brain worked differently, not worse, and how strategies could help him navigate the challenges. “The important thing,” she said with a warm smile, “is that you know you’re not alone. And now we know how to help.”

Over the next few months, Jordan started working with a tutor who specialized in students with learning differences. At first, the sessions were frustrating. It felt like he was relearning everything from the ground up, but this time with tools that made concepts click. The tutor taught him to break tasks into smaller steps, to read aloud to himself when the words swam on the page, and to take short breaks when his mind began to wander. He also started using audiobooks and specialized software that helped him with writing assignments.

One day, while working on his geography essays with the tutor, Jordan had a breakthrough. Instead of staring at the blank page, he recorded himself speaking out the main points as if he were explaining them to a friend. When he played the recording back, the ideas sounded clearer than they ever had before. Transcribing those sentences into written form felt natural, like piecing together a puzzle. For the first time, Jordan didn’t dread turning in his work.

At school, things started to change. He had a special learning plan now, which meant he could take extra time on tests and use assistive technology during assignments. His teachers became allies instead of critics, seeing his progress and cheering him on. His classmates noticed, too. Though Jordan still feared being labeled or teased, he found that most of his peers admired his efforts.

By the time high school rolled around, Jordan had built himself an arsenal of strategies and tools. He wasn’t perfect, but he no longer panicked when he encountered a challenge. Slowly but surely, his confidence grew.

One afternoon during sophomore year, his English teacher, Mr. Abbott, handed back their graded essays. Jordan’s heart raced. Essays were always the measurement of how far he’d come—and how far he still had to go. Mr. Abbott placed Jordan’s paper face-down on his desk and lingered for a moment. “Good work, Jordan,” he said, a small smile tugging at his lips.

Jordan flipped the paper over. At the top, in bold red ink, was an A-minus. His first ever.

He stared at the grade as if it might vanish before his eyes. Slowly, a grin broke across his face. He wanted to shout it from the rooftops. Instead, he folded the paper carefully and placed it in his backpack. When he got home, he would pin it to the bulletin board above his desk, a reminder that success was possible.

Years later, Jordan sat in a crowded auditorium, his cap and gown neatly pressed, the tassel swaying slightly as he adjusted his seat. The principal’s voice echoed through the room, reading through the list of names. “Jordan Adams,” she called, and he stood, a wide smile breaking across his face as his family erupted into cheers.

Jordan crossed the stage to receive his diploma, his mind racing with everything it had taken to get here—the late nights, the tutoring sessions, the moments when quitting seemed easier than continuing. He thought back to the frustrated boy at the desk, who wondered if he’d ever catch up, and he smiled. He had caught up. In fact, he’d done more than that. He had surpassed even his own expectations.

Jordan didn’t know exactly what the future held, but he knew one thing for sure: there was no challenge too big for him to overcome.
