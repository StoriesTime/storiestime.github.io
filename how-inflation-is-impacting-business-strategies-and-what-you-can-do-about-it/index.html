How Inflation is Impacting Business Strategies and What You Can Do About It


Chandra Patel always woke at 5:00, before the city’s caffeine had a chance to stir. Already, three generations of Patels had been running the blue-tiled bakery on the corner of Mason and 12th, taking little for granted—except that their mornings would start with flour and end with the kind of exhaustion that flour could never touch. But lately, Chandra had come to suspect even flour was plotting against her.

She would check her email, index finger hovering over the touch screen, to see the latest update from her supplier. Each week, a new notification: price adjustments, transport surcharge, altered delivery dates. “Market volatility,” the emails said, as if repeated enough times the phrase would apologize for reaching so deeply into Chandra’s ledgers. For a while, she had taken to reading the news aloud before the first batch rose, reciting numbers hot as ovens—gas, wheat, eggs, the price of every good that might be caught in the roaring slipstream of what pundits politely called ‘the economic climate.’

One unremarkable Tuesday morning, her accountant arrived for coffee—early, but not earlier than Chandra. Leo’s slush of receipts spilled on the countertop like loose tea leaves. “Fuel hikes just hit the supply chain again. Your delivery guy called. Wants a better rate per route,” he mumbled, sipping coffee that had doubled in wholesale price over the winter.

Chandra, used to reading stories in the small print of invoices, looked at her business with fresh urgency. Wheat up twelve percent. Ravaged crops from untimely frost. Packaging material, some days, seemed to cost more than the treats inside. Customers were reluctant to pay more for the same pastries, yet her costs ballooned with every ingredient.

By the next weekend, her strategizing went late into the night. She reached out to her small business group. Emails flashed across her screen: restaurateurs, florists, boutique owners, all tumbling together in a shared panic. The pattern was clear—rising production costs, unpredictable supplier contracts, squeezed profit margins. “We’re all improvising,” said Arjun from the trendy salad spot. “Dynamic pricing, smaller portions, rotating menus—we do what we can.”

But Chandra needed specifics. Data. And not just on paper.

On Sundays, the bakery was closed except for deep cleaning. Chandra used these hours to experiment. She tinkered with recipes, testing how far she could stretch ingredients without sacrificing her standards. She compared her signature date-and-walnut loaf, at three different price points and flour mixtures, lining up slices with a quiet sense of mild rebellion against economics. Her mother, Nana, frowned from the family’s recipe book.

“Cannot cut cardamom,” Nana said, eyeing a tray of samples. “Flavor is the last to go, not the first.”

And so began Chandra’s “inflation menu,” a rotating selection that responded to fluctuating ingredient prices and supply chain curveballs. She created smaller versions of crowd favorites, clearly labeled on the menu as “Bakery Bites”—a subtle shift, but psychologically, customers felt less like they were paying more for less. The business—once a monument to tradition—became a laboratory for agile decision-making, scenario planning, relentless adaptation.

She adopted digital inventory tools that tracked every sack of flour, every carton of eggs, pinging her phone with early warnings as restocking thresholds approached. Her supplier relationships became more like negotiations—bulk discounts in exchange for early payment, flexible delivery options in return for a longer contract.

Simultaneously, Chandra built a new layer of the business online: pre-orders, loyalty packages, “mystery box” treats that turned uncertainty into a selling point. Every box contained a selection dictated by what was freshest and most affordable from that week’s fluctuating market prices. What some saw as scarcity, Chandra reframed as variety.

Her staff noticed a shift, too. Before, menu changes were etched in stone. Now, they were dry-erase experiments. New staff meetings focused on innovation, cross-training, and spotting customer feedback—both positive and negative—as clues in a larger game of economic chess. Customers who grumbled quietly about smaller portions often bought coffee or a second snack, softening the impact.

Chandra realized her willingness to communicate honestly was just as vital as monitoring the balance sheet. She started posting short updates to social media: “Our croissants are a little smaller this month as we wait for wheat prices to drop. But every batch is baked with the same care.” Engagement rose. Some customers even offered support—pre-paying for weekly loaves, tipping bigger, giving word-of-mouth endorsements.

At her next business group meeting, Ritu the florist lamented having to pass along costs for imported blooms. “You’re lucky,” Ritu half-joked. “People have to eat bread. They don’t have to buy tulips.”

“I used to think that,” Chandra replied, “but now I realize everyone needs comfort. We have to be creative with how we deliver it.” Her notes filled with takeaways from other industries: service bundling, product substitutions, real-time analytics, and even cooperative purchasing agreements to share delivery costs with other small businesses.

One afternoon, Chandra found herself staring at a wall chart mapping profit and loss. The lines jostled like siblings in the back seat of a car. The trick, she mused, wasn’t avoiding volatility. It was keeping the car moving, steering into the hairpin turns.

Some strategies helped right away; others required patience. Alternative sources for flour sometimes meant quality wobbled until she found a reliable regional supplier. A promotional day for the “inflation menu” surfaced the most adaptable items—banana nut muffins, savory rolls, the star anise cookie that went viral thanks to a local food blogger.

Several months in, the bakery’s numbers stabilized—not at their old margins, but at least within a place that allowed Chandra to plan further than next Monday. She invested in staff training to handle new sales channels. Her digital order ledger bulged with unexpected orders from customers she’d never met.

Of course, uncertainty never vanished. Holiday seasons brought supply hiccups; a local distributor shuttered without warning, forcing snap decisions and hard negotiating. Chandra’s father, who had always run the business by “feel,” marveled at her spreadsheets, at one point muttering, “I can’t believe flour needs a forecasting report now.”

“So we forecast,” Chandra said, “and then we bake.”

By year’s end, the Patel family bakery found itself not only surviving but evolving—a little leaner, a little scrappier, yet more attuned to the rhythms of the world than ever. Chandra looked out the window one morning over the rising hum of customers and deliveries, realizing she had somehow managed to wrangle chaos into something almost like routine.

The key, she found, wasn’t just in slashing costs or raising prices. It was in finding new balance points: digital strategy dovetailing with analog warmth, flexibility underlying every recipe, open communication with both customers and staff. As inflation continued to ripple through the economy, Chandra stood in the center of her bakery, part scientist, part artist, knowing that the only certainty was change—and that sometimes, a smaller croissant, baked with care, was more than enough to keep a business, and a community, together.

What You Can Do About Inflation: Insights from Chandra’s Experience

While Chandra’s story is individual, the lessons aren’t limited to bakers. If you’re running a business fighting the headwinds of rising costs and unpredictable supply chains, several strategies have proven helpful:

  1. Agile Pricing: Don’t be afraid to update prices, repackage products, or offer new sizes to reflect current market realities. Transparency goes a long way.
  2. Inventory Management: Upgrade to digital inventory tracking, enabling timely purchases and flexibility in sourcing. This keeps you ahead of shortages and surges.
  3. Supplier Negotiation: Treat every supplier interaction as a negotiation. Seek term adjustments, explore group purchasing, and diversify where possible.
  4. Customer Communication: Openly share the “why” behind changes. Loyal customers understand, especially when you frame adjustments in the context of quality and care.
  5. Innovation and Diversification: Experiment with new offerings, bundles, or digital channels. Use uncertainty as a spur to innovate.
  6. Community Focus: Leverage relationships with other small businesses for shared transport or cross-promotion. Community support can soften sharp market shifts.

Inflation isn’t a problem to be solved once, but a puzzle to be managed day by day. Whether it’s flour, fuel, or flowers, adaptability, empathy, and a steady hand at the wheel help businesses not only endure but—sometimes unexpectedly—thrive.
