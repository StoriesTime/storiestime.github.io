Gods and Monsters: The Untold Stories of Mythology


It began in silence, in a time before the mortals. The gods, young and reckless, roamed the cosmos with abandon, crafting and unmaking with each breath they drew. This was their era of unchecked power, the great age of creation and destruction. Yet for all their grandeur, there were whispers even then—muted warnings carried by the windless void—that not everything within creation served their will. Beneath the cracks of their dominion, something ancient stirred.

Far to the edge of the known world, where Nyx’s shadows wove a veil too thick for mortal light and mortal vision, an ancient creature slumbered. It wasn’t a god in the way that Zeus or Ra or Odin might call themselves divine. No throne or temple honored it. It was something altogether older, a fragment of primordial chaos left behind when the cosmos gained its shape. Its name had never been spoken by mortal tongues, and it would not be remembered by mortals to come if the gods had their way. The creature’s true name was lost even to the gods, who only referred to it in hushed fear as “Khoraktis.”

Khoraktis was said to be born from the discord of the first shaping, a quasar of instability that followed the first act of divinity. While the gods’ ambitions carved order out of void, Khoraktis represented what they feared most: an unraveling. Led by the indomitable Zeus, a coalition of gods once tried to vanquish it during the cosmos’ infancy. By the time they realized their error, they had scarcely escaped with their immortality intact. They had bound the creature instead, weaving its prison from threads of time itself. It slumbered, hemmed in by eternity, but the ties were weakening. They always would.

This was the truth that the gods never told mortals. For all their pantheons and pantheon wars, for all their displays of supremacy over each other, their greatest fear had never been rival deities—or even humanity, with its unsettling defiance. Their greatest fear was what lay in waiting: a monster older than gods, one that did not live but was simply becoming.

The Warning

The first omen came in the form of dreams. Mystics across the mortal plane murmured of visions drenched in crimson skies and splitting earth. Seers screamed themselves hoarse, warning of impending chaos that the ruling mortal kings dismissed with laughter. They had become wise to such predictions—a king who heeded omens too often would find himself paralyzed by paranoia. Yet one mortal, a former oracle named Aletheia, awoke from a dream so vivid it burned with the weight of truth.

In her vision, Aletheia stood atop a black stone funneling threads of starlight into a maelstrom of shadows below. A voice, not hers but within her, whispered: They cannot cage me forever. When she awoke, her skin was etched with fine lines, as though her flesh had aged a decade overnight.

Aletheia knew where the black stones lay. She had once served the Oracle of Delphi as a priestess before the temple drove her away for defying Apollo’s dictates. She had dared to speak openly of the gods’ weaknesses, exposing their fickle cruelty. It was Aphrodite herself who placed a curse on Aletheia, muting her voice in the presence of any who would wield power. Now, years later, her visions returned tenfold.

By the next sundown, Aletheia was on the road, carrying nothing more than a satchel and an iron blade she’d purchased at the market. What could a mortal blade do against a god or even a monster of legend? Still, she gripped the blade tightly, as though daring fate to mock her.

The Gathering of Unseen Alliances

Far above, on the peak of Mount Olympus, Hera stormed into Apollo’s sunlit chamber. “Do not tell me you’ve felt nothing,” she hissed. Her voice, though low, carried a tempest’s sharpness. “The mortals stir. The seers we do not silence grow bolder. They have seen something—it.”

Apollo, lounging idly with a harp across his knee, stretched and smiled faintly. “You worry yourself into furies, my queen. What is it you believe the mortals know that we do not?”

“Do not play games with me,” Hera snapped. “The chains fray, Apollo. The prison speaks. Or do you think Zeus has barred us from Nyx’s gate out of his boundless good sense?”

At this, Apollo straightened. “If you are suggesting—”

“I am suggesting nothing. I am telling you that whether you acknowledge it or not, the prison breaks. If you won’t speak to Zeus, I will.”

Their conversation was interrupted by a shadow in the doorway. Athena stepped inside, wearing the bloodied cloak of victory from a distant skirmish with the Egyptian pantheon’s soldiers. “You will not need to say another word to our father,” she said coolly. “I was in his hall not an hour ago. He does not sleep.”

“Ah,” muttered Apollo. “So even great Zeus quakes.”

Athena turned to Hera, a frown creasing her immortal brow. “Aletheia moves to Nyx’s veil. We must stop her.”

“She’s mortal. What harm could she accomplish alone?” Apollo questioned.

“That blade she carries,” said Athena. “It is not bound by mortal logic. Hephaestus forged it in secret under old fires—before Olympus’ supremacy began. It is a key as much as it is a weapon.”

A Descent into Darkness

Far below the gods’ realm, Aletheia reached the threshold of Nyx’s veil. The night here was alive, so suffocatingly thick she had long since given up on any effort to see. Her other senses sharpened by necessity—the crunch of stone underfoot, the acrid tang in the air like burned copper. She whispered prayers to gods who had spurned her long ago and cursed her folly for coming here alone. Yet some deep instinct whispered louder than reason: You need not kneel to them. It was never their world.

When she placed her hand on the first black stone, a heartbeat rippled up her arm through to her chest. Time itself seemed to groan and bend beneath her feet. Her vision doubled, and in the flicker of overlapping spans, she saw them: overlapping silhouettes in the void. Khoraktis was stretching—the prison of time could no longer hold.

The Choice of Immortals

The gods arrived too late to stop Aletheia. Hera, Athena, and Apollo descended from Olympus to find mortals scattered—followers of shadow. Yet when they confronted her atop the black stone altar, she did not cower but raised the forged blade aloft like a torch.

“Knoraktis whispers to you,” She accused, silencing Athena curses again mid-form confrontation?
