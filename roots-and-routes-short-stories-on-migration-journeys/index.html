Roots and Routes: Short Stories on Migration Journeys


The bus hummed along the cracked asphalt, a serpent twisting through the fertile plains that once belonged to Miriam’s ancestors. Her fingers traced the frayed edges of an old photograph folded carefully in her jacket pocket. It was a picture of her great-grandmother, standing outside a small mudbrick house, her eyes steady and unyielding, framed by loose strands of silver hair. That house no longer existed—devoured by time and concrete—but its memory clung stubbornly to Miriam’s blood.

At twenty-eight, Miriam was a cartographer of memories, mapping not just lands but families, histories, and fragmented identities stitched together by migration. Her work involved unearthing the routes taken by those who left their homes—not always by choice—and the roots they planted in new soil, sometimes thriving, sometimes uprooted, always evolving.

First Stop: The Village of Whispers

Miriam’s first destination was a small village nestled in the crook of a river delta where her mother’s lineage began—now a ghost of a place swollen with the echoes of abandoned markets and silent streets. She walked barefoot through earth hardened by drought, eyes searching for traces of lives displaced.

“The elders say our ancestors packed nothing but their faith and stories,” her guide told her one evening as they sat beneath a swollen mango tree, gnarled like the village’s survival itself. “When drought took the river, many had no choice but to leave. Not everyone returned.”

Migration was never a simple physical crossing; it was a passage through spiritual and emotional landscapes, each departure a loss of a familiar rhythm of life. The villagers’ voices were rich with tales of forced exodus — memories of Goodbye nights pressed between hope and despair.

Here, Miriam learned how migration embedded itself in songs—the melodies held the geography of routes traveled, the pain of separation, and the hunger for belonging. These oral maps, transmitted across generations, were living archives.

The Sea Route: A Tale Written in Salt

From the delta, Miriam journeyed to the rocky shores of a port city where her great-grandmother had boarded a ship decades prior. This city was a mosaic of languages and promises, its skyline speckled with cranes.

She visited the harbor at dawn, where fishermen hauled their daily catch against the backdrop of an awakening city. The sea was restless, its surface sparkling deceptively calm—a reminder of countless crossings marked by peril and resilience.

Miriam met Fatima, an elderly woman whose father had sailed across the same waters. Fatima’s house smelled of cumin and old paper, a testament to lives stretched between continents. “The sea is both thief and giver,” she whispered, handing Miriam a worn notebook filled with sketches of boats and notations of tides.

Fatima recounted how her family’s migration was not an escape from famine, but a search for education and dignity—a less visible kind of route, motivated by aspiration rather than desperation.

Across the pages, Miriam traced the lines of countless journeys—the ebb and flow of desire, displacement, and identity.

Urban Roots: Building Home Afar

The final leg brought Miriam to the sprawling metropolis where generations of her family settled and planted their roots in foreign soil. Here, concrete towers replaced ancient trees, and neon signs buzzed incessantly.

In a cluttered café filled with the aroma of cardamom chai, she met Rafi, her cousin, who was born in this city but carried the stories of hunger and hope etched into his father’s voice. Rafi worked as a community organizer, striving to preserve cultural heritage amid the relentless tide of assimilation.

They spoke of roots—not just the biological kind but cultural and emotional anchors that tethered them to the past yet resisted stagnation. Community centers, language classes, and festivals were deliberate attempts to weave old traditions into new realities.

Miriam realized that migration was not a severing of roots but a reconfiguration—the weaving of routes that allowed fragmented identities to find coherence in a diaspora.

The Cartography of Belonging

As the sun set over the skyline, Miriam opened her notebook, now bulging with stories, sketches, and songs. Each migration journey was a thread in the tapestry of human resilience—a map charted not only in physical space but in memory, language, and culture.

Roots and routes were inseparable: to move was to carry seeds of home, and to establish roots was to honor the routes traveled. These stories, fragmented and diverse, illuminated the complex nature of migration—a dance between loss and gain, departure and arrival, history and future.

Her journey was far from over, but with every story collected, Miriam felt a deeper connection to her roots and an appreciation for the routes that weave the world’s peoples together in a shared human story.

With a final look at the fading light, Miriam folded her journal and prepared for the next journey, knowing that migration was more than movement—it was the relentless pursuit of belonging.

End of Story
