The Trolley Problem Revisited: A Philosophical Debate on Consequentialism and Morality


The sun had long since dipped beneath the horizon, casting an ethereal glow across the open fields. The quiet hum of a distant highway was the only noise that interrupted the stillness of the night. But for Professor Elena Garrison, it was a momentary distraction, one she barely noticed as her mind grappled with something much heavier.

The Trolley Problem.

She sat in her study, leaning against her wooden desk, as papers scattered across the surface reflected the soft light of the desk lamp. Her latest academic pursuit had centered on revisiting this age-old ethical dilemma — the one that posed the question of whether it is morally acceptable to sacrifice one life to save many. It had started as a simple exercise in her philosophy class, a hypothetical that would generate some thoughtful discussion. But over the years, Elena found herself returning to it more and more, until it became an obsession.

It wasn’t just about the decision itself, though that was important. It was about the framework in which such decisions existed. Consequentialism and deontological ethics had been at odds in her readings for as long as she could remember, and here, the Trolley Problem illuminated their tension like no other. The stakes weren’t just academic. They were existential.

Her phone buzzed on the desk, a reminder from her colleague, Dr. Samuel Gray, to meet at the old university café the following afternoon. It had been weeks since they’d spoken, and the brief exchange of words seemed to rekindle her earlier thoughts about the dilemma.

What if the problem was more than just a simple choice? What if the situation itself was a construct — a trap designed to push us into making decisions that weren’t really decisions at all? Elena’s mind wandered back to her last class discussion.

The Dilemma

“Imagine,” Elena began, her voice calm but her eyes intense, as she set the stage for her students, “you are standing beside a lever on a trolley track. The trolley is speeding toward five individuals who are helplessly tied to the tracks, unable to move. The only way to save them is to pull the lever, redirecting the trolley onto a different track where one person is tied. If you pull the lever, you save the five, but you sacrifice the one. If you do nothing, the five will die.”

Her students fidgeted uneasily in their seats. The air was thick with tension. A few raised their hands.

“Does anyone think it’s wrong to pull the lever?” she asked.

A student in the back hesitated before speaking. “But… isn’t it still murder? You’re choosing to kill one person. Doesn’t that make you just as responsible for their death?”

Elena nodded. “That’s the deontological perspective — the moral law states that it’s wrong to intentionally cause harm, no matter the outcome. This approach suggests that morality is about duty, not consequences.”

She could see the student’s confusion. “But what if you do nothing and the five people die? Are you not responsible for their deaths, too?” she pressed.

Another student raised their hand, a bit more confident now. “That’s a consequentialist view, right? The outcome matters more than the action itself. If the consequence is saving five lives, then pulling the lever is the morally right thing to do.”

“Exactly,” Elena agreed. “And this is where the tension lies. For a consequentialist, the goal is to maximize the good — in this case, saving five lives at the cost of one. But for a deontologist, it’s the act that matters, not the result. In this case, choosing to cause harm, even for a seemingly good reason, is inherently wrong.”

The debate continued for the rest of the lecture. Students were divided, each offering their perspectives. But as the class ended and they filed out, Elena was left alone, contemplating the limitations of both approaches. She had started to wonder if the Trolley Problem itself — a simplistic scenario designed to test the limits of moral reasoning — could be the problem. Perhaps morality wasn’t about following a set of rules or achieving the best possible outcome. Perhaps it was about something deeper. Something more complex.

The Revised Dilemma

The next day, Elena was sitting in her office, still lost in thought, when Dr. Samuel Gray arrived. He was an old friend, one she had worked with on many philosophical projects over the years. His sharp intellect and ability to argue for the most difficult positions made him an ideal interlocutor for Elena’s ongoing intellectual struggles.

“Have you been thinking about the Trolley Problem again?” Samuel asked, taking a seat across from her.

“I have,” Elena replied, tapping her pen on the desk. “But I’ve been revisiting it in a new light. What if the problem itself is flawed? What if we’ve been approaching it the wrong way all along?”

Samuel raised an eyebrow. “How so?”

Elena leaned forward, excited by her new train of thought. “What if the true issue isn’t about whether or not we should pull the lever? What if it’s about how we view human life and suffering in the first place? The Trolley Problem assumes we can assign value to human life in a way that makes sense mathematically — five lives versus one. But can we really do that? Can we reduce people to mere numbers?”

Samuel thought for a moment before responding. “I see what you’re getting at. You’re challenging the very foundation of the dilemma. What if life isn’t something we can quantify? What if the choice to pull the lever is already a fallacy because it doesn’t represent the real complexity of moral decision-making?”

“Exactly,” Elena said, her excitement growing. “What if our moral compass isn’t based on outcomes or duties at all, but on relationships? What if morality is about the connections we share with others, the empathy we feel for their suffering, and the responsibility we have to care for each other?”

Samuel paused, then leaned back in his chair. “So, you’re suggesting we scrap both the consequentialist and deontological frameworks entirely and focus on something more relational? I can see the appeal, but that would radically alter how we approach moral dilemmas.”

Elena smiled. “That’s exactly what I’m suggesting. Maybe we should start looking at these problems in terms of moral pluralism — understanding that different moral principles may apply in different situations, and none of them can give us a clear-cut answer to everything.”

The Test

A week later, Elena’s students were in for a surprise. Instead of the usual lecture, she had devised a new exercise. She set up the same trolley scenario, but with a twist: they had to decide not just whether to pull the lever, but also how to engage with the situation.

As they discussed the problem, Elena guided them to consider different perspectives: What if the one person on the track was someone they knew personally? What if the five people were strangers, but one of them was a child, someone they could imagine their own child growing up to be? What if they were responsible for the track being there in the first place, making them complicit in the situation?

One student, Sarah, raised her hand halfway through the debate. “I think I understand what you’re saying now. It’s not just about numbers or rules. It’s about the people involved — their lives, their relationships, and our duty to care for them. The moral thing to do might not always be the same in every situation. It depends on who we are, who they are, and what’s at stake.”

Elena nodded. “Exactly. You can’t just simplify these situations down to a formula. Life is too complex for that. And while consequentialism and deontology each offer valuable insights, they can’t fully capture the richness of human experience.”

As the class wrapped up, Elena felt a sense of satisfaction. She had guided her students to see that the Trolley Problem wasn’t just a test of moral philosophy — it was a mirror for the complexities of real life. There would be no easy answers, no clear-cut decisions. But that was the nature of morality: it wasn’t about finding one perfect solution. It was about engaging with the problem, questioning our assumptions, and striving to do what’s right, even when the path forward was uncertain.

In the end, it wasn’t about the lever. It was about the people.

The Conclusion

Later that evening, Elena sat in her favorite armchair, sipping tea and reflecting on the day’s discussions. She realized that her obsession with the Trolley Problem had led her somewhere deeper than she had anticipated. The real question wasn’t whether to pull the lever. It was about the kind of world we want to create. A world where we reduce people to numbers? Or one where we understand and empathize with the intricacies of human existence?

As the night deepened, Elena smiled. It was a question that didn’t need a final answer. The pursuit of understanding was the real journey. And with that, she closed her eyes, feeling the quiet hum of the world around her — a reminder that even in the silence, the complexities of life continued to unfold.
