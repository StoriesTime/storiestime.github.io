Existential Questions Through Engaging Short Stories


The city was quiet that evening, as it often was at the edge of dusk. The last remnants of daylight clung to the horizon, their orange glow stretching across the sky like the faded remnants of a forgotten dream. Beneath this fading light, an old man sat on a weathered bench in the park. His name was Dr. Elias Carter, a philosopher in his late sixties, with a beard as white as the clouds above him. He had spent much of his life searching for answers to questions that seemed too vast, too elusive for anyone to grasp.

But now, in the twilight of his years, the questions had begun to turn inward. They no longer had anything to do with the world outside. The thoughts that filled his mind these days were deeply personal. Who was he, really? Why did he feel the passage of time so intensely? And more than that: What did it all mean?

He had read thousands of books, studied countless theories, and attended seminars that had promised answers. But in the end, he was left with the same uncertainty that had plagued him from the beginning. He had discovered more about the vastness of the universe and the intricacies of the human mind, but he had not found peace. If anything, his understanding of existence had only deepened the complexity of his own internal struggle.

As Dr. Carter sat on the bench, staring at the fading sunlight, his mind wandered back to a time in his youth when he had believed that answers were waiting to be discovered. There had been a time when the questions seemed exciting—an invitation to learn, to explore. But now, the pursuit of answers felt exhausting, like an endless cycle of chasing after shadows.

“Perhaps there are no answers,” he muttered to himself, more a statement of resignation than realization.

A soft breeze rustled the leaves around him, carrying with it the scent of earth and fresh grass. In the distance, a young woman jogged past him, earbuds in, lost in the rhythm of her own world. For a moment, Dr. Carter envied her—her carefree existence, her ability to be immersed in the present without the burden of endless contemplation.

But he couldn’t escape the thoughts that occupied his mind. They swirled around him, asking questions that had no immediate answers. What was the purpose of it all? Was there even a purpose? Or was life, as some philosophers had suggested, simply a series of random events, a cosmic accident that somehow led to consciousness?

As he pondered these thoughts, a shadow fell across the bench. Dr. Carter looked up to find a young man standing in front of him, holding a cup of coffee in his hand.

“Mind if I sit?” the young man asked, his voice soft but clear.

Dr. Carter gestured to the empty space beside him, and the young man sat down. He was in his early thirties, with dark hair and a weary expression that suggested he had been carrying the weight of something heavy on his mind. The two men sat in silence for a moment, watching the fading light.

“Nice evening,” the young man finally said, though his tone betrayed that he wasn’t entirely focused on the weather.

Dr. Carter nodded. “Yes. But evening is a curious thing, isn’t it? It’s neither day nor night, a space between two states. Like life itself, perhaps.”

The young man glanced at him, intrigued. “What do you mean by that?”

Dr. Carter chuckled softly. “I’ve spent most of my life searching for meaning, for answers. But the more I search, the more I find myself asking the same questions. And lately, I’ve begun to wonder if there’s even a point to it all. Perhaps life is like this twilight—always in between, never fully one thing or the other.”

The young man took a sip from his coffee, his eyes narrowing in thought. “I get that. I’ve been feeling the same way lately. Like there’s something I should be doing, something I’m supposed to understand, but I don’t. It’s like… everything is just happening, and I’m not sure if I’m really living it or just watching it pass by.”

“Exactly,” Dr. Carter said. “It’s as if we’re both observers and participants in this strange, fleeting dance. But what’s the purpose of it? What’s the meaning of this act of existence?”

The young man’s eyes softened as he looked out at the park. “I don’t know. But maybe that’s the point. Maybe we’re not supposed to have all the answers. Maybe it’s the uncertainty that makes it all meaningful.”

Dr. Carter considered this for a moment. “You could be right. Perhaps the search for meaning is less about finding a concrete answer and more about embracing the journey, the act of questioning itself.”

The young man smiled, a small, thoughtful smile. “I think I like that idea.”

For a few minutes, the two of them sat in quiet contemplation. The park around them had emptied, and the last traces of sunlight had disappeared, leaving only the soft glow of streetlamps and the cool evening air. It was a time for reflection, a time to look inward.

“Tell me,” Dr. Carter said after a while, his voice gentle, “what brings you here tonight? What’s on your mind?”

The young man hesitated before answering. “I’m at a crossroads in my life. I’m not sure what direction to take. I’ve always tried to follow a clear path, but lately, nothing seems certain anymore. I’m questioning everything—my job, my relationships, even who I am. And I don’t know if that’s a sign that something’s wrong, or if it’s just a part of the process.”

Dr. Carter nodded. “You’re not alone in feeling that way. I think we all go through moments like that. The trouble is, we often believe that we need to have everything figured out, that we should know exactly where we’re headed. But the truth is, life is more about the questions than the answers. The uncertainty is what makes us alive, what makes us human.”

The young man seemed to take comfort in these words. He looked out at the horizon, his gaze distant. “It’s strange, though. I feel like I should have it all figured out by now. Everyone around me seems so sure of themselves. Why does it feel like I’m the only one who’s uncertain?”

Dr. Carter smiled softly. “Because uncertainty is part of being human. No one has all the answers. No one can claim to know the absolute meaning of life. The best we can do is to accept that uncertainty is a part of our existence and to live with it.”

The young man thought about this for a long time. “It’s comforting to hear that. Maybe I don’t need to have everything figured out. Maybe I just need to take things one day at a time.”

“Exactly,” Dr. Carter said. “Life is not a puzzle to be solved, but a mystery to be lived. The questions will always be there. They may never have answers, but that doesn’t mean they’re not worth asking.”

The young man smiled, the weight of his own thoughts lightened, if only for a moment. “Thank you, Dr. Carter. I think I understand now.”

As the evening deepened and the city around them grew quieter, the two men sat together on the bench, sharing in the silence, each lost in their own thoughts. They had discovered, in their brief conversation, something that transcended the need for answers: the shared experience of questioning, of seeking, and of living with the uncertainty that defined their existence.

And for the first time in a long while, Dr. Carter felt a sense of peace. The questions didn’t have to be answered. They were simply part of the journey—part of the beautiful, uncertain dance of life.

As the night fell completely, he closed his eyes and let the world drift around him. There was nothing more to do. There was nothing more to say. Just being was enough.
