The Final Stroke: Betrayal Carved in Blood


A sharp wind swept across the empty street, rattling the bare branches of the old oaks. The faint smell of burning wood mixed with the dampness of the earth, and a sense of foreboding lingered in the air. The moon was full, casting an eerie glow across the scene. In the alleyway, where shadows danced like restless spirits, a single figure stood at the edge, his breath shallow and quick.

Victor’s heart pounded as he gripped the hilt of the sword. His knuckles were white from the force. Sweat beaded on his brow, even in the chill of the night. It was an hour past midnight, the time he had promised himself for this moment. The time when everything would change.

He had waited for years, kept his silence, built up the rage that simmered beneath his calm demeanor. His thoughts flashed back to the day when it all began, to the first whispers of betrayal that began to poison the people he trusted most.

Victor had never believed in fate. He believed in power, in the strength of the will, and in the loyalty of those who fought beside him. A seasoned soldier, he had climbed the ranks of the Kingdom of Verdania not through bloodline or wealth, but through sheer determination and an unwavering sense of duty. His reputation was solid, his name respected in every corner of the kingdom.

And then came the whispers.

The betrayal was subtle at first, like the quiet sound of a stone being dropped into a still pond. Small rumors here and there, something offhanded said behind his back. His closest friends—his brothers in arms—had begun to act strange. But he was too focused on his own rise to notice, too caught up in the intricate politics of the kingdom’s court to suspect anything beneath the surface.

But soon, the whispers became louder, more insistent. And then came the letter.

It had arrived on a cold morning, sealed with the royal crest. His chest tightened as he broke the seal, already knowing what it would say. The words were written in the hand of his closest confidant, a man named Eryan—his comrade, his right hand.

Victor,

By the time you read this, I will have already acted. I’ve been working in the shadows, aligning myself with the kingdom’s true power. Our loyalty to each other is a bond that cannot be severed, but I must choose my future over past friendships. Forgive me.

E.

Victor had stared at the letter for what seemed like an eternity. He had thought he knew Eryan, thought he knew the depths of their bond. But this? This was a betrayal of the highest order. Eryan had not just turned on him—he had turned on everything they had built together. The entire kingdom’s political landscape had shifted, and in the span of a few days, the man Victor trusted most had become an enemy.

The weight of the revelation sank into his bones. Eryan had joined forces with the king’s most trusted advisor, a scheming serpent named Archibald. They had set their sights on the throne, and in the process, Victor was cast aside like a pawn.

Months had passed since the letter, but the betrayal still stung like an open wound. Victor had tried to move on, tried to focus on other matters, but every moment he spent in the kingdom was a reminder of what had been taken from him.

And so, he bided his time.

The kingdom was preparing for a great feast to celebrate the harvest season—a grand event where nobles from every corner of the land would gather. Victor knew this would be the perfect moment to strike. It was a dangerous gamble, but the only one that could bring closure to the years of pain.

The night of the feast arrived.

Victor had attended many of these gatherings over the years, always standing in the shadows, watching the nobility eat, drink, and exchange false pleasantries. But tonight, he wasn’t there to observe. Tonight, he was there to make sure Eryan paid for his treachery.

As he entered the grand hall, he felt the eyes of the guests fall upon him. It wasn’t a warm reception. Most of them had heard the rumors of his fall from grace, but none dared speak openly. Victor’s name was no longer spoken with reverence. Now it was only whispered in hushed tones, if it was spoken at all.

He moved through the crowd, his cloak swirling behind him like a shadow. The grand chandelier overhead gleamed with a thousand lights, casting long shadows across the walls. The nobles were oblivious to the storm brewing beneath the surface. They were too caught up in their own games to notice the figure slipping through their midst.

Victor’s eyes scanned the room, searching for the one man who had caused him so much anguish. And there he was—Eryan, standing by the king, a glint of satisfaction in his eyes. He was dressed in fine silks, his posture regal, and yet there was a hint of nervousness behind his smile. As if he knew something was coming.

Victor’s hand tightened on the hilt of the sword beneath his cloak. He had made his choice.

This would be the final stroke.

Eryan turned as Victor approached, his smile faltering for a brief moment. “Victor,” he said, his voice laced with false warmth. “It’s been a long time. I trust you’re well?”

Victor did not return the smile. Instead, his gaze was cold, sharp as steel. “I was well, until I learned of your betrayal.”

The words hit Eryan like a slap to the face. His eyes widened, but before he could respond, Victor drew the blade in a single fluid motion.

The sword glinted in the light, a streak of silver that seemed to tear through the air itself. Eryan’s expression shifted to one of disbelief as the blade pressed against his throat. “You betrayed me, Eryan,” Victor said, his voice low and steady. “You betrayed everything we stood for.”

Eryan’s breath caught in his throat. “Victor… Please…” he stammered. “You don’t understand. This wasn’t… this wasn’t about you.”

Victor’s lips curled into a cruel smile. “Oh, I understand perfectly. You chose power over loyalty. You chose to be the king’s puppet instead of a man of honor.”

The room fell silent, the guests frozen in shock at the confrontation. The king, standing nearby, watched with a mixture of amusement and apprehension. He knew the power that Victor held, knew that this could be the end of everything if he wasn’t careful.

“You can’t do this,” Eryan pleaded, his voice shaking. “You’ll ruin everything.”

Victor’s expression hardened. “It was already ruined the moment you chose to betray me.”

And then, with one swift motion, he brought the blade down.

The feast descended into chaos. Screams filled the hall as Eryan collapsed to the floor, blood pouring from the wound in his neck. The guests scattered, some running for the exits, others cowering in place, too terrified to move. The king, who had been content to watch from the sidelines, stood frozen, his face pale with shock.

Victor stood over the body, the blood still fresh on the ground beneath him. His breath was heavy, his chest rising and falling with the weight of what he had just done. But there was no satisfaction in it. There was no relief. The betrayal had been too deep, and the wound too severe.

It was over.

Victor turned his back on the chaos he had created and left the hall, the sword still in his hand. The night air was cold against his skin, the only sound the distant echoes of the kingdom’s disarray. The blade, now stained with blood, was the final symbol of a friendship lost—a betrayal carved in blood.

Victor never returned to the kingdom. Some say he disappeared into the wilderness, never to be seen again. Others believe he traveled far from Verdania, seeking solace in distant lands where no one knew his name. But no matter where he went, the memory of that night—of the final stroke—lingered with him forever.

The kingdom of Verdania was never the same after that night. Eryan’s death had sent shockwaves through the court, and Archibald’s plans were laid bare for all to see. The power struggle that followed tore the kingdom apart, and no one emerged unscathed.

Victor, though, found something else. In the darkness of betrayal, he had learned that loyalty could be both a strength and a weakness, and that sometimes, the only way to reclaim what had been taken from you was to take it back by force.

The final stroke was not just the end of a friendship—it was the beginning of a new world. One where betrayal would no longer be tolerated, and the blood of the innocent would not be spilled in vain.
