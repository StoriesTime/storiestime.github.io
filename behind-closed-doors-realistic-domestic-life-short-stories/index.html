Behind Closed Doors: Realistic Domestic Life Short Stories


The house was quiet, except for the soft hum of the refrigerator and the occasional creak of the old wooden floors as the wind moved through the cracks in the walls. It was an afternoon like any other in the Thompson household. Yet, if anyone had been watching closely, they would have seen the delicate tension in the air. It was one of those days when everything appeared normal on the surface, but beneath, there was a quiet unraveling.

Inside, on the worn-out couch in the living room, Kate Thompson sat, her hands tightly gripping a half-empty coffee cup. The mug was chipped, a tiny crack running along its rim. She stared into the dark liquid as if searching for answers that had long eluded her. The house around her was filled with the echoes of a life she had once dreamt of, yet it felt distant now, like a story told by someone else.

Her husband, Mark, was in the kitchen, his back to her as he rummaged through the cabinets. The rhythm of his movements was mechanical, methodical. He was used to the silence, used to the way their relationship had quietly shifted over the years.

“Do you want some lunch?” Mark’s voice broke the silence, though it was more of a habit than an actual offer.

Kate didn’t respond immediately. Instead, she took another sip of her coffee, her thoughts drifting back to the days before their life had become so predictable. Before the routine, before the silence. It hadn’t always been like this. They had once been full of hope and energy, both excited to build their life together. But now, it was as if they were just passing through the motions.

Mark turned around, his eyes scanning her face. She could see the familiar look in his eyes, the one that said he was waiting for her to respond, to engage. But she didn’t know how to anymore.

“I’m not hungry,” Kate replied, her voice quiet, almost lost in the stillness of the room.

Mark didn’t press her further. Instead, he returned to his task, chopping vegetables for his own lunch. The sound of the knife against the cutting board echoed through the kitchen, a constant reminder of the small, mundane tasks that filled their days.

Kate set the coffee mug down, her fingers lingering on the rim for a moment before pulling away. She stood up slowly, her body stiff from sitting too long. Her mind was a mess of conflicting thoughts, and she didn’t know how to sort through them.

She walked toward the window and looked out at the small backyard, where the overgrown grass swayed in the breeze. The garden, once carefully tended, was now neglected, much like the state of their relationship. She couldn’t remember the last time they had worked together on something, or even when they had shared a conversation that wasn’t about bills or the kids’ school schedules.

Their daughter, Emily, was at school, and their son, Jake, was at his friend’s house, so for the first time in what felt like ages, the house was empty. The silence between them stretched long, like a taut wire, threatening to snap at any moment. But neither of them seemed willing to be the one to break it.

“Mark,” Kate finally said, her voice trembling ever so slightly. “Do you ever wonder… where we went wrong?”

Mark didn’t immediately respond. He paused in his chopping, the knife hovering in midair for a second before he lowered it to the board. He didn’t turn to face her, but she could feel his attention shift.

“Wrong?” His voice was guarded, like he was choosing his words carefully. “I don’t think anything went ‘wrong.’ Things just… changed. Life happens.”

Kate nodded, though she wasn’t convinced. “But we used to have fun, didn’t we? I remember when we would stay up late talking about our dreams. About the future. It felt like nothing could stop us.”

Mark sighed, the weight of the years settling between them. “I remember that too. But life has a way of… grinding things down, Kate. It’s not always about dreams. Sometimes, it’s just about surviving the day.”

Kate felt a lump form in her throat. She had heard that explanation a hundred times before, but it never made it feel any easier. She wanted to believe him, wanted to believe that this was just a phase, that they could somehow find their way back to each other. But the more she thought about it, the more she realized how distant they had become.

Mark turned back to his work, his attention returning to the task at hand. The kitchen light flickered once, then went out, leaving them in a dim, muted light. Kate couldn’t help but notice how the room suddenly seemed smaller, as if the space around them had closed in. It felt suffocating, but there was no escape.

It wasn’t always like this. Kate remembered the early days of their marriage, when Mark would surprise her with flowers for no reason, when they would spend weekends exploring the city or going on long walks without a care in the world. They had been in love once, she was sure of it. But somewhere along the way, that love had shifted, eroded by the daily grind of life.

Their children had been a blessing, but they had also brought stress. Emily, their headstrong teenager, seemed to always be in conflict with them, while Jake, their quiet ten-year-old, was starting to retreat into his own world. There was never a moment of calm, no space where they could simply be themselves.

Kate closed her eyes, trying to remember when she had last felt truly seen, truly heard by Mark. Was it last year? The year before? Had it ever truly happened in the way she needed it to?

The sound of Mark’s voice pulled her back to the present.

“I’m going to take a shower,” he said, sounding tired. “I’ll be out in a bit.”

Kate nodded, though she didn’t speak. The water would run, and the silence would return. And then what? Would they just continue on in this endless loop of days, pretending that everything was fine?

She wasn’t sure how much longer she could live like this. The cracks in their relationship were getting harder to ignore, and no amount of silence could patch them up. But what could she do? She had tried to talk to him before, but the words always seemed to get lost in the air between them, hanging there, unspoken.

As Mark walked down the hallway toward their bathroom, Kate found herself staring at the family photos on the wall. There were smiles in those pictures—genuine, carefree moments frozen in time. But now, when she looked at them, they seemed like someone else’s memories. A different life.

Her phone buzzed on the coffee table, pulling her from her thoughts. She picked it up, expecting a message from Emily or a reminder about Jake’s playdate, but instead, it was an email notification. Kate opened it absentmindedly, but then her eyes froze on the words: “Reminder: Therapy appointment tomorrow at 10:00 AM.”

She hadn’t forgotten about the therapy sessions they had started attending a few months ago. The idea of working on their marriage had seemed promising at first, but now, she wasn’t so sure. They had been to two sessions already, but nothing had really changed. They sat there, talked about their feelings, but the connection between them still felt frayed, like an old sweater that was unraveling at the seams.

Kate placed the phone back down, the words from the email lingering in her mind. She had come to a realization: therapy might not be enough. She couldn’t fix something that wasn’t willing to be fixed. But was it too late? Was it too late to ask for more than just surviving?

Mark emerged from the bathroom, his hair damp from the shower. He glanced at her, but she saw the distance in his eyes, the same distance that had been there for so long.

“I’ll be in my office if you need me,” he said, his voice almost flat.

Kate watched him retreat down the hallway, and for the first time in a long while, she felt completely alone.

The sun began to set outside, casting long shadows across the living room floor. Kate stood up once more, her movements slow and deliberate. She walked to the door, her hand resting on the handle as she looked out at the fading light.

Maybe it was time to stop pretending. Maybe it was time to stop hoping that things would get better on their own. She had tried. She had fought for this family, for this life. But she couldn’t do it alone.

As the first star appeared in the darkening sky, Kate made a decision. She wasn’t going to wait for Mark anymore. She wasn’t going to wait for some magical shift to happen. She had to take the next step, whatever that might be.

She turned away from the door and walked back to the couch, the silence settling around her like a familiar cloak. The day had been long, and tomorrow would come, but for now, Kate sat there, the weight of the moment heavy on her chest, but with a sense of clarity that had eluded her for so long.

The door was closed, but the story was far from over.

The End
