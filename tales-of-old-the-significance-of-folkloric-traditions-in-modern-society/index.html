Tales of Old: The Significance of Folkloric Traditions in Modern Society


Rain drizzled down the glass panes of the Groveview Public Library, each streak catching the faint glow of the setting sun. Ava Drell tightened her grip on the ancient tome she had borrowed from the archives. Its worn leather cover smelled of earth and decay, the kind of scent that whispered of centuries gone by and the hands that had leafed through its brittle pages. What was it about old stories, she often wondered, that clung to the human spirit like roots to soil? Why had they endured, weaving themselves into the fabric of modern existence?

She had stumbled across the book almost by accident while conducting research for her graduate thesis. The thesis—aptly titled Tales of Old: The Significance of Folkloric Traditions in Modern Society—was her attempt to examine how stories from the past continued to shape the present. The book she now held, The Mirror of Naldor, was a rare volume of Western Mirkvanian folktales, a culture almost forgotten by history. Its entry on “Naldor’s Mirror” had particularly caught Ava’s attention.

The tale was simple, as most old fables often were. Naldor, a humble blacksmith with a heart weighed down by doubt, forged a mirror designed to reflect not appearance but truth. The legend claimed that those who gazed into the mirror would see their innermost self—untainted by vanity, pride, or illusion. Some who peered into Naldor’s mirror were comforted by what they saw. Others, overcome by despair, turned away from the revelation, unable to reconcile with the truth of who they were.

Centuries had passed since Mirkvania had vanished from the map, but the story of Naldor remained, circulating in fractured pieces across cultures. Ava had seen scattered hints of it in literature, art, and even popular culture. Just last week, she’d watched a film where a tarnished antique mirror unraveled secrets its owners had tried to hide. The threads of the past, she realized, never fully frayed—they wove themselves into the tapestry of the present in ways both subtle and overt.

But as modern as the echoes of the tale appeared, something about Naldor’s story gnawed at her. What truth could be so universal, so enduring, that even when its context was lost to time, its essence persisted? The rain outside intensified, hammering against the windows as Ava sank deeper into her thoughts.

The following morning, Ava decided to venture beyond theory and immerse herself in practice. She emailed her advisor at the university, declaring she needed to shift her approach for the thesis. Instead of examining folktales in textbooks, she would investigate the lived experiences of people who embraced traditional stories in their everyday lives. Her advisor was skeptical but intrigued, offering enough grant money to allow her three months of field research.

A map unfolded in Ava’s mind, plotting a journey across the globe, from tiny fishing villages in remote Icelandic fjords to bustling urban enclaves where traditional myths were revived in street art and festivals. It was a journey to see how folklore, like Naldor’s mirror, revealed truths about human society.

A week later, she stood ankle-deep in the cold waters of the Lyra Fjord, clasping the hand of a shaman who belonged to an ancient Icelandic lineage. The woman, whose name translated to “Keeper of Shadows,” agreed to show Ava how the stories of her ancestors bound the fragile community together through the long, dark winters. Her eyes crinkling at the edges, the keeper spoke of the River Spirit, a figure who lived in the currents and reflected one’s ancestral strength in the ripples. Each time Ava asked more, the shaman challenged her to look into the waters herself. The icy mirror below whispered the folklore’s enduring power: that reflection of strength could still guide people through their modern trials.

From Iceland, Ava flew eastward, arriving in Japan to attend the Yuki Matsuri, a festival devoted to the spirit of winter. Here, local legends were celebrated with towering sculptures crafted entirely of snow—majestic representations of Yuki-onna, the female apparition whose ethereal presence mirrored humanity’s reverence for the natural world. In conversations over warm bowls of miso soup, Ava met artists and storytellers who wove this ancient folklore into the rhythms of their contemporary lives. For them, the past wasn’t a distant place; it was a compass directing the present.

Yet perhaps Ava’s most profound experience lay across continents, deep within a remote Amazonian village. It was here that she found communities who remained largely untouched by globalized technology yet were deeply engaged in narratives that embraced the earth. Ava spent a week learning about the “Mother of Root,” a legendary figure symbolizing reciprocity between earth and man. Villagers spoke of her as both a myth and a guide, a reminder of the delicate balance needed even in the modern era to ensure survival. Ava realized that these ancient stories were not relics—they were tools reimagined to confront present challenges, from environmental degradation to social cohesion.

Through her journey, Ava grasped what had eluded her in the pages of dusty books. Folktales persisted because they were mirrors of the human condition, reflections of the fears, hopes, and desires that remained timeless. The motifs laid out in stories like Naldor’s mirror resonated because they transcended their original cultures. Truth, identity, and the human need to belong and understand were not bound by borders or centuries—they were universal.

When she finally returned home, laden with journals full of notes and a mind bursting with connections, Ava approached her thesis with renewed fervor. She began recounting not only the origins of various tales but also their transformation and reemergence in contemporary contexts. From Tolkien’s adaptation of European myths to the rise of animated films celebrating African gods, Ava unraveled a complex network of interdependence, where old traditions offered stability to a rapidly changing world.

Her greatest realization wasn’t in folklore’s ability to survive but in its necessity to thrive. In ages rife with uncertainty—whether from climate crises, political divisions, or personal existential struggles—these stories offered clarity. They forced modern society to look into its collective mirror, much like Naldor’s creation, and see what truths lay hidden beneath the chaos.

Five years later, holding her published book in hand, Ava stood before an auditorium filled with both academics and the general public. She concluded her presentation with words that encapsulated her journey:

“Folklore is not a relic; it is a bridge. It carries humanity across the vast chasms of history, whispering truths that resonate in every era. The question isn’t whether these stories are relevant in the modern world. The question is: will we dare to look into their mirrored truths?”

The applause was thunderous.

As Ava closed her laptop and left the stage, she noticed something in the reflective glass doors of the auditorium—her own face. For a brief moment, she remembered Naldor’s mirror and wondered what truths she might see if it existed today. Perhaps she already knew. The answers, after all, had been there all along, hidden within the tales of old.
