Unshackled: A Personal Story of Addiction and Recovery


The sun had barely risen when I felt the familiar buzz of my phone in the pocket of my hoodie. I looked at the screen and sighed, seeing the name of an old friend, someone I hadn’t heard from in years. My thumb hesitated over the call button. I had no interest in talking about the past, the memories that would undoubtedly come flooding back. But something in me, some old craving for connection, urged me to swipe and answer.

“Hey, it’s been a while,” she said, her voice warm, but there was a noticeable hesitation, as if she was testing the waters before diving in.

“Yeah, a while.” I could barely recognize the voice that came out of my mouth. My own words felt foreign, like they didn’t belong to me anymore.

We exchanged a few pleasantries, but it didn’t take long for the conversation to shift, as conversations often do, into the deeper, more vulnerable territories we once shared. It was about our lives, where we had ended up, and what had happened to us. I knew what she wanted to ask. I had known for years.

“How are you really?” she finally asked, and I could tell she meant more than just a casual “How’s life?”

The question hit me like a punch to the gut. “I’m fine,” I said quickly, but I could feel the lie curling in my throat, choking the truth that wanted to spill out.

We both knew the answer. After all, she had witnessed the transformation—the shift from carefree teenager to someone who couldn’t hold it together anymore. She knew about the drugs, the late nights, the desperate need to escape everything that life was throwing at me.

But that version of me, the one who was always seeking oblivion, no longer existed. Or at least, that’s what I had convinced myself.

The truth, though, was far more complicated.

It had started so innocently, with a couple of drinks at a party, then a pill here and there to “take the edge off.” It wasn’t long before I was chasing that first high, that feeling of total liberation, that fleeting moment where everything felt lighter, where the weight of my thoughts didn’t hold me down. At first, I convinced myself it was just a phase. I was in college, everyone experimented, right?

But somewhere along the way, that phase turned into something far darker, something I couldn’t control. It wasn’t about the fun anymore. It was about the escape. The numbing.

My life outside of the high began to erode. Relationships became strained, my studies faltered, and slowly, I withdrew from everything that mattered. The world around me became a blur, and the only thing that felt real was the next high, the next fix. I spent years in this cycle—caught between shame and the desperate pull of addiction.

I tried to quit a few times, of course. Who hasn’t? The first couple of attempts felt like breaking free from a cage that had been slowly closing in on me. But each time, the withdrawal was too intense, the cravings too overwhelming. I felt weak, like I wasn’t strong enough to fight this battle. Eventually, I stopped trying to quit altogether.

What I didn’t know was that every time I relapsed, I was digging myself into a deeper hole. The addiction wasn’t just physical. It was mental, emotional. It reshaped my entire existence. I became someone I didn’t recognize—someone I loathed.

It took a crisis to make me face the truth.

I don’t remember exactly how it all fell apart. One moment, I was sitting in a cheap motel room, alone and high, and the next, I was in an ambulance. My body had given out, and I had no choice but to finally confront the damage I had done to myself.

When I woke up in the hospital, I realized something crucial: I was at rock bottom. And the only way from there was up. I had to make a decision: keep going down this path of destruction, or try something different.

I chose recovery.

The road to recovery wasn’t a straight one. In fact, it was anything but. There were moments when I wanted to give up, moments when the cravings were so intense that I thought I might lose my mind. But something had shifted in me. I had tasted what life could be like without the cloud of addiction, and I wasn’t willing to go back.

I sought help. I joined a rehab program, started attending meetings, and for the first time in a long while, I began to speak the truth. Speaking about my addiction, my fears, my failures, and my regrets wasn’t easy, but it was freeing. I learned that I wasn’t alone in this fight. That was a lesson I had to learn the hard way—addiction thrives in isolation. But recovery thrives in connection.

There were countless hours spent in therapy, sessions where I had to face the parts of myself I had long buried. My therapist helped me peel back the layers, slowly revealing the root causes of my addiction—fear, trauma, insecurity, and pain. It wasn’t just the drugs. It was the years of emotional baggage that I had tried to numb away.

I also began to rebuild my relationships. I had pushed away my family, my friends, and even those who cared about me. I wasn’t proud of the person I had become, but they helped me understand that they loved me despite my mistakes. Rebuilding trust took time, but with patience, I started to repair the bonds I had shattered.

One of the most crucial parts of my recovery was discovering a new sense of purpose. For so long, I had been aimless, lost in the haze of addiction. But now, I began to rediscover who I was beneath the mask I had worn for so long. I began to set goals, pursue things I had once been passionate about, and reconnect with the person I used to be.

It wasn’t perfect, not by any means. There were setbacks, relapses, and moments where I doubted myself. But with every challenge, I grew stronger. I began to understand that recovery wasn’t just about staying clean. It was about learning to live again.

Looking back, I realize that addiction was never just about the substances. It was about filling an emptiness inside me that I didn’t know how to address. The recovery process was about finding healthier ways to fill that void, to confront the issues that had driven me to seek escape in the first place.

And now, as I stood in front of my friend, telling her my story—our story—I realized just how far I had come. The shame and guilt that once defined me had been replaced by a newfound strength and resilience. I wasn’t the same person who had spiraled into addiction all those years ago. I had fought my way out, and now I could look back with pride.

“How are you really?” she asked again, this time with a knowing tone.

I took a deep breath and smiled, a genuine smile that came from a place of peace I had never known before. “I’m doing better than I’ve ever been.”

And for the first time in a long while, I truly believed it.
