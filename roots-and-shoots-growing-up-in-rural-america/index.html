Roots and Shoots: Growing Up in Rural America


The barn stood at the far edge of the field, its weatherbeaten boards grayed by decades of sun, rain, and winter frost. It had been there longer than anyone in the town could remember, long before the county line was drawn or the highway cut northward through the hills. For the Thomason family, it wasn’t just a barn—it was the heart of the farm, the pulse of their generational story. And that story, woven into the soil and stretched across the rugged beauty of rural America, was about much more than farming. It was about roots and the delicate shoots that carried dreams upward.

Ellie Thomason always loved the mornings best. Fog rolled low over the fields, softening the world as if it weren’t finished waking up. The smell of fresh earth and dew clung to the air, and the sound of crickets slowly gave way to roosters crowing in the distance. At thirteen, Ellie felt tied to the land more than most kids her age. While others might view farm chores as dreary or monotonous, she felt a deep, almost sacred sense of belonging. Her hands learned early to cradle seedlings and feel for moisture in the soil, just as they learned to wipe smudges of dirt across her freckled cheeks.

“Ellie!” Her father’s voice boomed from the porch. He stood there with a mug of coffee in hand, his weathered face lit by the morning sun. “You going to check the beans today or let the crows have a feast?”

“I’m going, I’m going,” she shouted back, slinging her burlap sack over her shoulder. She darted down the dirt path, her boots kicking up little puffs of dust with each step.

The soybean fields were bordered by a crooked line of oak and maple trees. Ellie loved that line of trees, not just because it offered shade on hot afternoons, but because of the adventures it promised. Beneath those trunks lay treasures of arrowheads from long-lost tribes and forgotten trinkets from settlers who’d farmed this land centuries ago. Some of the roots jutted from the earth, massive, gnarled things that reminded her of fingers grasping at the soil.

She knelt to examine the soybean plants. Their bright green leaves stretched toward the sunlight, glistening with drops of dawn. The rows weren’t just perfect lines—they were her family’s livelihood and her own quiet reminder of how things grew. You couldn’t rush it; everything needed time to find its place.

Her younger brother, Joey, appeared suddenly at the edge of the field, dragging his fishing pole behind him. “Any monsters out there?” he quipped, pointing his chin toward the crops.

Ellie smirked. “None here. Pretty sure they’re all hiding under your bed.”

The two Thomason siblings were inseparable in a way only farm kids could be. Joey, a scrawny, gap-toothed nine-year-old with a shock of black hair that hardly ever obeyed a comb, idolized Ellie despite being too proud to admit it. And Ellie, though she often teased him, cherished his company. Together, they built forts from hay bales and sailed imaginary ships down the creek at the back of the farm.

“Are you coming to the creek with me later?” Joey asked, his pole now slung over his shoulder.

“Maybe,” Ellie answered noncommittally. “Depends how the rest of my chores go.”

The creek was home to minnows, water striders, and occasional snapping turtles that Joey insisted were just prehistoric relics waiting to bite off a toe. To Ellie, though, it was something more. When sunlight filtered through the canopy of trees and danced on the water’s surface, the creek felt like a secret world removed from the weight of responsibility.

Responsibility. For as long as Ellie could remember, her parents had emphasized its importance. Life on the farm wasn’t easy, and it wasn’t always fair. A late frost could wipe out entire crops. A bad storm might leave their barn roof in tatters. Livestock fell sick. Machines broke down. But her parents never let those setbacks define them. Instead, they approached each problem as though it were simply another part of the season—something to solve, endure, and eventually grow from.

“Ellie,” her mother would say, usually in the quiet hours just after sunset, when the cicadas began their evening chorus, “You’ve got to keep planting, even when the odds seem against you. The shoots don’t ask the sky to be clear. They just grow toward the light.”

Ellie’s mother, Josie Thomason, was the kind of woman whose strength was hard to describe but impossible to miss. She handled the accounts, mended fences, cooked meals that could fill even her father’s bottomless stomach, and somehow still found time to help Ellie with her schoolwork. Her hands were calloused, her shoulders strong, but her voice was gentle in a way that always made Ellie feel safe.

By the time the sun climbed higher into the sky, Ellie finished inspecting the soybean rows and began her trek back to the house. Joey had darted off toward the creek as soon as she released him from his audience, leaving her to walk alone. She stopped for a moment by the ancient maple tree that marked the path between fields. Its trunk was scrawled with names and initials carved decades ago, including those of her grandparents. Her fingers traced the faint “JT + RT” someone had scratched into the bark long before she was born.

Stories felt alive here. Not just her family’s stories, but those of everyone who’d set down roots in these hills, who’d hoed the first rows in the hard clay soil, who’d weathered droughts, fires, and floods. Ellie sometimes wondered if this patch of rural life had a different kind of time, one that ran slower, anchoring people deeper into memory.

At supper that night, the family gathered as they always did, bowls of stew steaming, the talk easy yet intentional. Her father recounted a story of fixing the tractor with a makeshift part while her mother rattled off tasks for the week. Ellie listened, liking the clink of forks against plates and the rhythm of voices that lulled her into a comfortable quiet. Life wasn’t exciting in the sense most kids her age would’ve wanted, but it was full in ways she hadn’t expected to appreciate yet.

That night, Ellie lay awake, staring at the patches of moonlight spilling across her room. She thought about their roots—not just the crops or the stubborn cottonwood trees that grew by the creek—but the kind of roots that stretched backward and forward in time. Her grandparents, their struggle through the Dust Bowl. Her parents, fending off foreclosure in the ’80s farm crisis. Every sacrifice etched into the land so that she and Joey might have a future.

And then she thought about the shoots—the fragile green things that pushed through the earth, defying frost and floods. Joey had recounted one of his stories at the dinner table earlier, about how he’d found a tiny sapling growing in a crack on the side of the creek bed. “It’s growing out of nowhere,” he’d said with wonder in his voice. Worn-down sandstone and a single patch of sunlight were all it had needed to take hold.

Ellie figured there was something to that. Rural America might not be easy, but all it needed was a kernel of persistence, a little hope, and the belief that growth—no matter how meager or slow—was still growth.

Seasons would come and go, just as they always did. Farms would rise and fall, just as they always had. But in between those shifts, roots deepened, and shoots stretched toward the light. For Ellie, that was enough.

It was more than enough. It was everything.
