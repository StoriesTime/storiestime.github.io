Lost and Found: Rekindling Old Flames


The first snow of December fell too early that year, frosting the branches in the city park where Mara Danvers stood shivering, hands shoved into her coat pockets, trying to decide if she was hopeful or out of her mind. Six steps away, the fountain dripped water onto a sheet of thin ice, and a flock of pigeons pecked through the dusting of white. Mara watched for the silhouette she’d seen a hundred times in her memory, never quite knowing if she wanted it to appear.

That morning, her inbox had held a single-line email:
I’ll be in town. Meet me at the place? 4 pm.
It was unsigned, but she knew the sender—Brendan Lake, the man she’d loved, lost, and never quite eradicated from her heart.

The park belonged to a different era of her life, one in which possibility was strung between every conversation. A decade ago, she and Brendan had sprawled on the grass under an August sun, mapping futures they didn’t realize would diverge. He’d moved for law school in Seattle; she’d gotten a promotion here in Cleveland. They’d muttered “we’ll make it work,” and then—inevitably, quietly—it hadn’t.

As the clock ticked towards four, a familiar figure appeared, a little grayer at the temples, a little less sure of himself, but unmistakably Brendan. He stopped a few feet away, running a hand over his mouth, then gave her a lopsided grin.

“Mara.” His voice wrapped around her, comforting and unsettling at once.

She tried to sound breezy, but nerves leaked in. “You’re late. Just like old times.”

He shrugged, the gesture half apology, half defense. “Some things stick.”

A silence drifted down, heavier than the snow, until Mara knelt to pat an overeager Labrador bounding toward her. Brendan watched her, hands jammed in his jeans.

“So,” she said, not looking at him.

“So,” he echoed. “Thanks for coming.”

Mara straightened. The world tugged at her periphery—children shrieking, cabs honking on the avenue—but everything funneled down to Brendan’s eyes, the same copper brown that had watched her cry and watched her laugh, and once, long ago, watched her walk away.

He nodded at the fountain. “Want to sit?”

They made their way to the icy rim, silent. She waited, watching her breath fog the air.

“I didn’t come back for a courtroom win,” Brendan said at last, voice low. “I came to see if I could get one thing right I haven’t managed to forget.”

She angled toward him, curiosity fighting cynicism. “You have a hell of a sense of timing.”

“I heard you weren’t seeing anyone.”

The directness stung; Mara shrugged. “There was Nick, but he moved to Atlanta. Rebecca left last spring. Basically, it’s just me and my mother’s constant WhatsApp messages.”

That crooked grin again. Brendan had always worn his heartbreak lightly, like a suit a size too big. “Funny. My mother’s upgraded to FaceTime. I can’t escape her frown lines.”

They sat, history running thick between them. Mara remembered his apartment, the reek of burnt coffee, the dent in his kitchen wall from when he’d flung a frying pan during an argument about nothing.

“It’s been ten years,” she said finally, letting her fingers brush a patch of snow. “Why now, Bren?”

He hesitated, weighing each word. “I think,” he began, “when you’re young, endings feel permanent. But I keep running into things that remind me of us. That book you lent me with all the notes in the margins. My Spotify shuffle landing on your old playlists. It’s like you’re tucked into the corners of my life, everywhere I thought I’d swept you away.”

For a moment, the world felt both crushingly huge and blindingly small. Mara steadied herself, thinking of all the people who mistake nostalgia for longing.

“And you think we should—what? Pick up where we left off?”

“I don’t know,” he admitted. “I’m not even sure what picking up would mean.”

Mara studied him. He was taller than she remembered, or maybe just less slouched. There was a scar under his jaw now, white against winter-reddened skin. She wondered about the intervening years, the girlfriends, the victories, the failures—everything that had grown behind the brick wall they’d built between them.

“I won’t lie,” she said slowly, “When you emailed, I almost deleted it.”

She watched a little color leech from his cheeks.

“But I don’t know—I guess I’m curious. Not convinced, but curious.” A small smile. “And maybe hungry?”

He relaxed by increments. “Same old Mara.”

“Not exactly,” she said. “But let’s get out of the cold. I know a place.”

The diner along Superior Avenue was nearly empty, neon lights reflected in the snow-muffled windows. A waitress named Sheila dropped mugs of coffee onto their table and winked. Mara felt the warmth seeping into her knuckles, thawing her resolve.

“How’s your dad?” she asked. She hadn’t known about his stroke until months after, when a mutual friend mentioned it at a party.

Brendan traced the rim of his mug. “He’s good. Or, as good as he’ll ever be. I moved back for a while after it happened. Never quite adjusted to the idea of being needed instead of just…visiting.”

He paused as the waitress set down grilled cheese and tomato soup, cheeseburgers and fries, the staples of the old days.

“And what about you?” Brendan asked, “Still writing those late-night poetry drafts?”

Mara shot him a look. “I work with numbers now. A non-profit downtown. Grants, forums, budgets. Poetry lives in a notebook I never open.”

“That’s a shame,” he said, surprisingly soft.

“Honestly,” Mara admitted, “Sometimes I don’t recognize who I used to be. You ever feel that?” She forced a laugh. “Maybe it’s just getting older.”

Brendan pushed a fry into his ketchup, studying her like he did when searching for an argument. “I feel like I keep meeting different versions of the people I love. Sometimes, they overlap. Sometimes, I realize I missed entire versions of them.”

She considered that. “So which Mara am I?”

He smiled. “Present tense. The only one who matters.”

Conversation warmed into something comfortable. Old jokes resurfaced, jostling against fresh wounds. Mara learned Brendan had almost married someone in Seattle, a violinist who loved hiking but hated his philosophy jokes. He confessed he drank less now, cooked more, and had subscribed to a service that sent him monthly socks.

Mara laughed, picturing serious Brendan unfolding argyle socks from tissue paper. It was both absurd and oddly endearing.

By the time they finished eating, dusk hovered outside, and Mara realized she wanted him to walk her home. She wanted the chance to see, from the corner of her eye, the way Brendan’s jaw tightened in the cold, the way his hands braced against his pockets. To notice the scars time had etched and the ones it had softened.

The route back wound through old neighborhoods. As they passed the elementary school, Mara stopped.

“Do you remember,” she asked, “that Halloween? When we dressed as Mulder and Scully?”

Brendan snorted. “You mean when I spilled cider all over my badge and you spent an hour pretending to be in the FBI?”

She grinned. “Those were good years. Too good, I think.”

He took a breath, clouds blooming from his lips. “Is there such a thing?”

Mara shook her head. “Maybe not. Your best memories feel impossibly distant one minute, and the next they sneak up, just close enough to hurt.”

Brendan paused, then reached out, lightly brushing a stray strand of hair from her eyes. The touch electrified her; she nearly stepped back.

“I missed you,” he said, voice just above a whisper.

She searched his face. “You missed who I was. We both did.”

He didn’t flinch. “I missed you. You and me together. Stupid inside jokes, late-night grocery runs, fights about the dishwasher.” He grinned sheepishly. “And maybe some of the other things, too.”

Mara released a slow breath, watching frost spiral in the streetlight.

“Okay,” she said after a long, heavy pause.

“Okay?” he echoed.

“Okay to…what, exactly?” she laughed. “I don’t have a plan here, Brendan.”

“Neither do I.” He offered his arm. “Let’s figure it out as we go. For once.”

They walked the last blocks to her apartment side by side, steps in sync, not quite touching but close enough to share warmth.

Weeks passed. Winter gathered in earnest, cruel and relentless, but Mara found herself looking forward to Brendan’s knock at the door, to shared dinners and arguments about which film noir hero aged the worst. Their lives were no longer seamless; they had grown their own routines, developed their own crusty habits, but something gentle and familiar flickered whenever they were together.

They agreed not to pummel themselves with old regrets. Instead, they took each day as its own story: sometimes light, sometimes hard, never guaranteed.

One night, while watching reruns of black-and-white sitcoms, Brendan asked, “Do you think we ever completely lose someone? Or does every ending have a little thread that can be picked up again?”

Mara poured another glass of wine, contemplating. “I think we tuck people away. Hide them in drawers, forget they’re there, and sometimes, if we’re lucky, we find them again. Changed, but not gone.”

He watched her, eyes soft. “Are you saying I’m a lost sock?”

She grinned, leaning into his shoulder. “Maybe. Or a favorite book I lent out and didn’t expect back.”

He squeezed her hand. “Guess it’s a good thing you checked the lost-and-found today.”

They laughed, and, for the first time in years, Mara felt the past and present braid together, not as rivals, but as partners in the same old dance. Lost and found, she thought. Rekindled, not resurrected. Something new, built from something old, neither a repetition nor a regret.

Outside, the snow gathered steadily. Inside, the fire flickered and warmed, stoked by the fragile, enduring hope that sometimes, the best stories begin again when you least expect.
