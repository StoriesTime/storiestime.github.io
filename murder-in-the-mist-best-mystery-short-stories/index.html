Murder in the Mist: Best Mystery Short Stories


The morning fog rolled in thick, dense, and impenetrable, shrouding the sleepy coastal town of Darrow Bay in a cold, ghostly embrace. It had been like this for days—each morning the mist hung heavier, like an invisible weight over the small town. The usual hum of seagulls and the soft rush of waves against the shore were muted, giving the place a hollow, desolate feel. To some, it was peaceful, but for others, it was a harbinger of something more sinister.

Detective Evelyn Pierce had grown up in Darrow Bay. She had left for a few years after high school, seeking adventure in the bustling city, but when her father fell ill, she returned. Now, with her father gone and the town still as unchanging as it had always been, she found herself back in the quiet town that seemed to hold more secrets than it let on.

That morning, however, the fog would bring more than just an eerie stillness—it would bring murder.

It had been the kind of morning where people stayed in, sipping hot tea by the window, watching the mist roll over the rooftops. But at the edge of the town, where the old pier jutted out into the churning sea, a body was found. It was a young woman, her pale skin almost blending with the fog as if she were a specter. She was dressed in a red coat, her long, dark hair splayed across the weathered boards of the dock like a curtain. She was cold, lifeless.

Evelyn arrived at the scene moments after the police had cordoned off the area. The familiar faces of local officers were scattered around, but there was no sign of the usual sheriff, Tom Matthews. Instead, a stranger stood near the body, taking notes. His sharp gaze and methodical approach set him apart from the rest of the team. He was tall, with dark, neatly combed hair and a black leather jacket that contrasted sharply with the grey morning.

“Detective Pierce, I assume?” The stranger’s voice was deep and controlled.

Evelyn nodded, scanning the scene carefully. The mist swirled in her peripheral vision, making it difficult to focus on anything for too long. “Who are you?” she asked, though her voice was more professional than confrontational.

“Detective Bennett,” he said, offering a faint but polite smile. “I’m from the city. They called me in to help with the investigation.”

Evelyn didn’t like outsiders, especially ones who seemed so calm and collected in the face of something so unnerving. But Darrow Bay wasn’t a place where murders happened every day, so maybe he had a point in taking charge.

“Any idea how she ended up here?” Evelyn asked, squatting beside the body. Her eyes studied the woman’s face, which was still beautiful despite the circumstances. There were no obvious signs of struggle. The red coat was pristine, almost too pristine. The fog seemed to cling to her skin, as though it was reluctant to let go.

“She was killed somewhere else,” Detective Bennett said, squatting next to her. “The body was placed here. It’s too clean. No blood on the dock, no signs of a struggle. Someone went to great lengths to make sure she wasn’t found in a mess.”

Evelyn’s brow furrowed. That wasn’t what she had expected. The docks were notorious for being a quiet place. People walked there to fish or enjoy the view, but the underbelly of Darrow Bay was rarely seen on the surface. Beneath its calm exterior lay years of unresolved issues, rumors, and unspoken tensions.

“Do we have an ID?” she asked.

“Not yet. But we found a small notebook on her,” Bennett said, pulling a small leather-bound book from a plastic bag. “No identification, no personal belongings. Just this.”

Evelyn took the notebook and flipped it open. The pages were filled with hastily written notes. There were names, places, dates—many of which she didn’t recognize. But one name stood out: M. Sullivan.

“What’s this?” she murmured.

Bennett leaned in. “Could be a clue, could be a dead end. But it’s something.”

Before she could respond, one of the officers approached. “Detective Pierce, you’ll want to see this.”

Evelyn stood and followed the officer to the far end of the dock. There, wedged between two large barrels, was a torn piece of paper. It was crumpled, damp from the fog, but the ink was still legible. It was a receipt for a local diner—a place called Rosie’s.

Evelyn’s eyes narrowed. Rosie’s was a popular hangout for locals, the kind of place where gossip was exchanged freely. It was possible someone there might know something.

“I’m heading to Rosie’s,” she said, her tone leaving little room for argument.

Inside Rosie’s diner, the low hum of quiet chatter filled the air, and the smell of bacon and freshly brewed coffee hung in the atmosphere. The waitress behind the counter, a woman with short, bleached hair and tattoos on both arms, looked up as Evelyn entered.

“What can I get you, hon?” she asked with a bright smile that didn’t reach her eyes.

“I’m looking for information,” Evelyn said, flashing her badge. “Do you recognize this woman?” She pulled out a photo of the young woman from the dock and placed it on the counter.

The waitress paused for a moment, her smile faltering. She studied the photo carefully before shaking her head. “Sorry, I don’t know her.”

Evelyn studied the woman’s face. The waitress seemed too calm, almost too practiced in her indifference. There was something about her reaction that didn’t sit right.

“Are you sure?” Evelyn pressed. “She was last seen here, maybe two days ago. She left a receipt behind.”

The waitress’ eyes flickered briefly to the side, then back to the detective. “I’ve got a lot of people in and out, lady. Sorry, can’t help you.”

Evelyn wasn’t convinced, but she couldn’t press too hard—not yet. “Alright. But if you remember anything, let me know.”

As Evelyn turned to leave, she caught a glimpse of a man sitting in the corner booth, his eyes scanning her every movement. He was an older man, perhaps in his late 50s, with a salt-and-pepper beard and thick-rimmed glasses. Something about him felt… off. His eyes lingered for just a moment too long before he returned to his coffee.

Evelyn stepped outside the diner, the cold air hitting her face. The fog had thickened, and she could barely make out the buildings down the street. The mist was like a veil, hiding everything, making it impossible to see clearly.

Back at the station, Evelyn spread out the notes from the woman’s notebook. There was something hauntingly familiar about the name “M. Sullivan.” It tugged at her memory, but she couldn’t place it. She had lived in Darrow Bay for years, and the name didn’t ring any immediate bells. But there was something about the way the name was written—a desperation, maybe—like it was important. She traced the letters with her fingers, her mind racing.

Suddenly, a knock at the door interrupted her thoughts. Detective Bennett entered, holding a file in his hands.

“Got something,” he said, placing the file on the table. “I did some digging. There’s an M. Sullivan living in town—an old man, one of the town’s recluses. He hasn’t been seen in months, but people say he used to be close to a lot of the old families around here. Could be a connection.”

Evelyn felt a chill run down her spine. The old Sullivan house was at the edge of town, hidden away behind a thicket of trees. It had been abandoned for years, but everyone knew about it. No one dared venture near it.

“Let’s go,” Evelyn said, grabbing her coat.

The Sullivan house loomed in the distance, an imposing, decaying mansion barely visible through the fog. It looked like a place where secrets were buried—literal and metaphorical. As they approached, Evelyn felt the weight of the air pressing in on her. This was it. The source of it all.

They knocked on the door, and there was no answer. But when Evelyn tried the handle, it creaked open, revealing a dark hallway inside. The musty scent of mildew and decay wafted toward them.

“Stay close,” she whispered to Bennett, pulling out her flashlight.

They made their way through the house, the floorboards creaking under their weight. The walls were lined with old photographs and dusty furniture, but the silence was oppressive. At the end of the hallway, they found a door slightly ajar. Inside, a figure stood in front of a large desk, his back to them.

“M. Sullivan?” Evelyn called out.

The man turned slowly, revealing a face wrinkled with age, his eyes bloodshot and hollow. He looked like someone who hadn’t seen the light of day in years.

“What do you want?” he rasped, his voice gravelly.

Evelyn stepped forward. “We need to talk about the woman. The one who was found by the docks.”

Sullivan’s eyes widened, then narrowed. “I knew her. She came looking for something. Something I couldn’t give her.” He paused, taking a shaky breath. “I should have told her to leave. But it’s too late now. It’s all too late.”

Evelyn took a step closer, her flashlight catching the edge of a worn-out ledger on the desk. She could see the name “Emily” written across the top.

“Who killed her, Sullivan?” she demanded.

Sullivan didn’t answer. Instead, he took a long, labored breath and looked past her, toward the open window where the mist swirled.

“Sometimes,” he whispered, “the past doesn’t stay buried.”

As the sun finally broke through the fog the next morning, Evelyn stood on the steps of the Sullivan house, her mind racing with unanswered questions. She didn’t have all the pieces yet, but she knew one thing for sure: Darrow Bay wasn’t done hiding its secrets. Not by a long shot.
