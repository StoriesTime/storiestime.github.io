Shadows of Oppression: The Right to Education


The waking sun glimmered behind a haze of dust, caught between the squat buildings of the old city, before spilling into the alleys where Mila ran barefoot. She knew how to move softly, treading where shadows thickened and brambles grew, away from the eyes of the guards. If she reached the quota of deliveries before noon, she’d be done early—done for the day and safely hidden.

The rag in her fist, once a white scarf, covered her mouth and nose from the swirling grit. Mila’s school bag was patched with mismatched cloth; it rode her hip, empty except for the one precious book she kept. The sight of it—its torn spine, its faded title—reminded her of a world that flickered just out of reach.

They said reading was a privilege in her quarter, and to need it was trouble. Mila learned the letter-shapes when her father still worked in the market, before the soldiers came and tore down the marble plaque above the old school. That was the day she learned the word “ban,” and the day many dreams withdrew to the corners of dirty rooms where candles guttered until morning.

She stopped near a crumbling wall where a strip of faded blue paint whispered a half-remembered word: “liberation.” The word meant nothing now, not with lessons forbidden and mouths to feed.

Across the alley, an older girl lounged in the shade, eyes hard, voice even. “You’re late.”

“I had to wait for bread at the last house. The lady with the sick boy paid in beads instead of coins.”

Nadia scoffed but handed Mila a satchel, heavier than yesterday. “The teachers are gathering. Tell your mother.” Her tone softened just a little. “Watch the checkpoints. Two boys were caught crossing last night.”

Mila clenched the satchel’s handles. She knew what caught meant. In her dreams, the word had razor teeth.

Their small concrete block, tucked behind the old toothless bakery, felt unfamiliar these days. Lately, her mother whispered the word “school” as if it were a dangerous secret—sometimes sweet, sometimes bitter. Home once smelled of spice and soap; now, it tasted of fear.

Inside, her mother’s eyes darted to the satchel, then to the curtains.

“They say the inspectors ask school questions—at the factory, at the water line. Questions out of nowhere.” She reached to pat Mila’s shoulder, as if the gesture could shield her from the winds that ruled their block. “Put your book away.”

Instead, Mila withdrew it and pressed her lips to the battered pages. “Why are words so dangerous?” she asked, though she already knew the answer.

Her mother’s fingers tensed. “Words are freedom. And freedom—”

She gestured helplessly, as if freedom was a bird she’d forgotten how to draw.

That night, Mila crept out as lamplight flickered and sirens echoed near the checkpoint. She found Nadia and a handful of others waiting behind the tin workshop, swapping news and glances. A trade of soft voices and gestures, with only nervous moonlight for witness.

Nadia gave the sign—two quick taps on the tin. Soon, an old woman with sharp eyes and a patchwork skirt emerged from the gloom, trailed by two boys and a girl not older than Mila.

“No names,” the woman whispered. “No real addresses. Only the language of numbers and signs.”

Her hands flashed as she drew a shape in the dirt: a square, a circle, a thick line—childish but electric. The old code they had all learned before the ban.

“We study tonight. We remember old maps, old promises. We sing only in the shadows.”

Someone snickered, but it was a hollow sound.

The lesson began: shapes and syllables, numbers chanted by candlelight. Pages copied on ripped paper. If anyone heard, if anyone betrayed them, the punishment would be impossible. Yet the children leaned forward, eyes hungry, tracing knowledge with their fingers.

Over time, the gatherings grew bolder. Late at night, more candles flickered throughout the block—traces of hope, or maybe acts of rebellion. Yet fear pressed on them at every turn.

One evening, the patrols swept through the neighborhood after whispers of subversion. They dragged families into the street, hands raised, voices trembling. Mila and her mother huddled in a corner.

“Where did you learn these numbers?” the officer barked at a boy, holding a scrap of arithmetic ripped from a ledger. The silence was knife-sharp.

“We used to go to the big school,” the boy stammered. “Before the plaques fell.”

He was yanked away. But for every child swept up by suspicion, two more picked up a fragment of memory—carrying numbers and poems in their fists, refusing to forget.

The authorities tried to smother questions with walls and warnings, but the questions slipped through the bricks. “Who decides what is forbidden?” Mila dared to ask her mother, who had lost her job for refusing to sign the loyalty statement.

“How do we survive?” her mother replied, voice flat, “if we do not adapt?”

Mila wasn’t satisfied. Adaptation, she decided, was another word for shrinking. With each lesson, each whispered poem, she felt something inside her expand instead.

When the blockade tightened—a noose around the city’s old heart—kids ran messages instead of bread. Secrets travelled folded three times in the heels of socks, tucked inside loaves, or murmured through the grates of locked apartments. Teachers risked everything: they charted patrol times, inventing new games that smuggled math and language into growing minds.

One night, the old woman led them in a litany of names: Galileo, Fatima, Basheer, Malala—voices from far places and times, stitched together with courage. Steps on a path she begged them not to forget.

“Not every wall is a wall. Some are bridges, waiting for hands to build them,” she said.

Mila copied each name carefully, pressing them into her palm as if that alone could keep them from fading.

The winter arrived early. It crept through broken windows, curled beneath Mila’s threadbare blanket. But within her, a different season bloomed.

She dreamed of a classroom with bookshelves to the ceiling, of voices raised in laughter rather than hurried whispers. Even hearing the sirens, she dreamed—she believed, in a way fiercer than anger, that a future existed where children could read openly, speak freely, and question without fear.

Her conviction grew in the silence between lessons, in the scars gathering on each memory. With every story swapped in secret, with every calculation whispered in the dark, she claimed a sliver of defiant hope. Education was her inheritance, passed down from hands trembling but unbroken.

Mila never forgot the day the wall came down. Not with bombs, but with a riot of bodies—children, parents, teachers, chanting names and numbers in the open. It was messy, desperate, and dangerous, but it carved a new crack in the regime’s facade.

The guards could not arrest them all. For the first time in years, the voice of the city didn’t choke itself. Instead, it rose—hoarse, glorious, unfinished.

In the weeks that followed, the authorities scattered. Some children returned to the ruins of the old school, sweeping broken glass from the floors, prying charred desks from the dusk. Volunteers arrived, lugging boxes of donated books. They set up canvases along the walls, and the blue word—liberation—returned, painted by scores of newly freed hands.

On the first true day of school, Mila stood in a cleansed room smelling of paint and hope. She gazed at the blackboard, fingers trembling.

Her mother sat on the back row, weeping without shame.

Beside her, Nadia squeezed her hand. “We did it.”

The teacher—a survivor, like them—called roll with a steady voice, and the children answered, one by one. Each voice was an act of reclamation—a declaration that the right to learn, to question, to dream, belonged to all.

In the cracked sunlight filling the classroom, Mila opened her book. The turning of a page was like a door swinging open. And from the dust and shadow, a future stepped quietly into the light.

Mila came to understand that freedom was not a bird but a seed, planted in the dark, watered with risk and resistance and the stories of those who refused to forget.

The right to education—she learned—lived stronger than any wall. And once it had taken root in the hearts of dreamers, no shadow could truly wipe it away.
