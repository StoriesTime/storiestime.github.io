The Wall Between Us: A Human Rights Short Story


The wall cut through the city’s heart like an old scar—concrete, cold, and austere. It rose from the ground during the summer of devouring heatwaves and rationed water, a strange summer when cousin flared against cousin, politics thundered down empty streets, and trust was carried off by a rolling cloud of tear gas.

Leena’s earliest memories swirled with the smell of jasmine on her grandmother’s windowsill and the low rumble of distant protests. Her childhood apartment once looked out at a sweeping plaza where street vendors sold roasted nuts and foreign tourists snapped photos of ornate fountains. Now, that view ended abruptly at a barricade topped with shimmering glass shards and warning signs painted in three languages.

She was ten when her uncle Amir stopped visiting. He had lived only fifteen minutes away, on the other side of the city’s old river, but overnight, the wall turned his home into territory forbidden to her family. For years, Amir’s absence returned in her dreams. In them, he would knock at the plastic green door with his crooked smile and a paper bag of sweets, and she’d try to reach him, pushing against a wall that grew higher the harder she fought.

Leena’s mother would never talk about Amir during breakfast or dinner. “He’s across the wall,” she would say simply—then change the subject, her voice heavy, her eyes darker. Sometimes, Leena listened to the late-night news on the radio, the whispers about restriction zones and relocation camps. Stories that fluttered like thin moths in a dark room.

When she turned seventeen, the rumors changed. Instead of more razor wire, there were cracks—a child found a tunnel, a smuggler bribed a guard, a piece of the wall fell during a winter storm. Leena felt the city holding its breath, people exchanging coded messages and nervous glances in the bakery and on the tram. Then, during Eid, her cousin Kaled came home with a secret.

“They say there’s a spot in Sector H where the patrols don’t reach. It’s just one camera. If you’re quick…”

She pressed him, desperate. Was it real? Had anyone crossed? Kaled chewed his cigarette stub and shrugged.

“Old Farida did. She went to see her sister for the first time in a decade. Came back with pomegranates and a new scarf. They say she walked so fast the camera only caught her shadow.”

Leena knew danger the way a dancer knows rhythm: always beating at the edges, present in the tightening of limbs or the flicker in a parent’s eyes. She weighed the risks—a bullet from a guard’s rifle, a month in the detention center, her mother’s heartbreak. But as the moon swelled and Ramadan began, longing gnawed at her like an old injury.

Amir would love to hear about the poetry contest, she thought, or the music she played on her battered oud. Most nights, she sat in the courtyard, strumming out melodies as old as the city, her sadness swirling with the jasmine and dust.

Then, one morning in May, when the streets shimmered with the first hot blush of summer, she found her mother sitting by the radio. The voice crackling through the speakers carried news of a new ban—no gathering near the wall after sundown, harsher penalties for trespassing, the border zone expanding another hundred meters.

“They’re taking more of the city,” her mother said.

Leena laced up her shoes that night, left a note on her pillow (“Gone for air. Love you.”), and slipped out with Kaled at her side. The wind tasted of burned rubber and river silt. They moved through alleys between buildings pockmarked by stray bullets.

At Sector H, the wall loomed taller than she’d imagined, monstrous and impassive. The camera, a single black eye staring down the alley, jerked to life as a cat darted across the cobbles. They waited. When the screen flickered back to idle, they moved. Kaled scaled the low fence and whispered Leena’s name.

The world shrank to the scrape of concrete, the panic-tight drum of her heart. She swung her leg over the barricade, torn shirt catching on rusted wire. Then, there she was—standing on the forbidden side of the city.

The silence stunned her. No applause, no gunfire, no sirens. Even the wind held its breath. On the horizon, Leena glimpsed the old river glittering under streetlights, and beyond it, the blocky silhouette of Amir’s apartment complex. She reached for Kaled’s hand, just to feel another living pulse.

They hurried through back lanes, memorized from years of peering at old city maps. Near the edge of the main square, a woman leaned against a shuttered coffee shop, arms crossed, headscarf trailing behind her. Her eyes darted over the siblings, sharp and wary, then softened into something like recognition.

“I remember when children played in this square until midnight,” she said. “No wall, just music and ice cream and laughter.”

Leena nodded, unsure if she should speak. There was a gravity here, the effect of a life spent waiting for things that never came.

“We just want to see our family,” Kaled ventured.

The woman’s lips twisted into a half-smile. “Family is a reason that’s hard to punish.”

She showed them a shortcut: a crack in the wall beside the pharmacy, hidden by an abandoned kiosk. They ran through the opening, barely ducking in time, hearts in their throats, until they reached the quiet of Amir’s street.

Here, the night was alive with quiet voices and open windows. Leena was struck by how… normal everything looked: the laundry draped across balconies, a child’s tricycle left outside, the flicker of a soap opera behind thin curtains. The wall divided the city, but not the dreams of those who lived beneath its shadow.

Amir’s building stood unchanged, the paint faded, doors open to the summer breeze. She climbed the rough cement stairs, searching for the right door. And when she knocked, the sound echoed down the hall, heavy and unmistakable.

The door flung open. Amir, older now and gaunt, stared at her as if she were something conjured from memory. For a second, no one moved. Leena’s breath caught, tangled in her chest.

“It’s me,” she whispered.

His embrace was fierce, smelling of rosewater and cigarettes and the strange freedom of belonging. For the first time since she was a child, Leena felt the weight of incompleteness lift.

They spent the night talking—really talking, not forced, not whispered through static phone calls or hurried letters. Amir made tea. They spoke of their city as one body bleeding from both halves, stories of lost jobs and secret gatherings, music played at half-volume so the border guards wouldn’t summon suspicion. Amir showed them drawings—tiny sketches of the city’s skyline as it once was, unbroken and bright.

Before dawn, Leena gazed through Amir’s window. The wall cut through the middle distance, indifferent as ever, its shadow across the sleeping rooftops. But she realized: every moment spent here, in this tiny apartment, chipped away at the wall’s power.

Kaled squeezed her hand. “We should go before the streets wake up.”

Amir nodded, his eyes sad but hopeful. “Promise me you’ll come again.”

She did.

Their return was quieter, a mirror of their journey in reverse, hearts hammering with fear that every sound was the drone of a patrol truck. When they slipped back through the crack, the city felt both wider and more fragile. The old wall was still there, but now Leena carried something beyond its reach—a fragment of belonging, a shimmer of light that no concrete could contain.

In the weeks that followed, Leena grew braver. She wrote poems about invisible wounds and handed them to trusted friends on both sides. She played her oud near checkpoint squares, weaving melodies of hope into crowded air. Young children, too young to remember when the wall wasn’t there, began to sing her songs after dusk, echoing them in playgrounds and between narrow alleys.

Word spread quietly of her crossing, and of others who followed. Not all were as lucky. Some were caught, detained, sent home bruised and silent. But hope, once seeded, found its way through cracks and gaps, through undercurrents of defiance and small everyday courage.

One evening, as the city trembled with summer storms, Leena’s mother found her on the balcony, staring out at the gleaming wall. She said nothing for a long time, just stood, hands curled around the railing.

“You saw him. You crossed?” her mother asked at last, voice hoarse, almost fragile.

Leena nodded, expecting anger or fear. Instead, her mother touched her cheek, a single tear rolling down the lines of fatigue and pride.

“That wall will not stand forever,” she whispered.

Leena smiled, a fierce and fragile joy blooming in her chest. She looked beyond the wall to the darkened city, knowing some day, the barriers of concrete and wire would fall—not with the force of armies, but with the gentle stubbornness of people who remembered what it meant to reach for each other, even when the world demanded they live apart.

She picked up her oud and played a song across the divide, letting her notes rise above the wall, curling into the night like wild jasmine, impossible to contain.
