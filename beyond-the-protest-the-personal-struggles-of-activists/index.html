Beyond the Protest: The Personal Struggles of Activists


The day after the march, Reema awoke with her throat raw and her feet aching. The city outside her window was low and gray, a leaden buzz of traffic and scattered newspaper stubs on the wind. She reached for her phone more out of habit than hope, already bracing against the harsh glare of notifications—comments, messages, tagged videos, snatches of her own voice chanting, “Not one more!” under raining banners and the sharp whistle of patrol cars.

She scrolled, her thumb flicking rapidly, letting the praise and vitriol blend into a blurry static. Reema had become a reluctant icon overnight for something she’d never meant to lead. But leadership was fickle. It arrived at protests at three in the morning, soaked through from an unseasonal downpour, bearing the weight of friends’ fears and family’s doubts.

Her cat, Juniper, leapt onto the bed and pressed her head to Reema’s chin, a gentle demand. The softness of fur against skin was grounding, a reminder that quiet mornings still existed, that the world’s crises could wait until after sunup—or at least until coffee.

But activism, Reema had learned, offered no grace periods. Even on days that appeared uneventful, the cause pulsed beneath everything: a notification, a news chime, a muffled siren two blocks away. It followed her through time, its urgency braided into the fabric of her life.

The Burden of Visibility

The first protest Reema attended was small and awkward—a handful of college students with scrawled signs standing nervously outside City Hall. She’d arrived by accident, spiraling out from a political science lecture, propelled by a friend’s cryptic text: You need to see this.

Then came the videos, the viral hashtag, and the unsolicited requests for interviews. Activism, in this era, was inseparable from social media. She watched as her name was retweeted so many times she stopped recognizing it, transitioned from person to trending topic.

It thrilled her at first, this sense of momentum, the feeling that history could be moved by sheer will and righteous fury. But it wasn’t long before strangers claimed ownership over her narrative. An old photo of her at a party was recirculated with insinuating captions. Messages alternated between hero-worship and thinly veiled threats. She wondered, more than once, if there was a way to step aside without betraying the cause. Every time the urge to retreat welled up, she remembered her friend Maya’s bruised jaw, the frantic text: They’re coming. Her need to keep fighting eclipsed her desire for privacy.

Strain on Relationships

The pressure reached inwards, straining all of Reema’s relationships. Family dinners became debates, her parents worried about her safety and future. Her mother, a quiet woman who worked at the city library, always encouraged her passion for justice but gnawed her nails to the quick when headlines turned ugly. Her father, a first-generation immigrant, viewed American politics through a cautious, skeptical lens. He’d lived through other failed movements, other crackdowns.

Reema’s conversations with friends began to revolve around risk assessments: Should she bring her inhaler to protests in case the police fired tear gas? Who had a vehicle ready for pickups if the crowd got kettled? When did self-care stop being an indulgence and start being an imperative part of the struggle?

There was a night after a particularly tense rally when she and her friend Tessa sat in muted exhaustion at a late-night diner. The overhead light hummed. They picked at waffles with syrup gone cold. Tessa blinked, slow and red-rimmed. “I missed my niece’s birthday,” she said. “Second year in a row.” She sounded matter-of-fact, but her eyes shone wetly. “I keep telling myself it matters. But—damn, sometimes I wish I could just…forget, even for a weekend.”

Reema nodded, her silence a form of shared consolation.

The Quiet Toll on the Mind

Every activist she knew carried invisible wounds. There was Danilo, who stopped answering texts when the anniversary of his brother’s shooting approached. There was Alex, who woke from nightmares about crowd crushes and armored vehicles and sometimes simply disappeared for weeks at a time, resurfacing pale but determined.

Insomnia threaded through Reema’s nights, strung taut with anxiety. She struggled not to refresh her feeds compulsively. The garish headlines, the outrage cycles, the ceaseless stream of new injustices to process—each one another notch in a belt wound tight across her chest. She attended therapy sessions subsidized by a community coalition, sitting in a circle with other activists, learning to name her triggers and strengths in a room that smelled faintly of lavender and stale coffee.

The therapist, a sharp-eyed woman named Dr. Wang, spoke about “soul fatigue.” She urged the group to create boundaries, to practice restoration as a radical act. Reema tried to tether herself to the advice, but it felt abstract, even indulgent, when outcry awaited just beyond the door.

Hard Fights, Small Victories

Amidst the exhaustion, victories emerged. She would never forget the day the city’s council announced it would review the use-of-force policy—the mayor visibly defensive, the room packed with activists in coordinated shirts, the air thick with anticipation.

Reema stood beside the others. Their hands shook as they read prepared statements, voices crescendoing with pain and hope as decades-old grievances finally found a public forum. Later, in the cramped hallway, a grandmother pressed her hand into Reema’s. “My son was gone before I got to say goodbye. You made them listen.”

It was never enough, but it was not nothing.

Community was forged in the heat of protest, in shared sandwiches, bail fund spreadsheets, and compiled evidence. They learned to lean on one another—to swap memes in group chats as armor against feel-bad news, to show up for birthdays and hospital visits and funerals.

Reema found solace in small acts of reclamation: turning off her phone for a morning, tending to her rooftop tomatoes, letting herself laugh at cheap cat videos, even when sorrow hung heavy in her chest. Wholeness, she realized, was not a state but something built, moment by moment.

The World Outside the Barricades

Not everyone understood. Old classmates popped up in her DMs plastering her with questions. “How can you do it?” “You’re always angry.” “Doesn’t it get old, fighting all the time?”

Reema wanted, sometimes, to reply: I am tired. I am not just angry. I am afraid and hopeful and full of doubt, but I am not allowed to quit, because quitting is a privilege I do not possess.

But instead she only typed, Thanks for checking in, and closed the message.

Work-life balance was a distant concept. Her day job at a local nonprofit bled into her weekends: phone banks, housing clinics, strategy sessions. Evenings were snatched for sleep or last-minute interviews.

Some nights, walking home late, she would pause at the corner where the march had swelled into a sea of chanting bodies just days before. The mural on the wall still gleamed fresh—bold black letters against painted faces.

Breathe, she reminded herself, seeing her own likeness among the crowd.

Facing Burnout

Six months after the first protest, burnout set in. It crept slowly, masked by routine, until one day she found herself sitting cross-legged on the floor, staring at a sign she’d made—Her Voice, Her Power—in thick, determined strokes of blue paint, her hands trembling.

She wanted to cry, but frustration cut the tears short. She was angry at herself most of all. For being tired, for losing patience, for fantasizing about escape. She hardly recognized the girl who had shown up, unprepared but hopeful, outside City Hall that first afternoon.

She called Maya and let the phone ring twice before hanging up, unsure what to say. A beat later, Maya texted: “Pizza and bad movies?”

In Maya’s living room, swaddled in blankets, watching a ridiculous sci-fi film, the heaviness in Reema’s chest loosened. They laughed at the absurd dialogue, drowned cheese sticks in sauce, and allowed the world outside to pause, if only for ninety-seven borrowed minutes.

Rediscovering Purpose

Rest, Reema discovered, was not the enemy of change. Slowing down wasn’t failure; it was a strategy. She learned to delegate, to trust the next wave of organizers. She stopped measuring her worth by the number of actions attended or articles published. The movement would survive without her name in every headline.

She began mentoring new activists—guiding them through legal basics, connecting them with support networks, urging them to protect their own minds as fiercely as their communities. She shared not just the shining moments, but the stories behind the scenes: the loneliness, the uncertainty, the nights spent wondering if any of it would matter. In telling the unvarnished truth, she found herself lighter, less brittle.

Her presence within the movement shifted. She started writing, essays about the weird, wild mundanity of activism—how hope persists through exhaustion, how laughter blooms in the dark. Her words found their way to others on the edge of burnout, echoing her own admission: You are allowed to step back.

Shared Humanity in the Struggle

Reema returned to the streets, still chanting but more selective about her battles. She learned to say no. She let herself care for her family and found satisfaction in small things—the resilience of the tomato plant refusing to die on her dusty windowsill, the giddy sprint of Juniper chasing a sunbeam across the floor.

The city’s struggles kept evolving, as relentless as ever. New injustices erupted, new coalitions formed, the work stretched onward. But even as the fight pressed on, Reema watched the faces in the crowd, new and old, voices tangled in rhythms of solidarity.

She understood, finally, that activism wasn’t about snapping the world into fairness overnight. It was about carrying one another through the long, hard middle—guarding each other’s softness, sharing the burden, building a future that was stubbornly, defiantly better than what they had inherited.

On her best days, Reema knew: the story of struggle was also the story of surviving it together. Beyond the protest, beyond the headlines and the hashtags, lay a landscape of unrecorded courage—ordinary people helping each other stay whole.

Her struggle was never just hers. And that messy, complicated truth, more than any victory, was what kept her from giving up.
