Through Different Eyes: Short Narratives on Racism and Bias


In a quiet town where maple leaves painted the streets gold in autumn, there stood an old oak tree in the community park. Its thick branches stretched wide, offering shade and shelter to anyone who paused beneath its boughs. For some, the oak was a peaceful refuge; for others, it was a symbol of exclusion.

Maya, a Black teenager with a passion for photography, often sat under that tree, camera in hand. She loved capturing the world as she saw it—bright, complex, full of possibility. One afternoon, Maya noticed a group of white teenagers hovering nearby, whispering and casting glances her way. She raised an eyebrow but stayed rooted, like the oak itself.

“Why do you always sit there?” a voice called out.

It was Liam, a boy from school with a reputation for being blunt. Maya set down her camera and looked up.

“Because it’s quiet,” she replied simply.

Liam shuffled closer, curiosity winning over his usual hesitance. “Isn’t it weird, though? The tree’s been there forever, but we never hang out here together.”

Maya smiled. “Maybe because we see the park through different eyes.”

That afternoon, beneath the sprawling branches of the oak, they talked. Maya shared stories about her experiences, about moments when she felt invisible or judged because of the color of her skin. Liam listened, sometimes surprised, sometimes uncomfortable—but always present.

For the first time, they saw the park—and each other—from a shared perspective.

Part 2: The Letter Never Sent

Jamal sat at his desk, staring at a blank sheet of paper. The classroom felt suffocating, white walls pressing in like the unspoken truths hanging between him and his teacher, Mrs. Peters.

Earlier that week, during a group presentation, Mrs. Peters made a remark that gnawed at Jamal’s thoughts: “You speak well for someone from your neighborhood.”

The words were meant as a compliment, but to Jamal, they stung with the weight of stereotype and bias.

He wanted to write a letter. A letter explaining how such comments, even when cloaked in kindness, chipped away at his confidence.

Dear Mrs. Peters,
I appreciate your encouragement, but…

He stopped. What if this letter was misunderstood? What if it made things worse?

Days passed, and Jamal never sent the letter. Yet, his feelings were no longer bottled up. He found himself talking to his friend Aisha, who shared her own experiences of subtle biases she faced daily.

Together, they started a small group at school—a safe place to share stories and challenge assumptions.

Sometimes, the most powerful changes begin with words left unsaid and connections quietly made.

Part 3: The Café Encounter

The smell of strong coffee filled the cramped café where Ana worked as a barista. The rain tapped softly against the windows, masking the sound of distant city traffic.

One day, a middle-aged man approached the counter, his face carrying a tired expression.

“Can I get a black coffee? Make it quick.” His tone was sharp but weary.

Ana prepared the order and handed it over with a smile. “Here you go. Have a good day.”

The man hesitated. “You’re… not from around here?” he finally said.

Ana sighed inwardly. This was familiar—the moment someone’s assumptions surfaced, uninvited.

“I was born here,” she replied calmly.

He frowned, embarrassed by his question. “Sorry, I didn’t mean—”

“It’s okay,” Ana interrupted gently. “I get asked that a lot. People see me and think they know my story.”

He nodded, gripping his cup tighter. “I guess I’m guilty of judging too quickly.”

Ana leaned in, lowering her voice. “We all do it. Bias is hard to avoid. But recognizing it—that’s where change begins.”

For a moment, strangers united by a shared humanity and the rawness of their own biases, found understanding in the hum of the café.

Part 4: Mirrors and Windows

In an art gallery filled with abstract paintings and sculptures, Naomi wandered alone, eyes scanning each piece. A new exhibit titled Mirrors and Windows focused on identity, experience, and perception.

A particular painting caught her attention: a blend of vibrant colors shaped like fragmented faces, some overlapping, some distinct.

Next to her, a man observed the same painting. “It’s like we’re all broken but painted back together differently,” he mused.

Naomi nodded. “It shows how we see ourselves and others—through windows into their lives or mirrors reflecting our own biases.”

He smiled. “Exactly. It’s hard to walk in someone else’s shoes, but art helps break down those walls.”

They spent hours discussing the pieces, sharing the stories they told, and reflecting on how personal bias colors every interaction.

The gallery was more than a place to admire art—it was a space to confront uncomfortable truths and envision a more empathetic world.

Part 5: On the Bus

Morning rush hour, a crowded bus rattled through the city streets.

A woman named Priya clutched her bag tightly, aware of the whispered comments from others about her accent, her skin, her hijab.

Across the aisle, a man fidgeted, his discomfort palpable. He wondered if she was dangerous, different, or just misunderstood.

Priya caught his gaze and offered a tentative smile. It was met with surprise, then warmth.

“Where are you from?” she asked gently.

He replied, “Here, but it doesn’t feel like it sometimes.”

They talked about their neighborhoods, their families, their dreams, peeling away layers of misunderstanding many carry unknowingly.

When the bus reached Priya’s stop, the man realized the distance between them was not physical—it was the gap bias had built, now beginning to crumble.

Epilogue: Seeing Beyond

Racism and bias are often invisible currents shaping daily life, influencing perceptions, actions, and relationships. The stories shared here illuminate the varied ways people experience and confront these realities—not always through confrontation, but through conversation, empathy, and moments of vulnerability.

The oak tree stands firm, the letter unsent, the café moment shared, the art gallery visited, the bus journey made—all small narratives threading a broader tapestry of understanding.

When we choose to look through different eyes, to listen deeply and with an open heart, we discover that the world is not divided by color or creed, but connected by shared stories waiting to be heard.

In those stories, we find the power to change—not overnight, but through countless small acts of seeing, acknowledging, and embracing one another’s humanity.
