Beneath the Surface: An Unlikely Bond Against Hate


Riley adjusted their snorkel and peered down into the churning waters beneath the dock. This was supposed to be a tranquil getaway, a three-day escape from endless news cycles, political divisions, and everything else that made modern life feel exhausting. Yet even the ocean’s familiarity didn’t calm them today. The invitation to join this small-town environmental cleanup project had seemed harmless enough—until they saw who else had signed up.

Luke, with his broad shoulders and tattooed forearms, leaned against a nearby piling. He was loudly recounting a fish story to a small group, and his deep laughter carried across the beach the way thunder rolled across a summer sky. Riley tried not to sneer. The last time they’d shared a room—at a mutual friend’s community fundraiser—Luke’s barbed comments about “wokeness” had quickly soured the atmosphere. Riley had left early, frustrated by how someone could casually mock others while claiming to be “just speaking freely.”

And now, both awkwardly tethered to the same project, they were expected to cooperate.

The volunteer leader, a wiry man with silver-threaded hair named Carlos, clapped his hands to gather the group’s attention. “Alright, folks! Over the next few days, we’re tackling a stretch of coastline that’s in bad shape. You’ll all be paired up—we find it’s safer, and let’s be honest, a little more fun that way. Makes hauling debris easier too. Let me assign teams so we can get started.”

Riley’s stomach tightened with unease as Carlos read through the list. Of course. What were the odds? Luke’s name was paired with their own.

“Riley! Luke! You’re handling the deeper waters at the edge of the reef. It’s a delicate spot with a lot of accumulated trash—careful not to disturb the coral.”

Luke gave Riley a quick grin as they approached him. “Well, guess it’s just us, huh? Don’t worry—I’ll do the heavy lifting.”

His tone was almost playful, but Riley’s patience was already precariously thin. “Right. Because clearly the only thing I’d bring to the table is lecturing the ocean about equity.”

Luke blinked at the sharpness of their response, but whatever retort he might have had was interrupted by Carlos. “Come on, people, let’s move! The kayaks are ready, and time’s ticking. We’ll meet back here at noon.”

As the watercraft scraped across the sand into the shallows, Riley waded in to help Luke lift their kayak. The chill of the ocean soaked through their shoes, but it didn’t distract them from the growing tension between them. They paddled in silence for the first fifteen minutes—awkward, stilted strokes that barely seemed coordinated.

The reef came into view, its jagged edges rising just below the surface. Riley couldn’t help but pause their paddling for a moment to marvel at it—the way sunlight refracted through the water like a prism, bringing the coral to life in shades of gold, crimson, and jade. Little flashes of motion darted between the nooks and crannies: clownfish, wrasses, and the occasional pufferfish.

Luke broke the quiet. “Beautiful, isn’t it?”

His voice carried a rare gentleness, a small glimpse of a man who wasn’t all swagger and noise. Riley nodded. “Yeah. It is.”

“Hard to believe this place has so much trash choking it up. People just…dump stuff without a second thought.”

“That’s usually how damage happens,” Riley replied. Their voice was carefully neutral, but Luke still caught a mild undercurrent of judgment.

They reached the designated cleanup area shortly after. Luke went under first, donning his mask and fins while Riley secured their gear. When he resurfaced moments later, a tangle of fishing line and a disintegrating soda can clutched in his hand, his expression indicated the extent of the pollution.

“It’s bad,” he said, lifting the mess into the kayak. “Real bad.”

Riley pulled on their own gear and joined him in the water. Below the surface, reality was far darker than the vibrant reef suggested from above. Plastic bottles lay nestled between coral heads. Pieces of Styrofoam swayed with the currents. A rusting battery sat ominously at the base of a rock, likely leaching toxins into the ecosystem with each passing day.

It was heavy, back-breaking work. With every visit to the surface to unload debris, Riley felt their muscles protest and their breath come shorter. But there was also something unexpectedly meditative about it, something soothing in the way they both dove, surfaced, and worked in sync.

At one point, Riley surfaced with an entire net that had embedded itself in the coral, a hazardous snare for marine life. They couldn’t free it on their own—and before they could ask for help, Luke swam over and joined them, his hands carefully navigating the delicate coral surrounding it. Together, they pried it loose. Even amid the roaring waves, their shared triumph was palpable.

“Teamwork, huh?” Luke wheezed as they heaved the net into the kayak. He gave Riley a crooked smile, and, though their response was a reluctant half-smile of their own, it marked a shift.

By the time they broke for lunch on the beach, there was less hesitance between them. While the younger volunteers chattered nearby, Riley and Luke found themselves sitting side by side in the sand, too exhausted to bother keeping their distance.

“I’ve gotta hand it to you,” Luke said, breaking their shared silence. “You’re a hard worker.”

Riley looked at him sideways. “Don’t sound so surprised.”

“It’s not that. I guess I just didn’t expect you to be…you know…”

“What? Someone who doesn’t live up to that little box you already put them in?”

The jab had a lightness to it this time, and Luke chuckled, albeit a bit sheepishly. “Fair. I guess we both had our assumptions.”

Riley raised an eyebrow. “Yeah? And what assumptions of mine are you dying to clear up?”

Luke rubbed the back of his neck, his earlier bravado fully gone. “I’ve got an awful big mouth sometimes. I know that. But just because I say dumb stuff doesn’t mean I hate anyone. I just…grew up different.”

“Different how?”

“Small town. Strong opinions. Things you hear at the dinner table, you know? You don’t question it unless someone really shakes you up. Truth is, I’ve been trying to figure myself out since moving to the city. I’ll admit, it’s not been…smooth.”

Riley stared into the distance, watching the tide ease in and out. “So what’s stopping you from listening instead of doubling down?”

Luke shrugged. “Habit, maybe. Ego too. And I guess I got defensive because people like you scare me a little.”

That surprised Riley. “Me?”

“Not you specifically. But you seem so sure of who you are and what you stand for. Meanwhile, I’m fumbling to even figure out what I stand against half the time.”

For a moment, Riley considered this. They’d never thought of someone like Luke—a big, gruff man who radiated certainty—as being scared of anything, let alone insecurity. It didn’t excuse his past behavior, but it explained the man behind it.

“Maybe,” Riley said slowly, “you don’t need all the answers yet. Maybe admitting you don’t have them is a start.”

Luke nodded, solemn. “Something to think about.”

The next two days continued much the same way. They pulled more trash from the ocean together, worked side by side on stubborn problems, and even laughed on occasion. And slowly, their conversations drifted into common ground—shared memories of road trips, childhood dogs, and a mutual, irrational hatred of cilantro. The cleanup effort became more than a battle against debris; it turned into the beginning of an unlooked-for friendship, forged in salty water and aching muscles.

By the third afternoon, when Carlos called the project a success and announced a group photo, Riley stood next to Luke without hesitation. When the camera snapped, it didn’t capture two people simply tolerating each other. It captured a partnership that had grown beneath the surface—against hate, against prejudice, and against every barrier they had unknowingly carried.

As they loaded the last of the waste into the removal trucks, Luke spoke softly. “Thanks, Riley. For calling me out—and sticking with me anyway.”

Riley gave him a small smile, one that carried far more understanding than before. “Thanks for sticking with me too.”

Somewhere under the waves, the reef exhaled in relief.
