Beneath the Skin: A Journey Through Racism and Prejudice


It began on a midnight train sliding through the rusted veins of the city, the only light coming from garish neon graffiti sprawling over every brick and steel girder. Maribel sat alone near the back, her forehead pressed against cold glass. The city was endless from there: high-rises dissolving into shantytowns, neighborhoods stitched together by neglect and defiance. Every station announced itself with a shriek of steel and a faint, bruised echo of a million stories.

Across from her, an older man in a faded blue suit coughed quietly, eyes half-closed; a pair of teenagers argued over headphones, each trying to drown out the other’s world. Neither seemed to notice Maribel’s presence. She was used to it. Her skin was darker than most in this part of the city, and each day was a study in the subtle choreography of avoidance. Every glance that slid past her, every tight smile or slow step away, was another tally on an invisible ledger.

That night, as the train rattled onward, Maribel thought of her mother’s advice: “Keep your head high. Stand your ground. Never apologize for your existence.” Simple words, yet every day felt like an apology, spoken in silence, the burden quietly settling between her bones.

Chapter 1: The New Classroom

A week earlier, she’d stood at the threshold of her new classroom, clutching her transfer papers and biting the inside of her cheek. The teacher, Mrs. Kelley, gave her a soothing smile that never reached her eyes. Rows of students sat, turning with a choreographed curiosity, as Mrs. Kelley announced, “Let’s welcome Maribel Sanchez, who just moved here from across town.”

The introductions were short. In the hours that followed, Maribel noted the subtle glances: the way hands drifted across phone screens, the chuckled whispers, the quick, darting looks when she raised her hand. No one asked about her background, though everyone seemed to have already decided for themselves.

During lunch, she found herself on the edge of the schoolyard, balancing her tray on the cracked concrete bench. The other kids clustered around sturdy tables, voices hopping from English to the soft diphthongs and clipped consonants of other, more familiar tongues. Every group was divided by invisible lines, drawn long before Maribel arrived.

A girl named Tara approached her, her voice bright but edged with a caution that Maribel recognized instantly. She offered a thin smile, made a joke about cafeteria food, and sat with her back to the rest of her group.

“Where are you from?” Tara asked, as if the information was a secret ingredient she could stir into her understanding of Maribel.

“Here,” Maribel replied quietly, but Tara pressed on. So, Maribel gave her the city name, her old school, even the apartment block her family had just moved into—the details that didn’t matter. Tara nodded, feigning interest, but Maribel saw the silent calculation in her eyes.

Chapter 2: Stones in the Path

On her way home that Friday, Maribel got off the train early on impulse. She wandered through unfamiliar streets, shoes crunching over broken glass and bottle caps. Each block seemed to radiate its own story; burnt-out storefronts, murals of faces and slogans carved into every fence.

A group of children played jump rope near a convenience store. The rope slapped the concrete, their laughter a brief interruption in the sullen quiet. Maribel lingered, watching, until a boy called out:

“Hey, you wanna play?”

She shook her head, but another girl, smaller but more insistent, tugged her sleeve. “It’s okay! We don’t bite. Not harder than you.”

So, Maribel joined. The rhythm was familiar, the songs different. They skipped and laughed, sweat stinging Maribel’s eyes as her hair whipped behind. In that sunlit moment, no one cared who she was, only that she could keep time.

Later, as she collected her things, the storekeeper leaned out.

“You’re new,” he observed. “You’re safe here. But be careful a few streets down. Not everyone is as welcoming.”

Maribel nodded, processing the unspoken map he offered—a guide to the boundaries policed not by laws, but inherited suspicion.

Chapter 3: Family Ties

At home, Maribel’s grandmother stirred a pot of arroz con pollo, humming a melody that always summoned childhood echoes. Her mother, Ana, came in from work, her shoes dusted with flour from the bakery. The television buzzed in the background, the evening news narrating another protest, another debate about who belonged and who didn’t.

Over dinner, her mother asked, “How was school?”

Maribel hesitated, searching for words that wouldn’t worry anyone. “It’s…different,” she finally said.

Her abuela nodded knowingly. “Everywhere, people try to measure you by your skin. They think they know the recipe for a person just by looking.”

“How do you change their minds?” Maribel asked.

Her abuela shrugged. “Sometimes you do. Sometimes you just survive them. But your story is yours to tell. Don’t let them snatch it from your hands.”

Chapter 4: Under the Surface

In history class, Maribel learned about civil rights activists and watershed moments. The teacher assigned essays on “The American Dream.” As she scribbled outlines, Maribel realized that each textbook page skipped over the friction, smoothing out decades into footnotes and inspirational quotes.

When she read her essay aloud, chronicling her family’s move, the coded language in news reports, the way her grandfather changed the spelling of his last name to secure a job, the classroom fell silent. Some students looked impressed—or at least, surprised that Maribel’s fluent English hid other languages at home. Others looked uncomfortable, peeling labels from water bottles, refusing to meet her gaze.

After class, a boy approached. His tone was hushed, almost deferential.

“That stuff about your family… that’s wild. I didn’t know things were, y’know, still hard.”

Maribel smiled tightly, not trusting herself to answer. How could she explain that “things” were always hard, hidden beneath the surface, like stones beneath an icy river?

Chapter 5: Cracks in the Armor

She grew tired of the daily theater—of the nervous jokes, the way teachers pronounced her name too carefully, students asking if she was “full,” “half,” or “something else.” She learned to dodge questions that were really statements, to parse “Where are you from?” for its real meaning: “Why are you here?”

One afternoon, as she hurried past a row of lockers, a boy called her a slur just under his breath. No one stopped him. Maribel froze, the word burrowing under her skin, sharp as broken glass. She told no one; another day, another scar. The silence in the hall echoed louder than any insult.

Later, she would replay the moment, wondering what she could have done. Rage simmered, mixing with shame. But in the quiet that evening, as her mother brushed her hair, she realized the poison was not hers to swallow. She would not turn invisible to make others comfortable.

Chapter 6: Finding Allies

Slowly, things shifted. Tara, with her careful friendliness, began to see Maribel as more than a new face; they traded stories—awkward, honest, healing. In their biology group, an immigrant boy named Arun joined; like Maribel, he carried accents in his laughter, history in his silences.

The trio grew. Connections blossomed in unlikely places: a teacher who remembered Maribel’s grandmother from a church fundraiser; a custodian who let her linger in the library after hours, telling tales of when he first arrived in the city. Each person added a thread to a growing tapestry, rich with resilience and memory.

With time, Maribel began attending local meetings—community centers brimming with relatives of all shades, activists, elders, and newcomers teetering between hope and exhaustion. There, she listened to speakers who wielded their pain with precision, dissecting laws and attitudes that shaped every day.

At one meeting, a young woman recounted her own odyssey: fleeing violence, navigating bureaucracy, learning how easily dignity could be bartered away. Her story was raw, unvarnished, and Maribel recognized herself in every line.

They made plans, built projects, started food drives and literacy nights. Slowly, gently, these acts built a foundation stronger than the prejudice that pressed in from street signs and whispered caution at every turn.

Chapter 7: Speaking Up

One year after her first day, the high school hosted a multicultural assembly. Maribel was asked to give a speech. Her hands trembled as she took the stage, but her voice steadied with every word.

She spoke of the things people don’t see: the wavering anxiety before entering a room, the weight of assumptions, the hundreds of micro-decisions squeezed into every day. She spoke of her grandmother, who carried stories in her cooking and built bridges out of laughter. She recalled the children skipping rope, the map drawn in warnings, and her own battle to claim her voice.

“The journey is not about erasing differences,” she concluded, “but about understanding that beneath the skin, every person carries maps of love, of pain, of hope. We can build something better—not by pretending to be the same, but by honoring the stories we all contain.”

The applause was real. Kids she barely knew clapped, teachers nodded, a few parents in the crowd blinked back tears.

Chapter 8: Healing and Hope

Racism and prejudice did not vanish that day; the challenge was unending, woven into bylaws, jokes, and the daily interactions that defined each block in the city. Yet Maribel found that her story, once buried under fear and shame, became a light for others. She made space for new arrivals, offering the comfort of shared experience.

Years later, as the city changed around her, Maribel became a counselor, weaving together everything she’d learned. She guided others over the same broken glass, through the same midnight trains and wary classrooms.

She taught them how to speak up, how to see the person beneath the skin. Sometimes they faltered, but together they kept moving—step by hopeful step—reshaping the city from the inside out.

And under the neon glow, in the rhythm of children’s songs, in the smoky kitchens and echoing halls, the story continued. No longer just Maribel’s story, but a collective one: the journey through hurt, through hope, toward a world where every life is honored—not despite its differences, but because of them.
