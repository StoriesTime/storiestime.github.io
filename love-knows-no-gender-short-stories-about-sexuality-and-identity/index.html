Love Knows No Gender: Short Stories About Sexuality and Identity


The first time Cass set eyes on Alex was a late September dusk, the air hanging thick with an anticipation that always seemed to settle over campus as summer bled into fall. The quad was all orange haze and echoing laughter, low voices murmuring about courses, clubs, and the endless promise of beginnings.

Cass had always been on the edges—edges of groups, edges of expectations, edges of everything. They didn’t claim pronouns the way others did, didn’t slot neatly into rooms designated by single letters on bathroom doors, didn’t see the point. Yet, here, in this new place, Cass wondered if maybe the world could stretch a little.

It was a poetry night, some impromptu gathering orchestrated by the literary club. Stale coffee in paper cups, students sprawled on the grass. Cass was hovering near the outskirts, back pressed against a sycamore, notebook in hand, scribbling impressions in their jagged, angular script.

Alex walked straight into their line of sight just as the sky dissolved into violet. Messy hair, a battered bomber jacket, and a dimpled grin that seemed to flicker with every word. They didn’t recite anything. Instead, Alex just talked, weaving stories out of thin air—a tale about a childhood friend who had vanished, the way loneliness could be sharp and bright, the soft language of hands on piano keys. People listened, rapt, laughter and hearts opening like morning glory blooms.

Cass felt a spark—a subtle flare, nothing like the scripted drama of movies or novels. More a gentle warming in their chest, a recognition.

The Conversation

After, as people drifted away, Cass found Alex on a bench beneath a broken streetlamp.

“Not much of a poet,” Cass tried, unsure if their voice was even audible.

Alex tilted their head. “Maybe not in form. But—did you hear those giggles? Got some response. That’s got to count for something.”

There was an ease in that answer, a casual acceptance. Cass noticed a pin on Alex’s lapel: a tangled mix of colors, each strand crossing over the other.

“Nice badge," Cass said, nodding at the pin.

“My sister made it. All the flags, all the ways. Sometimes she gets the colors wrong.” Alex smiled, and then asked, “What about you?”

That was the question, wasn’t it? Cass felt it on their tongue, heavy and awkward—the conversation they’d had a thousand times via text, rarely out loud. “I’m… just Cass. Too many boxes make me itchy.”

Alex grinned, full and genuine. “Boxes are for moving, not people.”

Something shifted—Cass could feel it, a gentle rearranging of invisible boundaries.

Maps and Possibilities

Over the next weeks, Cass and Alex grew closer. They lingered together after classes, strolled through the labyrinthine garden behind the dorm, hunched over mugs in the bustling campus café. Alex radiated openness, an easy confidence balanced by a knack for listening. Cass found themselves unraveling, unspooling ideas and truths they’d never said aloud.

One afternoon, rain barrel-thumped against the window as they shared a quiet corner of the library.

“Do you ever…” Cass hesitated, watching as the condensation on the glass blurred the world, “get tired of explaining?”

Alex looked out at the storm. “Sometimes. Some people want you to put a name on every feeling. Boy, girl, neither—like you have to choose, and then be done choosing.”

Cass nodded. “It changes for me. Day by day. Sometimes I want to tell someone, sometimes I wish it was just visible.”

“Visible how?” asked Alex.

“A shape in the air. Or colors. Or maybe… just a feeling you get standing next to me. A sense.”

Alex stretched, boots scraping the carpet. “Sometimes I think I could leak my heart out like fog and people still wouldn’t see it right. But it’d be nice to try with the right person.”

For a moment, the air between them was thick—shared, silent, charged.

Cracks in the Facade

There were others, of course. There always are. Every community dances on the delicate threads of acceptance and skepticism.

Maya, who ran the local LGBTQ+ group, invited Cass and Alex to join. The meetings were small inside the rainbow-drenched student union, folding chairs and tea bags and laughter loud enough to echo into the hall.

Afterward, walking home beneath a drape of stars, Cass asked, “Do you feel like you fit?”

Alex considered. “More here than anywhere. Still, not perfectly.”

Cass hesitated. Alex reached out, knuckles brushing. “You don’t have to, you know.”

It was a simple gesture, but it felt monumental. Cass smiled, a tentative, honest thing.

But not everything was gentle. There were professors who stumbled over pronouns, students whose jokes carried a sharp edge. Cass felt their confidence shrink, retreating into old, habitual silences.

One morning, waiting behind a flock of athletes in the hallway, Cass was asked bluntly, “So what are you, exactly?”

Cass’s stomach twisted. They looked at Alex, who stood a step away, casually. Alex simply met Cass’s gaze, steady and calm.

Cass answered, “I’m me. No label attached.”

The athlete shrugged, already bored. But Alex’s lips curved upward. Later, Alex said, “You don’t owe anyone your story—unless you want to share it.”

Old Letters, New Lines

Winter arrived, abrupt and severe. Ice slicked the paths; Cass spent more evenings inside, curled next to Alex, swapping old stories. One night Alex confessed their past—how at sixteen, they’d rehearsed their confession to their parents for weeks, only to eat the words and speak them much later, in a therapist’s office.

Cass dug into their bag and produced a battered envelope. “My best friend moved away last year. Wrote me this after I came out. Never opened it.”

Alex held it, fingers finding the seam. “Want me to read it to you?”

Cass nodded, heart pounding.

Alex slipped a fingernail beneath the fold and began:

“Dear Cass,

I don’t always understand, but I want to. I don’t care about who you love or what you call yourself—as long as you’re happy. If you don’t have the words, that’s okay. We’ve got more years to find them together…”

Cass blinked hard. Alex smiled, shutting the letter carefully. “You know, there’s no single right word. Sometimes there’s just… this.”

They squeezed Cass’s hand. The comfort was physical—warm, real, an anchor.

Loving in Translation

The seasons cycled. Cass and Alex explored each other’s boundaries, trying on labels, discarding them, inventing private names—nouns and verbs that fit only when spoken between them. Sometimes they kissed; sometimes they just held hands, or sat, tangled, on the floor, feeling the beat of music in their bones.

Through the highs and lows, Cass learned that connection was malleable. Some days Alex shimmered with masculine energy; others they drifted through androgyny. Cass wore dresses now and then, but found joy in tuxedos; sometimes their skin ached under scrutiny, sometimes they lit up at a stranger’s acceptance.

They both wrestled with the complicated equations of attraction—how bodies meant nothing without souls, how desire could be sharp or soft or neither.

They learned to speak openly. “Some days I love you like sunlight,” Cass said, “bright and clear. And other days, I love you like moonlight—soft, silver, a quiet thing.”

Alex answered, “I love you in words, but more in the spaces between them.”

Campus life moved on. There were parties and protests, exams and failures, friends won and lost. Each day brought fresh understanding—that feeling safety in love was an act of hope.

Beyond Definition

On graduation day, the quad was lush with emerald green and excited voices. Everyone moved quickly, loving their moment in the sun.

Cass slid a string of beads over Alex’s neck—a rainbow with a bit of black, a dash of yellow, a streak of blue.

“For all the colors,” Cass said. “For all the days.”

Alex pressed a kiss to Cass’s brow. “For all the ways.”

As professors called names and diplomas shifted hands, Cass and Alex stood together, hands bright with sweat and nerves. The future felt uncertain, a blank map. But for the first time, Cass looked out and saw infinite paths, not limits.

There was no manifesto, no final declaration, no label that captured the wild expanse of their bond. Just two people—whole, unnamed, and wholly enough—daring to love each other exactly as they were, all lines between them blurred by laughter, music, silent understanding.

And that was plenty.
