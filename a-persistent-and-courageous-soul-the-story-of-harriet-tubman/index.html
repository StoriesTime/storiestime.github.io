A Persistent and Courageous Soul: The Story of Harriet Tubman


In the hallowed halls of history, there exists a name etched with the deepest courage and resolute determination, a name that strikes fear into the hearts of oppressors and brings hope to the downtrodden: Harriet Tubman. This is the story of a woman who, despite the odds, managed to emerge victorious in a series of trials, wherein she played the most significant role.

Harriet Tubman, born in 1820 as Araminta Ross to a enslaved family in Dorchester County, Maryland, was destined for a life of strife and triumph. Orphaned at a young age, she suffered physical, emotional, and sexual abuse at the hands of her various owners. Tubman’s early life was marked by this tyranny, but her indomitable spirit was far from being crushed.

One of her most memorable escapes from bondage was when Harriet poisoned her overseer’s food in an attempt to incapacitate him, an act of great audacity that resulted in her narrowly escaping violent retribution. Yet, Tubman’s truest form of resistance lay in her supernatural skill – a gift that she had been bestowed with since childhood: her unique navigational abilities. Despite being illiterate, Harriet proved to be a brilliant navigator and leader, exhibiting remarkable skills in reconnaissance and strategy.

Utilizing these traits, Tubman began her journey towards freedom in 1849, when she fled from her owners’ plantation from the shape-shifting terror of slavery. Following the North Star and other natural guides while evading pursuers, she successfully arrived in Auburn, New York, a free state, after reaching an agreement with her owner to work for her. Upon reaching the North, she began working alongside William Seward, the New York Secretary of State, who assisted in her pursuit of freedom for her family.

As a conductor on the Underground Railroad, Harriet Tubman dedicated herself to leading hundreds of enslaved people to freedom, including several of her own family members. This act of heroism was only matched by her unyielding commitment to our nation’s cause during the American Civil War. She served as a scout, a spy, a nurse, and a lauded participant in the Combahee River Raid, which secured the liberation of over 700 enslaved people and dealt a devastating blow to the Confederacy.

In her later years, Tubman adopted a family of eight people, some of whom were previously enslaved, and they lived stringently by her pronounced belief in the virtue of love, work, and the right to one’s own labor. In further testament to her destiny, she maintained financial independence, donating her meager resources to support oppressed people and confirming a legacy passed down through the rise and fall of generations.

Harriet Tubman passed away in 1913 at the age of 70, but her fervent spirit remains very much alive today, as a symbol of strength and determination. What’s more, her powerful legacy withstands the test of time, serving as a permanent reminder of the need for vigilance in the face of societal adversity. Always guided by the love for one’s fellow man, she is an eternal symbol of hope and courage, whose story will forever remain a beacon of righteousness and a testament to her unique role in American history.
