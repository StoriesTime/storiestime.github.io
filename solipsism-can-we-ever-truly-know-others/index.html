Solipsism: Can We Ever Truly Know Others?


Ethan sat in his small, dimly lit apartment, staring at the empty coffee cup in his hands. The warm liquid had long since evaporated, but he found himself lingering on the sensation of its weight. His gaze drifted across the room, from the cracked window to the piles of books on the table, to the faint outline of a photograph tucked into the corner of the shelf. It was a picture of his family. His parents, his younger sister—Lena, the one who had always laughed so freely. And yet, as he looked at the photograph, a peculiar emptiness crept into his chest. They were all distant now, not just physically but in a way that went beyond geography. They were… strangers in his mind.

He had been asking himself the same question for days now: Can we ever truly know another person?

The question had settled into his mind like a persistent echo. It wasn’t just a philosophical curiosity, but an existential dilemma. Ethan had been reading extensively about solipsism, a theory that had piqued his interest more and more as his mind wandered deeper into introspection. The idea that perhaps everything around him—the people, the world, the very reality he experienced—was nothing more than a construct of his own consciousness was unsettling, to say the least.

Could it be true? Could he really never know anyone else? Was his perception of others only a reflection of his own mind, an illusion spun from the threads of his own thoughts?

The thought kept him awake at night. Each conversation he had, each interaction, felt like a fleeting moment of connection, but in the end, it always came back to him. His interpretation of those around him was inevitably filtered through the lens of his own experiences, biases, and emotions. Could anyone ever escape this cycle? Was anyone else, truly, real?

He stood up abruptly, walking over to the window. The city stretched out before him, a labyrinth of light and shadow, of lives and stories interwoven in ways he could never fully understand. Yet, even as he gazed upon it, he couldn’t shake the nagging suspicion that it was all just a projection of his own consciousness, a never-ending loop of perception without resolution. The people moving on the streets, the cars driving by, the distant hum of life—they were all figments, weren’t they?

It was an afternoon like any other when Ethan ran into Sarah. She was a neighbor, someone he had seen occasionally in the building, but never really spoken to. Today, however, as he was walking down the hall, he saw her standing outside her apartment, fumbling with her keys.

“Hey, Sarah,” he said, surprising even himself with the words.

She looked up, her eyes lighting up in recognition. “Ethan, hey! I didn’t expect to see you today.”

“Yeah, I just… I was walking around. Thought I’d say hi.” He paused, unsure what to say next. His mind had been so focused on his own thoughts, his theories, his doubts, that speaking to her felt strangely foreign.

Sarah smiled warmly, her voice light and inviting. “You know, you should come over sometime. I’ve got some new books. You’re always reading, right?”

Ethan hesitated. Part of him wanted to say no, to retreat into his solitude. After all, who was she, really? Another character in his mind, perhaps. But then again, wasn’t this what he had been questioning? The nature of connection, the desire to reach beyond his own perception?

“I’d like that,” he said finally, the words leaving his mouth before he could fully grasp their meaning.

That evening, he knocked on her door.

Sarah greeted him with a smile, her apartment cozy and warmly lit. She invited him in, and they settled on the couch with mugs of tea. They spoke about trivial things at first: books, music, their favorite shows. Yet, as the conversation stretched on, Ethan couldn’t shake the feeling that he was engaging in an experiment. Was she real? Or was she simply another manifestation of his mind, an extension of his own need for connection?

As they spoke, Sarah revealed little details about herself. She talked about her job as a graphic designer, her passion for painting, and her love for traveling. There was a sincerity in her words, a depth that made Ethan wonder if he could ever truly understand her. He was so wrapped up in his own doubts that he could barely focus on what she was saying. Every sentence felt like a puzzle piece, a fleeting moment that slipped through his grasp.

Sarah, in contrast, seemed completely at ease. She was open, engaged, and seemingly aware of the shared reality between them. But was that just an illusion? Could she, too, be questioning the nature of their connection? Perhaps she was playing a part in his mind, just as he was playing one in hers.

Days passed, and Ethan continued to see Sarah regularly. They began to build a routine of sorts—meeting for coffee, chatting about life, and sharing moments of vulnerability. Still, the question gnawed at him. He would find himself observing her, trying to catch glimpses of something real, something that existed beyond his perception. But each time, the answer eluded him.

One evening, as they sat in a small café, Sarah mentioned something that struck Ethan like a bolt of lightning.

“You ever wonder if we’re all just… actors in someone else’s story?” she asked, her voice tinged with a quiet sadness. “Like, what if we’re all just playing our roles, doing what we think is expected of us, without ever really knowing who we are or who anyone else is?”

Ethan froze. Her words seemed to echo his own thoughts, words he had been too afraid to voice. Could it be that she, too, was questioning the nature of their reality? Or was this simply another way for her to reflect his own doubts back to him?

“I think about that a lot,” he admitted. “I wonder if anyone ever really knows another person. I mean, we’re all just… existing in our own heads, right? How can we ever truly connect when everything we experience is filtered through our own perceptions?”

Sarah nodded, her gaze distant. “Yeah. It’s like we’re trapped in our own minds, forever. We think we know people, but in the end, how can we really know anyone if we can’t step outside of ourselves?”

Over time, Ethan’s obsession with solipsism deepened. He found himself withdrawing more and more from the world, questioning every interaction, every relationship. He stopped speaking to his family, unsure whether they were real or just echoes of his own desires. He stopped seeing his friends, unable to trust that they were anything more than figments of his imagination.

The world felt increasingly unreal, like a dream he couldn’t wake up from. And yet, in his isolation, he began to realize something. The more he withdrew, the more disconnected he felt—not just from others, but from himself. The question of whether anyone else was real became less important than the realization that his quest for certainty had driven him into a prison of his own making.

One night, as he sat in the quiet of his apartment, staring at the same photograph of his family, something shifted. He didn’t have the answers he had been seeking, nor did he know if he ever would. But he realized that perhaps that was okay. The pursuit of certainty, the desire to know others completely, might always be unattainable. But in the process, he had learned something about the fragility of connection, the beauty of uncertainty, and the acceptance that some things might never be fully understood.

The world outside, filled with its messiness and its imperfections, was real enough for him. And maybe that was enough.

In the end, Ethan never found a definitive answer to the question of solipsism. But in learning to accept the ambiguity of human connection, he found a kind of peace. Perhaps, he thought, the true meaning of connection wasn’t in fully understanding another person, but in the willingness to share in the uncertainty of their existence, to meet them in the space between knowing and not knowing.

And so, he continued to live, not with the certainty of understanding, but with the openness of possibility.
