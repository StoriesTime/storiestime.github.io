Shadows of Prejudice: A Tale of Unseen Bias


Lena had always loved the attic. As a child, the attic was her secret kingdom—a world spun from dust motes swirling in golden beams, the scent of old cedar, and the gentle tick of a grandfather clock that no longer kept time. Now, at thirty-two, Lena found herself drawn back to the attic’s embrace, searching not for treasure or forgotten toys, but answers about her family’s complicated past.

It was midwinter, and outside, gray clouds bruised the sky. Inside, Lena unlatched the attic door. Cold air bit at her cheeks as she ascended, carrying a cardboard box filled with donated books for her next community book swap. She juggled the box, shivering, and looked around.

The attic was as she remembered—sagging boxes, brittle albums, a trunk with brass corners. She put down the new box and picked her way across creaking beams to a heavy, dust-covered chest, one she’d always been told not to open. Her father claimed it was filled with tax records, her mother with Christmas ornaments long past saving.

But there was something—an impulse more urgent than curiosity—that drew her to it now. Lena knelt and wiped away decades of grime before easing open the lid. The hinges wailed in protest.

Inside, not reams of paperwork or broken baubles, but bundles of letters tied with faded green ribbon, a battered camera, and a velvet pouch. It was the scent that struck her first—a faint trace of lavender and, beneath it, something metallic. Lena traced one finger over the first letter and drew it out, heart jittering.

The handwriting was elegant, looping—signed “Your friend, Ivy.” Lena frowned. Ivy was not a name she recognized.

She unfolded the letter. The words leapt off the page:

Dearest Ruth,

I saw you in the hallway, standing as if you were made of light. I know you will not sit with us. Mrs. Harrington says it is better you eat with your own kind. But I wish you would sit with us. I wish things could be different.

It aches to pretend I do not see you.

Something shivered inside Lena. The letter was dated 1963.

She rummaged further, pulling out more letters. Each was written by Ivy to Ruth—apologies for accidental slights, invitations unheralded and refused by some unseen barrier. Each was saturated with longing, regret, and the unspoken burden of rules that kept girls apart.

Lena pieced together the story. Ruth was her grandmother, but Ivy—a name never spoken at family gatherings—must have been excluded for reasons already invisible, calcified by time.

What had kept them apart? Lena’s mother once said Ruth had attended a segregated school; nothing more. Lena read on.

Sometimes I think we are sisters separated at birth, only to wake one day and find ourselves trapped behind glass. If only the glass would shatter.

The cool attic transformed. Lena saw, in her mind, her grandmother Ruth—a dignified woman with a quick laugh and sharp wit—traversing those school corridors under watchful, judgmental eyes.

She thought about her own life: her circle of friends, how quickly people judged based on accent, neighborhood, profession, even the cut of jeans. Sometimes Lena had caught herself, in the privacy of her mind, forming opinions that flickered away before she could grab them.

A spike of discomfort—had she inherited more than old photo albums and teacups?

She turned the camera over in her hands. Old-fashioned, sturdy. Lena clicked it open, revealing a roll of undeveloped film. She hesitated, then tucked the letters and film into her tote and hurried downstairs.

The next week was cloudy. At work, Lena couldn’t concentrate on her open tabs or even the prospect of Friday-night trivia. The letters gnawed at her; so did questions about her grandmother—and about herself.

At lunch, she met her friend Mario, who listened as she described the attic discovery. “It’s surreal,” Lena said, voice low. “Even the words sound so… gentle. But it’s still painful.”

Mario sipped his coffee. “You ever talked to your grandmother about it?”

“She’s… private. She’ll deflect. Or say things ‘were different then.’ But the thing is, Ivy was clearly reaching out.”

Mario shrugged. “You could ask her. Sometimes even opening the door counts for something.”

The notion scared Lena, but she considered it all day.

Saturday afternoon found her on Ruth’s porch, the letters bundled in her bag. Lena watched her grandmother prune dead leaves from a spider plant, the autumn sunlight tracing bright veins across her skin.

Ruth glanced up. “You’re early.”

“I came to talk, actually. About the attic. Can I show you something?”

Inside, Ruth made them tea, her hands steady. Lena laid the letters between them.

“I found these. Ivy wrote them to you.”

For a moment, Ruth said nothing. Her fingers hovered above the ribbon, uncertain.

“Would it bother you,” Lena said gently, “to talk about her? Who was Ivy?”

Ruth let out a slow breath. “She was my best friend for a little while. We sat next to each other in math class. Then the rules changed. I had to eat somewhere else, with my ‘own kind.’” She spat the last words like bitter fruit.

“Why… the change?”

“Some parents complained. They didn’t want their daughters sitting with someone like me.”

Lena knew that in the 1960s, in this town, “someone like me” could mean race, religion, class, anything. But Ruth’s gaze was steady, her jaw set.

“Ivy wrote these after they split us up. I never answered—it was… too dangerous to be noticed. If I had answered, my mother would have been scolded at church. I would’ve lost what little I still had.”

Lena thumbed the velvet pouch. Inside was a hair ribbon, still faintly green.

“I wish I’d heard these stories sooner,” Lena said, her voice thick.

Ruth shrugged. “When you get old, you hope the shadows die out. But sometimes I see them in how people talk—to me, to you, to that boy across the street whose last name no one can pronounce.”

They sat, quiet, the sun through the window patterning their hands with gold.

Later that night, Lena lay awake.

She thought about the shadows Ruth mentioned—unseen, but lingering. Lena considered her own life: coworkers given less credit, neighbors whose trust she’d never earned, assumptions she’d made without realizing.

She resolved to develop the film.

The photos, developed by a friend, arrived in a folder. Lena sat cross-legged in her living room, spreading them like cards.

Girls in plaid skirts, arms entwined, laughing. Ivy and Ruth, their joy luminous and effortless. In another frame, the same girls—separated, eyes downcast. In the background, adults with stiff smiles.

Lena wept.

She took one photo and brought it to Ruth. Her grandmother’s breath caught; she pressed the image to her chest.

“We could write to Ivy’s family,” Lena offered.

Ruth nodded. “There’s things I wish I’d said. Maybe now’s the time.”

Over the following weeks, Lena learned more—about Ivy’s later life, the ways she, too, had navigated the invisible barbed wire of her time.

Lena started hosting the book swaps in her grandmother’s living room. Guests came: old, young, from every block of their city. The letters became a quiet legend, sparking conversation that grew brave and sometimes uncomfortable.

The attic chest stood empty, the dust replaced with fresh hope.

Lena never forgot the shadows. She knew she still carried some, their shape changing with time. But she understood now: the shadows of prejudice could only linger so long as the stories remained hidden.

By unearthing her family’s truths—and her own—Lena helped warily cast off the weight of the past. Bit by bit, each new story let in more light.
