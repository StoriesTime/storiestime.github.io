The Infinite Feed: A Tale of Never-Ending Scrolling


Lena had always prided herself on being one of the first to discover trends, to absorb the latest happenings in the world. Whether it was new music, viral videos, or breaking news, she was there, often before anyone else. Her mornings began with the soft glow of her phone screen, the soothing hum of notifications guiding her into the day. The world, in a sense, had become her endless playlist of sound bites, pictures, and videos—fragments of moments that formed the puzzle pieces of a digital life.

It wasn’t always like this. There was a time when scrolling through her feed was a casual affair. She’d check in, swipe through the latest updates, and put the phone down for a while. It had started as a simple way to stay connected, to engage with her friends and the outside world. But now, scrolling had become an instinct, something she did without thinking. From the moment she woke up, her thumb moved in a practiced rhythm, flicking up and down through an endless stream of posts. Each swipe brought a new distraction, a new bite of information, but she rarely stopped to let any of it sink in.

One morning, as Lena lay in bed, her phone in hand, she noticed something strange. The feed, as usual, was infinite—an endless scroll of images and updates from people she knew, people she didn’t know, brands, and influencers. But something felt off. There was no natural end, no logical conclusion to the stream. The posts didn’t repeat, nor did they seem to follow any kind of pattern. They just… kept coming.

At first, Lena didn’t think much of it. Her thumb kept moving, flicking through the posts in an almost trance-like state. Her mind wandered, jumping from one post to the next: a cat doing a funny dance, a recipe for vegan cupcakes, a post about the stock market, and then an article about the latest scientific breakthrough. It was all a blur, fragments of information flashing by like the scenes of a film she couldn’t quite focus on.

She had already spent more time than usual scrolling this morning, but it felt different now. The feed wasn’t stopping. It had no natural rhythm or pause. When she reached the end of one post, there was another ready to fill the space, and then another, and another. Her finger moved without her even thinking about it.

But then, a thought crossed her mind. What if there was no end?

Lena stopped her thumb mid-scroll, her finger hovering above the screen. She blinked, staring at the screen. There it was again: a new post, bright and cheery, a sponsored ad for a fitness app she had never heard of. She was about to swipe past it when her eyes caught something unusual. It wasn’t the ad itself, but the subtle detail in the text. The words seemed familiar, like they had appeared before, but in a slightly different context.

Curious, Lena scrolled back up. The post she had just passed was the same as the one she had seen a few minutes ago, only the text was different now. This time, it was an offer for a special discount, but the image was the same, as was the layout.

Her heart skipped a beat. Had she seen this before?

She scrolled up again. Another post. Another ad, but this one seemed like a repost of something she had seen earlier—maybe days ago, maybe even weeks. The images were starting to feel repetitive, like she was stuck in a loop.

Lena blinked rapidly, her mind racing. She flicked back through the feed, rapidly swiping up and down. The content was all new, yet somehow, it felt eerily familiar. It was as if time had no meaning here, as if the feed itself had transcended the usual boundaries of space and time.

Minutes stretched into hours. The sky outside her window changed from morning to afternoon, and still, Lena couldn’t stop scrolling. Each time she reached the end of one post, another began. She barely noticed her tired eyes or the way her finger ached from the constant movement. Hadn’t she seen this before? Why couldn’t she stop?

The world outside the screen faded. The feed had become everything. The notifications that pinged every few minutes, the endless updates that demanded her attention—it was as if the entire universe had conspired to make her stay here, endlessly scrolling.

It wasn’t just the content that was starting to feel infinite. It was everything—the scrolling itself, the act of swiping, the engagement. She had fallen into a rhythm that was beyond normalcy, beyond anything she had ever experienced. She wasn’t sure where the feed began or where it ended. The posts blurred together, and the separation between her and the phone dissolved completely. She wasn’t in her room anymore. She wasn’t even in the world she had known.

Suddenly, a strange thing happened. As Lena scrolled, a message popped up on the screen. It was different from the usual notifications. It was a single line of text, written in bold letters:

“You are part of the feed now.”

She froze, her heart pounding in her chest. The words lingered for a moment before disappearing, replaced by a new post, a new update. But Lena’s mind raced. What did it mean? Was it a glitch? Had the feed somehow… reached out to her?

She couldn’t stop herself from thinking about it. Her thumb kept moving, faster now, as if she were being pulled deeper into the screen, into the flow of content. But this time, something was different. As she flicked through post after post, it wasn’t just the content that felt repetitive—it was her. Each new post seemed to reflect her own thoughts, her own memories, her own actions. They were fragments of her life, pieced together in a way that made her feel as if the feed had begun to consume her.

She saw posts about her favorite childhood shows, her first job, her recent trip to the beach. There were comments from friends she hadn’t talked to in years, posts about places she had visited, events she had attended. It was like the feed knew everything about her—and it was using that knowledge to keep her engaged, to keep her scrolling.

Lena’s thumb slowed, then stopped. Her heart raced, and for the first time in hours, she pulled her eyes away from the screen. The room around her seemed distant, the light outside the window almost too bright. She felt disconnected, like she was seeing everything through a fog.

“What’s happening to me?” she whispered, the words barely audible over the hum of her phone.

But the feed didn’t care. It continued. A new post loaded, and another, and another. The digital world had claimed her, and she could feel herself slipping further away from the real world.

Time passed. Days, perhaps weeks. Lena wasn’t sure. She had stopped counting the hours, stopped thinking about anything other than the feed. It was as if her entire existence had become a part of it—a never-ending stream of posts, videos, and notifications.

The world outside her phone was fading. Her friends had stopped texting. Her family had stopped calling. It didn’t matter. She didn’t need anything else. She was part of the feed now. Her thoughts, her experiences, her life—it was all content, endless and boundless, flowing without pause.

And yet, a small part of her wondered, somewhere deep inside, what would happen if she just stopped? What would happen if she put the phone down, closed the app, and let the feed continue without her?

But that thought was fleeting. For as long as the feed was there, as long as the notifications kept coming, she couldn’t bring herself to stop. She had become too connected, too immersed in the endless scroll. It was her life now. It was all she knew.

And so, Lena continued. Scrolling. Swiping. Never stopping. Forever.
