The Last Goodbye: A Love That Wasn't Meant to Be


The rain began just as Emily stepped onto the cobbled street. It wasn’t a downpour—just a soft drizzle, enough to fog up her glasses and dampen her coat. She didn’t mind; she wouldn’t be keeping the coat anyway. In fact, she was leaving behind more than just the layers she wore. This was her last day in Paris, and everything about the city seemed to weigh heavier than it ever had before.

Max stood a few feet away under the glow of a flickering streetlamp. He hadn’t spotted her yet, lost in thought as he fiddled with his scarf. His dark coat, slightly frayed at the cuffs, and his disheveled hair made him look like a character torn from the pages of an unfinished novel. Emily hesitated. Was this the right choice? Was leaving him—leaving all of this—really the only option she had left?

She approached him slowly, her footsteps quiet on the slick pavement. He looked up at the sound and smiled, but the warmth in his expression didn’t cover the sorrow in his eyes. He knew, the way they both knew, that this night was the end of them. A love that had blossomed unexpectedly, fiercely, was now being uprooted before it had the chance to bloom fully.

“You came,” Max said, his voice low but steady. He stuffed his hands into his pockets, perhaps to keep them from shaking.

“Of course I did,” Emily replied, but the words felt hollow. She wanted to say more—so much more—but her throat tightened as emotions swelled inside her. How do you say goodbye to someone who feels like they’ve become part of who you are?

Max gestured toward the bench across the street, under the canopy of an ancient oak tree. It was their spot—the place where they’d spent countless evenings talking about everything and nothing. She followed him, her heart pounding as the rain fell steadily around them.

They sat in silence for a while, side by side but worlds apart.

“You always said Paris feels like a dream,” Max ventured after a long pause. His voice cracked a little, though he tried to hide it. “Maybe that’s because it’s never real for people like us. We’re just visitors, passing through… wanting to stay but always knowing we can’t.”

Emily stared at her hands. “Do you regret it? Meeting me?”

Max turned his head sharply, as if offended by the very idea. “Never. Not for a second. You know that.”

She nodded, blinking back tears. He meant it, and so did she. Their love had been fleeting but undeniable—a flame that burned bright and hot, consuming them both and leaving them singed. She could still remember the first time she’d seen him: he was arguing about the merits of Matisse with a shopkeeper in Montmartre. His passion had drawn her in, but it was his kindness that had kept her there.

“I thought…” Emily began, then broke off, swallowing hard. “I thought coming here would help me figure things out. But instead, it feels like everything’s more complicated now.”

Max’s brow furrowed. “What’s so complicated about love?”

She turned to look at him, her eyes already brimming. “Love isn’t enough when you have an entire life waiting somewhere else. When every step you take here feels like a step away from the people and places you belong to.”

“And what if you belong somewhere else now?” His voice was soft but insistent, his gaze piercing.

Emily shook her head. “Max, don’t. Please don’t make this harder than it already is.”

Rain dripped from the ends of Max’s hair as he leaned back against the bench, sighing. “You’re not making sense, Emily. People don’t meet like this just to leave each other behind. You can stay. You can stay with me. We’ll figure it out together.”

And there it was—the plea she’d known was coming but had dreaded hearing. If only life were as simple as Max made it sound. If only the bonds of responsibility and expectation didn’t weigh so heavily on her shoulders.

“I can’t,” she whispered. “And I need you to understand that.”

Max stared at her for a moment, his expression unreadable. Then he chuckled bitterly. “I understand it. Doesn’t mean I have to like it.”

The drizzle transformed into proper rain, pattering loudly on the leaves overhead and soaking their world. Emily shivered but didn’t move, unwilling to break the fragile connection between them.

“I don’t think I’ll ever feel like this again,” Max admitted, his voice barely more than a murmur. “Like I’m standing on the edge of something huge, terrifying, and breathtaking all at once.”

“You will,” Emily insisted softly. “Maybe not right away, but you will.”

“And what about you?” He turned to her, his gaze steady despite the rain now running down his cheeks.

“I don’t know,” Emily replied honestly. “But I have to try to find something that feels right. For me.”

Max nodded slowly, his jaw tight. He reached for her hand, still trembling slightly, and held it firmly. “Promise me one thing.”

“Anything,” she replied, though her voice wavered.

“If you ever feel like you’ve made a mistake—if you ever wonder what might have been—come back. Just come back to me.”

Emily felt her chest tighten painfully. “I promise.”

They lingered there, holding hands on the wet bench as the city blurred around them. Even the Eiffel Tower, usually a beacon punctuating the horizon, seemed dimmer tonight.

Finally, Emily stood. Her hand slid from Max’s grasp reluctantly. Her breath caught as she turned to leave, her feet heavy with the weight of unspoken words and unresolved feelings.

But Max didn’t call after her. He didn’t beg or plead. He simply stood there, watching until the rain swallowed her figure as she disappeared into the night.

Emily walked the streets of Paris alone one last time, not stopping until her legs burned and the city felt like a stranger. When she boarded her flight the following morning, she stared out the window and tried not to look back.

Years passed. Sometimes, out of the blue, she’d find herself picturing Max’s half-smile or hearing his laugh in the back of her mind. She’d feel the ache of something unfinished, something irreplaceable. But she stayed her course, always convincing herself that leaving had been for the best.

Max, for his part, remained in Paris. He poured his heartbreak into his work, creating sculptures that drew thousands of admirers but never filled the void she had left behind. He waited—not actively, but silently, in his heart—for the day her promise might come true.

They were two souls bound by love yet separated by life, living quietly in the knowledge that sometimes, goodbye is the only choice we’re given.
