Once Upon the Night: Enchanting Stories for Bedtime


The frame of an ancient grandfather clock ticked faintly in the corner of the room, marking the start of another night. A soft hush fell across the household as distant winds teased the edges of the nearby woods. Adira—an eight-year-old girl with a head full of dreams—huddled beneath her cozy blanket. This was her sanctuary, her time for wonder. Every night she stared at the faint glow of her star-shaped lamp and awaited the moment her mother would arrive with the musty tome they’d come to cherish together, the one lovingly titled Once Upon the Night.

The nightly ritual wasn’t simply a routine for Adira; it was an experience that transformed the mundane into magic. Her mother, Elara, with a voice like running water, always began by saying, “Each story carries a spark, and tonight, we’ll kindle a new light.” Adira loved the way her mother spoke—like every syllable ticked with secret possibility. And tonight was no different. But there was something special in the air, something heavy but exciting. Adira felt it pulling at her thoughts even before the first page turned.

Elara sat beside her daughter, brushing a strand of hair away from her forehead. The room seemed to breathe in synchronization with them, still but alive.

“It’s time,” her mother whispered.

As the old tome opened to its weathered pages, a faint sound—a melodic hum—seemed to emanate from the book itself. Adira sat up with wide eyes. She sometimes fancied the book had a life of its own. It thrummed softly tonight, as though aware of the stories trapped inside.

Elara’s finger swept over the page, halting at the title of tonight’s tale: The Song Thief.

The Song Thief

Once, in a village perched high above a valley of silver rivers, there lived a girl named Linna who could sing the stars from the sky. Her voice wasn’t merely enchanting; it was said to cradle the deepest wishes of those who heard it. When Linna sang, crops grew taller and more golden, mothers held their laughter longer, and tears seemed to hold no weight.

But Linna’s gift was a secret she kept mostly to herself. She sang beneath the moonlight, far from prying eyes, letting her melodies drift over the hills and into the ears of the enchanted wildlife that gathered to listen. One such night, as she poured her soul into the emptiness, an unsettling figure emerged from the shadows at the edge of her favorite glade.

He was tall, draped in tattered, dark robes that whispered as he moved. His cloak glimmered faintly, as though it held the echoes of forgotten dreams. Linna stopped mid-song, her voice faltering as the figure stepped closer.

“Such a gift,” he said, his voice smooth but sharp, “but gifts unshared are gifts that risk fading. Do you not wish to spread your music further?”

Linna sensed the wrongness in his smile. “I sing for the world,” she replied, her tone guarded. “My melodies drift wherever the wind carries them.”

The man shook his head in mock disappointment. “What a pity, little soul. If you will not let the world know your power, perhaps it is best I take it… for safekeeping.”

Before Linna could react, he lifted his hand, and an icy breeze swept through the glade. The stars above her seemed to shudder, dimming as the melody that had once danced among them was pulled from her lips. She stumbled, mute and disoriented, clutching her throat in panic. The man laughed softly and disappeared into the night.

Adira gasped as her mother read the chilling turn of events. “What happens next?” she whispered, unable to mask her curiosity. Elara gave her a knowing smile and continued.

For days, Linna wandered the village in silence, her voice locked away in the clutches of the sinister stranger. The villagers, noticing her sudden quiet, became uneasy. Their crops seemed less vibrant, their joy more muted than before. Though they didn’t fully understand why, they felt the absence of Linna’s melodies like a hole in the fabric of their lives.

Desperate to reclaim her gift, she decided to climb the mountain that towered over the valley. Legends spoke of a wise woman who lived at its icy peak, a keeper of secrets and remedies for human misfortune. It would be a grueling journey, but Linna was determined.

The climb was merciless. Frostbitten winds lashed at her cheeks, and the steep paths threatened to crumble underfoot. But Linna pressed on, guided by faint memories of her songs and the hope that she might hear them again.

At last, she reached a stone arch guarded by two statues—a wolf and an owl. As she passed beneath it, she felt warmth return to her body. The wise woman’s dwelling was a cavern, glowing with the soft light of candles and strewn with peculiar crystals that hummed faintly. The woman herself was small, her silver hair flowing like quicksilver.

“You seek your voice,” the woman said before Linna could speak, her eyes glimmering with understanding. “And I see that your silence weighs heavier than the mountain’s snow.”

Linna nodded.

The wise woman gestured to a pool at the heart of the cavern, its surface rippling with golden light. “This water will show you what it takes to reclaim your stolen gift. But be warned—the path to reclaiming oneself often requires leaving parts behind.”

As Linna peered into the pool’s depths, she saw visions of herself descending into the shadowed realms below the earth. The Song Thief lived there, surrounded by crystalline jars that held the stolen voices of countless beings. To reclaim hers, she would need courage and the strength to confront her greatest fear.

“Are you willing to tread that path?” the wise woman asked.

Linna took a deep breath, her determination solid. “Yes.”

Adira clung to her blanket, her imagination spiraling with images of Linna facing an underground realm filled with glimmering jars of stolen voices. Her mother’s voice brought her back to the comforting rhythm of the story.

Linna descended day by day, deeper into the earth, guided by trails of faint, harmonic echoes that led to the Song Thief’s domain. The passageways grew darker, the air heavier, but she pressed on. At last, she reached a cavern lit with shards of blue light. There, at its center, stood the man who had stolen her gift.

“Back so soon?” he mused, his icy voice oozing amusement.

Linna spotted the jar that held her voice on a pedestal behind him. Its faint glow pulsed like a heartbeat, and her yearning to retrieve it almost overwhelmed her. But fear wouldn’t win tonight.

“I’ve come to claim what’s mine,” Linna said firmly.

The Song Thief chuckled, spreading his arms. “By all means. But gifts are never truly reclaimed without sacrifice. What will you offer in return?”

Linna hesitated. She thought of her melodies, how they had been her solace and her joy. She thought of the villagers, whose lives had grown quieter in her absence. Then, she realized the truth of her gift: her songs were never fully hers alone. They were meant to be shared.

“I offer my promise,” Linna said, her voice steady. “If I regain my gift, I will use it to bring light to my people—to the world. No more shadows to hide in.”

The Song Thief’s grin faltered, his form beginning to shimmer under the power of her resolve. The jar behind him trembled, and in the next instant, it shattered, sending a silver stream of melody rushing back to Linna. As her voice returned, the cavern echoed with the beauty of her song, driving the shadowy figure away into nothingness.

Elara paused, giving Adira time to absorb the ending. “And from that day forward,” she concluded, “Linna sang not only for herself, but for all who needed hope. Her voice became a beacon, a thread of light that wove through the village and beyond.”

Adira smiled. “That was the best story yet!” she exclaimed. But as the lamp’s glow dimmed with the deepening night, her voice softened. “Can you sing stars out of the sky, too, Mommy?”

Elara tucked her daughter in snugly and kissed her forehead. “Everyone can, my love—in their own way.”

The window beside them framed the night sky, and as the world settled into its own melody, Adira drifted to sleep. Somewhere—perhaps within the pages of another tale—the stars themselves began to hum.
