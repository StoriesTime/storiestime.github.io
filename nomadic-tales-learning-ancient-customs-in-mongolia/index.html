Nomadic Tales: Learning Ancient Customs in Mongolia


The wind swept across the vast steppes of Mongolia, rustling the tall grasses and carrying with it the smell of wild herbs and earth. It was a landscape where time seemed to stand still, where the sky stretched endlessly and the horizon met the land in a soft, blurry line. In this place, where the rhythm of life was dictated by the seasons, I found myself on a journey of discovery — a quest to learn the ancient customs and traditions of the Mongolian nomads.

I had come to Mongolia with a simple goal: to experience the lifestyle of the nomads, who have roamed this land for centuries. In the modern age, where technology often disconnects people from their roots, the nomads of Mongolia had maintained a deep connection to their heritage. Their customs were passed down through generations, not written in books, but lived and breathed in the daily rhythms of life.

The First Encounter: A Nomad’s Home

My first encounter with a Mongolian nomadic family was in the heart of the steppes, a few hours’ ride from the nearest town. As I approached the small ger — the traditional Mongolian tent — I could see the smoke rising from the chimney, a sign of warmth and hospitality. The ger is more than just a dwelling; it is a symbol of the nomadic way of life. It is portable, allowing the family to follow the seasonal movements of their herds, and it is structured to withstand the harsh winds and extreme temperatures that the steppes are known for.

Inside, the air was thick with the smell of fresh bread and meat simmering in a pot over an open flame. The walls were adorned with tapestries depicting scenes of pastoral life — herders on horseback, animals grazing under the vast sky, and the occasional mythical creature from Mongolian folklore. The family was gathered around a low table, and the matriarch, a woman in her sixties named Mungun, stood to greet me with a smile.

“Welcome to our home,” she said, her voice warm and welcoming. “You are here to learn, and we are here to teach.”

As I stepped inside, I felt a sense of being transported back in time, to a world where daily life was not governed by the ticking of clocks but by the sun, the moon, and the rhythms of nature. Mungun’s family — her husband, two sons, and a daughter-in-law — all greeted me with a mix of curiosity and warmth. They were eager to share their way of life, but more than that, they were excited to show me the wisdom of their ancestors.

The Art of Herding

Mongolia’s nomadic culture is deeply intertwined with animal husbandry. The vast expanses of land are perfect for grazing livestock, and for centuries, the Mongolian people have raised horses, camels, sheep, goats, and cattle. The herding lifestyle is not just about the animals; it is about a deep respect for the land and an intimate knowledge of its cycles.

In the days that followed, I learned the basics of herding — although it would take years of practice to truly understand the subtle cues of the land. Mungun’s sons, Batbayar and Tseren, took me out into the fields, where we rode horses across the undulating grasslands, guiding the herds to fresh pastures.

“Every step of the herd is calculated,” Batbayar explained as we rode alongside a group of goats. “You must watch the animals, listen to them, and know when they need rest or when they are ready to move.”

I quickly learned that herding was as much about intuition as it was about skill. A good herder could read the wind, the position of the sun, and the behavior of the animals to predict the weather and determine the best grazing spots. I was fascinated by how every movement — the way the horses nudged their heads, the way the sheep bleated in unison — carried meaning.

The family’s connection to their animals was evident in the way they spoke about them. Horses, in particular, were held in high esteem. They were more than just animals; they were considered sacred and a symbol of the Mongolian spirit. Riding a horse was not merely a practical activity but a spiritual experience. The way Batbayar spoke of his horse, a sturdy chestnut named Tenger, was almost reverential.

“Every horse has a spirit,” he said, “and it is our job to understand them, to ride with them, not against them.”

As I spent more time on horseback, I began to understand this connection. The rhythmic movement of the horse beneath me became almost meditative, and the vast open spaces of the steppe seemed to shrink, enclosing me in a world where the only sounds were the beat of hooves and the call of birds overhead.

The Tradition of Horsemanship

One of the most important aspects of Mongolian culture is horsemanship. It is said that the Mongol people were born on horseback, and this connection runs deep in their identity. The art of horsemanship is passed down from father to son, mother to daughter, and it is a skill that begins at an early age.

One morning, Mungun’s granddaughter, Amraa, a bright-eyed girl of about eight, invited me to join her for a lesson. With a confidence that belied her age, she led me to a small paddock where several young horses were kept. Amraa’s hands were steady as she approached the horses, speaking softly to them in a language I could not understand. Her movements were fluid, almost like a dance, and the horses seemed to respond to her every gesture.

“Horsemanship is not about force,” she told me, her voice serious for someone so young. “It is about respect. You must learn to speak with your horse, not shout at it.”

Amraa’s lesson was a simple one. She taught me how to approach a horse with calmness, how to mount it without startling it, and how to maintain balance while riding. Her instructions were not just practical but philosophical — every movement on horseback was a reflection of one’s inner state. A calm mind, she explained, would lead to a smooth ride, while anxiety or impatience would only lead to a restless horse.

That afternoon, I took my first solo ride across the steppe. The wind tugged at my face, and the horizon stretched endlessly before me. It felt like I was part of something much larger than myself, a living tradition that had existed for thousands of years. Riding across the open land, I realized that Mongolian horsemanship was not just a skill; it was a way of understanding the world.

The Importance of Ceremony and Rituals

As I spent more time with the family, I was introduced to the spiritual side of Mongolian nomadic life. The Mongols have a deep reverence for the natural world, and their customs are steeped in rituals that honor the land, the animals, and the ancestors. Every year, the family gathers to perform a ceremony known as the tsagaan sar, or the White Moon Festival, which marks the beginning of the lunar new year.

The festival is a time of renewal and celebration, a time when families come together to honor their ancestors and seek blessings for the year ahead. I had the privilege of witnessing this ceremony, which was a beautiful and humbling experience. The family prepared special foods, including boodog (a dish made from roasted meat) and airag (fermented mare’s milk), and offered them to the spirits of the land.

Mungun explained the significance of each item. “This food is not just for us,” she said. “It is for the spirits that guide us. We honor them with our offerings, and in return, they watch over us.”

As the family gathered around the fire that evening, they shared stories of their ancestors, of great warriors and leaders, of hardships endured and victories won. It was a time to remember that the nomadic way of life was not just about surviving; it was about thriving in harmony with nature, about maintaining a balance between the physical and the spiritual.

The ritual ended with a prayer to the spirits of the steppe, asking for a bountiful year and protection for their herds. It was a simple act, yet it carried with it a profound sense of gratitude and respect for the forces that shaped their lives.

A New Understanding

By the time I left Mungun’s family and the vast steppes of Mongolia, I had gained a deeper understanding of the nomadic way of life. It was not just a lifestyle; it was a worldview, a philosophy rooted in respect for nature, community, and tradition. The customs of the Mongolian nomads were not confined to rituals and ceremonies; they were woven into every aspect of their daily lives — from the way they herded animals to the way they treated each other and the land.

As I boarded a plane back to the city, I couldn’t help but reflect on my time in Mongolia. The lessons I had learned were simple, but they were profound: to live in harmony with nature, to respect the wisdom of the past, and to honor the spirits that guide us. These ancient customs, passed down through generations of nomads, had not only shaped the lives of the Mongolian people but had also left an indelible mark on my own understanding of the world.

In the heart of the Mongolian steppes, amidst the vast, open spaces, I had discovered something timeless — a way of life that had endured for centuries, and that, despite the encroachments of modernity, still thrived in the hearts of those who called the steppe their home.
