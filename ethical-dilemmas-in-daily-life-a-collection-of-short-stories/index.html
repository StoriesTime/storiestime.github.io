Ethical Dilemmas in Daily Life: A Collection of Short Stories


Sophie stood in line at the café, scanning the menu on the wall, her fingers tapping the phone screen for a quick glance at the weather. She noticed the man in front of her paying for his coffee, then moving quickly to the door, completely oblivious to the wallet he left behind on the counter. The wallet was thick, bulging slightly as if filled with cash and cards. Sophie froze for a moment, her mind racing.

Should she take it to the counter? What if the man needed it for something urgent? On the other hand, what if no one noticed and it went unclaimed? There were cash cards inside. Sophie couldn’t help but wonder if she’d be able to use one of them—just a small transaction, maybe. She had bills to pay.

But, then again, Sophie wasn’t that type of person. Or was she?

She glanced around. The café was busy, and the only staff member was in the back preparing drinks. Sophie bit her lip, fighting the temptation. Finally, she reached out and grabbed the wallet, hurrying to the counter.

A few minutes later, the man returned, looking frantic.

“Did anyone find a wallet?” he asked, his voice thick with worry.

Sophie smiled, handing it over. “Here you go.”

The man took a deep breath, almost relieved. “I swear, I’d be lost without it. Thank you. You just saved me a lot of trouble.”

As he left, Sophie couldn’t help but feel a small sense of pride. In a world full of temptation, it felt good to make the right choice, even when no one was watching.

Story 2: The Office Printer

It was just a typical Tuesday afternoon at the marketing firm, and Mark was stuck in a long queue for the office printer. It had been printing out endless promotional materials for a client that had requested an urgent campaign for their new product launch. Mark had been working late for the past few days, getting everything organized. When his turn came, he hit print on a document that had been sitting in his inbox for hours.

However, as the printer began to churn, Mark noticed a document that wasn’t his. It was a draft of a proposal for an upcoming project—one that had been submitted by his colleague, Sarah. She had been working on it for weeks, and from what he had overheard in the office, it was a big deal for her career.

The document had some mistakes, obvious ones, but if Mark used the ideas in it—just the key points—it could save him hours of work. In fact, he could even improve the proposal by tweaking the language and presenting it as his own.

But then he thought about Sarah. She was a hard worker, always staying late just like him, and she had been upfront about how much she cared about this project. Mark was proud of his own work and didn’t want to take advantage of someone else’s.

The printer clicked as it finished printing his own document, and he quickly walked away. Later, when Sarah was at her desk, he approached her.

“I saw the proposal you were working on. It looks great, but I noticed a few things you might want to fix. I’d be happy to help.”

Sarah smiled. “Thanks, Mark. I really appreciate it.”

Story 3: The Stray Cat

It had been a long day for Nora. The evening had fallen, and the soft hum of the streetlights signaled the end of another workday. As she walked to her car, she heard a soft mewing sound from behind a trash can. Nora paused, looking around. She saw nothing at first, but the sound continued.

As she walked closer, she found a small, scruffy kitten, no older than a few months, curled up in a corner. Its fur was matted, its eyes wide and fearful. It looked like it had been abandoned. Nora’s heart softened as she crouched down, extending her hand slowly.

The kitten didn’t move at first, but then, hesitantly, it crawled toward her, mewing louder. Nora picked it up gently. It was light, almost too light for a cat of its size, and its ribs were visible beneath the fur.

But Nora had her hands full already. She lived in an apartment that didn’t allow pets. Her boyfriend had a severe cat allergy. And she was allergic too. She wasn’t even sure if she had the time to take care of the cat.

She looked around. The streets were empty, and the little creature in her arms was in need of care. Nora hesitated. Was she ready to make a commitment she wasn’t prepared for?

At that moment, Nora made a decision. She placed the kitten in the back seat of her car and headed for the nearest animal shelter. She might not have been ready for a pet, but she wasn’t about to turn her back on something that needed help.

Story 4: The Lost Phone

Adam walked through the crowded subway station, his hands deep in his pockets. He glanced at his watch—he was late. As he pushed through the crowd, something shiny on the ground caught his eye. A phone, left behind on the platform, face down and abandoned. He picked it up, scanning the screen. There was no lock on it, and he could see some notifications. Text messages from a name he didn’t recognize. The phone looked new—likely an expensive one.

Adam weighed his options. He could take the phone. He had no idea who it belonged to, but with no lock, he could probably access the contacts, maybe even find a way to sell it. He didn’t need to be told that the world ran on consumer electronics, and he needed some extra cash. A quick sale, a little extra padding in his bank account. No one would know.

But then, his thoughts drifted. What if it was important to someone? What if the person had lost their phone in a rush, or maybe they had left it on purpose to get it back later? There was a chance the owner would be looking for it. It could be the difference between their job or missing a critical appointment.

Adam sighed. He had just enough time to call the owner, if there was a contact number on the screen.

He dialed the number.

The phone rang, and after a few moments, a voice answered. “Hello?”

“I found your phone,” Adam said. “I’m at the subway station. Where can I meet you?”

Story 5: The Charity Donation

Lily was always on the lookout for ways to give back. Every year, she would donate to different causes. Animal shelters, food banks, children’s hospitals—she felt it was her duty to do her part. So when she received an email asking for a donation to a charity that helped underprivileged children, she didn’t hesitate.

She clicked the link and was about to enter her credit card information when she noticed something strange. The website was well-designed, but it didn’t seem familiar. The logo wasn’t one she recognized, and the lack of contact details made her second-guess. She clicked on the “About Us” section, but it was empty. A wave of suspicion washed over her. Was this a scam?

Lily was faced with a dilemma. She wanted to help, but she also didn’t want to send money to an organization that might misuse it. She didn’t have the resources to waste money on fraudulent causes, but what if she was wrong? What if this was legitimate and she passed on the opportunity to make a difference?

She closed the window and took a deep breath. Instead of donating to this mysterious charity, she would reach out to organizations she knew and trusted.

Lily knew that even when her heart wanted to give, there were risks involved. She wasn’t ready to risk her generosity being taken advantage of.

Story 6: The Favor

Ella stood in the hallway, staring at her friend’s apartment door. She had promised to pick up her friend’s dry cleaning, but the truth was, she wasn’t sure she could handle it. Her work had been stressful lately, and she was barely keeping up with her own tasks. The dry cleaning was an extra favor, but her friend had been there for her countless times in the past.

She knocked on the door, only to be greeted by a frazzled-looking Emily, who smiled weakly. “Hey, sorry to bother you again, but I just realized I forgot my lunch for work. Could you grab it for me?”

Ella’s stomach sank. Another favor. The pressure to always be the one helping others was beginning to feel suffocating. Ella had her own responsibilities, and while she loved being there for her friends, there was only so much she could take before she felt stretched too thin.

“Of course, Emily,” Ella said with a forced smile. She grabbed her friend’s lunch bag and rushed out the door. As she walked to the café, Ella reflected on how easy it was to say yes to someone else’s needs, but how difficult it was to admit when you were at your breaking point.

When she returned, she asked Emily, “I think I need to start saying ‘no’ a little more often. You get that, right?”

Emily paused for a moment and nodded. “I get it. Maybe I need to start helping you, too.”

These stories highlight how everyday decisions often present us with ethical dilemmas. In each case, the characters must weigh their actions against their values, balancing the needs of others with their own principles. From honesty and responsibility to self-care and empathy, these moments in daily life challenge us to act in ways that define our character.
