Quarantine Chronicles: Life in the Shadows


The city had hollowed out long before the whispers began, long before the silent streets betrayed their former vibrance. Once, throngs of people rushed past one another in hurried exchanges—not anymore. The year it began extended into a suffocating rhythm that no one had anticipated, and by its second winter, life had become entirely unrecognizable. People talked of “light at the end of the tunnel,” but for most, it felt more like an endless labyrinth in the shadows.

I remember the day the announcement came: an indefinite quarantine. The concept felt like an abstraction at first, something theorized in offices over long conference calls—a contingency plan for an improbable scenario. But nothing could prepare us for the reality of it. It wasn’t just a matter of staying indoors or donning masks anymore; it was a full-scale descent into isolation.

The Beginner’s Handbook to Darkness

By the third week, I’d developed systems. Routine became the scaffolding for sanity, the thread that tethered me to the illusion of normalcy. Dawn broke softly through the grey haze beyond my curtained windows, and I rose at the same time each morning. Stretching, meditating, counting breaths—things I barely had time for back when life brimmed with movement. The heavy echoes of silence lay atop the apartment like a blanket no one asked for.

Once vibrant with sunlight and chatter from neighbors, my block now felt like a relic of a forgotten civilization. The walls seemed to close in, their shadows creeping farther into my thoughts each day. I found solace in the little things: the hum of the refrigerator, the distant song of a bird who hadn’t yet forgotten the melody of joy. But it wasn’t enough.

Technology became the bridge to the outside world, but even then, it came with compromises. Zoom calls flattened faces into pixelated masks, voices half-swallowed by stuttering bandwidth. Social media spiraled into noise—an endless scroll of fear, opinions, and an exhausting stream of nostalgia for life that felt both alien and impossibly close. My world shrank from a city to five rooms and a screen.

Shadows Dance More Freely in Isolation

It was on one of those endless evenings that my attention curiously wandered toward the space beneath my bed. A fleeting notion, just a second-long glance. A shadow rested there—not one cast by sunlight or by the glow of my desk lamp. It was darker than the room warranted, as though it swallowed the meager light around it. For a moment, I dismissed it as nothing. Surely, my mind craved distraction so desperately that it fabricated intrigue.

But the shadow didn’t leave.

Every night at approximately 9 PM, it seemed to grow bolder—the edges of its inky depths creeping ever so slightly outward, as though daring me to acknowledge its existence. By the second week, the shadow had fully extended itself to the foot of my bed. Perhaps I should have known something was strange when my cat refused to approach it, arching her furry back and hissing anytime she strayed too close.

Human beings are curious creatures. And solitude has a way of amplifying that curiosity. One unremarkable Tuesday amid the monotony of lockdown, I decided to investigate. Armed with nothing but my phone’s flashlight, I crouched at the side of my bed and peered underneath, squinting into the murky dark.

What I found wasn’t a shadow at all.

The Neighbor in the Void

This is where explanations falter. Beneath my bed rested something. No—someone. A figure so indistinct it might as well have been smoke. Its shape shifted as my flashlight flickered over it—an impression of a person, but just barely discernible. No eyes. No expression. Just…presence.

My first instinct was panic. I scrambled backward, collapsing onto the floor, but the figure didn’t move. It didn’t follow me at all. Instead, I heard—or rather felt—its voice. The sound was less like speech and more like thought transferred directly into my mind.

“You found me,” it said, its tone resolute. “I’ve been here longer than you realize.”

I couldn’t speak. My throat felt fused shut, chest constricted under the weight of fear.

“Why?” was the only word I managed to choke out.

The figure almost seemed to shrug, its shapeless form dipping slightly as though indicating indifference. “Because your world slowed down enough to see me. I am always here. Always will be. You were merely too distracted with life before the shadows, too blind to notice.”

“No one else…” I stuttered. “No one’s mentioned…”

“No one dares,” it interrupted flatly. “To admit your hopelessness is to confront me. People give me names: loneliness, despair, isolation. But I am not those things. I am the shadow beneath all that thrives in darkness.”

Turning Toward the Gloom

Time stood frozen for what felt like hours, though it must have been mere moments. As I backed into the farthest corner of my bedroom, clutching my knees to my chest in trembling silence, I thought about the truth of its words. It wasn’t wrong—I recognized this shadow, this entity. It had lingered at the edges of my consciousness since quarantine began, whispering reminders of lost connection, failed expectations, mortality itself. Though I dared not admit it, the figure had become familiar.

It didn’t threaten me. It didn’t advance. It simply existed, waiting in the void beneath my bed. And over the coming days, I noticed it wasn’t confined just there—it followed in other rooms, pooling in corners whenever light dared dim.

There came a morning when I stopped looking away from it.

To stare directly into shadows is neither natural nor easy. We tune ourselves to brightness, because anything less promises unknowns we cannot bear to face. But the quarantine had left me little choice. If my world had grown so small and unencumbered by society’s noise, perhaps it was inevitable that I’d confront the darkness staring back at me.

Day after day, I sat with it. The figure never addressed me again but remained constant, an unyielding companion. Over time, my fear twisted into something more ambiguous—an acceptance mingled with curiosity. I began to wonder: Could it be possible that darkness was not an enemy, but a truth unearthed by silence?

Life in the Shadows

Months passed, though I barely marked their drift. Quarantine dragged on as the outside world struggled to contain whatever catastrophe had unraveled our former lives. The walls of my apartment no longer felt as suffocating as they had before; instead, they became a frame for reflection. The entity in the shadows no longer filled me with dread. It wasn’t comforting, yet neither was it threatening. It simply was, no more deceptive than the sunrise or the air I breathed.

Each evening at 9 PM, the shadow would emerge beneath my bed once more, an ink blot that expanded across time, memory, and silence. And as my cat curled cautiously by my side, I let it stay.
The outside world would return eventually. Streets would hum with life again, chatter would spill from bustling cafes and store-fronts. But the quiet had taught me something few dared to learn: there was life in the shadows too—truth fits most comfortably where distraction cannot follow.

Maybe that figure beneath my bed wasn’t confined to quarantine after all. Maybe it had always been there, waiting for the world to stop spinning just long enough to be noticed.
