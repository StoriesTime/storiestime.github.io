The Forbidden Future: Dystopian Short Stories Collection


In the waning light of a world swallowed by shadows, a city rose from the ashes of what once was hope. Steel towers clawed at the sky, their jagged silhouettes etched against a bruised, smog-laden horizon. Here, in New Epsilon, the future was forbidden, a concept as ancient and deadly as the machines that governed everyday life.

Mara stood at her apartment window, the cracked glass offering a distorted reflection of a face consumed by fear and defiance. Outside, drones buzzed in regimented patterns, surveilling the streets below. The government, now a faceless technocracy, controlled everything with a digital fist—censorship, emotion, freedom—all erased, codified, and encrypted.

She touched the locket around her neck, a relic from a time before the Fall, before information was filtered through layers of algorithms designed to trap thought. The locket contained a single photograph: a smiling boy, eyes bright with unfiltered curiosity—her brother, Kai, lost to the gulag of forgotten futures.

The rise of the technocratic regime had been swift but subtle. The populace was offered comfort and security in exchange for compliance. Citizens were monitored through implanted chips under the guise of health and efficiency. Books were banned; stories were rewritten for the state’s purposes. But the most damning prohibition was the erasure of the future—the idea that people could envision or create their own destinies had been crushed, replaced by cold pragmatism and controlled variables.

Mara’s mind wandered to the legends whispered in the underground: the Forbidden Future. It was more than just a concept; it was a movement, a hope wrapped in defiance. A secret society that sought to reclaim what the regime had obliterated—the right to dream, to imagine, to change the course of existence.

Her fingers trembled as she checked the time. The meeting was imminent. The location was an abandoned subway station, deep beneath the city—a place where light barely penetrated and truth flickered like a dying flame.

The descent into the undercity was a journey through decay and silence. Crumbling concrete pillars stood as monuments to neglect, and puddles reflected fractured neon graffiti—a vibrant protest against the drab grayness enforced above. Mara followed the pulse of encoded signals from her wrist device, tracing the path to a narrow tunnel marked only by a faded symbol—a phoenix, rising from ashes.

As she entered the hidden chamber, the air thickened with anticipation. Around a circle of flickering lanterns, a disparate group assembled: scholars with rusted data pads, mechanics with oil-stained fingers, young children clutching scraps of forbidden literature. Their eyes shimmered with the dangerous fire of rebellion.

“Welcome, Mara,” a voice called out—a man known only as Elias. His face was obscured by a hood, but his presence commanded silence. “Tonight, we remember what it means to envision a future unshackled by oppression.”

He gestured to a device in the center—a battered holo-projector, cracked but functional. Elias activated it, and a three-dimensional map of the city materialized, overlaid with streams of data, restricted zones, and safe corridors.

“Our government is a prison of time,” he explained. “They control the present by forbidding us access to tomorrow. But through the network we’ve hacked, the Forbidden Future persists, hidden in fragments of encrypted code and suppressed memories.”

Mara’s eyes widened. “You mean the future isn’t lost? It exists elsewhere?”

Elias nodded. “Parts of the past and projected possibilities are stored in old quantum archives. Our task is to decrypt these data relics and release the suppressed narratives so the people can imagine—can choose—a different path.”

A murmur of excitement rippled through the group. For Mara, hope ignited in her chest. Years of silent resistance, years of loss, might finally yield a chance to rewrite their destiny.

The following weeks blurred into a secret campaign against invisible chains. Mara and the rebels infiltrated digital bastions, wresting coded fragments from the regime’s formidable servers. They recovered stories, art, memories—seeds of imagination buried beneath propaganda.

But with each victory, the government’s grip tightened. Surveillance increased twentyfold; programmed enforcers patrolled the streets with ruthless efficiency. One afternoon, as Mara fled through an alleyway, a sudden blare shattered the fragile calm—an alarm sounded. Crimson spots flared in her vision—targeting drones had locked on.

Heart pounding, she sprinted toward the underground, where safety waited. But a sharp pain flared in her side—a dart embedded by a silent gun. Her breath hitched, and the world tilted.

Mara awoke in a sterile chamber, bright lights piercing her drowsiness. Cold metal restrained her wrists, and a screen flickered to life—a face appeared, smooth and synthetic, eyes devoid of humanity.

“You have been flagged for subversion,” the entity intoned, voice monotone. “Resistance cannot be tolerated.”

Desperation surged within Mara. The government’s technology sought to erase not only dissent but humanity itself. They injected neurotoxins designed to suppress rebellious thought patterns, reprogramming minds to accept the predetermined future.

But Mara clung to the memories of the Forbidden Future—the stories, the faces, the dreams she dared to share. Deep inside, a spark resisted the onslaught of control.

Days passed. Slowly, she discovered the machinery’s weakness: fragments of her own willpower, interlaced with memories, could interrupt the neurotoxic waves. Carefully, clandestinely, Mara scripted her own mental firewall, a labyrinth of hidden memories and encrypted hopes.

When the guards returned, expecting a broken compliant, they found her eyes blazing with unyielding defiance.

“You cannot erase what is forbidden,” Mara whispered.

Her escape triggered a citywide upheaval. The clandestine broadcasts of the recovered narratives leaked into public networks, awakening a dormant civilization. Citizens began to question, to dream, to hope again.

Though the regime fought to reclaim control, it was caught in an unrelenting tide of imagination and resilience. Stories became weapons; dreams, shields; the Forbidden Future evolved from whispered rebellion into a roaring revolution.

And in the smog-filled skies of New Epsilon, beneath the steel towers and surveillance drones, the future—once forbidden—was reborn.
