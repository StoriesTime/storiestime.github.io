The Charge of the Light Brigade: Miscommunication in Battle


The air was heavy with tension as the British Army of Lord Raglan, consisting of 5,000 soldiers, stood on the North Shore of the Balaclava Bay, ready to lead the Charge of the Light Brigade into battle. The objective was to capture the strategic Fedyukhin Heights, overlooking the Oscarian Valley, in an attempt to nullify the Russian fleet's defensive position. However, a series of critical miscommunications had set the stage for disaster, setting the stage for one of the most infamous battles in history. It began innocently enough. The British commanding officers believed that the Russians were unaware of the planned assault, a piece of intelligence that proved to be grossly inaccurate. This miscalculation, coupled with the mistakenly assumed vaporization of fog, significantly altered the readiness of the cavalry. The light brigade, led by General Lord Raglan and General Airey, believed they would launch into an unseen spell, clouding their judgment with every step. General Admiral Putnik, aware of the British plans through intercepted communications, deployed units to correct for the mistake. In the heat of battle, Raglan had arrived in front of the heavily fortified fortress to lament structural damage that the Fedyukhin heights disaster did nothing to alleviate.
