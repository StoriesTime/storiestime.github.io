Invisible Chains: The Struggle with OCD


The morning light filtered through the sheer curtains of Mia’s bedroom, casting delicate patterns across her room. To an outsider, it might have seemed like a perfect day, with the sun shining and the world in full bloom. But for Mia, each morning opened a door to a battle she felt she was losing. The moment she awoke, the invisible chains of her obsessive-compulsive disorder tightened around her, reminding her of the rituals she had to perform to keep the chaos at bay.

Mia sat up slowly, her heart racing as her mind began its relentless calculations. “Three times,” she whispered to herself, a mantra she had adopted over the years. Three times to check if the door was locked, three times to ensure the stove was off. Each action required a tangible proof of safety, yet the reassurance was always fleeting. The comfort she derived from these rituals was like a mirage; it vanished just as quickly as it appeared.

As she swung her legs out of bed, her feet met the cool wooden floorboards, sending a shiver up her spine. She hesitated, staring at her slippers. They were neatly aligned, but the left one seemed slightly askew. A wave of anxiety washed over her. She felt the weight of the world pressing down on her, the invisible chains tightening. With a deep breath, she knelt down and adjusted the slipper, aligning it perfectly with the right one. Only then did she feel a momentary sense of relief.

Mia’s day unfolded in a series of checks, balances, and rituals. Each task was laden with the heaviness of her disorder. In the kitchen, she prepared breakfast, counting each ingredient meticulously. A dash of salt required a double-check, while the bread needed to be placed back in the bag just so. She could feel the eyes of her thoughts upon her, critiquing every move. “What if I forget to lock the door? What if I leave the stove on?” The questions swirled, creating a tempest in her mind.

Every tick of the clock echoed her fear, reminding her of the time she lost to these compulsions. Mia was a prisoner of her own mind, shackled by the chains of her obsessions, yet she wore a brave face to the world. At work, she was known as the diligent employee, the one who never made mistakes. But behind that facade, she was wrestling with an internal storm that threatened to consume her.

As a graphic designer, Mia was tasked with creating captivating visuals for her clients. Yet her creativity was often stifled by her compulsions. She would spend hours perfecting a single line, adjusting its curvature repeatedly until it felt just right. The pressure to produce flawless work was immense; mistakes were not acceptable in her world. Every time she deviated from her self-imposed standards, guilt washed over her, reminding her that she was not good enough.

During lunch breaks, Mia would often find refuge in a small café down the street. The barista, a cheerful young woman named Lila, would greet her with a warm smile. “The usual?” Lila would ask, already preparing Mia’s favorite caramel macchiato. It was one of the few moments of normalcy in Mia’s day, yet even that was tainted by the chains of her OCD. She had to sit at the same table, and if it was occupied, she would leave rather than risk an uncomfortable experience sitting somewhere else.

One particularly challenging day, Mia sat outside the café, tapping her foot against the pavement. The sun was warm, yet her heart felt cold, weighed down by the thoughts swirling in her mind. “Just breathe,” she told herself, trying to ward off the panic that threatened to rise. But as she sipped her coffee, her gaze fell on a group of children playing in the park across the street. They were carefree, free from the chains that bound her. Laughter rang in the air, a sound so foreign yet beautiful. “What would it be like to be so free?” she wondered.

That evening, as Mia sat in her dimly lit apartment, she decided to confront her fears. She picked up her sketchpad and began to draw. The pencil glided across the paper with a life of its own, creating shapes and curves that danced together in harmony. For the first time in months, she felt a flicker of freedom. Her mind quieted, the chains loosening their grip as creativity flowed through her veins. But just as she was lost in the moment, doubt resurfaced. What if the drawing was not good enough? What if she couldn’t replicate this feeling again?

Frustrated, she slammed her pencil down, sending it clattering to the floor. Tears of anger and despair streamed down her face. It was a vicious cycle—one moment of clarity quickly overshadowed by the heaviness of her disorder. She felt like she was fighting an uphill battle, and the mountain only grew steeper.

Days turned into weeks, and the turbulence within Mia persisted. She sought help from a therapist, Dr. Harper, who specialized in cognitive-behavioral therapy. On her first visit, Mia sat in the plush chair, fidgeting with the hem of her shirt. “I don’t even know where to start,” she admitted, her voice barely above a whisper.

Dr. Harper offered a compassionate smile. “Let’s begin with understanding your thoughts. OCD thrives on uncertainty, and together we can challenge those thoughts.” Their sessions became a sanctuary for Mia, a place where she could unburden herself. They worked on exposure and response prevention—the idea of confronting her fears without engaging in rituals. It was terrifying yet liberating.

One day, Dr. Harper suggested a simple exercise. “Try to leave your house without performing your locks check. Just walk away and see how it feels.” The mere thought sent Mia into a spiral of anxiety. “I can’t do that,” she protested, her heart racing. “What if I leave it unlocked?”

“Let’s explore that fear,” Dr. Harper encouraged gently. “What’s the worst that could happen?”

Mia took a deep breath, contemplating the question. “Someone could break in,” she replied hesitantly.

“And if that happened, what would you do?”

“I… I would call the police,” she stammered, the logic breaking through her fear. It was a small step, but it felt monumental.

Eventually, Mia found the courage to try. On a stifling Saturday morning, she stood at her front door, hand poised over the lock, her heart pounding in her chest. “Just breathe,” she whispered to herself. With a shaky hand, she turned the knob and stepped outside without checking. The world outside felt different—vivid and alive, yet tinged with the possibility of chaos.

As she walked through her neighborhood, she felt a flicker of freedom. The invisible chains were still there, but they felt less suffocating. Mia strolled through the park, watching families enjoy their weekends, their laughter echoing her newfound hope.

With each passing day, she practiced more exposure tasks. Leaving certain items out of place, skipping the rituals, and even allowing herself to make mistakes in her work. It was terrifying, but each small victory nudged the chains further away from her heart.

Months later, Mia sat at her desk, a sketchbook open in front of her. The sun streamed through the window, and instead of perfecting every line, she let her pencil wander across the page, free from the weight of expectation. The drawing took shape—a whimsical landscape filled with vibrant colors and joyful creatures. It was chaotic, imperfect, and yet, it filled her with joy.

Mia smiled, realizing that she was more than her OCD. She was a complex tapestry of experiences, emotions, and creativity. The chains were still there, but they no longer defined her. She was learning to navigate the world differently, finding beauty in the chaos and strength in vulnerability.

The journey was not linear; there were still days when the chains felt heavy, but now she had tools and strategies to cope. She had found a support system, and most importantly, she was learning to be kind to herself. The road was long, but with each day, she took another step toward freedom, unraveling the invisible chains that had once bound her.

And as the seasons changed outside her window, so did Mia. Winter thawed into spring, layers of ice breaking away to reveal the vibrant life below. With each passing day, she embraced her journey, knowing that she was not alone in her struggle. There was a community of others fighting their own battles, and together, they were slowly but surely breaking free from their invisible chains.
