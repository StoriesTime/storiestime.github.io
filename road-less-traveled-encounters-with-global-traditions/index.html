Road Less Traveled: Encounters with Global Traditions


Murphy Donahue’s boots crunched against gravel as the narrow path ahead twisted into the unknown. He had stepped away from the well-oiled itineraries of group tours, the polished brochures promising sanitized encounters with culture, and chosen instead to carve a path deeper—into the heart of traditions untouched by the rush of modernity.

His friends called him eccentric. Who else would leave behind a high-paying advertising job in Chicago, pack a journal, camera, and a thirst for something more, and head off to places Google Maps hadn’t yet charted? Maybe there were easier adventures, but Murphy wasn’t searching for easy. He was searching for meaning.

The Rhythms of West Africa

The first leg of his journey brought him to Burkina Faso, where the hammocks of shadow dappled under baobab trees and the air thrummed with the cadence of drums. He had heard whispers of a village near Bobo-Dioulasso, rumored to keep alive one of the world’s oldest musical traditions.

As Murphy approached the village, the distant vibration of drums began to grow, carrying on the breeze as if the earth was breathing. When he arrived, a circle of musicians stood in a dusty clearing, each striking their djembe with a precision that defied the simplicity of the instrument. They didn’t play in isolation; their rhythms intertwined with an intensity that told stories of harvests, reunions, and ancestry.

Murphy introduced himself, fumbling through his French and sporadic Dioula phrases until a man named Issouf placed a drum into his hands. The patterns weren’t complex if taken individually, but the magic lay in the synergy—and Murphy’s clumsy attempts at joining them were met with patient smiles.

It was after dusk, under layers of stars brighter than any city streetlamp, that Issouf explained the true meaning of the drumming. It wasn’t merely music; it was life. Each beat was a reminder that individuals are threads in a vast, communal tapestry. For the first time, Murphy felt the pulse of tradition beneath his hands, an unbroken rhythm that stretched back centuries.

Tea and Silence in the Nomadic Deserts

Murphy’s next journey took him across the ocean to Mongolia and its stark, unforgiving Gobi Desert. He marveled at the vastness, where horizons stretched endlessly and the land told its secrets only to the determined. Here, he sought a deeper understanding of the nomadic herders who lived in harmony with the earth’s rhythms.

After hours of trekking on horseback, Murphy’s guide led him to a cluster of gers—the circular, felt-lined tents that dotted the landscape. A young husband and wife invited him in, herding him with grace and the authority that comes from living on the edges of the world. The inside of the tent was organized yet modest. Every item held purpose.

A pot boiled over a small stove in the center as the wife prepared suutei tsai—a traditional milk tea. The aroma was warm and rich, filled with a humbling simplicity. There was little conversation, but Murphy didn’t mind. The silence was punctuated only by the occasional neighing of horses tethered nearby and the whisper of wind against the tent fabric.

Later, as the family showed him how they cared for their goats, Murphy couldn’t help but notice the seamless blend of human and nature. Here, survival was tied to tradition. When he left a few days later, he carried their quiet wisdom with him, realizing that this silence spoke louder than the noise that often drowned his own life back home.

Rituals of Renewal in South India

The monsoon rains fell like silver threads as Murphy arrived in a coastal town in Kerala, India. The annual Theyyam festival was in its peak season, drawing locals to temple courtyards in celebration as ancient rituals unfolded.

One evening, Murphy stood amid the crowd, his senses overwhelmed by the colors and motions that defied imagination. Men and women adorned themselves with elaborate costumes, intricate face paint, and towering headdresses. Flames cast dancing shadows onto their features, intensifying their transformation.

Murphy spoke with Arun, a performer in his late twenties, whose turn to embody the divine that night had arrived. Arun explained that each Theyyam artist merged with the spirit of a deity, becoming a vessel for blessing and balance. It wasn’t mere performance—it was transcendence.

When the drumming intensified, Arun moved into a trance-like state. His leaps and twirls defied exhaustion or hesitation, cutting through the humid air with the precision of an ancient dance perfected over generations. The crowd pressed closer, enamored by the palpable divine energy that radiated from every move.

Murphy later learned that this annual ritual wasn’t just a show—it was a measure of devotion, renewal, and connection between humans and their gods. In the ebb of the festival, watching exhausted performers bow and the crowd disperse with satisfaction, Murphy felt something shift inside him, too—a quiet realignment.

Seeking the Spirits in the Andes

Weeks later, Murphy found himself perched high in the Andes of Peru. He was drawn to a ceremony known as the Despacho, an intricate offering ritual held by the Quechua people as a way of communing with Pachamama, or Mother Earth.

Led by an elder named Valeria, the ritual began with the assembly of an offering. Flowers, seeds, coca leaves, and grains were arranged in spiraling patterns onto a cloth, each ingredient chosen with care and imbued with intention. Valeria spoke softly in Quechua, her words soft as the mountain breeze, as she invited Murphy to add his own contribution to the offering.

What struck Murphy most wasn’t the ritual itself but the reverence with which every participant approached it. When they finally folded the cloth and burned it in a small fire, Valeria explained that the smoke carried their gratitude and prayers into the realms where unseen energies dwell. Murphy realized that spirituality here wasn’t an abstract concept—it was a tangible practice tied to everyday survival and gratitude.

As Murphy watched the smoke dissipate into the thin mountain air, he thought about the storms of his own life—the deadlines, the sleepless nights, the unspoken anxieties. In those rising tendrils, he saw possibilities for healing.

A Journey’s Unfolding

When Murphy finally returned home to Chicago, his boots had worn significantly thinner, his journal fat with notes and pressed leaves from distant places. But more than that, he carried new threads of understanding stitched into himself. He had not merely traveled; he had listened.

The djembe rhythms of West Africa now inspired the cadence of his breaths. The quiet tea of the Mongolian desert brought him peace when city chaos felt too loud. The transcendent ritual dances in Kerala gave him courage to leap into unfamiliar territory, and the Andean ceremony reminded him to create space for gratitude, even amid the busyness.

Murphy had discovered that the road less traveled wasn’t a place—it was an approach. Whether in Burkina Faso, Mongolia, Kerala, or Peru, the essence of tradition lay in seeking connection with the roots of existence. And though the paths he walked had ended, their wisdom continued to guide him, shaping the rhythms of his everyday life in ways he hadn’t imagined.

Perhaps the best gift the road offered wasn’t the chance to leave—it was the ability to return, carrying the world within.
