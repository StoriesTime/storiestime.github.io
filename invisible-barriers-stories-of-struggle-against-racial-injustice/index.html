Invisible Barriers: Stories of Struggle Against Racial Injustice


The sound of the subway train rattling down the tracks was familiar to Elijah, a constant reminder of the struggles and rhythms of life in New York City. It was early morning, the streets still wet from last night’s rain, and the city felt like it was still waking up. The commuters packed into the subway car as the doors slid shut with a hiss. Elijah was standing near the corner, leaning against the pole, his mind elsewhere, his eyes fixed on the map above the door as the train plunged deeper into the tunnels beneath the city. He’d been doing this same routine for years, the repetitive motions offering some comfort. But recently, things felt different.

Elijah had always known about the barriers, the invisible ones—those societal restrictions that seemed to shape his life in ways he could not control. He could never put his finger on them, but they were there: in the way people looked at him when he walked into a room, in the way his skin marked him before he even spoke a word, in the way doors never fully opened for him, no matter how hard he tried.

The world wasn’t fair, and Elijah knew that well. But what hurt him most wasn’t the open hostility that came from individuals or the public – it was the subtle, insidious ways that racial injustice crept into his life. It wasn’t just about being Black in a country that had its history of racial oppression. It was the feeling of being always just outside the lines, never quite allowed to cross into spaces where his potential could fully be realized.

As the train screeched to a halt at 14th Street, Elijah stood still, watching the doors open. A sea of people surged in and out of the subway car, each lost in their own world, moving past him without a second thought. And yet, despite their indifference, Elijah felt the weight of every glance that lingered on him, the quick assessments that people made based on his appearance. As if that alone told the story of who he was, and what he could achieve.

The previous week had left Elijah exhausted. He had gone to yet another interview at a firm he admired, a place he had dreamed of working for years. The moment he sat down at the polished conference table, the atmosphere shifted. The interviewer’s smile was tight, and his eyes never quite met Elijah’s. Every answer Elijah gave seemed to fall flat, the questions too pointed, too focused on his past in ways that others didn’t face. His resume was stellar, his experience impressive, but somehow, he was always the candidate who didn’t quite fit the bill.

He left the building that day feeling like he was suffocating in the very air around him. It wasn’t the first time it had happened. The weight of invisible barriers had become a familiar companion—bars that existed not in the physical world but in the minds of others. He had heard about these barriers all his life, but today, they felt heavier than ever.

That night, Elijah met his friend Marcus at a small bar in Brooklyn. Marcus, like Elijah, was a lawyer—a successful one—but even he could not escape the confines of those invisible walls.

“Man,” Marcus started as they settled into their seats with drinks in hand, “I’m telling you, it’s like you’re doing everything right, and still, the door gets slammed in your face. They say it’s about merit, about being the best, but that’s a lie. It’s about being the right kind of person, fitting into the mold they’ve already set. And that mold… doesn’t look like us.”

Elijah leaned back, rubbing his temples. “I can’t even tell you how many times I’ve felt that. I’m good at what I do, but every time, I wonder… if I were just a little bit more like them, would I be enough? Would I be seen?”

Marcus paused, his face serious. “You know what the real issue is? It’s not just about you or me. It’s the system, the culture. It’s the idea that certain people—people like us—aren’t supposed to be in certain spaces. They don’t want us to succeed, not because we can’t, but because we weren’t meant to. It’s not about what you can do; it’s about who you are. And that, my friend, is the invisible barrier.”

They sat in silence for a moment, the weight of Marcus’ words sinking in. Elijah had heard those words before, but tonight they felt sharper, more real.

A few days later, the city’s skyline loomed large as Elijah sat in his small apartment, going through emails and job listings. He had sent out countless applications, but the results were always the same—thank you, but no thanks. The rejection letters were almost polite, offering vague reasons about “other candidates being a better fit,” but Elijah knew the truth. He had tried everything. He had gone above and beyond, working tirelessly, attending conferences, networking with every influential person he could find, but still, he was told he didn’t belong. The bar was always raised higher.

In one of his darkest moments, Elijah thought of leaving. He considered moving out of the city, perhaps finding a place where he could start over, where the pressure of the invisible barriers wouldn’t be so overwhelming. But the thought made him angry. Why should he leave? Why should he have to run from the systemic injustices that defined every part of his existence?

Elijah was about to turn off his computer when a new email notification popped up. It was from a former professor who had recently started a non-profit organization aimed at providing legal support to underrepresented communities. The professor’s name was Dr. Williams, and she had always been a fierce advocate for racial equality.

“Elijah,” the email began, “I hope this finds you well. I wanted to reach out personally to see if you’d be interested in joining our team. We need someone with your expertise, your passion, and your drive. I know that the journey has been difficult, but this is an opportunity to make real change. You’ve been a voice in this struggle, and I believe that now, more than ever, your voice is needed.”

The words struck Elijah like a lightning bolt. He had been waiting for a sign, something to tell him that all the effort, all the years of struggle, were worth it. He wasn’t sure if this was the moment of change he had been waiting for, but something inside him stirred. Maybe, just maybe, he had the chance to do something that could help break down the invisible barriers for others like him.

Weeks later, Elijah was sitting in a community center in Harlem, looking over a case file for one of their clients. The case was a clear example of systemic discrimination, the kind of thing that people often overlooked, preferring to believe that racism was a thing of the past. But Elijah knew better. He had seen it up close. His job now was not only to fight against the systems that held people back but also to give them the tools and the support they needed to fight back themselves.

He had found a new sense of purpose in this work. The barriers were still there, still deeply ingrained in the fabric of society, but Elijah had learned something invaluable over the past few months: there was strength in solidarity. There was power in standing together and demanding that things change.

One afternoon, as he walked through the streets of Harlem after a long day of meetings, Elijah passed a group of young men hanging out on the corner. They looked like they belonged to the neighborhood, laughing and chatting, the kind of joy that was rare but beautiful amidst the struggle.

One of the men looked at Elijah, and for a moment, their eyes met. The young man smiled and nodded. Elijah, moved by the moment, returned the gesture. It was a small moment of recognition, of connection, but it reminded him of why the fight was worth it.

In the fight against racial injustice, there were no easy answers. But Elijah knew that if he kept pushing forward, if he kept fighting against those invisible barriers, he wasn’t just fighting for himself. He was fighting for everyone who had ever been held back, for everyone who had ever been told that they didn’t belong.

And slowly, the walls began to crack.
