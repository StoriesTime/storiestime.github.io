Echoes of Influence: How Social Media Shapes Our Identity


The neon glow of her phone illuminated Eva’s face in the dimly lit room. Thumb poised, she scrolled absentmindedly through the endless river of images, likes, and opinions. Outside the window, the world murmured—a steady hum of cars, distant laughter, and the occasional bark of a dog—yet inside, the world seemed to revolve entirely around this handheld universe. It wasn’t just entertainment anymore; social media had become her mirror.

Eva had always thought of herself as ordinary. Average in height, average in looks, average in interests. But two years ago, she’d stumbled upon an opportunity to remake herself online. It wasn’t strategic or premeditated; it began with one post—a snapshot of her morning coffee taken unwittingly at an artistic angle. People liked it. Simple, but compelling. Encouraged by the unexpected engagement, she posted again—a shot of her sneakers against wet concrete, then the surface of her desk during a late study session. Every post seemed to gather momentum, gently tugging at an invisible thread she didn’t know could be unraveled.

Gradually, Eva became someone new, someone worth following. She wasn’t just posting snippets of her life anymore; she was curating an identity, perfecting her digital reflection. Feedback from strangers came in waves: “Love this aesthetic!” “Following for more!” “You’re literally my inspiration!” The praises were addictive. The more they adored her, the more detached she felt from herself in real life.

Her mirrored self on Instagram reflected a persona that Eva couldn’t always recognize. She went out of her way to stage breathtaking moments, waking at dawn to catch the golden hour or arranging elaborate flatlays of food she wouldn’t eat. Followers multiplied, brands reached out, and her inbox swelled with collaborations. Yet, as her online presence grew, Eva began to sense a fracture within her.

One evening, curled up on her couch, she stared at the photo she’d just posted—a candid laugh captured against the backdrop of wildflowers. Comments rolled in that praised her joy, her vibrancy, her authenticity. Only, Eva wasn’t smiling when the shot was taken. Her laugh had been precisely timed, a carefully instructed performance staged by her best friend, Sarah, who she had dragged along for the entire exhausting shoot. In fact, within moments of the camera clicking, Eva had snapped at Sarah over a misplaced angle. She couldn’t remember the last time she’d laughed spontaneously.

Why was it so easy for strangers to adore this version of her? And why was it so hard for her to feel connected to herself? The thought unsettled her. She had merely dipped her toes into uncertainty when a notification buzzed, reminding her of a brand deal she had yet to fulfill. The distraction was welcome.

As weeks passed, her curated life showed no sign of slowing. But Eva couldn’t shake the feeling that she was losing something vital. The life she built online was nothing like reality. Her days became consumed by planning content rather than living in genuine moments. Her friendships frayed as she missed birthdays and canceled plans for shoots. Her once honest laughs became exhausted sighs behind the scenes. Amid all this, her follower count continued to climb, giving her a bizarre sense of accomplishment even as everything else fell apart.

The shift came unexpectedly during her mother’s birthday dinner. Her family had chosen a small family-owned restaurant downtown, far removed from the polished aesthetics Eva had come to favor. Surrounded by flickering candles and aging walls plastered with photographs of strangers, Eva felt the urge to capture her surroundings. She raised her phone instinctively to frame the scene, only for her brother to place his hand firmly over hers.

“Can we just enjoy this moment for once?” Peter’s voice was calm but pointed. He wasn’t angry, but his sentiment stung hard.

Eva hesitated, lowering her phone. She glanced at him, feeling a stab of guilt she couldn’t ignore. Her family carried on eating as if nothing happened, but the silence around the exchange rang loud in her ears. For the first time since she’d chased likes and hearts, Eva saw herself through someone else’s eyes—not as an enchanting figure worthy of admiration, but as a fragile girl, disconnected from the world around her.

Later that night, Eva combed through her camera roll to delete unused pictures, only to find herself lingering over the haphazard candid shots from dinner. Her mother’s slightly blurred laugh, her father’s hand resting on Peter’s shoulder while they argued over the check, the wilted cake candle that refused to stay lit. None of these were artful, none of them polished, yet something about them resonated deeply. They told stories—not her attempts at storytelling built for social media, but the raw, imperfect patchwork of real life.

Eva didn’t post that night—nor the next. Days blurred into weeks, and her followers noticed, commenting things like, “You okay?” “Miss your posts!” and “Please share something soon!” Yet Eva found herself retreating. She gave half-hearted replies, buried emails from brands, and let her online persona gather dust while she reconnected with her offline life.

By stepping away, something strange happened. The pressure faded. So did the weight of living for an audience she’d never meet. She felt the same guilt, the same worry about losing her fleeting popularity—feelings that came less and less each day. Instead, she found herself smiling when she wasn’t prompted, laughing over dinner without checking angles, and breathing deeply without worrying about filters. Her life began to feel uncurated but fully hers.

One rainy afternoon, Eva opened Instagram after weeks of ignoring it. Her follower count had dropped; there was no question. But in its place, she felt something else—freedom. Her finger hovered over the app for a while before closing it altogether. The satisfaction wasn’t in deleting the digital presence she’d built—it was recognizing that she didn’t need it anymore.

The world outside the window murmured again, still present, still alive. For the first time in years, it wasn’t drowned out by the glow of her screen. And somewhere in that stillness, Eva felt complete.
