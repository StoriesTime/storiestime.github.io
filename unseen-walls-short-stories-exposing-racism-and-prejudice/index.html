Unseen Walls: Short Stories Exposing Racism and Prejudice


Amira sat on the worn leather seat of the bus, her fingers anxiously tapping the edge of her backpack. It was a Tuesday morning, the sky overcast, and the streets of the city felt unusually quiet. As she glanced out the window, she noticed the same scenery she had seen for the past several years — the same brownstones, the same towering skyscrapers, the same street vendors hawking their goods. But today, something felt different. There was a weight in the air, a heaviness that clung to the silence of the people around her.

Her destination was a high school in a predominantly white neighborhood, and for the past few months, she had noticed a pattern. The stares. The whispers. The way people seemed to look through her as if she were invisible, yet somehow, more like an outsider. She had grown accustomed to this feeling, but today it bothered her more than usual. Perhaps it was the weight of the new semester, or maybe it was just the burden of carrying a name that wasn’t familiar to the rest of the world.

Amira had grown up in a small, tight-knit community on the other side of town. The kind of place where everyone knew everyone else, where diversity wasn’t a concept but a lived reality. It was a place where the color of your skin wasn’t something you had to explain or justify. But now, as she sat on the bus heading toward a world that felt unfamiliar and cold, the unspoken walls of difference loomed large.

Chapter 2: The Walls Between Us

At school, the walls were harder to ignore. They weren’t physical barriers, but they might as well have been. The hallway conversations, always a mix of voices, suddenly seemed to fade when she walked by. It wasn’t as though the other students were overtly hostile. No one shouted slurs or made cruel remarks, but there was an underlying sense of detachment, a subtle but unmistakable feeling of “otherness” that clung to her every step.

In her history class, when the topic of civil rights came up, Amira listened as her classmates offered opinions about the struggles of the past. They spoke of Martin Luther King Jr. and Rosa Parks as if they were distant, abstract figures, removed from any current reality. There was a sense of accomplishment in their tone, a feeling that progress had been made, that the work was done. But Amira knew better.

She raised her hand, the only Black student in the class, and when the teacher called on her, she began to speak. Her voice was steady, but her heart raced as she tried to find the right words.

“Yes, we made progress,” she said. “But there are still walls. They’re not always physical, but they exist. In the way we’re treated, in the way we’re expected to fit into a world that wasn’t built for us.”

There was an uncomfortable silence that followed. The teacher shifted in his seat, a nervous laugh escaping from his lips. “Well, Amira,” he said, “you bring up an interesting point. But let’s not forget how far we’ve come.”

But Amira knew he didn’t understand. It wasn’t about how far they had come; it was about how far they still had to go. It was about the microaggressions — the casual dismissals of her experiences, the polite racism that never fully crossed the line into confrontation but still left a sting.

Chapter 3: The Silent Rejection

The following week, during lunch, Amira sat at her usual spot by the window, a solitary figure in the bustling cafeteria. Her eyes scanned the room, watching her peers as they gathered in their familiar cliques. There was laughter, a buzz of energy, but it never seemed to include her. She had tried to make friends, to bridge the gap, but the invisible wall that separated her from the rest of the students was always there. She wondered if they could see it too, or if it was something she imagined.

“Hey, Amira!” came a voice from behind. It was Rachel, a girl from her math class. She had always been friendly, but there was something hesitant in her tone today. “Do you mind if I sit here?”

Amira turned, surprised. Rachel was one of the few who had never ignored her, who had always smiled when their paths crossed. But there was an awkwardness now, an uncertainty that Amira couldn’t place.

“Of course,” Amira replied, shifting her backpack to make room. Rachel sat down slowly, the air between them thick with something unspoken.

For a few moments, they ate in silence, the noise of the cafeteria swarming around them. Amira couldn’t help but wonder if Rachel had felt the shift — the subtle distancing that had started happening in the past few weeks. But she decided not to say anything. Maybe it was just her imagination.

Rachel cleared her throat, breaking the silence. “Amira, I’ve been thinking… You know, it’s just that sometimes I feel like people don’t understand you. Like, I don’t know… maybe they don’t get that you’re different, you know?”

Amira’s heart skipped a beat. The words were innocent, but they hit her like a punch to the gut. “What do you mean by ‘different’?” she asked, trying to keep her voice calm.

Rachel’s eyes darted around the room, as if searching for the right words. “I mean, like, your background. Your culture. It’s just… I don’t know. It’s not really like what we’re used to, you know?”

Amira’s fingers tightened around her lunch tray, but she said nothing. This was the moment when the walls she had felt for so long became real, solidified in Rachel’s words. It wasn’t just that she was an outsider. It was that her difference, her identity, was a concept that her peers couldn’t fully grasp — and maybe they didn’t want to.

“Rachel,” Amira said slowly, “I’m just a person. I’m not some puzzle to solve or some novelty to be studied.”

Rachel’s face flushed. “I didn’t mean it like that, I swear. I just don’t know how to be… I don’t know… like, how to make it not weird.”

Amira stood up, grabbing her tray and pushing it toward the trash bin. “It’s always going to be weird, Rachel, as long as you see me as different. We’re all just people. And until you can see that, it will always feel like there’s a wall between us.”

She walked away without looking back.

Chapter 4: The Unseen Weight

That night, Amira sat in her room, the weight of the day pressing down on her chest. She had never been one to dwell on things for too long, but today felt different. The conversation with Rachel echoed in her mind. The words, though unintentional, had struck a nerve. It wasn’t that she hadn’t heard them before — she had. Over the years, there had been so many conversations like this, ones that started with curiosity but ended in discomfort.

But it wasn’t just Rachel. It was the constant feeling of being on the outside looking in. It was the look of confusion when she spoke about her experiences, the way her peers often asked questions that felt more like tests than genuine curiosity. It was the realization that her presence, her very existence, was something that needed to be explained, categorized, and understood before she could be fully accepted.

Amira glanced at her reflection in the mirror. She saw herself — not as others saw her, but as she saw herself. She was a person, not an object of fascination. Her heritage, her culture, her background — they were pieces of who she was, but they didn’t define her. She wasn’t a caricature of herself, nor was she a lesson for others to learn from.

The next day at school, she noticed something strange. The invisible walls that had once seemed so firm, so impenetrable, felt a little less solid. She wasn’t sure why or how, but there was a sense of determination in her steps. The walls that others had built around her, the walls of racism and prejudice, would never fully disappear. But they didn’t have to define her. She could be more than their narrow perception.

As Amira walked down the hall toward her next class, she caught a glimpse of Rachel. This time, there was no hesitation, no uncertainty in Rachel’s gaze. It wasn’t much, but it was a start — a crack in the wall, a small sign of progress.

Amira smiled to herself. Change, she realized, didn’t always have to be monumental. Sometimes, it was enough to simply keep moving forward, one step at a time.

The End
