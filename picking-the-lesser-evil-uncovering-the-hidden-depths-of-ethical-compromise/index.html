Picking the Lesser Evil: Uncovering the Hidden Depths of Ethical Compromise


As a moral philosopher, Dr. Emma Levenson had spent years elucidating the intricacies of moral distinctions. And yet, as she stared at the binary options on her laptop screen, she wished she could call upon her academic expertise to help her choose between the two.

Option A was the candidature of Senator Aldrich—a man known for pandering to the highest bidders. His promises, however, could pave the way to a greener, more inclusive society. Option B was his arch-nemesis, Senator Cavanaugh, who embodied cutthroat conservatism, reluctant to any form of social reform. She couldn’t deny that this choice could secure relative stability and welfare.

A dreaded knock on the door interrupted her thoughts. Emma’s research subject, John, was supposed to interview her for his magazine piece. Emma didn’t particularly want to discuss her moral conundrum with the curious journalist, but John was due for an early departure, hence the unscheduled visit. Heaving a sigh, Emma opened the door.

John, sporting a baseball cap and a camera strapped around his neck, became the epitome of journalistic intrigue in her modest study. “Aren’t you fond of Aristotle, Dr. Levenson?” he asked, eyeing her bookshelf.

The philosopher hesitated, yet found herself responding: “I favor ‘The Nicomachean Ethics’, but not today.”

Noticing her disheveled demeanor, John prophetically observed, “You seem preoccupied. Do you mind if I mention your complex decision-making process to modernize America?”

Perplexed by his chutzpah, Emma allowed him a modicum of insight. “Before I unravel that, I’m curious about yours. I mean, how do you justify choosing your subjects?”

A grin spread across John’s face. “Well, first, I try to observe the lesser evil. I extract meaningful stories by witnessing a range of experiences and feel for the most nuanced and delicate subject.”

His words resonated with Emma, echoing her own approach to moral dilemmas. Senator Aldrich seemed the lesser evil, and perhaps the second best chance for progress in a world tinged with grey. “Then, perhaps,” she admitted, “you might understand mine.”

Emma’s tale highlighted the growing complexity of political decisions. Her moral canvas painted a defenseless society caught between an imperfect good and an undesirable evil. In the end, though hardly justifying her choice, she affirmed, “It’s a dance we must all perform—between perceiving the world as it is, as Aristotle tells us to do, and seeking a better version of it. The only option is to embrace these pains, these complications, and strive forward.”

John’s gaze underwent a metamorphosis. The journalistic curiosity morphed into something deeper—compassion. He memorialized Emma’s story in his piece, capturing the essence of a modernist’s philosophical illness, and an insatiable hunger for a meaningful existence.

As tensions grew deeper in the world of politics, the public was captivated by the opus composed by the moral philosopher and the journalist. It exposed the blurred lines between monochromatic good and evil, leaving both thought-provoking and moving.

So while Senator Aldrich’s campaign gained traction, Emma emerged from the shadows of moral complexity, guiding the country through her writings and discussions. She had unraveled the hidden depths of ethical compromise, ultimately becoming a beacon of hope for a society dancing through the gray.

End of the Story.
