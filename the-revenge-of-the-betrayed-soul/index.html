The Revenge of the Betrayed Soul


Rain veiled the narrow alleyways of Lunedale; the city’s cobblestone arteries glistened in wavering lantern light. Few dared to roam out after midnight, whispering tales of the one who haunted the labyrinthine streets—a spirit wronged, marked by treachery and bound by vengeance. Yet tonight, someone ran through puddles, chased by more than just human fear.

Clara Weave pressed her soaked notebook to her chest as she darted between shadows. She’d spent months investigating disappearances tied to urban legends, scoffing at stories of curses and wandering ghosts. But now, her skepticism eroded with every frantic heartbeat.

Behind her, footsteps echoed where no living soul lingered.

Gasping, she wrenched open the oak door of the tiny bookshop where she worked. The bell didn’t chime; something had stilled the air itself. She slammed the door just as a cold draft brushed her neck. Thunder growled overhead. She pressed her back to the wood, listening. Only the rhythm of the rain kept her company.

Or so she thought.

In the flickering candlelight, she saw her own breath clouding the air. Shadows clustered in the corners, swirling like dark silk, pulling faint moans out of the silence. One shadow detached, and the stench of old earth filled the room. A figure—half-tangible, half-whisper—emerged from the gloom. His eyes were twin embers, lit by ancient fury.

“Why do you disturb me?” His voice was both wind and grave, laced with bitterness.

Clara’s hands trembled, but she steadied herself. “I—I didn’t mean to. I want answers—a name for the unrest in these streets. Who are you?”

For a moment the ghost’s fury eclipsed his pain. “Once, I was Lucien Vane. Trusted, beloved… until the knife in my back.”

Clara, heart tripping, tore through her memory. Lucien Vane. She’d read of him: a merchant found drowned in a wine barrel, his fortune vanished, his legacy tainted by accusations of sabotage and theft. His death spawned decades of rumors, eclipsed only by stories of retribution on those who wronged him.

“You walk these alleys seeking truth,” Lucien hissed, “but truth is a weapon, and every weapon has its wielder and its victim.”

The room cooled as he drifted closer. “Is it forgiveness you seek? Or justice?”

Clara’s voice steadied. “Neither. I want the story. Your story.”

Lucien’s laugh was brittle. “Then you must see it.” The air shuddered; the candle flickered and died. Time unwound around her, and Clara felt the world tilt—

1. The Fall

She blinked and found herself standing beneath a blood-orange sunset. Market stalls lined the square. Lucien’s mortal face was younger, brighter. He presided over the stalls with steady confidence, greeting vendors, sharing smiles with children.

But darkness crept at the edges. Watching from afar, a quartet of “friends”—their eyes hard with envy, bitter with silent resentment—plotted against him. Clara watched, unseen, as whispers became slander, and slander slid into betrayal.

One night, Lucien was summoned under the pretense of a business deal. Beneath the arches of St. Eithne’s Bridge, wine flowed freely. Promises curled like smoke in the lamplight.

And then—a flash of steel. A hand to his back. The wine barrel’s curved wooden sky. The salt tang of fear as breath turned to choking gasps. Faces, blurred with agony. The warmth of blood, the ache of confusion. Betrayal carved into his last gasp.

The vision faded. Clara gripped the bookshop counter for balance, cheeks wet with Lucien’s remembered death. The ghost loomed, grief and wrath entwined tighter than ever.

2. The Vengeance

“I lingered for centuries, watching their lineages thrive,” Lucien’s whisper crackled. “The prosperous Graftons, the pious Sheeres, the vaunted Malorys—descendants of those who conspired against me. Generations fattened on lies.”

Images tore through Clara’s mind—candles in windows, laughter at banquets, children unburdened by their ancestors’ sins. But shadows twisted round those houses. Unanswered knocks in the night. Chilled rooms where fires refused to light. Quiet words cut short by icy dread.

At first, Lucien merely watched. Years passed, and as memory faded for men, agony sharpened for him. He learned to gather his pain, make it into power—a shroud he wore as armor. The first of his justice was subtle: ruined crops, ailing animals, a pricking sense of eyes on napes.

As years turned to decades, he grew less gentle.

A Grafton heir, drunk and boasting of business conquests, fell to a fever no doctor could name, screaming of cold hands at his throat. A Sheeres daughter vanished, last seen walking alone on a fogbound morning; her red scarf found later, tangled in river reeds. It wasn’t enough—would never be enough—but each act soothed the raw wound in Lucien’s spirit.

But as time crawled on, vengeance soured.

Names changed; bloodlines faded or mingled. The guilt he’d cursed so carefully slipped from one generation to the next, never fully touching those who’d harmed him. Anger twisted into a hollow echo. He was a shadow among shadows, feared but not understood—a lesson unlearned.

3. The Cost

Clara listened, chills braiding up her spine. “Are you satisfied?” she asked.

The ghost recoiled. “Would you be? Betrayal is a stain that will not wash away. But now I wonder what remains of me besides vengeance.”

“You’ve haunted Lunedale for generations,” Clara said, voice daring. “Fear lingers, but does truth? Or are you only a myth, a caution without a face?”

Lucien’s gaze burned. “You want to end the story. To wrap it in neat lines and sell it from your shop.”

Clara shook her head. “No. I want to free you. Or at least, to let your story change. Haven’t you earned that chance?”

For a fading moment, Lucien’s face softened. Then rage spiked again, stronger than death. “Why trust you, when trust cost me everything?”

The storm outside intensified, rattling glass and flickering lanterns. Clara, desperate, lifted her notebook, its pages damp but intact. She thumbed through her research: accounts of Lucien’s life, the trial that smeared his name, the whispers of guilt from the guilty men in their later days. Buried in unsorted notes, an entry caught her eye—a long-lost letter, never public, its handwriting elegant but weary. It was penned by Callum Grafton, one of Lucien’s betrayers.

She read aloud, her voice a lifeline across centuries:

“Each night I see your face, friend I have wronged. The wine was bitter, the pact broken by greed and cowardice. I ask, if spirits walk, for forgiveness not for my sake, but to still the weeping in my dreams. I am sorry. I am sorry. I am sorry…”

The shadows receded a fraction. Lucien hovered, less wrath, more weariness. “He never found peace. Nor did I.”

“Maybe you both can now,” Clara whispered.

4. The Choice

The shop fell silent but for the soft patter of rain against glass. For the first time in hundreds of years, Lucien was faced not with cowering victims or the blank, oblivious stares of strangers, but with a witness and a plea.

“Let me tell your story,” Clara said softly. “Let people see the man, the crime, the cost of forgotten bonds. Let them—let you rest.”

Lucien considered her. Above their heads, the candle relit itself, its flame steady and golden.

“My vengeance fed me, but it also trapped me.” His form faded at the edges, as if he were drifting beyond the reach of both pain and anger. “Tell the world. Break the pattern. If a soul can be betrayed, perhaps it can also be redeemed.”

Clara nodded, tears mingling with the rain tracing down the shop window.

Lucien smiled for the first time—wistful, almost grateful. As dawn drew its first pale stripe across the city, the ghost dissolved like mist, leaving only a faint scent of wine, soil, and sorrow behind.

5. The Legacy

Clara wrote Lucien’s story, painstakingly piecing truth from fiction. She published his tale under the title “The Betrayed Soul of Lunedale: A True History.” Skeptics dismissed it; devotees left flowers by the old bridge. The families of the Graftons, Sheeres, and Malorys read it in private, grief and relief mixing on their tongues.

The hauntings stopped. Lunedale’s streets breathed easier. The city’s heart, once shadowed, began to heal. Some called it superstition; others called it closure. Clara moved on, but whenever rain veiled cobblestone and lantern light gleamed in alleyways, she remembered the cold, sad eyes of a spirit who had finally been seen—and, in being seen, was set free.

Epilogue: Beyond Revenge

Whispers endure—of betrayal, of vengeance, of souls that walk when wronged. But there is another story now, one that threads through the city’s dreams. It is not about endless nights of terror, but about the long road from pain to peace—a story that says healing is as stubborn, and as possible, as the wrongs that first demand it.

Through truth—raw, tender, unflinching—the betrayed soul did not merely haunt. He taught, and in that, found release.

The rain passes, but the story remains.
