The Time Traveler's Guide to the Milky Way


The deep hum of the time engine was the first thing Maren heard as she stepped onto the bridge of the Chronos Drift. The ship, long since weathered by millennia of cosmic voyages, shimmered faintly in the dim light, its hull etched with runic symbols from civilizations lost to history. Maren pressed her fingertips against the cold alloy of the navigation interface, her mind already racing. This wasn’t just another routine jump to observe history unfold—it was her chance to unlock the secrets of the Milky Way. The galaxy itself was a tapestry of stories, and Maren was determined to document them all, chapter by chapter.

The Chronos Drift was no ordinary vessel. Built from materials mined on Tython-6, a planet that had seen its own extinction, the ship was equipped with tech designed to pierce both time and space. Inside its walls, there were libraries of data detailing monumental galactic events: the formation of constellations, wars over habitable worlds, trade routes carried on stellar winds. For someone like Maren, who had spent years studying cosmic archaeology, this was a dream realized. There wasn’t a planet, star cluster, or asteroid belt in the Milky Way she hadn’t researched—but to experience them firsthand was another matter entirely.

Maren calibrated the ship’s systems. Her destination wasn’t some notable historical landmark or bustling hub of alien culture; it was the Scar of Velora—a region of the galaxy surrounded by myths so thick even alien scholars hesitated to explore it. If the stories were true, the Scar was created during one of the galaxy’s earliest epochs by an energy event so immense it tore through time itself. The result was a rippling anomaly that refused to adhere to the known physics of the universe. Maren suspected more than just physics had taken a hit. There were whispers of beings who lived parallel to time, creatures who could gaze across the centuries like humans might watch fish in a pond. In many ways, her mission wasn’t just scientific—it was personal. If the Scar could tell stories, she intended to listen.

The Drift surged forward, pulling itself into the slipstream of spacetime. Maren watched as the stars blurred, their bright lights elongating into ribbons of color outside the ship’s viewports. She resisted the urge to look too long at them; prolonged observation during a temporal jump tended to cause headaches resembling what humans from the 22nd century called “hyper-dilation migraines.” Instead, she focused on the navigation display, confirming their trajectory while her hands danced across the controls. This section of the Milky Way had been mapped centuries ago, but maps were fallible. The galaxy had a knack for moving objects—stars shifted, asteroid belts dispersed, and rogue planets wandered. Time travelers often compared piloting through spacetime to navigating a maze that was constantly rebuilding itself.

After what felt like hours, the hum of the engine dulled. Maren’s surroundings flickered briefly; the peculiar sensation of “temporal dehydration” washed over her—an effect described as feeling like you’ve lived three fast-forwarded lives all at once. When the stabilizers confirmed they had landed within temporal bounds, Maren exhaled. The Scar of Velora blinked into view.

It was breathtaking.

A vast, multicolored chasm stretched before her, dotted with planets suspended like floating islands above a whirlpool of cascading lights. The anomaly radiated an ever-shifting blend of violet, silver, and gold hues, casting shadows that danced in impossible directions. Its strange beauty was accompanied by an eerie stillness, as though sound itself had been swallowed by the Scar’s presence. Though every piece of research on the region had warned of its dangers, Maren couldn’t suppress a quiet excitement. She was here, staring into the heart of the Milky Way’s greatest mystery.

She stepped into her exploration suit, a lightweight marvel of technology that protected her from temporal fractures and space debris alike. As the airlock hissed open, she disembarked onto one of the floating platforms. The gravity here was light and forgiving, making her feel both weightless and invigorated. Maren activated her recording equipment, scanning the data coming in through multiple sensors. The readings were… bizarre. Energy spikes fluctuated far beyond known limits, like candle flames stretched by invisible winds. Temporal currents rippled near the anomaly’s edge, distorting time itself.

Carefully, she approached the nearest artifact. From her database scans, it appeared to be an ancient vessel—a craft that had drifted through the Scar unshielded. Scratches covered its hull, though its design suggested it belonged to an advanced alien race known as the Heliorans, beings who had vanished more than eight thousand years ago. Maren reached out to the craft, brushing its surface. A flash of energy jolted up her arm—not violent, but purposeful. Then came a blooming of images in her mind.

She saw events unfold like a holographic slideshow: the Heliorans constructing their city-ships, basking in the light of stars that no longer existed. She witnessed their leaders studying the Scar, ultimately deciding to send vessels across its threshold in search of knowledge. She saw them vanish, swallowed by the anomaly. But then their story changed. She glimpsed them reappearing on distant worlds centuries later, their technology even more advanced, their bodies altered by their experience. If the Scar had somehow evolved them, then its significance was far greater than anyone had imagined.

The overwhelming imagery faded, and Maren staggered backward, trying to steady her thoughts. Her heart raced; she’d just experienced something no time traveler had before—a direct connection to the memories of another civilization through the Scar’s influence. But this raised deeper questions about the anomaly. Was it simply a tear in space or an ancient device designed by forces unknown? Had it changed the Heliorans deliberately, or was its power beyond comprehension?

Her communicator beeped. The Drift’s systems detected rising temporal instability, a sign that the anomaly might begin to shift, pulling objects into its depths. Maren couldn’t risk getting trapped. She backed away from the artifact and returned to the safe zone near her ship. Yet her mind refused to rest. The Scar had revealed tantalizing fragments, leaving her with more questions than answers.

Before she left, Maren set up a series of autonomous probes to remain in orbit around the Scar. They weren’t just meant to record its activity; she programmed them to interact with the anomaly, sending gentle pulses of energy to map its reactions. If the Scar contained secrets, the probes might uncover them, or at least offer clues for whoever ventured here next.

Back onboard the Drift, Maren reviewed her notes. The Milky Way teemed with wonders: pulsars singing cosmic hymns, civilizations thriving beneath ice planets, and black holes warping reality like mirrors bent in a funhouse. Yet the Scar of Velora was unlike anything she had ever encountered. Time travelers often spoke of the galaxy like a living organism—breathing, changing, remembering. But what if the Scar wasn’t just remembering? What if it was writing new chapters of its own, bending the past and present into stories no being could yet decipher?

As the Drift prepared for its next jump, Maren allowed herself one last glance at the anomaly. Her thoughts swirled in rhythm with its shifting hues. Somewhere in the far reaches of the Milky Way, she knew there were more mysteries waiting. But as a time traveler, discovery wasn’t just inevitable—it was her purpose.
