Starlight Protocol: Mission to Proxima


The command pod shimmered with streaks of distant starlight and the gentle green of instrument panels. Commander Adira Mikkelson sat at the helm, fingers poised above sleek interfaces, eyes fixed on the swirling nebulae beyond the composite glass. Two hundred trillion kilometers from Earth and the silence was deeper than the vacuum—a silence built over decades of planning, billions of dollars, and the collective hope of a species eager to become more than just an Earthly memory.

She exhaled, breaking the hush of Mission Day 1,128 with a soft sigh. The journey to Proxima Centauri—the closest exoplanetary system—wasn’t merely survival by radiation-shielded hull and suspended animation. It was a crucible, one that had already seen cracks.

Diary Entry, AD 2161. Mission Chrono: 03:51

Life between the moments: Leonor says the stars are brighter when you accept they might never be reached. Dylan says anything in a vacuum glimmers. I’m still waiting to decide.

Beneath Adira, the Starlight Protocol’s living quarter decks rose and fell with the gentle hum of machinery. Here were her crew: Leonor Figueira, the sensor chief from Recife, always awake at odd hours. Dylan Hwang, chief biologist—a rare breed, equal parts ruthlessness and hope, tending to the greenhouse module as though chlorophyll and oxygen were his own lifeblood. Janik Wu, comms genius and unofficial philosopher; Zubin Chau, flight engineer; Madeline Severin, ship’s medic. Together, five human minds and a chorus of bound subroutines watched as time blurred around their slow-crawling ark.

Yesterday, the proximity sensors had flickered: faint signatures, unnatural in pattern. Adira carried it in her mind like a splinter. The mission was already five years out from launch and, contrary to the simulation models, things drifted off-plan. Inertial stabilizers ran ragged. A microfracture had been detected in hull sector G7. Supplies had been rationed with exquisite calculation, but the “human factor”—as Mission Control called it—meant some days felt longer, some minds frayed.

Still, failure wasn’t in the lexicon. The Protocol—the sacred code running through every AI node and crew order—demanded one goal: reach Proxima b, plant the flag, transmit home.

Adira tapped the comms badge, feeling the three-note chime vibrate against her collarbone. “Bridge to greenhouse. Dylan, status?”

A pause. The static resolved into Dylan’s low voice. “Tomatoes are flowering, beans at twenty-six centimeters. We’ve still got root rot on tray four. Biofilters need another clean; can Zubin take a look?”

“I’ll send him your way. Hold up the ripe ones—we’ll use them for the Founders’ Day meal.”

“Copy that, Commander.” Dylan’s usual gruffness softened into the camaraderie formed by innumerable shift overlaps and disagreements aired over synthetic coffee.

She keyed into the crew ring: “Zubin, madbay to greenhouse. Leonor, bridge in ten.”

One by one, familiarity found her: footsteps on titanium, the clink of harnesses, voices colored by sleep and stardust.

The Sight

Leonor slipped into the bridge with a datapad cradled to her chest. Her hair was salt-and-pepper now, the journey’s stress etched in a spray of wrinkles at her temples.

“I’ve rerun last night’s anomalous scan,” said Leonor, unfurling a star chart that painted the console with polychrome light. “It’s not meteor debris—not the right composition, and the trajectory’s…well, off. I’m flagging it for further study.”

Adira leaned in. There it was: a faint echo, threading between the signatures of cosmic radiation and the remnant heat of vessel transit.

“Is it following us?”

Leonor shrugged. “Not yet. It’s probably an old comm satellite, maybe even debris from Project Starwave. The vector’s non-hostile.” She wore a smirk, lightly mocking her own unease.

And yet, Adira’s mind itched. Protocol dictated curiosity, but also caution.

“Put me on the loop if anything changes. We can’t afford surprises this far from home.”

Leonor nodded, offering that rare, lopsided grin. “That’s what you keep me for.”

She departed, and Adira let herself breathe. In the thick, processed air of the bridge, there was little room for fear; only trust, sometimes faith, and the quiet monotony of interval checks and worst-case drills.

Memory Lanes

With comms clear and diagnostics running, Adira took a rare hour to herself. She drifted through the central walkway, boots magnetized to the deck. Pictures lined the corridor: a battered Polaroid of Janik’s family on Luna Base, Leonor’s first relay antenna, Dylan with his moss cultures from Earth’s last untouched forest. She lingered on her own—taken at launch, eyes wide, hope incandescent within.

Never forget you are more than the sum of your equations.
—From the Protocol, Section One.

Her mother’s voice, long-dead, wove through her mind as she passed the hydroponic units: “You’ll be the first, Adira. Keep your heart open.” She wondered if children on Earth even knew the word “adventure” now, or if they only saw the stars in VR lectures and half-remembered fables.

She found Dylan pruning wilted lettuce, gloves smeared in bacterial agar. Overhead, the spectral lamp bathed the greenhouse in perpetual spring.

“Care for a story?” he asked without turning.

“I could use one,” Adira said.

“Today’s lesson,” Dylan replied, “is about resilience. You cut off a limb, plant it in dirt, some things regrow. Others die. No way to tell until you try. Sometimes the roots go deeper than you ever guessed.”

“Ship or crew?” she teased.

“Both,” Dylan said, twirling a leaf. “It’s always both.”

In the corner, young carrot shoots reached for ceiling like thin, grasping fingers. Life—a small, stubborn promise.

The Unknown

Three days later, the anomaly returned. This time, Leonor’s voice arrived urgent and clipped.

“Commander—object closing. Estimated range, nine thousand kilometers and dropping. I’m seeing a signature—heat bloom, too regular for debris.”

Adira barely had time to rouse the others. The command pod became a whir of action: shields deployed, comms encrypted, the ship’s core braced for impact.

On exterior feeds, something gleamed—angular, cold, trailing a string of mathematical precision: repeating pulses, a signal. For a breathless moment, Earth’s finest minds watched as the first artifact of non-human origin drifted into view.

Janik let out a shaky laugh. “We’re not alone, then. Not out here.”

Data poured in: linguistic analysis algorithms parsing the pulses, spectral analysis dissecting the craft’s mineral shell. Zubin tracked energy readings. Madeline watched for signs of EM interference or pathogenic risk. Each of them abandoned protocol in favor of awe.

The vessel kept pace but did not intercept. Its hull shimmered in spectrum, a lattice of unknown alloys. They hailed it in a dozen languages—Mandarin, Spanish, Hindi, code-burst, Morse. For hours, the silence held.

Until, at last, a response—a burst of radio: static interleaved with a pattern echoing the hydrogen line, the universe’s equivalent of a handshake.

Adira’s hands shook only slightly as she recorded her log. “We have made contact,” she said, voice braver than she felt. “No aggression. Unknown intent. Crew mood: mixed between exhilaration and existential terror.”

Leonor gazed at the screen with glassy eyes. “If they tracked us, they could have found Earth long before we left.”

Dylan knelt, collecting a fallen sprig of mint. “Maybe they have. Maybe we’re late to the party, but at least we showed up.”

Echoes

Negotiation with the unknown unfolded in slow, graceful increments—each pulse of data a tentative bridge, each translation attempt a prayer. They sent mathematics, prime numbers, rudimentary maps. The reply: additional pulses, geometric constructs, images that rendered as intricate fractals.

In dreams, Adira saw towers spiraling beneath strange stars, oceans that sang like glass, cities woven from the bones of comets. She told no one, but the others reported the same—a psychic resonance, or perhaps an artifact of displacement, an effect of proximity to the other vessel.

Communication, such as it was, sustained itself over weeks. No weapons fired, no threats issued. Humanity’s first encounter outside solar warmth unfolded in an orbit over Proxima b—a world almost glimpse-able below, cotton-wrapped in clouds.

It came time to choose.

Earth’s protocols were clear: broadcast coordinates, accept peaceful approach, assess risk of contamination, assess risk of opportunity lost. The choice, Adira reasoned, was not only scientific. It was mythic, chiseled in the stone of every migration and greeting from their distant past.

The alien ship drifted closer, main panels splayed like petals. The crew gathered at observation.

“We go, or we don’t,” said Madeline.

“We owe it to the people who built this ark,” said Zubin.

“And to ourselves,” Janik added. “History’s made from the bold, not the safe.”

The vote was unanimous.

First Steps

Adira led the way. Through the airlock, armored suits gleaming, each member tethered to the Protocol and to each other by nerves made steel through so much isolation. Ramps descended from the alien ghostship, light frost sparkling with each cautious footfall on uncharted alloy.

They met the Others inside—a trio, bodies like living sculptures, all symmetry and iridescent skin, faces unreadable but postures open. Language came not as sound but as pulses directly to their helmets, rendering as color and shape within their visual fields.

A handshake, or the universal equivalent.

-greeting, you journeyed far, you are seen, are you of peace?-

-we are. and you?-

-we remember those who came before, we honor those who travel, we share.-

In the minutes that followed, impressions flowed: images of their sun, their oceans, the silent ache of their own lost homeworld. They had come from farther still, carried by wanderlust and warning, the pain of a galaxy haunted by civilizations too isolated to flourish.

They spoke of coexistence, the importance of beginnings, the challenge of translation—not only of word, but of intent. They offered guidance: safe landing procedures, atmospheric data, warnings of microbial life.

“We’re not the first, then,” said Adira when comms reconnected. The implications rang through her bones like a bell.

Dylan nodded, tears in his eyes. “First among equals. Or maybe just the first to survive the trial.”

The Colony

The Starlight Protocol landers made planetfall at dawn. Proxima b’s sky pressed down in a riot of colors filtered through complex molecules. Shorelines curled around basalt crags. Winding rivers cut through emerald valleys glazed with faint frost.

Earth’s flag unfurled on the ridge. Not in conquest, but as promise: that those who left home would make new ones, and that the echoing song of intelligence would not be answered by ruins alone.

They established an outpost, powered by sun and atom. Alien neighbors helped integrate. Earth’s distant voice—years delayed by the tyranny of light—would one day receive their message: the Mission to Proxima succeeded, and much more.

Adira’s last log, filed at planetary sunrise, told of hope: a seedling grown from unfamiliar soil, a coalition forged not by necessity, but by kinship among travelers. Leonor documented atmospheric readings. Dylan cataloged new life—bioluminescent insects, photosynthetic stones. Janik set up the relay beacon for next-gen missions, while Zubin planned what became Proxima’s first landing craft workshop.

They made mistakes. Nothing worked as planned. And yet, humans still adapted, still argued, still laughed, still dreamed under a sky never seen by ancestors.

Epilogue: Letters to Home

Years passed. Children were born—some with Earth accents, some singing in the tonal clicks of their neighbors. Adira faded into legend even as she lived, ivory hair a crown among the settlers. Starlight Protocol became both law and myth: go bravely, listen, choose peace when you can.

Some day, those on Earth might look up and see a flickering light in Proxima’s direction, feel the urgency of journeys not yet made. There would be new missions. There would be new protocols. But the lesson held:

The universe was vast, and waiting.

And in the silence between stars, their story had only just begun.
