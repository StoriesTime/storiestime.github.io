Not Just a Label: The Impact of Racism on Identity


Marisol Mendoza always loved the feel of earth beneath her fingers—the cool dampness of soil in the small garden behind her family’s cramped apartment in Queens. The garden was not just a patch of green; it was a sanctuary where she could escape the noise, the judgment, and the heavy weight of a world eager to define her in simplistic terms.

She was seventeen, caught between two worlds: the vibrant, multi-generational Latino household where Spanish filled the kitchen air with warmth, and the broader American society that often reduced her and people like her to stereotypes. The labels clung to her like shadows—“immigrant,” “troublemaker,” “low-income,” “other.” None of which captured the essence of who she was or who she wanted to be.

The First Encounter

In her sophomore year at a predominantly white high school, Marisol’s quiet routine shattered. It started simply enough. At lunch, she overheard a group of classmates whispering behind her back, using words that stung sharper than any physical blow. “Spic,” “illegal,” “go back to where you came from.” She had heard these slurs before, but hearing them close, from peers she thought neutral, shook her deeply.

She didn’t lash out or cry. Instead, she retreated into herself, canceling plans, avoiding the crowded halls. The incident left an invisible bruise on her sense of belonging. Her identity—a complex tapestry woven from her culture, experiences, dreams, and fears—felt like it was being torn apart by these reductive insults.

Home: A Different Conversation

At home, Marisol’s parents spoke softly about dignity and resilience. “They don’t see us,” her mother said one evening, stirring a pot of arroz con pollo. “But you must never forget who you are.” Her father nodded, adding, “Identity is not just what others say. It’s what you believe about yourself.”

But even their loving reassurances couldn’t fully shield her from the internal storm raging inside. Questions gnawed at her: Was she just a victim of the labels others imposed? Was she destined to be defined by prejudice? Could she claim an identity that was truly hers—uncolored by fear, anger, or stereotype?

Discovery Through Words

One rainy afternoon, Marisol found herself in the school library, escaping the noise of the cafeteria. She stumbled upon a book of poems by Langston Hughes, a powerful figure who gave voice to the struggles of Black Americans through haunting and beautiful words.

As she read, a spark ignited inside her. Hughes’ poetry spoke of pain and pride, of being more than a label assigned by society. Marisol realized her own story was part of a larger narrative of people fighting against reductive categorizations to reclaim their complexity and humanity.

She started writing—poems, essays, even a blog—sharing her experiences with racism and her journey towards self-definition. Through words, she found strength and a medium to challenge the simplistic labels that had sought to confine her.

The School Debate

Encouraged by her English teacher, Mrs. Carter, Marisol entered a statewide debate competition with a speech titled “Not Just a Label: The Impact of Racism on Identity.” On the day of the competition, standing in front of a packed auditorium, Marisol spoke candidly:

“Racism doesn’t just hurt our bodies; it wounds our sense of self. When society labels us without context, it strips away our individuality, reducing us to stereotypes that are easier to fear or dismiss. But identity is a rich, multifaceted experience that no one else can define.”

Her words resonated deeply with many in the audience, sparking conversations long overdue in that school and beyond. Slowly, peers who once whispered prejudiced slurs began to see Marisol not as a label but as a person with stories, dreams, and resilience.

Community Roots

Marisol began volunteering at a local community center, helping younger kids whose identities were also caught in the throes of discrimination. She shared her story with them, emphasizing that their worth did not depend on society’s prejudiced definitions.

Together, they painted murals celebrating their diverse backgrounds, organized cultural events, and created safe spaces where young people could explore their identities free of judgment. For Marisol, this work cemented a powerful realization: identity is not a fixed attribute imposed from without—it is a dynamic, living creation shaped by personal and collective experience.

Looking Ahead

Graduation day arrived with mixed emotions. Marisol was proud but still wary of the wider world beyond her neighborhood and school. Yet she carried with her more than just a diploma; she carried a belief in the power to define herself beyond the narrow constraints of racism.

She planned to attend college, study sociology and creative writing, hoping to shine a light on the impact of racism on identity and inspire others to resist the confines of simplistic labels.

Marisol’s story wasn’t just about surviving racism—it was about transforming it into a force for growth and self-discovery. It was a declaration that people are never just a label. They are complex, whole, and irrevocably human.

Reflection

Racism’s impact goes far beyond discriminatory acts or words; it seeps into the core of personal identity, challenging how individuals see themselves and their place in society. By sharing stories like Marisol’s, communities can foster understanding and solidarity. Through education, dialogue, and creative expression, the damaging effects of racism on identity can be countered—empowering people to assert their full humanity beyond society’s imposed labels.

Marisol’s journey reminds us that identity is a garden tended by understanding and courage, blooming most beautifully when nurtured by self-love and the refusal to be boxed in by prejudice.
