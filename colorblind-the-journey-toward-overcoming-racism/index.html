Colorblind: The Journey Toward Overcoming Racism


Jacob adjusted the lens of his camera and squinted through the viewfinder. The late afternoon sun painted the neighborhood in hues of golden warmth, but his eyes fixated not on the colors, but on the people scattering across the busy street. Faces, expressions, movements—moments waiting to be captured. His fingers hovered over the shutter, yet his mind drifted, caught in a struggle far more complex than any photograph could frame.

He thought about the phrase his mother often repeated: “Be colorblind.” To Jacob, it sounded noble—an ideal where skin color did not dictate opportunity, judgment, or fate. Yet, standing in the heart of a city vibrant with diversity, the phrase felt over-simplified, even reductive.

Jacob had grown up in a predominantly white suburb, immersed in stories and school lessons that painted race either as a problem to be solved or a forgotten “post-racial” reality. But college threw him into a kaleidoscope of experiences, where race crawled out of textbooks and into daily interactions—sometimes subtle, sometimes painfully raw. And slowly, he began to realize how the notion of “colorblindness” often hid ignorance rather than fostered understanding.

On this particular day, Jacob was preparing for a photo essay aimed at dissecting modern racial dynamics. His project was painfully personal, an attempt to untangle the threads of his own upbringing and confront the uncomfortable truths lurking beneath society’s surface.

The Meeting

Jacob’s first interview was with Malik, a community organizer in his late thirties, dedicated to racial justice in the city’s neglected neighborhoods.

Malik welcomed him with a firm handshake and a warm smile. “So, colorblind, huh? That’s a popular starting point,” Malik said, leading Jacob into a small coffee shop filled with the scent of roasted beans and the murmur of conversation.

“I guess I used to believe in it,” Jacob admitted, setting up his recorder. “That not seeing color was the way to overcome racism.”

Malik shook his head slowly. “I get that. But when you say, ‘I don’t see color,’ what you’re often saying is, ‘I don’t see your experiences.’ Color is part of identity. It’s a lived reality. To be truly anti-racist, you have to acknowledge that reality head-on, not pretend it doesn’t exist.”

Jacob took notes furiously. “So, you’re saying that ignoring race—to be ‘colorblind’—actually perpetuates racism?”

Malik nodded. “Exactly. It erases the history and systemic problems. It lets people off the hook instead of encouraging them to confront inequality and bias.”

The conversation dug deeper—how systemic racism wielded itself through education, policing, housing, and employment. Malik illustrated with stories of families trapped by conditions centuries in the making, and how acknowledgment and targeted action were crucial for any real change.

Seeing Through the Lens

Weeks passed as Jacob interviewed more people—scholars who dissected the social construction of race, activists who challenged segregation and prejudice, and everyday citizens navigating the often-invisible barriers imposed by stereotypes. Each story added layers of complexity to the simplistic notion he’d always carried.

One afternoon, Jacob revisited a childhood friend, Sarah, now a professor of sociology. Over steaming cups of tea, Sarah shared her own reckoning with “colorblindness” in her classroom.

“I used to tell my students, ‘Let’s be colorblind,’ thinking it was promoting equality,” Sarah said thoughtfully. “But the students of color would often feel invisible, like their experiences and struggles were being dismissed. It turns out that acknowledging difference respectfully creates space to heal and build empathy—colorblindness can feel like denial.”

Jacob nodded, understanding beginning to bloom in his mind like a slow dawn. It wasn’t about erasing color, but about actively facing and dismantling the inequalities shadowing those hues.

The Mosaic of Experience

Jacob’s camera captured more than just images; it began to capture stories, emotions, and truths. There was Rosa, a Latina educator fighting to diversify her curriculum using inclusive histories. There was Amir, a Muslim teenager confronting Islamophobia in school. There was Elaine, a white ally learning that good intentions weren’t enough without humility and listening.

Each revealed a fragment of a larger mosaic—a society grappling with the legacies of prejudice and the urgent need for genuine connection.

Jacob found himself stepping outside his comfort zone: attending protests, participating in community dialogues, and facing moments where his own unconscious biases surprised him. Journals filled with reflections and questions replaced old certainties.

Reckoning and Redefinition

One night, as rain tapped gently on his apartment window, Jacob reviewed the photographs and recorded interviews. An image of a young Black boy waving confidently in a sunlit park stood next to a portrait of an elderly white woman smiling with tears in her eyes—both were faces of hope and resilience.

He realized his journey wasn’t about “being colorblind.” It was about seeing color fully—embracing complexity, confronting discomfort, and committing to change from a place of honesty.

His photo essay evolved into an exhibition titled “Seeing Color: Stories Toward Healing and Justice”. It told stories of pain, resistance, empathy, and hope. The public’s response was powerful—many admitted it challenged their own views, and a few confessed gratitude for opening their eyes to realities they’d long ignored.

Moving Forward

Jacob’s mother visited the exhibition one afternoon. She stood quietly, absorbing the faces and voices swirling around her. Finally, she asked, with a tremor in her voice, “So, being colorblind isn’t the answer?”

Jacob smiled gently. “No, Mom. It never was. It’s about seeing and valuing every shade and every story. Only then can we build bridges—not erase them.”

She nodded slowly, her eyes reflecting new understanding.

Jacob had started with a camera aimed at capturing moments, but ended with a newfound purpose: to listen deeply, to learn continually, and to engage in the ongoing, imperfect work of dismantling racism—not by ignoring differences, but by honoring them.

Colorblindness, in its idealized form, suggests neutrality, but neutrality in the face of systemic racism can mean complicity. Jacob’s journey highlights the urgent need to shift from ignoring color to embracing a nuanced understanding—where every shade tells a story, every experience shapes reality, and every individual deserves to be seen fully to heal collectively.

Reflection:

In conversations about race, the metaphor of being “colorblind” frequently resurfaces, motivated by the desire to treat all people equally. However, genuine equality emerges not from denial of difference, but from the acknowledgment of historical context, personal experiences, and ongoing disparities.

By transitioning from colorblindness to color-consciousness, communities can foster empathy, encourage honest dialogue, and promote policies addressing systemic inequities. This journey toward overcoming racism is continuous—marked by reflection, education, and action. Recognizing the kaleidoscope of human identities enriches society, turning diversity from a challenge into an opportunity for growth and justice.

Jacob’s story is a reminder: Seeing color is not a barrier to equality but the essential foundation for true understanding and change.
