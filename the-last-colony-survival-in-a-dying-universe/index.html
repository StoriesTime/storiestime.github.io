The Last Colony: Survival in a Dying Universe


Mira’s boots scraped against the jagged surface of Zeron-7, kicking up volcanic dust that clung to her suit like a curse. Every breath she took was filtered through a failing oxygen unit, a reminder that survival on this barren world was more than just a challenge—it was a desperate gamble against odds stacked high and unforgiving.

The colony ship, Endurance, had landed here ten years ago when the dying embers of Earth could no longer sustain human life. The universe was unraveling, stars collapsing into black holes at an alarming rate, leaving civilizations scattered like ashes in the cold void. Zeron-7 was chosen not for its promise, but because it was one of the last habitable rocks left spinning in the chaos.

“Central to Mira. Report status,” crackled a voice over the comms.

“Still alive, Central. Searching sector Z-12 for mineral deposits,” she replied, squinting at the horizon.

These expeditions were essential. The colony’s dwindling supply of power couldn’t be supplemented by fusion reactors alone, not with the heavy toll the environment took on their machines. The harsh winds of Zeron-7 and its unforgiving terrain made each sortie feel like a fight against inevitability.

Mira adjusted the tether on her magnetic surveyor, scanning for traces of trithium, the rare element that kept the colony’s life support systems operational. Without it, the settlement—already fragile—would join the countless others lost in the aftermath of Earth’s collapse.

Her scanner beeped faintly. A signal. Mira dropped to one knee, brushing away the ash that blanketed the ground like sorrow. Beneath the powdery surface, her instrument detected faint readings of trithium.

“Central, I’ve got something. It’s faint, but it’s solid,” Mira said, her voice betraying a trace of optimism.

“You know the drill,” the operator said. “Mark the site and return for extraction. Be swift—storm warnings are coming through.”

Storms on Zeron-7 were nothing like those Mira remembered from her childhood on Earth. Here, the skies boiled with corrosive acid rain, and winds strong enough to shred steel made shelter a matter of survival, not comfort.

She planted a beacon at the discovery site, took a final reading, and started back toward the safety of the colony. Time was a currency no one could afford to waste, not when death loomed with every fluctuation of the atmosphere.

Back at the colony’s perimeter, Mira crossed the energy shield checkpoint, its faint hum reassuringly constant against the chaos outside. The settlement was a patchwork of modular habitats, solar panel arrays, and makeshift greenhouses struggling to support the dwindling population. A decade of hardship had whittled down the colony’s initial numbers to fewer than 500 souls, each carrying the weight of survival like stones tied to their spirit.

The command center was bustling with activity—scientists, engineers, and technicians worked tirelessly, many of them slumped with exhaustion but unable to stop. Mira strode in, tossing her helmet onto a nearby rack.

“Found another site,” she informed Dr. Batiste, the colony’s lead geologist.

“We’ll send an extraction team as soon as the weather clears,” Batiste said, not bothering to look up from the holographic display in front of him. His sunken eyes seemed permanently fixed on a horizon of impossible solutions.

Two young engineers entered the room, bickering about calibration errors in the shield generator. Mira sighed. Though conflicts were rare, tensions stretched thin over time, snapping like overworked wires.

“Focus, people,” she called sharply. “If we lose sight of what matters, we’re dead. Let’s keep it together.”

Her words carried weight. Mira wasn’t just a scavenger—she was a leader, chosen for her resilience and ability to inspire hope even when optimism felt foolish. Her decisions shaped the colony’s survival strategies, and her presence reminded them that humanity hadn’t given up just yet.

Later that evening, Mira sat in her quarters, staring out at the fractured landscape beyond the reinforced window. Far in the distance, violent storms lashed against mountains reduced to crumbling ruins. Sometimes the sky seemed to glow faintly, a reminder of the universe’s slow demise, its beauty dulled by entropy.

She opened her personal log and began to type.

“Day 3,646 since arrival. Found another trithium deposit today. It’ll buy us more time, maybe another month if the yield is high. But time means nothing if entropy keeps accelerating. Stars collapsing faster than any calculation can predict. The black hole density in this sector is rising, crushing space into unrecognizable forms. I wonder if the very laws of physics will fail us eventually. Maybe surviving here is only prolonging the inevitable. But I can’t give up. For them, I won’t.”

Mira closed the log, steeling herself for another day.

The colony wasn’t just fighting against time—it was battling the collapse of reality itself. The arrival of the Endurance had been heralded as hope, but no one had anticipated how quickly the universe would decay. The collapse wasn’t random; it felt deliberate, as though something ancient and malevolent had awakened.

Rumors had spread among the settlers about whispers in the dark, strange signals intercepted from deep space, indecipherable except for the repeating phrase: “The Last Will Remain.”

When the storm finally cleared days later, Mira joined the extraction team at the site she’d marked. Drills churned noisily against the ground, slicing through layers of molten rock to extract the precious trithium.

“How does it look?” she asked Kellan, one of the extraction engineers.

“Promising. If readings hold, this could be the largest yield we’ve hit in years,” Kellan said, wiping sweat from his brow despite the suit’s cooling systems.

But just as the drills hit their deepest, the ground beneath the site trembled. The tremors weren’t natural—Mira recognized that immediately.

“Stop everything!” she shouted, but it was too late.

With a deafening roar, the earth split open, revealing an impossibly vast chamber. The drills had punctured something buried deep beneath Zeron-7—something alive.

The colony sent its best minds to survey the discovery. Within hours, Mira was back at the site, staring into the abyss that had opened up beneath their feet. Faint patterns glinted on the walls of the chamber, etched into the stone like ancient runes.

“Is it a structure?” Dr. Batiste muttered, examining the patterns with a handheld scanner.

“Not a structure,” Mira replied, her voice low. “It’s a warning.”

They couldn’t understand it, not fully. But as they stood over the chasm, the whispers began—first in the static of their communications, then faintly in their ears.

“The Last Will Remain.”

Mira felt her chest tighten as the words echoed through her mind. Her instincts screamed to flee, to seal the chamber and pretend they’d never disturbed its slumber. But she knew better. The universe was unraveling, and Zeron-7 had just revealed its darkest secret.

“Mira,” Batiste said, his voice shaking. “I think we’ve found something older than time itself. It might explain why the universe is… dying.”

This was no longer about survival. It was about understanding, about facing the truth before annihilation swept away the last of humanity.

Mira stared into the void. For the first time in years, she felt something close to fear. Or was it awe?
