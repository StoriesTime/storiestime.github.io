Beyond Color: Short Fiction on Fighting Prejudice


In the small town of Eldridge, colors had names and stories. Red was courage, blue was sadness, yellow was joy, and black… well, black was often misunderstood. It was a color too deep, too dark — a color people sometimes avoided.

Mira had lived in Eldridge all her life. She spent her days in the old library, dusting shelves and reading stories that painted pictures wilder than the town itself. She loved stories — stories about heroes, about journeys, about change. But there was one story she never quite understood: the story of the Color Wall.

The Color Wall was an ancient painted mural, a swirling chaos of hues on the brick wall beside the town square. At least, that’s what it was supposed to be. To Mira, it was also a symbol of division. Every year, the elders of Eldridge would gather and redraw the Wall, splitting it into neat segments — red here, blue there, yellow in a separate corner, and black always separated, like a shadow that couldn’t mingle.

One summer afternoon, a new family moved into town — the Jays. They were unlike anyone Eldridge had seen. The Jays’ skin was the deepest shade of black, glowing with richness under the sun. Children whispered, parents turned their heads, and the Color Wall suddenly felt heavier to Mira.

Curiosity tugged her toward the Jays’ home, a quaint yellow house on Willow Street. She found Amari, the youngest of the Jays, sitting on the porch steps, sketching with charcoal on scraps of paper.

“Hey,” Mira said softly. “Your drawings are amazing.”

Amari looked up and smiled, a quick flash of teeth brighter than any color Mira knew. “Thanks! I like to draw things no one really sees.”

“Like what?”

“Like feelings. Like stories hidden between words. Like the colors beyond the Color Wall.”

Mira sat beside him, fascinated. “What do you mean, beyond the Color Wall?”

Amari’s eyes sparkled. “I mean the colors people forget to see. Black isn’t just a shadow or absence. It’s depth, strength, history. It holds all the colors inside it if you look close enough.”

That night, Mira couldn’t stop thinking about Amari’s words. She wandered back to the town square, under the fading light. As she gazed at the mural, the colors felt less confined. The boundaries between them blurred and seemed to pulse with possibility.

For days afterwards, Mira visited the Jays often. Through Amari and his family’s stories, she learned about the world beyond Eldridge — about unfair labels, about barriers built from fear, about generations fighting to be seen as more than a color.

With each visit, her own view of the Color Wall transformed. It no longer looked like a prison but a canvas yearning for a new picture.

One morning, as summer ripened, Mira gathered a group of children from the town square. “Let’s paint the Color Wall together,” she proposed. “Not by dividing colors, but by blending them. Let’s show Eldridge that no color stands alone.”

The children cheered, and even some hesitant adults came to watch. Brushes danced, colors bled into each other, and the Wall blossomed into a mosaic of stories — reds melting into blacks, yellows intertwining with blues, all swirling freely.

When the elders returned to see the new mural, they were silent. There was no neat order, no clear divisions — only a living, breathing tapestry.

News of the transformed Color Wall spread beyond Eldridge. Visitors came, curious and inspired. Eldridge began to change, too. Conversations opened, friendships formed across old divides, and the community started to see itself differently.

Mira stood by Amari in the dawn light of a new day. “We painted beyond color,” she said quietly.

Amari nodded. “Because beyond color, there’s something everyone shares — a story waiting to be heard.”

And for the first time, Eldridge looked beyond color, and saw with new eyes.

In Eldridge’s tale lies a universal truth: Prejudice is a wall painted with fear and misunderstanding, but it can be transformed into a bridge painted with empathy and connection. Stories like Mira’s remind us that before we see color, we must see people — with all their depth, history, and light.
