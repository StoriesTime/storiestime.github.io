Caught in the Scroll: Tales of Social Media Addiction


It was 6 a.m. when I woke up, just like every other morning. The hum of the city outside, the faint glow of my phone’s screen piercing the darkness of my bedroom, and the inevitable pressure of the day ahead—the moment I opened my eyes, my thumb was already poised over the screen, ready to dive into the abyss. For a brief moment, I wondered if I was addicted.

But I didn’t dwell on it. Not yet. I had a habit to feed.

Scrolling through social media was now the first thing I did every morning. Twitter’s endless threads of outrage and fleeting commentary, Instagram’s curated lives of unattainable perfection, and TikTok’s short bursts of dopamine, all beckoned me like a siren call. Even the simple act of checking the weather or catching up on the news had long been overshadowed by the habit of checking notifications.

That morning, like any other, I had no particular reason to check. But as soon as I did, I got lost. The notification bell flashed, signaling a flood of new likes, comments, and messages. It was impossible to ignore. My thumb flicked through posts and pictures mindlessly, heartlessly, as if I were chasing a feeling that could never be caught.

By the time the clock read 7 a.m., I had already wasted an hour of my day. A hollow, familiar emptiness settled in me, yet I didn’t have the strength to stop. It wasn’t like I didn’t know I had a problem. Everyone did. Everyone knew about social media addiction—it was a hot topic in the media, something that was discussed in articles, interviews, and documentaries. It was right there in front of me, in my screen, yet I couldn’t stop. It had me.

The deeper you get into social media, the more it sinks into your brain. First, it’s just a scroll here and there—an occasional look while you wait for your coffee to brew, or a quick glance during a break at work. Before you know it, hours slip by, and you don’t even realize how much time you’ve lost. You’re trapped, but you don’t want to escape. At least, not consciously.

I used to tell myself that social media was a tool. A means of connecting with friends, learning new things, and finding inspiration. At first, it was all about staying updated, enjoying the memes, and interacting with people I knew in real life. But slowly, the shift began. My sense of self-worth started to depend on the number of likes and comments I got, on the validation that came with a post’s popularity. It wasn’t even about connecting with others anymore. It was about proving something to myself—and to the world.

I’d caught myself in a loop.

It wasn’t just the casual scrolling. It was the way it felt when you checked your feed for the fiftieth time that day, hoping for something new. I wasn’t looking for real connection. No, I was searching for the illusion of connection, a false sense of engagement that left me feeling more isolated than ever.

As the day wore on, I could feel my mind shifting. Social media was no longer just something I browsed in my free time; it had become a part of my identity. Every notification, every “ping,” became a demand for my attention. The world seemed to revolve around my phone, and I couldn’t stop looking at it. Not because I wanted to, but because the need to be connected, to see what others were doing, was overpowering. It had become a reflex, an automatic action, like breathing.

By noon, my colleagues and I had gathered for lunch. We sat around the table, each of us on our phones. It was a strange thing, the way we were all physically together but mentally apart, wrapped in the cocoon of our screens. It was common, almost expected now, to spend every spare moment with your nose buried in your phone, flipping through endless streams of content. Occasionally, one of us would comment on something someone had posted—a video, a tweet, a meme—but it felt forced, as if the conversation was merely a way to justify our collective digital addiction.

“Have you seen that new trend on TikTok?” Jessica asked, looking up briefly from her phone.

“Yeah,” I said, not really paying attention, “I saw it this morning.”

We fell silent again, lost in the glow of our devices, only breaking free to eat and take occasional sips of water.

After lunch, I returned to my desk, still entranced by the screen in front of me. I wasn’t even working anymore—I was just scrolling. I should have been focused, should have been tackling my to-do list, but my brain was trapped in the loop of consumption. The task at hand seemed insignificant compared to the pull of the next update, the next post. My mind wandered back to the apps, my thumb itching to swipe.

And that was when I realized how much of my life had been lost in this addiction. How many hours had slipped by unnoticed, how many conversations had been ignored, how many opportunities had been missed because I was more interested in the virtual world than the real one?

I picked up my phone and opened Instagram, scrolling past the polished images of vacations, relationships, and accomplishments. I couldn’t help but feel a wave of inadequacy. The lives of others were so perfect, so curated, so controlled. And yet, I knew it was all fake. But the comparisons didn’t stop. They never did. The more you scroll, the more you compare. The more you look at what others have, the less you appreciate what you have. It was a dangerous game.

I put the phone down for a moment, trying to focus. But the urge to pick it back up was overwhelming. A notification flashed—someone had liked my latest post. I reached for it without thinking, without hesitation.

I was caught.

By mid-afternoon, I had fallen deep into the rabbit hole of social media. I started with checking my messages, then moved to posts, then into the never-ending cycle of mindless scrolling. An hour passed, then two. I was barely aware of time moving. But then something strange happened. My phone froze. The app I had been scrolling through stalled. My pulse quickened as I clicked again, but it wouldn’t load. I pressed my phone screen harder, convinced it was a glitch. But nothing happened. I tried closing the app and reopening it. Still, nothing.

I stared at the screen in disbelief. For a brief, fleeting moment, I was free. The digital floodgate had closed. And for that brief moment, I felt… relieved. It was like a breath of fresh air, a moment of silence. But then my mind started to fill the void. I began to wonder what I was missing. What had I missed in the last five seconds, the last minute?

I realized just how dependent I had become on the noise, the updates, the constant rush of information. It was hard to break free. But in that small moment of stillness, I saw the truth: I didn’t need the noise. I didn’t need the constant validation. What I needed was silence. What I needed was space.

When my phone finally refreshed, the notifications came flooding in again, but I didn’t reach for it. I just sat there. For the first time in a long time, I was present in the moment.

I was caught in the scroll, yes. But now, I was beginning to see the path out. It wouldn’t be easy. It wouldn’t happen overnight. But maybe, just maybe, there was hope for a life beyond the endless pull of notifications and likes. Maybe it was time to take back my time.

And so, I made a choice. The next morning, I didn’t reach for my phone first thing. Instead, I opened a book. It felt strange, but good. I still had a long way to go, but this was a start. A small step, but a step nonetheless.

The scroll had me. But maybe, just maybe, it didn’t have to.
