Chasing Shadows: Reviving Vanishing Traditions


The morning sunlight poured through the gaps in the thatched roof, illuminating the dusty wooden planks on the floor. Beneath that humble canopy, sixty-eight-year-old Ravi Patel sat cross-legged, arranging thin bamboo strips into the beginnings of a mudiyettu mask—a headpiece used for performance rituals in Kerala. His hands, with their wrinkled elegance born of decades of practice, worked methodically, moving without hesitation.

The mudiyettu tradition had been his life’s work, yet the stories it told, the rhythmic beats of the chenda drums accompanying its theatrical dances, and the vibrant masks of gods and demons were all slipping into obscurity. As crowds dwindled and younger generations embraced modernity, Ravi began to feel the heavy weight of this quiet vanishing. There were only a handful of elders left in his village who still remembered the songs, the ancient chants, and the precise steps of the ritual dances.

“Grandpa!” a small voice pulled Ravi out of his thoughts. His granddaughter, Amaya, bounded into the hut, carrying her sketchpad. “Look, I drew something!” She handed him the paper, grinning as she rambled on about her school’s art project.

Ravi accepted the drawing with a kind nod, admiring her enthusiasm. On the page was a roughly sketched image of a mudiyettu performer. Though it lacked proportional finesse, Amaya had managed to capture its boldness—the intricate lines of the mask, the billowing garments, and the fierce, dynamic stance. Her curiosity was undeniable.

“You know, this,” Ravi pointed to the performer on the drawing, “was how we told stories without spoken words. Long before televisions came to our homes, this was how gods and demons came to life.”

Amaya stared at him, crinkling her nose with interest. “Will you teach me?”

Her words jolted something inside Ravi, something dormant but unforgotten. Could the traditions survive if he shared them with the younger generation? He had thought about it before, but the lack of interest from others had left him skeptical.

“Teaching these stories isn’t simple, child. You’ll need discipline, patience, and…” He paused, glancing at the sketch again—Amaya’s fascination, her tiny hands clutching the pencil as if it were the key to solving mysteries. Perhaps there was hope in this young spark.

Amaya nodded vigorously. “I can do it!” she said with the kind of certainty only children could muster.

That day marked the beginning of a partnership that Ravi didn’t realize he needed. Amaya’s curiosity bloomed as she pored over ancient texts Ravi had kept hidden away. Together, they combed through ochre-painted scripts detailing lineage histories of gods Shiva and Kali, studying the symbolic significance of each movement and phrase.

Months passed, transforming Ravi’s secluded hut into a living museum. In the mornings, they painstakingly molded masks, layering color after color until the characters seemed alive. The afternoons brought the thundering reverberations that filled the air as Ravi taught Amaya how to strike the chenda drums, timing her beats with the intricate twirls of his choreographed explanations.

Amaya quickly learned that the mudiyettu was no simple performance—it was a ritual dance steeped in spirituality. Every detail was symbolic: the masks, the music, the movements, and even the colors represented cosmic forces, histories, and struggles between good and evil. It was art with a purpose, and it demanded respect.

While Ravi nurtured Amaya’s growing expertise in the tradition, whispers began circulating in the nearby village of Devapuram. Ravi Patel—the forgotten artisan—was suddenly alive again, teaching the old ways to his granddaughter. Curious elders started dropping by the hut for tea, subtly asking Ravi about his work. Young villagers, cradling smartphones and speaking fluent English, hovered around the edges of their gatherings, torn between skepticism and intrigue.

It wasn’t until one stormy evening when fate—and Ravi’s newfound hope—fully revealed itself.

The annual harvest festival was approaching, and the village committee had secretly invited Ravi to perform mudiyettu at the temple grounds. Excitement rippled through the community like lightning. For most, it was an opportunity to witness something ancient; for others, it was a rediscovery of forgotten roots.

“Grandpa, are they really going to let us?” Amaya asked one evening as they applied the finishing touches to their masks. She had taken on an apprentice-like role, completing smaller tasks with an eagerness that Ravi admired.

“They haven’t seen mudiyettu in twenty years,” Ravi replied, his voice carrying an air of pride and determination. “This will be more than a performance—it will be a revival.”

The day of the harvest festival arrived with bursts of sound and color. The air carried the aroma of deep-fried snacks and bursts of laughter as men, women, and children bustled through stalls selling handcrafted trinkets and sweets. At the center of the chaos stood a stage draped in freshly cut banana leaves symbolizing prosperity.

Ravi and Amaya appeared just as the sun dipped below the horizon, casting fiery hues over the temple grounds. Their masks shimmered, reflecting dim lanterns flickering like sentinels. Ravi could feel his heartbeat syncing with the rhythmic hum of the drums—a pulse that seemed to call the gods themselves to witness.

The performance began with measured precision. Ravi moved with the calculated grace that years of dedication had given him. His steps didn’t falter even once as he twirled, leaped, and stomped to the persistent beat. Amaya played the chenda alongside her grandfather, her timing impeccably merging with his movements. Together, their synergy transformed the stage into a portal between the seen and the unseen.

The crowd was captivated. Eyes widened as Ravi reenacted the cosmic battles of deities, translating centuries-old myths into visceral imagery. For the elders, it felt like a reunion with distant memories; for the youth, it was an awakening to heritage long buried under layers of modern distractions.

By the time the drums quieted, and the masks were lowered, applause swept through the crowd like a tidal wave.

Ravi—standing tall amidst a sea of joyous faces—felt the weight on his shoulders lighten. Amaya, smiling as she wiped the sweat from her brow, turned to him and said, “Grandpa, they loved it! They really loved it!”

He nodded, placing a hand on her shoulder. “Traditions come alive when shared, child. Keep chasing shadows, and you’ll find light.”

Following the performance, a curious shift took hold of Devapuram. Colleges invited Ravi to lecture on mudiyettu and ritual storytelling, while artists sought his guidance on working with natural dyes and bamboo. Amaya, eager to involve her peers, started organizing workshops where teenagers learned the basics of mask-making and drum-playing.

What Ravi once feared was irretrievable had begun its journey back into the light. Young minds, like Amaya’s, carried the echoes of history forward, proving that even shadows could be revived.

And so, in the heart of Kerala, traditions found their second breath—not as relics of the past but as living embodiments of a timeless soul.
