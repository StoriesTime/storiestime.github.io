Through Their Eyes: A Journey Beyond Racism


In the heart of a bustling city, where the skyline kissed the clouds and the streets pulsed with life, there existed a small, unassuming café named “The Common Ground.” It was a place where the aroma of freshly brewed coffee mingled with the laughter of patrons, a sanctuary for those seeking solace from the chaos of the outside world. But what made this café truly special was not just its warm ambiance or delicious pastries; it was the stories that unfolded within its walls.

One rainy afternoon, as droplets danced on the windowpanes, a diverse group of individuals found themselves huddled together at a long wooden table. They were strangers, each carrying their own burdens, yet united by a shared desire to understand one another. Among them was Maya, a young African American woman with a passion for social justice; Raj, a first-generation Indian immigrant navigating the complexities of his new life; Elena, a Latina artist with dreams of breaking stereotypes; and Thomas, a middle-aged white man grappling with his own privilege.

As the rain drummed a rhythmic beat outside, Maya broke the silence. “What if we shared our stories? Not just the highlights, but the struggles, the fears, and the moments that shaped us?” Her suggestion hung in the air, a fragile thread connecting them all.

Raj nodded, his eyes brightening. “I think that’s a great idea. We often see each other through the lens of our backgrounds, but what if we looked beyond that? What if we saw each other through our experiences?”

Elena leaned in, her curiosity piqued. “I’ve always believed that art can bridge gaps. Maybe we can create something together, a mural that represents our stories and the journey beyond racism.”

Thomas, who had been quietly listening, finally spoke up. “I’ve spent so much of my life unaware of the privilege I carry. I want to understand how my actions, or inactions, have impacted others. I’m ready to learn.”

With a collective sense of purpose, they decided to embark on a journey—one that would take them beyond their individual experiences and into the heart of their shared humanity. They agreed to meet every week at The Common Ground, each session dedicated to exploring a different theme related to their identities and the societal constructs that shaped them.

Week One: The Roots of Identity

As they gathered for their first session, the atmosphere was charged with anticipation. Maya began by sharing her story, recounting her childhood in a predominantly Black neighborhood. She spoke of the beauty of her culture, the music that filled her home, and the challenges she faced in a world that often marginalized her identity.

“I remember the first time I was called the N-word,” she said, her voice steady but laced with emotion. “I was just a kid, and it shattered my innocence. It made me question my worth and the world around me.”

Raj listened intently, his heart heavy with empathy. “I can relate to that feeling of being an outsider. When my family moved here from India, I struggled to fit in. I was bullied for my accent and the food I brought to school. It made me feel ashamed of my heritage.”

Elena chimed in, her eyes glistening with unshed tears. “As a Latina, I’ve faced stereotypes that reduce me to a single narrative. People assume I’m just a maid or a gardener. But I’m an artist, and my work reflects the richness of my culture.”

Thomas shifted in his seat, feeling the weight of their words. “I’ve never had to face those kinds of prejudices. My privilege has shielded me from the harsh realities you all experience. I want to understand how to use that privilege to uplift others.”

As the conversation flowed, they began to peel back the layers of their identities, revealing the complexities that lay beneath. They laughed, cried, and shared moments of vulnerability, forging a bond that transcended their differences.

Week Two: The Impact of Stereotypes

The following week, they delved into the theme of stereotypes. Maya opened the discussion by sharing her experiences with media representation. “I often see Black characters portrayed as criminals or victims. It perpetuates a narrative that dehumanizes us.”

Raj nodded in agreement. “In Bollywood, there’s a tendency to portray Indian characters in a certain light—either as tech geniuses or as the ‘exotic’ love interest. It’s frustrating because it doesn’t reflect the diversity of our experiences.”

Elena added, “In art, I’ve faced pressure to conform to stereotypes. People expect me to create ‘traditional’ Latino art, but I want to break free from those confines and express my unique voice.”

Thomas listened, feeling a sense of responsibility. “I realize that I’ve often laughed at jokes that perpetuate these stereotypes without considering the harm they cause. I want to be more mindful and challenge those narratives.”

As they shared their experiences, they began to brainstorm ways to combat stereotypes through their art.
