Lunar New Year Lanterns: A Family Reunion Across Generations


The winter in Nanjing had crisp edges, the kind that nipped at fingertips and left breath visible even at noon. It was the eve of the Lunar New Year, a day mapped out in tradition—steamed dumplings, red paper squares, incense burning in old shrines—and for the Jiangs, it meant a reunion.

Lin Jiang, aged eighteen, pressed her face to the cab window as ancient city walls swept past. The airport had been a blur—grey suits, a parade of masked faces, the weight of her backpack. But she was here, she’d made it, alone this time, her parents somewhere in New York keeping a restaurant open through another holiday.

The family home was a square-timbered, two-story relic cradled by persimmon trees, red lanterns bobbing at the entrance. Inside, the din of multiple generations was already a tidal wave—cousins swapping stories, aunts shouting instructions, elders complementing and offending each other in measured turns.

“Ai-ya, Linlin! When did you get so tall?” Her grandmother’s voice, cackling with the warmth of sesame oil, wormed its way through the haze of noise. Lin grinned, dipping her head as grandma pinched her cheek.

They gathered like migrating birds that night, reconvening every year to honor those who had gone before and those who would come after. The kitchen swirled with spices and flour dust. Jade bracelets clinked on wrists, and silk creaked as great-aunts carried basin-sized platters through doorways.

After dinner—the relentless parade of fish, sticky rice cakes, and whole roast chicken—came the moment Lin both dreaded and longed for: the making of lanterns. It was an old tradition, one her grandfather insisted on, though his hands shook these days, and his eyes watered more than they used to.

In the sunroom, the supply boxes were already out: paper, glue, wire, ribbons, gold foil. Lin’s cousins began crafting elaborate shapes—dragons, koi fish, mythic rabbits—laughter echoing through the house. Her own approach was less artful. She preferred the basic globes, letting her fingers follow the memory of movements learned as a child.

Seated across from her, Grandpa Jiang worked in silence. He traced lines on velvet red paper, tongue poking from the corner of his mouth, the way all the Jiang men did when concentrating. Lin wanted to ask him things—a million questions about his youth, about their ancestors, about why he always seemed to look through her rather than at her. Instead, they worked side by side in a hush that wasn’t quite comfortable but wasn’t unwelcome either.

“Come, Linlin, help your little cousin with the glue,” her Aunt Wen yelled, but before she could stand, Grandpa reached for her wrist.

“Let her finish here,” he said softly. “She should know the pattern.”

When midnight came, fireworks began popping in the courtyards surrounding their street. The older children whooped and ran outside. Inside, the lanterns glowed softly, warm against the frigid dark beyond the glass.

Each lantern was more than a bit of colored paper; it was a memory, a prayer, a wish for the year ahead. The house filled with the rustle of coats and thick scarves as everyone gathered at the threshold, bearing new lanterns. The cold outside bit through wool, but hearts were fortified by rare togetherness.

Grandpa led the procession, holding his lantern high. Lin followed close, her breath puckering into frost clouds. They made their way along the alley, lanterns swaying gently.

Neighbors drifted from their doors, carrying their own flickering lights. The crowd grew, a living river of color through the night. At the end of the lane, the old temple courtyard waited. Tradition demanded that lanterns be released here, their wishes carried to the ancestors by the rising currents.

Grandpa turned to Lin and pressed the paper handle of his own lantern into her hand. “For luck,” he said. “And so you remember.”

Lin hesitated, feeling the rough press of his palm. It struck her then—he had made his lantern as a child, too, in this very courtyard. She wondered about his father, and his father before that, if they too had stood under the stars in throngs of family and neighbors, hoping and remembering amid red light.

She approached the wooden rails, standing with cousins, aunts, and uncles, as the first lanterns sailed upward, buoyed by the warmth of their small flames. Wishes for health, for fortune, for forgiveness and for reunion, all threaded invisibly through the cold darkness.

Lin let Grandpa’s lantern go. It wobbled, then steadied, climbing past pine branches that sparkled with the year’s first dusting of frost. She watched it rise, and with it, a cluster of family hopes, strung across generations.

At her feet, a little voice piped up. Her cousin Mei’s mittened hand tugged at her sleeve. “Lin-jie,” she whispered, “do you think Mama can see the lantern from far away?”

Lin knelt so they were eye-to-eye. “I think she’ll see every single one.” Mei smiled, and in that small moment, it didn’t matter that half their loved ones were on the other side of oceans. They were together in the light of a thousand lanterns, sharing a tapestry of belonging bound in tradition.

The crowd lingered, chatting softly as the lanterns lifted one by one. Their light merged with distant fireworks, painting the night with color and a sense of connection deeper than language or even memory.

Lin held Mei’s hand, feeling history pulse in her own veins. Across miles and decades, across lives begun and ended, their family gathered, year after year. Each reunion a beacon, each lantern a promise that no one is ever fully gone so long as wishes ascend and stories are told—again, and again, under the same moon.

As the night waned and the last lanterns winked like new constellations on the horizon, Lin knew she’d return, again and again, to sustain the glow that tethered her not just to this generation, but to all those before and all those still to come.

And among the glowing lanterns, drifting higher and higher, the hopes of her family soared—timeless, weightless, unbroken.
