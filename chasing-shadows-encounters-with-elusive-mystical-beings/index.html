Chasing Shadows: Encounters with Elusive Mystical Beings


The first time Lucien saw one, it was barely more than a flicker at the edge of his vision—a shimmer in the moonlight that disappeared as suddenly as it appeared. He had been hiking deep into the Blackstone Woods, a dense, ancient forest whispered about in local legends. The villagers often spoke of shadows that moved without source and figures that vanished into thin air, but Lucien, the skeptical historian by trade, dismissed it as mere folklore. He carried his camera, his journal, and a nagging curiosity that refused to let the old stories rest.

It was not the kind of curiosity borne from belief; quite the opposite, in fact. Lucien wanted to demystify the woods, debunk the tales of elusive beings that supposedly dwelled within the shadowy expanse. Yet, standing under the waning crescent moon that night, surrounded by the towering silhouettes of blackened pines, he found himself wavering. That shimmer couldn’t have been a trick of the light—could it?

An Unspoken Pact

The days following Lucien’s encounter blurred together in an obsessive haze. He pored over the testimonies he had once dismissed, studying every whispered name and cryptic account recorded by generations past. The Skethel, the villagers called them—beings neither fully tangible nor entirely immaterial, said to be protectors of something unseen. People spoke of their silent approach, their glowing eyes visible only in the dark, their movements like liquid shadows.

He interviewed the locals, people who claimed to have crossed paths with the Skethel. Most were reluctant to speak at first, their voices low and their eyes constantly scanning for eavesdroppers. Eventually, though, he compiled threadbare descriptions—remnants of encounters pieced together like fragments of a forgotten language. The Skethel preferred solitude, the villagers insisted, and they punished those who sought them out for selfish reasons.

“If they spare you," one grizzled hunter warned, her face creased with lines of fear, “it’s not out of mercy. It’s because they see something in you that even you don’t understand… yet.”

Lucien dismissed the cryptic warning with a polite nod, but the words lingered with him. They ticked at the edges of his thoughts when the nights grew long and the silence too heavy.

Into the Heart of Blackstone

When Lucien returned to the forest, he came prepared. A well-stocked pack rested on his back, filled with rations, a high-powered flashlight, a handheld recorder, and a penknife he barely knew how to use. His camera hung from a strap around his neck, and his journal, worn and dog-eared, lay snug in his pocket. If the Skethel were real, he intended to document them.

The deeper he trekked, the denser the woods became, their thick canopy blotting out the daylight. Hours passed as Lucien navigated trails that seemed to twist and shift, as though the forest sought to disorient him. It was then that he began noticing peculiar things: a cluster of stones arranged in perfect symmetry, the faint smell of burning herbs where no fire could be seen, and the sensation—unmistakable—of being watched.

Dusk fell, bringing with it an oppressive silence. Even the creatures of the forest retreated, their chatter replaced by a stillness so profound it rang in Lucien’s ears. That’s when he saw it again—a shimmer, brighter this time, dancing briefly across his path before disappearing behind the nearest tree.

This time, he did not hesitate. Gripping his camera tightly, Lucien followed, his boots crunching softly on the bed of leaves and twigs below. The forest seemed to stretch on endlessly, each tree indistinguishable from the next, yet he never lost sight of the faint glow ahead. It moved with a grace that was neither hurried nor hesitant, as though it wanted him to follow but dared not let him come too close.

The Clearing of Echoes

After what felt like hours, Lucien stumbled into a clearing bathed in an ethereal light. It took his eyes a moment to adjust, and when they did, his breath caught.

Before him stood a figure unlike anything he had ever imagined. It was humanoid in form, but its body shimmered and flickered, as though made of liquid starfire. Its features were indistinct—its face a shifting tapestry of light and shadow. Its eyes, however, pierced through the unnatural glow: twin orbs of molten gold that seemed to bore into Lucien’s very soul.

Lucien’s instincts screamed at him to run, but his legs refused to obey. The figure tilted its head, a gesture both curious and unnervingly familiar, as though it were mimicking something human.

“I… I’m not here to harm you,” Lucien stammered, realizing only after the words had left him how absurd they sounded.

The being did not respond, at least not in words. Instead, the surrounding clearing began to change. The trees pulsed with a dim, rhythmic light, and the air grew thick with an energy that vibrated against Lucien’s very bones. Images flashed in his mind—visions of things he did not recognize but somehow understood: a spiraling black sky, a river flowing backward, a swarm of creatures neither bird nor beast. They were memories, but whose?

The being raised one shimmering hand and pointed to the journal peeking out of Lucien’s pocket. Slowly, cautiously, Lucien pulled it free and opened it. The being leaned closer, a low hum filling the air as its glowing fingers traced the air just above the pages. Symbols began to appear, unlike anything Lucien had ever written, as if the journal itself were answering to the Skethel’s presence.

The Warning

The symbols glowed faintly, pulsing in tandem with the clearing’s strange rhythm. Lucien, entranced, dared to speak. “What are you trying to show me?”

The being’s eyes flared brighter, and the images in Lucien’s mind grew more coherent. The black sky was now filled with winged shadows, and the river was no longer flowing backward but overflowing its banks, consuming everything in its path. The swarm was spreading, their screeches deafening as they blotted out the stars.

A single word echoed in his mind, though no voice spoke it: Threshold.

Lucien struggled to make sense of it. “Threshold for what? What are you warning me about?”

The being stepped back, its light flickering erratically, as though weakened. The forest trembled, and the trees seemed to whisper in a language just beyond comprehension. The Skethel pointed once more toward Lucien’s journal, then to the ground beneath their feet.

Lucien knelt, brushing away the leaves and dirt until he uncovered a smooth, obsidian-like stone etched with the same symbols now glowing in his journal. Hesitantly, he touched the stone. A surge of energy shot through him, overwhelming and disorienting. When he finally opened his eyes, the being was gone. The clearing was silent and empty, the ethereal glow replaced by the dim light of the rising moon.

Beyond the Shadows

In the weeks that followed, Lucien tried to share his findings, but every attempt was met with skepticism. The symbols in his journal faded into illegibility, and the obsidian stone he brought back crumbled into dust the moment it was exposed to the outside world. Yet the images, the warnings, remained etched in his mind.

He returned to Blackstone Woods several times, hoping to see the being again, but it never reappeared. The villagers, who once mocked his dedication to their myths, began treating him with a wary respect. His stories, though unverifiable, carried a weight that unsettled even the most cynical.

Lucien continued his work, though now his writings carried a new purpose. He wasn’t chasing shadows anymore; he was preserving them. There were truths woven into the fabric of the unknown, truths that couldn’t always be held in hand or viewed through a lens. And as terrifying as the encounter had been, Lucien couldn’t shake the feeling that the Skethel had spared him—for a reason.

The shadowed corners of the world, he realized, held secrets that humanity was not yet ready to confront. But one day, they would be. And when that day came, his story, his tiny fragment of the greater whole, might serve as a beacon for those willing to follow the light.
