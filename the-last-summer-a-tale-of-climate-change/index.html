The Last Summer: A Tale of Climate Change


Anna woke just before dawn, as she always did, but this morning something felt strange. The air, even at this hour, felt thick and heavy—so humid it slicked her skin before she even kicked off her quilt. Listening, Anna heard nothing but the low drone of cicadas, that endless rasp now undulant and sluggish, a backdrop that seemed to amplify the silence all around it. At the edge of her little town, Lake Willoughby, the water used to draw the morning mist into intricate patterns across the valley. Lately, the shore was splotched bare and the lake had shrunk, ceding sandy stretches to the sun.

Anna yanked her faded shorts from the floor and stepped from the trailer. She felt the pulse of the heat already coiling up from the tarmac; it would be another brutal day. Her sister, Mia, was reading in the rickety plastic chair by the porch. The air already shimmered between them.

“Can’t sleep?” Anna asked, softening her voice.

“Too hot,” Mia replied, not looking up. “My book makes more sense than the news, anyway.”

Anna sat on the steps and looked out over the uncut grasses, which hung limp in a brown-green patchwork. She remembered when mornings in June were crisp and clean, when they used to run barefoot into the dew-damp grass to chase away the geese. Now, even the geese had learned to stay away longer and travel farther, extending their migrations as habitats shriveled and failed.

She fished her phone from her pocket, not for messages, but for weather updates. The forecast scrolled repeat: excessive heat warnings, ozone advisories, water-rationing reminders. Underneath, another fire alert to the west. Anna fingered her sunburnt shoulder, remembering the eerie orange sunsets that came last month, when the smoke had drifted all the way from the mountains.

She scrolled to the community board, where a post pleaded for volunteers to help at the reservoir. That’s where she planned to go. There was nowhere else worth being—not work, not anywhere with walls. Besides, the lake was Anna’s place, where everything that mattered took place: summer bonfires, midnight swims, secrets traded as the world turned quietly malignant.

Anna pedaled her bicycle past empty lots where grass didn’t bother growing and up the lazily curved track to the lake. The air tasted scorched, the sunlight fierce and relentless. Farmers had abandoned their fields early this year, the soil cracked and the irrigation ditches empty. She saw only one other cyclist on the winding path—a man in full sun gear, his face hidden under his helmet—who waved without slowing. Much of the old crowd stayed indoors, sheltering with recycled air, but Anna preferred reality even with its new sharpened edges.

Near the dam, the volunteers gathered in loose, sweating knots. There was Roy, the manager, fifty and sun-browned, with a crow’s nest of gray hair and creased hands. Beside him, Esi and Jonah, who used to swim races with Anna and now spent their days hauling buckets and running hoses, waiting for instructions. There were others too—a rotating cast of locals and outsiders, familiar faces grown unfamiliar under lines of worry and disuse.

Roy saw Anna and gave a short nod. “You’re just in time. We need hands fixing the main pipe. There’s been a leak for days.”

Anna knelt beside Esi, who handed her a wrench. “My dad says if the reservoir drops much more, we’ll be buying water from up north. If there’s water to spare, anyway.”

Anna nodded, grim. “We’ll patch it. Whatever it takes.”

They worked quietly, sweat mingling with dust, their motions automatic from weeks of repeated labor. Anna’s hands slipped once, scraping skin across hot metal. She hardly noticed. The drone of the insects and the distant roar of the pumps overlapped in a low, relentless rhythm.

After a while, Esi broke the silence. “Do you remember last summer?” She gestured toward the water. “It used to come up to that big rock. Now it’s all mudflats.”

Anna nodded. Last summer, she and Mia had spent hours floating on their backs, legs scissoring through cool water, clouds drifting overhead. Now the lake retreated every day, a little more gone each night.

Esi leaned closer, lowering her voice. “I heard about the fires out west. Some kids at school said rain will never come again, not really. That it’s the beginning of the end.”

Anna shook her head, hiding the spike of anxiety. “That’s just rumors.” But her voice was too thin, not convincing even to herself.

By noon, the sun was merciless. Anna found herself feverish and thirsty, staring out over the cracked, shrinking waterline. Everyone left gathered under the communal awnings, eating sandwiches and pouring tepid water onto their faces.

Jonah shuffled over, carrying a battered radio. “They say it hit 44 degrees in the city,” he announced. “Record highs. Another blackout rolling through the neighborhood grid.” He squinted at the horizon, eyes glassy from heat. “My dad says we ought to move further north. He can’t farm here anymore.”

“Where would you go?” Anna asked.

“Where it’s cooler. If there is such a place.”

Anna felt something cold and sour churn inside her. People always talked about leaving, but the routes were jammed with refugees—or so the stories went. Rationing, riots, towns shuttered on arrival. It seemed safer to stay, safer to keep working for every last drop.

The afternoon crawled on, a thick, unending expanse of hot air and muddled thoughts. Anna found herself drifting, staring at the diffuse haze that coated the world. Trees quivered in the distance, their leaves brittle.

When evening finally came and the heat relented, Anna biked home under the huge, disappointed sky. The hills, once gold and inviting, hunched in shadow. She locked her bike and met Mia, who was scrawling in her notebook.

“Did you ever think about leaving?” Mia asked, eyes hard.

Anna sat on the step beside her. “Sometimes. But it wouldn’t matter. Every place is changing. It’s not the where—it’s the when.”

Mia snorted, skeptical. “You sound like Jonah. He says the same thing.”

Anna smiled, the gesture softening her worry. “Maybe it’s true.”

They sat together, listening to the hush that arrived with the night, punctuated by the distant howl of the wind turbines at the ridge. Anna remembered a story she’d read about people who once believed that progress was endless, that the earth would always provide. It seemed naive, now. Each day, everything old disappeared a little more—ice gone from the mountains, snowmelt a faded memory, orchards withered into dust.

Days bled into weeks. The town shrank as more people gave up, boarded windows, and drove away. Grocery deliveries grew unreliable, shelves emptying then staying that way. Anna’s world narrowed to the lake, her family, the pack of volunteers eking out small victories. One day, Roy’s voice cracked as he tallied the reservoir’s depth: barely enough to last the summer.

Anna retreated to the dam after work, watching the water’s slow disappearance. It felt like she was slipping away alongside it—her laughter, her childhood, her sense that the future was solid and vast.

One evening, Mia surprised her by coming to the water’s edge. She dangled her feet into the muddy shallows, shivering at the unfamiliar cool.

“What if this is it?” Mia murmured. “The last summer before it’s all gone. The last season of things as we know them?”

Anna thought of their father, who once taught them to fish in this lake, who pretended not to feel the slimy reeds just to make them laugh. She thought of Esi and Jonah and Roy, of the threads of community that held everything together even as it all unraveled.

“It doesn’t have to be the last,” Anna said, trying to believe it.

“But it sort of is,” Mia answered, voice very soft.

Anna considered the horizon. Change had always seemed like a distant storm, something to be feared and avoided. Now it was happening piece by piece—each day subtly different, each loss accumulating until the world was unrecognizable. She hated the helplessness, the knowledge that so much depended on forces too vast to stop.

At twilight, the townsfolk gathered for a meeting. They sat in a semicircle beneath cloudless sky, every face carved with fatigue. Roy called for silence and spoke in a voice that echoed across the dusty lot.

“We have to ration more. Skip watering, skip anything non-essential. If the rains come, we buy another season; if not…”

His words trailed off. He didn’t finish the sentence. A silence followed, heavy and anxious. Anna noticed the old couple from the bakery holding hands, their grip white-knuckled. Children fidgeted in parents’ laps, bored but sensing trouble.

Esi stood. “I don’t want to just wait out the summer,” she said, her chin trembling. “We should prepare for next year. Try to store what we can, talk to the folks up north about a plan.”

Jonah replied, “My family tried. The panels aren’t enough for AC anymore, the generator costs more in fuel than we can earn.”

Roy raised his hands, palms up. “All of us have lost something. But we’re still here. For now, let’s do what we can.”

There were no more solutions that night, just resignation and a determination not to give up. Anna saw it in their faces, a dogged refusal to let go of their home, even as nature pulled the rug from beneath them.

Anna found herself keeping a journal. She wrote about the people, the water, the moments of beauty embedded in the chaos: the way the sun set magenta over the dying hills; the chorus of frogs that survived in the ponds choking with algae; the feeling of diving, just once more, into the chill, silty water that once seemed infinite.

She wrote, too, about hope—a battered kind, not the naive optimism of her childhood, but a stubborn resolve. Hope that maybe if they worked together, if they listened to scientists and neighbors, they could buy a little more time. Hope that the rain would come, even as clouds rolled past, indifferent.

As the season waned, Anna noticed a subtle shift. Volunteers from nearby towns arrived, sharing ideas for new irrigation channels, experimental crops, cooperative farming. A few outsiders brought supplies—seeds, water tanks, spare parts. The community became tighter, more determined.

On the last day of August, the sky finally cracked open. Rain fell in erratic sheets, splattering on parched ground, pooling on rooftops, sending shouts of surprise and relief through the town. Anna and Mia ran outside, letting the water soak them, laughing madly for the first time in months.

It wasn’t enough to refill the lake—not yet—but it was a reprieve. Enough to plant fall vegetables, enough to summon a tentative smile from Roy, who brandished the first cucumber in years as if it were gold.

That night, Anna returned to the rocks by the lake, toes sinking into new mud. There was no illusion that everything would return to normal; normal was gone. The world burned and thirsted and changed in ways that could not be reversed.

But in the middle of that loss, something survived—a capacity for adaptation, for resilience. Anna thought of generations before her who had weathered storms, wars, diseases. She could not control the climate, but she could choose how to respond.

She squeezed Mia’s hand, feeling the future like a shimmering mystery ahead. Not the future they’d once imagined, but one shaped by new realities and new forms of courage.

Anna closed her eyes, letting the cool breeze skate across her skin, and held onto the memory of this last summer—a summer not of endings, but of beginnings forged in the crucible of change.

In a world transformed by climate forces, Anna and her community learned to thrive not by looking backward, but by forging solidarity, adaptation, and hope from the patchwork remnants of what remained. The last summer burned into their memories, not as an elegy, but as a challenge—one that echoed into every year that followed.
