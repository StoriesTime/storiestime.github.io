Behind the Red Banner: Stories of the Cultural Revolution


The clamor of drums rolled down from the parade square, carrying with it a wind heavy with slogans and the metallic scent of anticipation. Chrysanthemum Alley, a meandering ribbon of crumbling gray brick and single-story courtyards, sat at the margin of the city’s fever—the banners barely reached its mud-soaked stones, yet the echoes of revolution pressed up against every door.

Lu Fen, fourteen, pressed her nose to the frosted glass, watching the crowd swirl beyond the avenue’s mouth. Her mother’s voice rose from the kitchen, muffled with urgency, “Close the curtains, Fen!” Fen obeyed, drawing the patterned cloth so the marchers would see nothing but shadow.

On the street, shouts punctuated by the sweep of arm-bands: “Down with the Four Olds!” “Long live the revolution!”
Fen listened, heart rattling inside her chest like a caged sparrow. The old words carved above their door—Virtue brings prosperity—had already been chiselled half-out by neighborhood zealots the week before.

Grandfather had not left his kang bed since then. Four decades a teacher, poems dancing on his tongue, he now lay staring at the patched ceiling, his breath rasping in the cold. The Party had called him “bourgeois,” then “reactionary.” The Red Guards spat as they ransacked his cupboard, unearthing his battered classical texts. Fen remembered how he begged, “Please, let the children keep that one, it’s just poems.” The leader, hardly older than Fen herself, had torn the book in half, quoting, “The past is poison!” The sliver of paper with Li Bai’s name still waited in Fen’s pocket.

School stopped existing as she knew it. Morning meant lining up to recite quotations; afternoons swirled with denunciations from students she once thought were friends. She watched them transform themselves, grudges blooming new purpose. Yinying, who wept when her puppy died, now stood on a makeshift platform, accusing a teacher of “bourgeois tendencies” for correcting her calligraphy. Mr. Xie stood silent, his face a blank wall.

The campaign infiltrated their home. Father returned less and less, blaming “work with the committee.” Sometimes Fen saw blood on his cuffs. Mother scrubbed at it, lips compressed into a thin line. “Don’t ask questions,” was the reply to everything these days.

At night, Fen listened through the window as whispers snaked down the alley—neighbors turning on neighbors, hunting for hidden “past poisons.” The old lady three doors down vanished overnight; her parlor, always fragrant with tea leaves and gossip, now featured only a red paper pasted across the door: “Class enemy.”

Fen wondered what had become of her. Sometimes she tried not to think at all.

It was March when all the students in the district were told to gather in the Square. Fen’s hands trembled as she threaded into the crowd, uniforms swelling with borrowed pride and concealed anxiety. A gust tugged at hand-painted placards: “Revolution is not a dinner party.” Echoes rebounded between the hard stone buildings. A knot of students encircled the stage.

She spotted Mr. Xie on the dais, his spectacles crooked, eyes rimmed with purple. His crime? “Speaking fondly of Confucius.”

As students paraded up to the microphone, shouting rehearsed denunciations, Fen felt herself wrenched in two. She remembered his patience, his joy in teaching, how he once hid a sparrow’s nest from the janitor so the chicks might survive. Now he only nodded, eyes shut, each accusation etching lines into his face. An organizer prodded Fen up the stage steps. She opened her mouth, the words catching like dry leaves in her throat.

That night, Fen lay awake, haunted by how her voice had trembled—a drop in a storm of indignation. She remembered her grandfather’s poems, the quiet lessons, the faces of friends gone blank with fear. Beside her, her mother sat mending clothes under the jittering bulb, hands moving insistently, as if time itself could be patched and smoothed.

Spring arrived, yanking magenta blossoms from trembling branches. The Red Guards returned, louder this time, raiding houses in midday brightness. The old temple at the alley’s end was desecrated, its statues toppled into the river; incense replaced by torn banners, “Superstition must be eradicated!”

One afternoon, Fen saw Grandfather in the garden, gazing at the fallen petals. “Do you think the chrysanthemum cares whose leader the wind follows?” he whispered, so only she could hear.

Without thinking, Fen pressed the sliver of poetry into his hand. For the first time in months, he smiled—a small and fractured thing, but real.

As the weeks coiled forward, rumors seeped through the alley: “Purges” in the countryside, students sent away for “re-education.” Some, like Yinying, left with fiery pride; others wept into their sleeves, terrified and ashamed.

Fen’s family shrank inward. Her father, gaunt and silent, quit coming home for dinner. Her mother began trading old possessions for broth. “All things pass,” her mother would murmur, as if comforting herself.

Inside, Fen grew a secret calendar. Every time she caught someone showing kindness—a neighbor slipping bread through a window, a Red Guard looking away from an old woman—she marked a dot on the paper, her private tally of surviving gentleness.

The loudspeakers blared ceaselessly, broadcasting reports of national progress. Yet the city shrunk, contractions of suspicion pulling at the seams. In the summer, Fen watched from the shadows as the Red Guards finally moved on, leaving the alley scraped raw and hollow.

Several teachers, once paraded with dunce-caps, disappeared. No explanations. One evening, Fen found Yinying sitting on the curb, shoes coated in dust. Her armband was gone.

Yinying turned, eyes hollow. “I thought we were making something new. But I keep waking up from nightmares.” She reached blindly for Fen’s hand. For a long time, they sat in silence.

Years limped past, bruising the alley, even as the city outside rebuilt, layer by layer. The political slogans faded to dull memories, red banners fraying to shreds. Fen’s family endured. Grandfather never taught again, but his poetry resurfaced; sometimes, over tea, he recited quietly for the children of the alley’s survivors.

Yinying apprenticed as a seamstress by the docks, hands learning gentleness. Fen found herself tending the battered garden plot, coaxing life from stony soil. The chrysanthemums bloomed each autumn, defiant and gold, their existence a stubborn stamp of resistance against erasure.

The revolution’s storm receded, leaving silence and questions. In Chrysanthemum Alley, what was old and new mingled, neither destroyed nor triumphant. In the living rooms and kitchens, at dusk, remembrances surfaced—not just of terror and suspicion, but of kindness in corners, rebellion in small acts, survival against the machinery of ideology.

Fen hung the faded sliver of poetry above her door, a quiet promise: that even beneath the banner-red glare, stories could persist, fragile and unbroken, sunlit in the alley’s uncertain dawn.
