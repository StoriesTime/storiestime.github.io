The Forgotten Files


The rain pattered softly against the cracked window of Ethan Grainger’s dusty office. He sat quietly, the flickering light of his desk lamp casting shadows across the walls. Stacks of manila folders were piled precariously high atop his cluttered desk, each labeled with faded, smudged writing. These were The Forgotten Files—a collection of cold cases and unsolved mysteries that had haunted his department for years. Most officers had dismissed them as lost causes, but Ethan couldn’t let go. There was something about staring into secrets buried by time that filled him with a peculiar determination.

Tonight, one file lay open before him.

A Stray Lead

The case was titled “The Halloway Incident,” dated November 2004. It involved a missing woman named Lily Halloway, who vanished under bizarre circumstances. She had been last seen leaving a holiday party in downtown Mercer, walking toward her apartment. Witnesses described her as cheerful, nothing amiss. Yet she vanished somewhere between the golden glow of streetlights and the misty shroud of fog rolling off the hills.

The police had briefly investigated. Friends and acquaintances were interviewed; there was no foul play, no sign of a struggle. Lily Halloway had vanished like a whisper, and the file was filed away—forgotten by everyone, except Ethan.

His attention lingered on the final witness statement in the folder: a note from a bartender who had served Lily that night. “She seemed uneasy,” the statement read, “She kept glancing at the guy in the corner booth. Nobody else seemed to notice him, but she did. And then, just before she left, she whispered something under her breath—something like, ‘Not again.’ I don’t know what that meant.”

Ethan scribbled notes. The case had reopened, if only in his head. The Forgotten Files weren’t just names with endings to avoid; they were riddles begging for resolution.

A Deepening Mystery

Hours into his research, Ethan discovered several similar disappearances cataloged across nearby counties, each with eerie parallels. Women, most young, some alone on the streets, others vanishing from their cars. The circumstances varied, but one common thread stood out: many of these names had ties to Mercer—employees, visitors, or residents.

One particular case caught his attention. A musician named Claire Redding disappeared in 2011 while touring the region. Though her case hadn’t been connected to Lily Halloway, Ethan found himself pouring over her reports as though reading a tragic duet, the same eerie refrain weaving its way through. The final notation in Claire’s file sent a chill crawling up his spine: “Witness spotted her pacing near Central Park under the lampposts. She seemed distracted, repeating the words ‘not him’ several times.”

He stretched his aching back, scanning for a connection. “Not again. Not him.” The words gnawed at him. Even the late hour couldn’t deter him; the thrill of following a trail kept him brimming with relentless curiosity. He sent a few emails to old colleagues still working dispatch and pulled up archived photos from city surveillance cameras.

A Stranger in the Fog

Weeks passed, but Ethan’s pursuit remained steadfast. Slowly, pieces began to form a fragmented puzzle. His persistence led him to a recurring figure—a man often on the periphery of these disappearances. He appeared in grainy footage near Lily Halloway’s last known location, visible in the background of stoplight camera feeds. Footage near Claire Redding’s hotel also showed fleeting glimpses of him: always in a hooded jacket, his face obscured.

Ethan began calling the figure “The Stranger.” His coworkers thought he was chasing ghosts; even his boss grumbled about wasting time on cases that had gone ice-cold. “If you don’t drop this obsession, Grainger, I’ll send you packing,” his boss threatened.

But Ethan couldn’t stop. He fervently studied every detail he had. The Stranger moved with purpose but always seemed out of focus, his presence fleeting. Ethan felt as though he was trying to grasp smoke—an impossible task. Still, the trail led him closer to Mercer’s abandoned industrial lot, a scattered sprawl of warehouses shut down decades ago. One structure in particular stood out for its repeating appearance in local files—a cement factory shrouded in rust and vines.

Confronting the Forgotten

It was past midnight when Ethan made his way into the old factory, the atmosphere heavy with an odd quiet. A flashlight in one hand, his other rested lightly on the grip of his handgun. As he stepped over crumbling pavement, the air seemed thinner, colder, and wet with a whisper of rain blowing through shattered windows.

Dust coated the remnants of machinery and stacks of forgotten crates. Ethan scanned the area carefully, his gaze sharp against the cloak of darkness. He could hear faint noises—perhaps shifting metal or the wind coursing through narrow gaps.

Suddenly, he froze. Footsteps.

They were slow, measured. His heart thudded as he turned toward the sound. In the distance, he saw the figure. The Stranger emerged partially into his flashlight beam, the hood down and revealing a thin, hollow-cheeked face marked with crooked scars. The man’s expression was devoid of emotion, yet the depth of his gaze carried a weight Ethan could physically feel.

The Stranger didn’t speak, didn’t flinch, didn’t run.

Ethan stepped forward cautiously, speaking. “You’ve been lurking in the shadows for too long. If you’ve done anything—if you’ve taken anyone—you’re going away tonight.”

The Stranger tilted his head, his lips twitching as though forming the bare beginnings of a grin. “I suppose you’ve come to play hero. But… what happens when the hero becomes forgotten, like all the rest?”

The Vanishing Truth

Ethan reached for his radio, but the static buzzed louder than usual. Confused, startled, he backed toward the entrance—but the atmosphere began twisting. Whispers rippled through unseen walls; memories from the cases he’d studied seemed to reverberate in the echoing darkness. Not again. Not him.

The ground seemed to shift beneath him, blurring reality. Ethan made a dash, his flashlight trembling in his grip. He reached that broken threshold in desperate motion—but something unexplainable dragged the world inward. The cement factory collapsed around his awareness, folding into an unnatural nothingness.

When Mercer police stumbled onto the site after days of searching, Ethan Grainger’s desk sat cleared and his apartment vacated—not a trace of him existed beyond files scattered loosely, haphazard like forgotten thoughts. He—like Lily Halloway and countless others—had joined unspoken legend.

And The Stranger returned to the fog.
