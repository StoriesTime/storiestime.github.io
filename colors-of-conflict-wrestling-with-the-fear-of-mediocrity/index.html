Colors of Conflict: Wrestling with the Fear of Mediocrity


The wind swept through the bustling city streets, carrying with it the hum of life—a mosaic of voices, footsteps, and distant melodies. Amid the noise and chaos, Maren felt herself slipping away from the vibrant hues of existence and into a gray void. It wasn’t that her life was lacking in action or that her dreams were hollow; rather, it was the gnawing fear that she’d become an indistinct smudge on humanity’s canvas—a person who tried, but never shined.

Maren had been drawn to color since childhood, fascinated not just by its aesthetic but by its emotional resonance. Reds always struck her as passionate and volatile, greens conjured calm and balance, and deep blues evoked longing and mystery. In her spare room, she’d created a sanctuary filled with paints, pastels, and canvases. It was her place of hope—an escape from the mundane grind of her accounting job, where numbers drained the color from her soul—her attempt to wrestle with mediocrity one stroke at a time.

Her latest painting began as a bold conceptual piece. It was meant to symbolize conflict, a clash between angst and aspiration. She envisioned swirls of fiery orange tangling with somber grays, vibrant yellows battling deep indigos—a storm of color that reflected the turmoil within her. Yet as the work progressed, something felt missing. The canvas wasn’t alive; it was merely paint smeared into aesthetic cohesion. Mediocrity stared back at her from the surface, its mockery turning her stomach.

One drizzly evening, Maren found herself sitting in her tiny studio, surrounded by jars of paint that seemed to mock her indecision. She gazed at her unfinished artwork, its lifelessness fueling the doubt in her chest. If this failed, what was left? What if she was destined to be just another shadow in a city full of flames—unnoticed, unremarkable, quietly extinguished?

A knock at the door jolted her out of her spiraling reverie. Maren wasn’t expecting visitors, particularly not tonight when she craved solitude. With hesitation, she opened the door to find her neighbor, Vivienne—an effervescent woman whose career as a dancer seemed drenched in success. Vivienne’s life was a symphony of color; her performances drew crowds, her laughter echoed in rooms, and even her clothing seemed to shimmer with vibrancy.

“I brought you this,” Vivienne said cheerily, handing over a small, battered box. “Think of it as inspiration.”

Maren blinked, surprised. “What is it?”

Vivienne smiled conspiratorially. “Open it and see.”

Once Vivienne left, Maren carefully peeled back the tape that sealed the box. Inside was an assortment of objects—a bundle of dried flowers, faded photographs of people she didn’t recognize, scraps of fabric dyed with rich pigments, and a small book titled The Colors of Conflict. At first glance, the collection seemed random, but as Maren sifted through the contents, she noticed something about the photos and fabrics. These items belonged to people who clearly didn’t live ordinary lives. They mourned boldly, dreamed outrageously, and loved without fear. Their worlds had color and texture—not because of their possessions, but because of how they recklessly embraced their humanity.

The book was an anthology of stories from artists, activists, and dreamers wrestling their own fears, limitations, and uncertainties. They weren’t perfect, but their flaws were what made their narratives worth reading. It wasn’t just their achievements that gave their lives vibrancy—it was their struggles, the intimate wrestling with doubt and dread that created the sparks they spread to the world. The weight in Maren’s chest shifted slightly as she delved through the text, notes scribbled in the margins guiding her through others’ battles.

The next morning, something inside Maren felt raw and alive, as if the storm inside her had finally found a path to roar. She pulled her painting off the easel and sat on the floor with it, staring at the swirls of muted colors, her heart hammering. She reached for her brush with trembling fingers but didn’t pick up paint this time. Instead, she grabbed the dried flowers from Vivienne’s gift and pressed them into the painting’s surface. At first, the act felt futile—what difference would it make? But as she stepped back, layering the flowers and fabric scraps across the canvas, she noticed an evolution. It wasn’t just color anymore—it was texture, dimension, and chaos. It screamed. It sang.

For hours, Maren worked feverishly, tugging at her hair and pressing her palms onto the canvas. She added shards of dried leaves, glittering fragments of broken beads, and even slashes across the paint that exposed the raw canvas underneath. Her heart raced as she realized this wasn’t just about aesthetic anymore. This was a story—a portrait of her wrestle with mediocrity, her defiance against living as a “gray.”

When she finally stepped back, her chest burned and her body quivered. What she had created wasn’t conventionally beautiful. It was unsettling, even controversial. But it wasn’t mediocre. And it was unmistakably hers.

Word spread quickly in her social circle about the piece, which she titled Collision. Some loved it passionately, while others muttered about its chaos and lack of polish. Vivienne declared it a masterpiece, insisting it belonged in a gallery. Maren thought about her words carefully. She wasn’t sure if it belonged anywhere, but she knew at least one thing for certain: it didn’t belong in the back of her closet.

At a small art exhibition two weeks later, Maren stood beside her painting with a glass of water, watching strangers approach. Some tilted their heads thoughtfully, others frowned, and one even stormed off, muttering about “pretentious modern art.” Maren smiled softly, realizing she wasn’t afraid anymore—afraid of rejection, of imperfection, of missing greatness. What mattered wasn’t the approval of crowds but the honesty of the creation itself.

Then came an older woman dressed in vibrant layers—scarlet scarf, cobalt jacket, emerald boots. She stood silently before Collision for a long time before turning to Maren. “This has a pulse,” she finally said. “You may not know it yet, but you’ve done something transformative.”

Maren felt her face flush as gratitude and disbelief battled in her chest. “Thank you,” she said quietly, the words trembling but sincere.

As she walked home that evening, the city’s colors seemed sharper, more pronounced—the peachy hues of faded graffiti, the stormy grays of rain puddles, the golden glow of streetlights. She realized it wasn’t that the world had changed; it was that she had finally learned to see it. The fear of mediocrity hadn’t vanished entirely, but it no longer dictated her every move. Instead of seeking greatness, Maren had found something deeper: the courage to embrace conflict, to wrestle mightily with the shadows lurking in her own soul, and to paint her story no matter how messy the palette might become.
