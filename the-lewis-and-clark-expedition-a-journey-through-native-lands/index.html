The Lewis and Clark Expedition: A Journey through Native Lands


The year was 1803, and President Thomas Jefferson had a grand vision for the new United States of America. He wanted to expand the nation’s borders and gain a deeper understanding of the vast territories that lay to the west. To accomplish this, he turned to two of his trusted explorers, Meriwether Lewis and William Clark. These two men, along with their crew of skilled frontiersmen, embarked on an adventure that would change the course of American history: the Lewis and Clark Expedition.

The Journey Begins

Lewis and Clark assembled a group of thirty-one brave men, each with unique skills and backgrounds. The Corps of Discovery, as they came to be known, set out from St. Louis, Missouri, on May 14, 1804. Their objective was to find a practical and navigable route from the Missouri River to the Pacific Ocean, and to document the geography, flora, and fauna of the newly acquired Louisiana Purchase territory.

The men traveled up the Missouri River, navigating treacherous rapids and portsaging their boats around dangerous stretches of water. Along the way, they encountered the first of many native tribes, the Mandan Indians. The Lewis and Clark party made a mutual alliance with the Mandans, who proved to be invaluable allies in navigating the complex web of tribal rivalries and alliances that made up the Great Plains.

The Expedition Meets the Native Lands

As the journey continued, the group encountered a wide variety of native tribes, each with their own customs and languages. Lewis and Clark made a point of showing respect and building alliances with these peoples. They did so by adhering to unwritten codes of conduct among Native communities. For instance, exchange of gifts or gestures of goodwill served to create trust bonds between strangers from different cultures.

The expedition reached a major milestone when they met the Shoshone tribe, under the leadership of the powwow, Sacagawea. Through her guidance and skills as an explorer, the Corps of Discovery successfully navigated the treacherous Snake River and the Rocky Mountains. Sacagawea’s knowledge of the region proved invaluable, and she ultimately played a crucial role in the success of the expedition.

A Journey of Discovery

As they journeyed westward, Lewis and Clark kept detailed records of their observations. They documented the plant and animal life, geological formations, and Native tribes they encountered. The collection of data and discovery of new species started an era of exploration and scientific advancement. The observations made by the Corps of Discovery would pave the way for future generations of explorers, naturalists, and scientists.

In November 1805, the Lewis and Clark party reached the Pacific Ocean. Their journey was far from over, though. With their objective achieved, the explorers turned back eastward, intent on returning to St. Louis and delivering their findings to President Jefferson.

A New Era

Upon their return, Lewis and Clark presented their findings, including maps, detailed journals, and plant and animal specimens, to the President. Jefferson’s goal of expanding the young nation’s knowledge of the newly acquired western territory had been accomplished.

The Lewis and Clark Expedition opened up new territories and deepened understanding of native lands. The information gathered during the journey allowed for the mysterious West to be tamed into the familiar landscape of the United States. The Corps of Discovery was an exceptional group of men, who left an indelible mark on the history of the nation. They truly bridged the gap between the known world and the wild frontiers of the new American West.
