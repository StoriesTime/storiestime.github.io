Silent Struggles: Realistic Stories on Racism and Prejudice


It was the third time this week that Maya had been passed over for a project. She sat quietly at her desk, staring at the blinking cursor on her computer screen, pretending to be busy. She could hear her coworkers laughing and talking in the next cubicle, but she didn’t join them. Maya had learned early on that to fit in here, she had to keep her distance. Her colleagues, mostly white and male, never seemed to notice her presence unless they needed something from her. But that was fine—she had long stopped expecting to be part of their group.

Maya had been with the company for almost two years. She was bright, capable, and always went above and beyond to meet deadlines. Yet, every time a new project came up, her name was mysteriously left off the list. The first few months had been discouraging, but over time, Maya had convinced herself that it wasn’t personal. Maybe the others had more experience, or perhaps the projects just didn’t require her skillset.

But today, as she stared at the latest email announcement, she couldn’t ignore the pang of frustration that gripped her chest. Another important project. Another group of people. And once again, Maya’s name was nowhere to be found. This time, the email was signed by John, the department head.

“Why does it feel like I’m invisible?” Maya thought, pushing her chair back and standing up. She walked slowly to the break room, needing a moment to collect herself before she went back to her desk.

It wasn’t that she hadn’t tried to speak up. She had. During the first few months, she’d done her best to advocate for herself, offering suggestions during meetings, volunteering for extra tasks, and always showing her worth. But no one ever seemed to notice. She often wondered if it was her accent, the way she wore her hair, or the fact that she was one of the few Black women in the department. It was hard not to notice how the others seemed to connect in ways that she could never quite manage.

“Morning, Maya,” a voice broke through her thoughts. It was Claire, the receptionist. Claire was one of the few people who always made an effort to chat with Maya.

“Hey, Claire,” Maya said, forcing a smile as she grabbed a cup of coffee.

“You okay?” Claire asked, her eyes scanning Maya’s face.

“Yeah, I’m good,” Maya replied, her voice more even than she felt.

Claire looked unconvinced. She knew Maya well enough to see through her attempts at masking her feelings.

“Don’t let it get to you, okay?” Claire said softly, her hand resting on Maya’s shoulder. “You’re smarter than all of them, and you know it.”

Maya swallowed the lump in her throat. It was nice to hear, but she had stopped believing it herself a long time ago. Claire didn’t know what it was like to be the only one in a room where everyone else seemed to be cut from the same mold. To be the person who always had to prove herself twice as hard, who had to work harder to earn the same respect as her colleagues. To be the person whose skin color, accent, and even her name sometimes made her a target for prejudice, whether overt or subtle.

A few weeks later, Maya was summoned to John’s office. Her heart sank as she walked down the hallway. She had a sinking feeling this meeting wasn’t going to be good. John was always polite but distant. His style of leadership never felt inclusive, always treating people as if they were interchangeable parts in a machine.

“Good afternoon, Maya,” John greeted her as she entered the room. His smile was tight, professional, the kind of smile she had grown used to.

“Good afternoon, John,” Maya replied, trying to keep her voice steady.

“Please, have a seat,” he gestured to the chair across from him. “I’ve been reviewing the team assignments for the upcoming project, and I wanted to talk to you about your involvement.”

Maya sat down, her palms slightly damp. She had been anticipating this conversation for a while. Every time a new project came up, she’d find herself hoping for an opportunity, but that hope always faded.

“Maya, I’ve noticed that you’ve been doing well on your individual tasks, but I think there are still areas where you need to improve before we put you on something like this,” John began, his tone professional but detached.

Maya’s breath caught. “What areas?” she asked, trying to keep her voice neutral. She had been waiting for this moment to understand what she could do differently, to hear the feedback that might help her get a seat at the table.

John shifted in his chair. “Well, it’s not necessarily about your skills, Maya. It’s more about your presence. You don’t always speak up during meetings, and sometimes it feels like you’re a little… distant from the team. We need someone who’s going to be more… engaged, you know? Someone who can really take charge and lead.”

Maya nodded slowly, but inside, she felt a sharp pang of anger. She had been in enough meetings to know that speaking up had never been the issue. Her ideas had always been met with either silence or a quick dismissal. But when her white male colleagues offered the same suggestions, they were praised as “innovative” or “out of the box.”

“I understand,” Maya said, trying to keep the bitterness out of her voice. “I’ll work on it.”

But as she left John’s office, she couldn’t shake the feeling that this was just another way of pushing her back. Another way of making her feel like she wasn’t enough, no matter how hard she tried.

Over the next few weeks, Maya tried harder. She spoke up more in meetings, offered suggestions, and made an effort to participate in group activities. But it didn’t take long for her to realize that nothing had changed. Her colleagues still overlooked her, and her ideas still went unnoticed. The silence of exclusion was deafening, and it felt like the weight of every microaggression, every snide remark, and every dismissive glance was pressing down on her shoulders.

Maya was exhausted. She had always prided herself on being resilient, on being able to handle challenges with grace. But the emotional toll was starting to break her. She wanted to leave, to find a place where she could truly thrive, but the thought of starting over somewhere else felt just as exhausting. She had already spent so much of her energy proving herself.

One evening, after a particularly tough day at work, Maya sat in her apartment, scrolling through social media. She came across an article about a woman who had left her corporate job after facing similar struggles. The woman had written about the constant burden of proving herself, the exhaustion of navigating a system that seemed built to keep her out, and the toll it had taken on her mental health.

Maya paused as she read the words. It was as if the woman had written exactly what Maya had been feeling for months, for years. The article didn’t offer easy solutions, but it gave her a sense of validation. Maya wasn’t alone in this fight. There were others who had felt the same pain, who had struggled in silence, and who had eventually found their own paths to healing.

Maya closed her laptop and leaned back in her chair. She knew the journey wouldn’t be easy, and she didn’t have all the answers. But she wasn’t willing to let the silence define her anymore. She had fought too hard to be here, and she wasn’t going to let anyone make her feel small.

Tomorrow, she would start fresh. She would no longer wait for permission to be seen. She would take her space, her voice, and her place at the table, whether the world was ready for it or not. The struggle for recognition and equality was far from over, but Maya was ready to face it head-on.

Weeks turned into months, and slowly, things began to change. Maya didn’t change herself; she simply stopped hiding. She became more vocal in meetings, more confident in her ideas, and more intentional in building connections with others who understood her struggles. She found allies, people who saw her for her talent and not just the color of her skin or her gender.

It wasn’t easy, and it wasn’t fast, but Maya found strength in her resilience. She learned to navigate the subtle and not-so-subtle racism and prejudice that had been a constant presence in her life. Some days were harder than others, but Maya knew that the silent struggles were a part of her journey. And while they would never define her, they had shaped her into someone stronger than she had ever imagined.

In the end, Maya didn’t just fight for a seat at the table. She built her own table, one where her voice could be heard, her contributions valued, and her presence respected.
