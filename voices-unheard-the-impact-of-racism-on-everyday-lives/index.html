Voices Unheard: The Impact of Racism on Everyday Lives


Maria had always been aware of the little things—the subtle moments where she felt different, the slight shifts in the way people interacted with her. They were nothing dramatic, nothing she could point to directly, but she had learned to recognize them like an unspoken language. Growing up in a predominantly white town, Maria’s experience was colored by the fact that she wasn’t like everyone else.

Every morning when she walked through the front door of her school, there was a shift in the air—a slight pause, a lingering look as she passed by the hallway. It wasn’t obvious. It wasn’t the glaring stares or the outright dismissals, but the little things that piled up day after day. The way her classmates seemed surprised when she spoke with confidence, the way they seemed to find it hard to pronounce her name as though it were something strange. It was as if she was a riddle to them—something they couldn’t quite understand, so they ignored it.

One of her teachers, Mrs. Carter, had a habit of mispronouncing her name repeatedly. It wasn’t an accident anymore. Maria had been in her class for months, and every time Mrs. Carter called on her, the name was twisted into something barely recognizable. Other students seemed to notice too, chuckling under their breath, exchanging looks. Maria never corrected her because it wasn’t worth the fight. She had grown tired of explaining her heritage, of defending the very essence of who she was. In those moments, the weight of the world seemed to rest squarely on her shoulders.

At lunch, she sat with a small group of friends—some Latino, others Black—but they all shared similar experiences of feeling out of place in the sea of whiteness that surrounded them. They had learned how to cope with the sideways glances, the dismissive gestures, and the quiet exclusion. In a town where diversity was rare, they were outsiders. They were always the ones who were different.

But Maria had come to understand that racism wasn’t always loud or obvious. It wasn’t always in the form of slurs or hate speech. No, it was much more insidious than that. It was in the way people avoided eye contact when they passed you in the street, in the way people held their bags a little tighter when you stood next to them on the bus. It was in the jokes they made about your culture that didn’t feel like jokes at all, in the way they smiled and said “You’re one of the good ones,” without realizing the weight of their words. It was the constant, unspoken reminder that you didn’t truly belong.

Chapter 2: The Burden of Silence

It wasn’t just Maria. There were others like her in every part of town, and they all felt the same strain, the same burden. Daniel, a tall, quiet Black teenager, had grown used to the suspicious looks he got when he walked into a store. The owner, a middle-aged man who had owned the local convenience store for years, always greeted him with a tight smile, his eyes flicking warily over Daniel as if he might be a thief.

It was the kind of subtle racism that was easy to dismiss if you weren’t the one on the receiving end. It wasn’t enough for Daniel to say something to anyone. It wasn’t overt enough to justify a confrontation, yet it gnawed at him every time he walked into the store. He knew the owner didn’t treat his white customers the same way. He had seen it before—how the man’s face would brighten when a white customer walked in, how he would offer to help them with a smile.

Daniel learned to ignore the discomfort. He didn’t have the energy to explain it to anyone who wouldn’t understand. Besides, his friends got it. They all understood. They had each experienced their own version of racism, from the careless comments to the unfair treatment. They had become experts in navigating the invisible barriers that separated them from the world around them. But in those quiet moments, when he walked through the aisles of the store, Daniel couldn’t help but feel the weight of every glance, every gesture. It was as if he wasn’t meant to exist in those spaces.

His friends had their own stories. Nina, a light-skinned Latina, told stories of being mistaken for a servant at parties or events, simply because of the color of her skin. It happened often enough for her to stop counting. She had learned to smile through it, to play the role of the “good Latina” that people expected her to be. When she was with her white friends, she was seen as the token “ethnic” one—diverse enough to seem interesting, but not too different to raise suspicions.

And then there was Malik, whose experience was entirely shaped by his Blackness. Malik’s family had lived in the same neighborhood for years, but still, he was seen as an outsider. He was used to being followed in stores, to hearing the slight change in tone when people asked where he was “really” from. It wasn’t that they meant to hurt him, but their ignorance stung just the same.

They were all tired, worn down by the everyday racism they had to face. It wasn’t the big, dramatic moments that left the scars. It was the quiet, relentless accumulation of small offenses, the constant weight of having to prove you were worthy of being seen.

Chapter 3: The Burden of the Unseen

It wasn’t just the younger generation that carried this burden. Maria’s mother, Laura, had lived through decades of racism, her experiences building up in layers until they became part of who she was. Laura was a teacher at the local elementary school, and though she loved her job, she couldn’t ignore the ways in which her race shaped her interactions with students and their parents.

One of her students, Rachel, a shy, blonde girl, always arrived late for class, her backpack disheveled. Laura had often found herself staying after school to help Rachel catch up, offering encouragement when others had written her off as just another “troubled” kid. But Rachel’s mother, Mrs. Foster, didn’t seem to appreciate Laura’s efforts. Instead, she would often stand at the door, giving Laura a look of suspicion, as if to say, “Why are you spending so much time with my daughter?”

Laura could feel the unspoken judgment in the air. Mrs. Foster’s eyes would flicker over her, always lingering a little too long. There were times when Laura would hear the faint whispers of other parents, murmuring about how the “immigrants” in the school were “taking up space” and “distracting the teachers from their real work.”

It wasn’t overt. It wasn’t a shout, but a whisper, just below the surface. But it had a way of settling in your bones, of making you question your own worth. Laura had spent years trying to make peace with the subtle racism that followed her like a shadow. She had worked hard to rise above it, to be better than the whispers and glances. But there were days when she simply couldn’t shake the feeling that no matter what she achieved, it would never be enough.

Chapter 4: The Power of Words

One day, Maria had had enough. It wasn’t just Mrs. Carter mispronouncing her name or the sideways glances. It was everything. The constant feeling of being an outsider in her own community, of being treated like an afterthought. She was tired of it.

In the middle of class, as Mrs. Carter called on her once again with the wrong name, Maria stood up. The room fell silent as she took a deep breath, the weight of every moment finally catching up with her.

“My name is Maria,” she said, her voice steady but loud enough for the entire class to hear. “It’s not Maria-huh, or Maria-uh. It’s just Maria. I’ve been in this class for months, and you’ve called me everything but my name. So I’m going to say it one more time, just so we’re clear.”

The room was still. Mrs. Carter blinked, surprised. Maria could feel the eyes of her classmates on her. She wasn’t sure what would happen next, but for the first time in a long time, she felt seen.

Mrs. Carter apologized, but the damage had been done. It wasn’t just about the name. It was about the constant erasure of who she was. It was about being seen as less than, invisible in a world that demanded conformity.

Later that day, Daniel stopped by the convenience store. For the first time in a long while, the owner greeted him with a casual smile and a nod. It wasn’t perfect, but it was a small step. A shift in the way things had always been. And that small shift mattered.

Chapter 5: Moving Forward

The reality of racism didn’t vanish for Maria, Daniel, Nina, Malik, or Laura. It wasn’t something that could be fixed in a single conversation or in one act of defiance. But every day, in little ways, they began to speak up. They began to hold their heads a little higher, to demand that their voices be heard, even in a world that tried so hard to silence them.

Racism was a weight, yes, but it didn’t define them. Their stories, their experiences, their voices were just as important as anyone else’s. And though the world around them was often indifferent, they would no longer be silenced.

In the end, it was the quiet strength of the unheard voices that had the power to change everything.
