The Ethics Test: Short Fiction on Morality Under Pressure


Jacob sat alone in the sterile, dimly lit room, his fingers twitching nervously as he glanced at the clock on the wall. It had been an hour since he’d been escorted in here, and the silence was starting to feel oppressive. The only sound was the soft hum of the air conditioning and the faint ticking of the clock’s second hand.

He had heard the rumors about the Ethics Test before—whispers from colleagues, stories from strangers. It was said that this test was unlike any other, a final trial of moral judgment, where every decision would shape the course of a person’s future. Jacob had volunteered for the test, although in hindsight, he wasn’t sure why. He had always been proud of his ethical standards, his firm belief in doing the right thing, no matter the cost. But now, as the seconds ticked away, he couldn’t shake the growing unease in his stomach.

The door opened with a soft click, and a woman in a gray suit entered. She was in her mid-thirties, with sharp features and an air of calm authority. Without speaking, she took a seat across from him and placed a thin, black folder on the table.

“Jacob, correct?” she asked, her voice smooth and precise.

“Yes,” he replied, his voice cracking slightly.

“Good. Let’s begin.” She opened the folder, pulling out a sheet of paper and sliding it toward him. “The Ethics Test is designed to measure your ability to make decisions under pressure. It’s not about right or wrong, per se, but about your moral compass and how you navigate the complexities of human choices. Are you ready?”

Jacob nodded, his heart beating a little faster.

“Great,” she said, her eyes flickering to the paper in front of him. “Here is your first scenario.”

Scenario One: The Trolley Problem

Jacob read the words on the page, his mind immediately grasping the familiar dilemma. It was the classic trolley problem.

A trolley is speeding down a track, and ahead, it will crash into five people who are tied to the rails. You are standing next to a lever that can divert the trolley to another track, where it will kill one person instead. Do you pull the lever?

Jacob’s breath caught in his throat. He had studied philosophy in college, and he was well-versed in ethical theories. Utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics—he had analyzed the trolley problem from every angle in his academic days. But now, sitting here with the question before him, it felt different. He wasn’t in a classroom anymore, discussing abstract theories with classmates. This was real. And the stakes felt much higher.

The woman across from him was watching him closely, waiting for his response.

“Pull the lever,” Jacob said, the words leaving his mouth almost instinctively. He had always considered himself a utilitarian—believing that the right action was the one that produced the greatest good for the greatest number.

“Interesting,” the woman said, her voice neutral. “Let’s move on.”

Scenario Two: The Lifeboat

The next scenario was more personal, more visceral.

You are on a lifeboat with five other people after a shipwreck. There is limited food and water, and the boat is sinking. One of the people on the boat is gravely injured and is slowing the boat’s escape. If the person is left behind, the rest of you can survive. If you keep them, the boat will surely sink, and everyone will perish. Do you throw the injured person overboard?

Jacob’s stomach twisted. This wasn’t just a moral question—it was a question of survival. The emotional weight of the scenario was heavier than he had anticipated. Could he really condemn someone to death for the sake of his own survival? But then again, could he allow everyone to die?

He felt a surge of panic and then a cold clarity. There was no easy answer here. But he had to choose.

“I would… throw the injured person overboard,” Jacob said reluctantly. He could feel his heart pounding in his chest as he said it out loud. “It’s the only way for the rest of us to survive.”

The woman nodded, jotting something down on her notepad. “Thank you. Let’s continue.”

Scenario Three: The Charity Donation

The third scenario was deceptively simple, yet it posed a deep ethical dilemma.

You are walking down the street when you see a homeless person holding a sign asking for food. You have $50 in your wallet, but you need that money for an upcoming rent payment. Do you give the homeless person your money, or do you keep it for yourself?

Jacob paused, his mind racing. This scenario wasn’t about life and death. It wasn’t about survival. It was about empathy, generosity, and the tug between personal responsibility and social responsibility.

The decision should have been easy. After all, he had always considered himself charitable, a person who believed in helping others when he could. But the $50 wasn’t just spare change. It was money he had carefully set aside for his rent. Without it, he could risk losing his apartment.

“I… I would give the money,” Jacob said, though his voice faltered. “It’s the right thing to do.”

The woman’s gaze softened, but she said nothing. She simply wrote something down.

Scenario Four: The Whistleblower

This scenario was different from the others. It wasn’t about life or death. It was about integrity, loyalty, and the consequences of truth.

You work for a large corporation, and you have discovered that the company is illegally dumping toxic waste into a nearby river. The company has promised you a significant promotion if you stay quiet about it, but your conscience is bothering you. If you report them, you risk losing your job, and the company could retaliate by ruining your career. Do you blow the whistle, or do you keep quiet and secure your future?

Jacob felt a familiar knot in his stomach. This was the kind of situation that he had always feared—where doing the right thing came with personal sacrifice.

He thought of his family, his career, and the future he had worked so hard to build. But he also thought of the people living near the river, the children who swam in the water, the future generations who would inherit the consequences of the company’s actions. Was he willing to risk everything for the sake of doing what was right?

“I would report them,” Jacob said after a long pause. “I couldn’t live with myself if I didn’t.”

The woman’s expression remained neutral, but there was something in her eyes—something that seemed to acknowledge the gravity of his answer.

Scenario Five: The Kidnapper’s Demand

This final scenario was the most intense, the most harrowing of them all.

You receive a phone call from a kidnapper who has taken your child. The kidnapper demands a large sum of money in exchange for your child’s release. However, you don’t have enough money to meet the demand. If you don’t pay, your child will die. If you do, you risk your own life savings and potentially the financial security of your entire family. Do you pay the ransom, or do you refuse and risk your child’s life?

Jacob’s hand shook as he read the scenario. His chest tightened, and a wave of nausea hit him. The thought of losing his child, of having to choose between financial ruin and his child’s life, was unbearable. But what kind of person would he be if he didn’t do everything in his power to save them?

He couldn’t—he couldn’t let his child die.

“I would pay the ransom,” Jacob said, his voice hoarse. “No matter the cost, I would pay it.”

The woman looked up from her notes, her face unreadable. “Thank you, Jacob. That’s all the scenarios for today.”

Jacob sat back, his body tense, his mind racing. He had answered the questions quickly, without hesitation, but now, in the silence that followed, the weight of the decisions hung over him.

The woman closed the folder and stood. “You’ve completed the Ethics Test. The results will be reviewed, and you will be contacted with your outcome.” She paused for a moment, then added, “Remember, these scenarios were designed to challenge your moral judgment. The true test isn’t about making the right decision—it’s about how you respond when the pressure is on.”

Jacob nodded, but he felt a chill crawl up his spine. He didn’t know if he had made the right choices, but in that moment, he realized something deeper. The test wasn’t just about the scenarios—it was about who he was when faced with moral pressure. It was about understanding the complexities of human nature, and how the smallest choices could ripple out to shape the course of a life.

As the door closed behind him, Jacob felt the weight of the test settle into his bones. It wasn’t over yet. The real challenge was just beginning.

The woman watched Jacob leave, her gaze fixed on the door as the silence returned. She had seen many people take the Ethics Test, but Jacob’s responses were particularly intriguing. She scribbled a few notes in her folder, already thinking ahead to the final review.

The true purpose of the Ethics Test was not just to evaluate one’s morality—it was to uncover the hidden truths about the choices people made under pressure. And Jacob had passed, just as many others had before him. But the real question lingered: Was the test truly over for him, or was this only the beginning?

As she closed her folder, she smiled, knowing full well that some of the toughest decisions were yet to come.
