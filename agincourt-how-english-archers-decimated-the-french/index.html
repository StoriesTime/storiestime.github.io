Agincourt: How English Archers Decimated the French


The morning fog hung thick over the battlefield. It was October 25, 1415, and the dense mist seemed to merge with the cold, damp earth beneath. On the rolling plains of northern France, just outside the village of Agincourt, two great armies were preparing to clash in what would become one of the most remarkable battles in medieval history. But as the sun began to rise, there was an eerie silence in the air — one that would soon be shattered by the thunderous sounds of war.

The English army, led by King Henry V, had marched through France for weeks, their morale high, driven by a desire to reclaim the French throne. The French, with their numerical superiority, were confident that this day would be theirs. Little did they know that it would be the English archers, positioned in the distance, who would change the course of the battle and secure one of the most unlikely victories in history.

The Prelude to Battle

The English forces were outnumbered. Historians estimate that Henry V’s army numbered around 6,000, while the French forces, under the command of the Constable of France, Charles d’Albret, were estimated to be between 12,000 to 36,000 strong. The French had a diverse army, consisting of heavily armored knights, men-at-arms, and foot soldiers. On the other hand, the English were a smaller, but more mobile force, primarily consisting of archers and a handful of knights.

The English had been on the march for several weeks, having laid siege to the French town of Harfleur, only to be forced to retreat in the face of illness and dwindling supplies. The march had taken its toll on the soldiers, and many were weary. They were now in hostile territory, surrounded by the French army. It seemed inevitable that they would be overrun, but King Henry, known for his determination and tactical brilliance, had other plans.

With no possibility of retreat and few options left, Henry decided to face the French army head-on at Agincourt. Despite the odds, he had one advantage that the French army had underestimated: the English longbowmen.

The English Advantage: The Longbow

The longbow had become a fearsome weapon in medieval warfare, and by the time of Agincourt, it had reached its peak of efficiency. Made of yew wood, the longbow could fire arrows with incredible speed and power. A skilled archer could release up to ten arrows per minute, each one capable of piercing through the armor of a knight. The English archers, who made up a large part of Henry’s army, had honed their skills over years of practice. Many of them had been raised on the farm, where they had trained with the bow since childhood. It was their weapon that would be decisive in the coming battle.

The French, on the other hand, relied heavily on their cavalry and heavily armored knights, who were accustomed to charging at the enemy in tightly packed formations. However, they had underestimated the devastating potential of the English archers, who could strike from a distance, well before the French knights could engage in hand-to-hand combat.

The Field of Agincourt

As the day of battle approached, both armies took their positions on the field. The English, though outnumbered, took up a defensive stance on a narrow strip of land, flanked by dense woodland on either side. This would limit the French cavalry’s ability to outflank them, and it allowed the archers to take advantage of the terrain. The muddy ground, soaked by the previous day’s rain, was another factor that worked in the English favor. The heavy French cavalry would struggle to move effectively in such conditions.

Henry V positioned his army with great care. The longbowmen were stationed on the flanks, with a core of men-at-arms and knights in the center. The archers were armed with their longbows, their quivers filled with sharp, bodkin-pointed arrows designed to pierce armor. The English knew that the success of the day would depend on the effectiveness of their archers, and Henry had planned for this.

The French army, confident in their superior numbers, began to form their lines. They too had positioned their cavalry, but the terrain and weather conditions would soon make those advantages irrelevant. As the fog began to lift, the English could see the French forming up in the distance, their banners waving in the breeze. There would be no turning back now.

The Opening Salvo

At the sound of the signal, the English archers drew their bows and released a volley of arrows. The sight was an awe-inspiring one: thousands of arrows arcing through the air, covering the battlefield with a dark, ominous cloud. The first wave of arrows fell upon the French forces with terrifying precision. Though the French knights were protected by heavy armor, the sheer volume and speed of the arrows caused chaos among their ranks. Some knights were struck before they could even react, while others were brought down by the powerful blows to their horses.

The longbowmen’s arrows were so powerful that they could pierce through the armor of the knights and penetrate their flesh. The French cavalry, already struggling to maneuver through the muddy terrain, found themselves increasingly vulnerable to the English archers. The devastation caused by the first volley was immense, and the French forces were thrown into disarray.

For the English, this was just the beginning. Henry’s orders had been clear: fire again and again, until the enemy could no longer stand.

The French Counterattack

The French, realizing the threat posed by the English archers, tried to advance with their cavalry, hoping to break the English lines. But the mud and the relentless barrage of arrows made this nearly impossible. The knights, clad in their heavy armor, struggled to move forward, their horses sinking in the soft earth. The longbows continued to rain down, cutting down the men-at-arms and knights as they tried to charge.

As the French knights were bogged down in the mud, their morale began to falter. The fear of the unseen archers, who could strike with deadly accuracy from afar, grew among the French ranks. Desperation set in as the French commanders realized they could not close the distance and engage in hand-to-hand combat, where their superior numbers and heavy armor would have given them the advantage.

In a desperate bid to break the deadlock, the French attempted to bring forward their infantry, but the narrowness of the field and the dense rain of arrows prevented them from forming any effective formations. They, too, were easy targets for the English archers, who continued to fire without pause.

The Decimation of the French

The French had underestimated the power of the longbow, and this miscalculation would prove disastrous. By mid-day, the French army was in disarray. Their knights and cavalry were trapped, unable to move due to the mud and the continuous arrow fire. Many had been slain, while others had fled the field in panic.

The English, on the other hand, remained largely intact, their archers still delivering devastating volleys. Henry’s men-at-arms and knights, though fewer in number, moved forward to engage the disorganized French infantry, who were now on the verge of collapse. The French, overwhelmed by the relentless assault from both the archers and the English infantry, were forced to retreat.

But it was too late. The battle was already lost.

The Aftermath

By the time the fighting had subsided, the battlefield was littered with the bodies of the French. Estimates vary, but it is believed that as many as 10,000 French soldiers were killed or captured, including many of their most prominent nobles and knights. The English, by contrast, suffered only a fraction of those losses, with around 400 to 600 casualties.

The victory at Agincourt was not just a military triumph; it was a symbolic moment in the Hundred Years’ War. The English had proven that they could defeat a far larger force, and they had done so through tactical ingenuity and the sheer power of the longbow. The French were left to pick up the pieces of their shattered army, and the English would go on to gain significant ground in the war.

Agincourt would remain a testament to the power of the English archers, who, with their skill and determination, had changed the course of history on that fateful day in 1415. The battle was a reminder that sometimes, it is not the size of the army that matters, but the strength of the tactics and the men who wield them.
