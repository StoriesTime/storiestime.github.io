To Lie or Tell the Truth: A Story of Ethics


The ringing of the school bell was sharp and jarring, cutting through the quiet hum of conversation in the classroom. Alex Moore didn’t move from his seat, even as the room quickly emptied of students eager to escape their last class of the day. He stared down at the spiral-bound notebook in front of him, his handwriting scrawled messily across the most recent page. Written out was the truth, raw and unfiltered, about what he had seen that morning.

He could still hear the words he had overheard, the sharpness of anger in Mr. Cartwright’s voice and the panicked response from his longtime colleague, Ms. Bennett. He had only caught fragments of the conversation before they noticed him standing awkwardly by the lockers. But it had been enough—more than enough—to realize something was deeply wrong. A lie had been told, a serious one, and not just to save face or shield feelings. This was something more consequential, something that could unravel many lives if revealed.

Alex sighed and flipped the notebook shut. The secret wasn’t his to carry, yet here he was, weighed down by its enormity. Could he tell the truth? Should he? The ethical implications twisted through his mind as he slung his backpack over his shoulder and trudged out of the classroom, his sneakers squeaking faintly on the polished tile floors.

The dilemma stayed with him long after he returned home. Supper was a quiet affair, his parents too wrapped up in their own lives to notice Alex’s preoccupation. His younger sister chattered on about her science project, but her words drifted over his head like leaves in the wind. After helping clean the dishes, Alex retreated to his bedroom, where he sat on the edge of his bed, staring again at the notebook.

Inside its confines was the power to expose dishonesty but also to invite chaos. He felt resentment toward the adults who had placed him in such a precarious position without even knowing it. The conflict he faced was not one of what he wanted to do—it was what he ought to do. But who determines what is right in such a situation? Alex had long believed that honesty was the best policy, drilled into him by countless childhood fables and the stern lessons of his mother. Yet, for the first time in his life, he wasn’t so sure.

His phone buzzed on the nightstand, ripping him from his thoughts. The screen lit up with a message from his best friend, Jordan. Hey, gaming tonight? Alex didn’t have the energy to respond. His mind was consumed by the decision he had to make. He flopped back onto the bed with a groan.

The next morning, he made up his mind to talk with someone—a neutral party who might help him see things clearly. Unfortunately, this neutrality was harder to find than he anticipated.

He started with Ms. Harper, the school counselor. Her office was meant to be a safe haven, but even as Alex stepped in, he hesitated. Could he really trust her? What if what he shared ended up sparking an even larger fire?

“What’s on your mind, Alex?” she asked, her tone gentle but perceptive. She’d undoubtedly noticed the way he kept shuffling his weight from foot to foot, the furrow on his brow.

Alex tried to speak but faltered. “It’s—hypothetically speaking—what if you knew something was wrong? Something big. But if you told the truth, it could get messy for a lot of people. Does that mean it’s better to stay quiet?”

Ms. Harper leaned forward slightly in her chair, her expression unreadable. “Truth can be powerful, Alex, but it isn’t easy. Sometimes, telling the truth helps fix a problem. Other times, it can cause unintended consequences. Hypothetically,” she said with a small, knowing smile, “you’d have to weigh what’s more important: the potential correction or the damage that might be caused.”

Her response wasn’t the clear answer Alex was hoping for. He left her office feeling even more conflicted than before.

By lunchtime, Alex found himself standing by the lockers where it had all started. The voices replayed in his mind. “…I don’t know what to do,” Ms. Bennett had said, her voice trembling with guilt. “The test scores were lower than expected. If we report this honestly, the school could lose its funding—or worse.”

Mr. Cartwright’s reply had been firmer, commanding even. “No one’s asking you to rewrite the results. Just…adjust a few trends. It’s not fraud; it’s for the greater good. They’ll never notice.”

Alex had heard enough. Though they hadn’t noticed him, their words carried all the weight of a direct confession. A lie transformed into survival. But was it justifiable? Would the potential fallout of exposing them—the loss of trust, funding, or reputations—help anyone in the long run?

Jordan tapped him on the shoulder, snapping him out of his daze. “Dude, I’ve been calling your name for, like, thirty seconds. You okay?”

Alex shrugged, though the tight tension in his shoulders suggested otherwise. “Just thinking.”

“About what?”

Alex’s first instinct was to deflect, but then the dam broke. He unspooled the entire story—the overheard conversation, his ethical dilemma, and his uncertainty about what to do. Jordan listened, his easy-going demeanor replaced by rare seriousness.

“That’s…wow,” Jordan said when Alex finally finished. “So, what are you gonna do?”

“I don’t know,” Alex admitted. “I feel like I’m stuck between two bad choices.”

Jordan rubbed the back of his neck. “Well, my mom always says, ‘Do what you can live with.’ Maybe that’s what you need to figure out.”

That night, Alex lay awake, replaying Jordan’s words over and over. “Do what you can live with.” Could he live with himself if he stayed silent, knowing he had let dishonesty slide? But on the other hand, could he bear the weight of knowing the havoc his revelation might wreak?

In the end, Alex made his decision. He spent hours crafting an anonymous letter, detailing what he had overheard. He didn’t accuse or condemn; he simply presented the facts as neutrally as possible. Once finished, he slid it into an envelope and addressed it to the school board.

It didn’t feel good, exactly, but it felt right. And sometimes, Alex realized, that’s all a person could ask for. In a world full of gray areas, the pursuit of truth had to be worth something—didn’t it? Time would tell.
