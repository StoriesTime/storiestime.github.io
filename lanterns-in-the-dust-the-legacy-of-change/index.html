Lanterns in the Dust: The Legacy of Change


The dust was everywhere, choking the horizon and dimming the sun as if the world itself had forgotten how to shine. It clung to every surface it touched—ravaged streets, fractured homes, and despondent faces. But amidst it all, the lanterns remained—a fragile yet glowing testament to hope.

Rael was young when the world had begun to shift. His earliest memories were of green fields and bustling cities alive with the sounds of endless possibilities. Roadside markets sold wares with reckless energy, children chased kites beneath open skies, and the world hummed along to the rhythm of its impossible ambitions. But that was before the Collapse.

No one could agree when it started, how it began, or what tipped the balance. Some whispered that it was humanity’s craving for control—a demand for dominance over nature so insatiable that the earth rebelled itself. Others told stories of sabotage, unseen forces pulling strings for profit and power while the rest simply stood by, unable to imagine their limits. Cities emptied first, then the countryside followed, leaving behind ghost towns layered thick with a gritty pallor of dust that only grew heavier as months turned to years.

The lanterns had existed before all of this chaos. Simple artifacts in a simpler time, cast from iron and glass, lighting paths and quiet porches in distant memories. They became more than tools once the dust came—a beacon, a desire carved deep into human instinct amid creeping desolation.

Rael stood at the edge of what used to be a park, his lantern slung across his shoulder. Its flame, once nurtured by common oil, was now fed by a distilled substance scavenged from forgotten dumpsters and crumbling industrial sites. He could feel its slight warmth despite the chill in the air, an odd contrast against the brutal wind that cut across his face.

Ahead lay Tarsian’s Bazaar. It wasn’t a marketplace in the traditional sense—not anymore. People didn’t barter with coins and goods like they did in stories of before. Here, the measure of wealth was possibility. Ideas were exchanged, secrets sold, and inspiration traded for the tools that could shape existence itself. Among the bazaar’s traders, the lanterns had unmatched value; they symbolized continuity amidst grinding disruption, light carried forward despite oppressive shadows.

Rael found Marlis in her usual place, seated behind a sprawling tapestry stained in colors that once might have been vibrant. She was older than most of the bazaar-goers, her eyes sharp with experience, scanning each passerby like a hawk hunting in twilight.

“You’re late,” she said in a voice brittle with age but firm in intent.

Rael set his lantern down carefully. “The storms pushed harder than I expected. Have you heard anything about the convoys? There’s talk they might reach the Crossing soon.”

Marlis gave a quiet laugh, head tilted just enough to reveal skepticism. “Those convoys are dreams dressed as promises. No convoy could fix this. Not anymore.”

Rael pulled his scarf tighter, unnerved by her words but unwilling to dispute them—she had seen more than he ever would.

As the bazaar moved around them—voices in argument, footsteps on uneven ground, and carts pushed with labor from weary arms—Marlis reached beneath her tapestry and pulled out a lantern of her own. Unlike Rael’s, this one burned with a steady blue light that flickered occasionally, as though shifting between realms invisible to human eyes.

“What’s that?” Rael asked, spellbound.

“Change,” Marlis replied with a cryptic smile. “This lantern doesn’t just light paths; it reveals them. And what lies beyond.”

Her words held weight. In the fragmented world they inhabited, the idea of paths seemed illusionary at best, absurd at worst. The dust swallowed everything, leaving even the keenest minds struggling to construct futures out of barren landscapes.

Rael’s fingers hovered near the lantern, uncertain but curious. “How does it work?”

“You ask too many questions,” Marlis said gently, though there was an inherent fondness in her tone. “Some things aren’t meant to be understood—only embraced. This one will lead you where you need to go, though not always where you want to.”

Rael hesitated. Following paths was all anyone really did since the Collapse. Some followed legends of abandoned cities that clung to survival; others traced routes carved by whispers of scavengers who claimed there were caches of old technology that still functioned amidst the wreckage. Rael had followed many of those paths himself, and they’d led him to dead ends or false promises more often than not.

“If it doesn’t lead where I want, then why would I follow?”

“Because wanting and needing aren’t the same,” Marlis said, her tone growing sharper, as though she were lecturing him.

Rael took the lantern in both hands, its cold surface sending small pulses into his palms. The blue light flared slightly before shrinking back into its quiet glow, pulling his gaze deeper into its depths. He turned toward the broken skyline in the distance, where skeletal remains of towers stretched thin against the choking clouds.

The sensation was magnetic. His feet felt drawn to move without intention, as if the lantern compelled him forward one inch at a time. As steps followed, Marlis’s voice floated softly behind him.

“One legacy anchors us—but another frees us, Rael. You will come to understand which is which.”

It was not a promise nor a threat. It was instruction—calm, certain instruction—and Rael knew better than to fight it.

The path was strange, layered in quiet contradiction. The lantern’s light revealed things that should not have existed. A garden walled off by shipping containers, still blooming with flowers whose pollen clung defiantly to drifting dust. A community of wanderers repairing solar synthesizers near a torn riverbank. Broken libraries, too fractured to preserve books, yet harboring undeniable vitality through whispered conversations and traded dreams.

Each step opened something invisible—a fragment of continuity hidden beneath collapse. Yet there was tension, too. The paths revealed by the lantern contrasted with the ruins sprawled across his peripheral understanding. Some things survived, though many cracked in their attempt. Marlis hadn’t lied; this wasn’t about what he wanted.

It was the third day on this unwelcome journey when Rael found himself beneath a bridge strewn with twisted steel choking out promises of past function. Beneath its shadow lay a gathering of lanterns—blue, gold, crimson—all glowing with steady flames that danced in unity across an endless swirl of swirling dust.

Here was where legacies gathered.

The people among them moved like shadows, exchanging untethered words and crafting ideas into shapes Rael could barely comprehend. Every lantern had its story; every light resembled a clash between past continuity and future dreams.

Rael felt his lantern pulse strongly now, its brightness refusing submission. This was the legacy it had guided him toward—one unshackled by Collapse itself. While many wandered lost in the dust, the gathering beneath the bridge built something new.

Yet when Rael lifted his lantern once more, scanning its light across determined faces and half-finished ideas, he noticed the distinct absence of one thing.

The dust never clung to their dreams.
