The Persians, with their vast numbers and cavalry, charged forward, but the Greeks held firm, their shields interlocked and their spears protruding like a forest of deadly spikes. The battle raged on for hours, with the Persians trying to outflank the Greeks, but the defenders of Thermopylae were too well-prepared. They had carefully chosen the narrowest point of the pass, where the cliffs rose steeply on one side and the sea stretched out on the other. Any attempt by the Persians to outflank the Greeks would be met with crushing force, as they would have to fight their way up steep cliffs or wade through the cold waters of the Aegean Sea. Despite being vastly outnumbered, the Greek phalanx stood firm, cutting down wave after wave of Persian invaders. Leonidas himself fought bravely, his spear slicing through the press of enemy soldiers, his shield protecting him from the hail of arrows and spears that rained down upon him. But even the most heroic of warriors cannot hold back an army of tens of thousands forever. As the sun began to set on the second day of the battle, the Greeks found themselves surrounded by a sea of enemy soldiers, their only escape route cut off by the rising tide of the Persian army.
