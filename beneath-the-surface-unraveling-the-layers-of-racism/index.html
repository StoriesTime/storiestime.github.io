Beneath the Surface: Unraveling the Layers of Racism


The summer heat in Blackwood was relentless, even as the sun dipped beneath the horizon. A faint orange glow lingered over the town, casting long shadows across the cracked pavement. Blackwood was a town that prided itself on its history, its traditions, and its tightly-knit community. But like many small towns, it had its own deep-seated problems, the kind that people whispered about behind closed doors but never truly faced head-on.

Racism in Blackwood wasn’t as overt as it had been decades ago, but it still lingered in subtle ways, woven into the fabric of everyday life. For some, it was as natural as breathing, an invisible force that shaped interactions and expectations. But for others, it was a reality they fought against every day, a quiet, often unnoticed enemy that threatened to suffocate their hopes and dreams.

I. The Encounter at the Diner

Jasmine Thompson was used to it. The side glances. The hushed whispers. The way people gripped their purses a little tighter when she walked into a room. She had grown up in Blackwood, and though she loved her hometown with its small-town charm, she was all too familiar with the undercurrent of racism that ran through its streets.

As a child, she’d been told to “stay in line,” “keep your head down,” and “don’t stir up trouble.” Those were the rules her mother had drilled into her, the same rules that had kept her safe—or at least, alive—throughout her childhood. But now, as an adult, Jasmine had begun to ask herself: Why should I have to stay in line? Why shouldn’t I stir up trouble if it means making a change?

The diner was a staple of Blackwood’s downtown, a place where everyone seemed to gather, from truck drivers stopping for a cup of coffee to the elderly locals discussing the latest gossip. Jasmine had worked there since high school, and despite the looks and the comments, she loved the sense of belonging it offered. But tonight, as she walked through the diner’s swinging doors, she felt the tension in the air.

The usual crowd sat scattered across the worn leather booths and chrome-topped tables. A couple of regulars waved to her, their smiles wide and welcoming. But in the back corner, a group of unfamiliar men sat hunched over their plates, their conversation low and serious. As Jasmine approached the counter to take her place, she overheard a fragment of their conversation.

“… just don’t know how much longer we can stand it. Everywhere you go, it’s like they think they own the place. I swear, I can’t even look at a kid today without thinking they’re going to try and tell me how to raise mine…”

Her heart sank. She recognized the type of talk. It wasn’t loud or aggressive, but it was insidious, the kind of casual racism that slipped through the cracks unnoticed unless you were paying close attention.

Jasmine turned away, her fingers tightening around the tray she held. Her manager, Rick, caught her eye from across the room. He gave her a brief nod, signaling that he needed her help with a table in the back. She could feel her blood pressure rise, but she pushed it down. Jasmine had learned long ago that reacting was often a dangerous game in a town like Blackwood. She focused on the task at hand, though her mind couldn’t stop racing.

II. Hidden History

It wasn’t until much later, when the diner had cleared out and the silence was palpable, that Jasmine and her childhood friend, Carla, found themselves sitting in one of the quiet booths. Carla had been Jasmine’s confidante for years, a fellow Blackwood native who had also felt the pull of something bigger than the small town, something that urged her to challenge the status quo.

“I saw you tense up earlier,” Carla said, her voice soft, but knowing. “What happened?”

Jasmine hesitated before answering. “It’s the same old thing, Carla. The talk. The looks. The way people don’t even realize they’re doing it, but they do it anyway. It’s exhausting.”

Carla nodded. “I know. It’s the thing no one talks about, right? The thing that gets swept under the rug, but it’s there, always. And it’s so much worse than they realize.”

Jasmine thought for a moment. “It’s not just the overt stuff. It’s like, we don’t even realize how much we’re absorbing it. We’re all just playing our parts in this bigger system, and no one knows how to break free.”

Carla leaned in, her eyes alight with passion. “Maybe it’s time we start talking about it. Like, really talking about it. Not just in hushed tones between the two of us, but out in the open. People need to understand that it’s not just about a few bad apples. It’s systemic. It’s built into everything—history, culture, economics.”

Jasmine looked at her friend, the weight of her words sinking in. “How do we even begin to talk about that? Everyone’s so defensive. They don’t want to hear it. They think things have changed, that racism is gone.”

Carla chuckled darkly. “That’s the problem. They think it’s gone. But it’s just buried under layers, layers that we’ve all been taught to ignore. What people don’t understand is that it’s still there, still festering, because no one’s been willing to dig deep enough to see it for what it really is.”

III. The Layers of Racism

As the weeks went by, Jasmine and Carla began to see the layers of racism that existed in their community more clearly. It wasn’t always something you could point to directly, but the effects were there, subtly shaping the way people lived and interacted with each other.

The more they talked about it, the more they realized that the roots of racism in Blackwood ran deeper than they had initially understood. It wasn’t just about individual prejudice or hatred. It was about a system, one that had been built over generations, that shaped the opportunities available to different people based on the color of their skin.

Jasmine had always known that Blackwood wasn’t the most welcoming town for people of color. But as she began to peel back the layers, she saw that the problem was much larger than she had imagined. It was in the schools, where children of color were more likely to be disciplined, less likely to be encouraged to dream big, and more likely to face barriers to success. It was in the housing market, where redlining practices had limited the ability of Black families to buy property in certain neighborhoods. It was in the workplace, where promotions and opportunities often went to people who were perceived as “fitting the mold,” regardless of their qualifications.

And it was in the way people talked. The casual remarks that went unchecked. The jokes that were made without a second thought. The microaggressions that people didn’t even realize they were committing.

Carla was right. Racism wasn’t just about hate—it was about a system that had been put in place long ago, and no one had bothered to dismantle it. It wasn’t enough to simply “get along” or “live and let live.” What was needed was change—real, lasting change.

IV. A New Beginning

One night, as Jasmine walked home after another grueling shift at the diner, she found herself standing at the edge of the town square, staring at the old clock tower that had stood for over a century. It was a symbol of Blackwood’s history, its past, and its pride. But tonight, as she gazed up at it, she saw something else—a reminder of how much needed to be changed.

Her phone buzzed in her pocket, interrupting her thoughts. It was Carla.

“I think it’s time,” the text read. “We start talking. Let’s meet at the diner tomorrow. We need to figure out what comes next.”

Jasmine smiled. It was a simple message, but it was the start of something bigger than either of them had imagined. It was time to peel back the layers of racism, to face the truth, and to begin the hard work of dismantling a system that had been allowed to persist for far too long.

The conversation would not be easy. It would be uncomfortable. It would challenge everything people thought they knew. But it was necessary. For their town. For themselves. And for the generations to come.

As she turned to head home, the weight of the task ahead felt both daunting and empowering. There was a long road ahead, but Jasmine knew one thing for certain: The journey had already begun.

And there was no turning back now.
