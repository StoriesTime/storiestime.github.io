Spiritual Sojourn: Finding Peace in Bali's Temples


The air was thick with the scent of frangipani flowers as Eleanor stepped off the small wooden boat onto the sandy shores of Bali. The rhythmic lapping of waves against the hull and the distant hum of island life filled her ears, but her mind was elsewhere. She had come in search of something intangible—something she couldn’t name but felt the absence of deeply. The stressors of her fast-paced life in London had finally begun to weigh too heavily on her shoulders. When a close friend had suggested taking a spiritual retreat in Bali, Eleanor had hesitated. Yet here she was, drawn by the promise of serenity in Bali’s ancient temples.

A local guide named Ketut awaited her at the shore. His bright smile and warm demeanor immediately put her at ease. Ketut was dressed in a traditional sarong and carried a small wicker basket filled with offerings—rolled banana leaves filled with flowers and rice. He raised his hand in a simple greeting.

“Welcome, Miss Eleanor. Are you ready to begin your journey?”

She nodded, feeling an unexpected lump in her throat. “I think so. I’m not entirely sure what I’m looking for, though.”

Ketut smiled knowingly. “The temples have a way of guiding you to what you need, even if you do not know what you’re searching for.”

The Trail to Tirta Empul

Their first stop was Tirta Empul, a centuries-old water temple located in the village of Tampaksiring. The temple, built around a natural spring, was one of the most sacred purification sites on the island. As they ascended the steps leading into the temple, Eleanor was struck by the intricate stone carvings decorating the entrance gate. Ancient deities, guardian figures, and intricate patterns told stories she could only begin to guess at.

Inside, the sound of flowing water greeted her. Pools of crystal-clear spring water shimmered under the early afternoon sun, and devotees dressed in white were lining up to partake in the melukat, a traditional cleansing ritual.

“Would you like to join?” Ketut asked her, his voice gentle.

Eleanor hesitated. She had read about the rite beforehand but felt unsure about participating, given her unfamiliarity with the customs. “Is it okay if I’m just… observing?”

Ketut nodded. “Of course. Observing is also a way of learning.”

He explained that the ritual was designed to wash away both physical dirt and spiritual impurities, helping people realign with their inner peace. As Eleanor watched the devotees submerge themselves under the stone spouts, one by one, she couldn’t help but feel the profound energy of the place. The air felt different here—heavy with purpose, yet serene.

Her heart stirred with something she couldn’t define. Perhaps it was envy at the surrender she saw in the eyes of the participants, openly seeking peace. She wondered what it might feel like to let go so fully of one’s burdens.

Uluwatu and the Cliffside Temple

The next day, Ketut led Eleanor toward Uluwatu, a clifftop temple dramatically perched high above the Indian Ocean. The journey involved a winding road, dotted with lush greenery and traditional Balinese homes. Eleanor had always thought of temples as grounded places, built low and close to where worshippers would naturally gather in villages. Uluwatu turned that perspective on its head.

Standing on the temple grounds, Eleanor was met with a breathtaking panorama. The ocean stretched endlessly before her, the waves far below crashing against the limestone cliffs. The views were awe-inspiring, but what caught her attention most was the deep sense of stillness in the temple itself. Despite the steady stream of visitors, there was something timeless here, as if the temple had become one with its surroundings.

Ketut explained that Uluwatu wasn’t just a vantage point for spectacular sunsets. The temple was also considered one of Bali’s six key “spiritual pillars” and was dedicated to guarding the island from evil spirits. Eleanor found herself paying more attention to the details—the dance of the breeze through the trees, the scent of incense wafting ever so faintly in the air, the quiet authority in the posture of the stone statues.

As the sun began to dip below the horizon, the temple hosted a special Kecak dance performance. Dozens of men sat in concentric circles, chanting rhythmically and moving in unison to tell stories from the Ramayana. Eleanor could feel the energy in the dance, blending seamlessly with the fading light and the crashing waves below. For the first time, she felt a flicker of release, as if the energy of the place had begun prying loose the worries long lodged inside her.

A Pilgrimage to Besakih

On the third morning, Ketut told her they were going on a “pilgrimage” to the Mother Temple—Besakih. Located on the slopes of Mount Agung, it was the largest and holiest temple complex in Bali. Eleanor knew from her research that this site had been a sacred place of worship for more than a thousand years. Still, she was unprepared for the sheer magnitude of it.

Besakih wasn’t just a single temple, but an entire complex of more than eighty temples, each dedicated to different deities and aspects of worship. The layout was steeped in symbolism, with Mount Agung representing the spiritual axis of Bali. Eleanor followed Ketut as they climbed higher and higher up the stairs, pausing often to take in the elaborate shrines and pay their respects with small offerings.

As they reached the main temple, Penataran Agung, Ketut suggested Eleanor perform a simple prayer ceremony. It was a quiet, personal moment—Eleanor kneeling barefoot, hands pressed together, whispering words that only she could hear. Ketut handed her a small sprinkling of holy water at the end, which she sipped and then used to anoint her forehead.

“How do you feel?” he asked as they started the descent.

Eleanor thought for a moment before smiling softly. “Lighter, I think. Freer.”

Ketut nodded as though he understood. “The mountains hear you, and the gods, too. They help you let go.”

It wasn’t a dramatic transformation, but Eleanor could feel the layers of tension she’d come to Bali with beginning to dissolve. Letting go wasn’t sudden. It was happening in small, quiet steps.

The Hidden Cave of Goa Gajah

On the final day of her journey, Ketut brought Eleanor to a hidden gem: Goa Gajah, commonly known as the Elephant Cave. The entrance, carved from solid rock, resembled a demonic mouth, a striking contrast to the tranquility she had found at the other temples. Ketut explained that this juxtaposition was intentional—it symbolized the necessity of confronting one’s fears before reaching spiritual enlightenment.

Inside the cave, the atmosphere was cool and damp. Statues of Hindu and Buddhist deities lined the walls, while the air was thick with incense. Here, the spiritual and the natural overlapped in a way that felt different from the other temples. The waters of a nearby bathing pool seemed to hum softly.

Eleanor found a quiet corner near the temple grounds to sit and reflect. She still couldn’t fully articulate what had brought her to Bali, but something about her journey was becoming increasingly clear. Peace wasn’t something she would stumble across all at once. It wasn’t waiting in a specific place or bound to a single moment. Peace was a practice—one she could cultivate, just like the rituals she had observed throughout the week.

Coming Full Circle

As they made their way back to the shore where her journey had started, Eleanor looked at Ketut and said, “Thank you. For everything.”

He offered his characteristic smile. “You will carry the temples with you now.”

And she did. Whether it was the flowing waters of Tirta Empul, the wind-swept cliffs of Uluwatu, the soaring heights of Besakih, or the quiet depths of Goa Gajah, Eleanor felt as though tiny fragments of each temple nestled within her heart. She didn’t emerge from Bali with all the answers—but for the first time in years, she felt at peace with the questions.

As she boarded the boat to leave, Eleanor inhaled deeply, catching one last whiff of the frangipani flowers. She knew she’d return to the rhythm of her life in London with renewed strength and a newfound sense of clarity. Bali had given her not the solution but the tools—a guide to finding peace, wherever she was.
