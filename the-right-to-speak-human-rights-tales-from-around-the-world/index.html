The Right to Speak: Human Rights Tales from Around the World


María’s first word was “agua.” Her mother, Lidia, told her the story each birthday, of the little cup she’d clattered against the chipped blue table in San Miguel, El Salvador, hollering for water until someone listened. María, now wide-eyed and hungry for other languages, wanted to see who else in the world grew up thirsty and what they whispered, shouted, or sang when finally, someone listened.

She started a journal on her fifteenth birthday after Lidia bought her a notebook with a dream-colored cover. María pressed her pencil to the first page. She wrote a single question: What does it mean to have a voice?

Her journey—though she did not know it—would intertwine with voices that ricocheted across continents, threading through bustling cities and silent corners, all clamoring for the right so many took for granted: the right to speak.

Streets of Rangoon

The first tale drifted to María through whispers online; late-night posts, snatches of video, edited in and out by the blue glow of her phone. Myanmar: thousands surging in the streets of Rangoon, faces blurred with scarves, signs trembling with words: “We have the right to speak.”

It was early 2021. Phones everywhere trembled with urgency. News anchors said the generals had taken power, and people—students just like her—flooded empty roads to yell. María watched twenty-year-old Khun erase her old name and paint a new one on the cardboard: AUNG THU—Freedom for All.

They streamed, day and night, through water cannons and tear gas plumes, their chants echoing off broken buildings. Sarees, jeans, backpacks. Khun’s hands shaking with the cold, clutching a sign no army could swallow up.

At home, María filled her notebook: “Voice is standing in a crowd, hand aching, throat dry, still yelling.”

The Uzbek Window

María’s teacher told her once that you can find any story if you ask good questions. So she did, messaging friends in distant time zones, swapping tales and snippets.

Alim, a boy in Tashkent, sent her a typed message: “My grandfather played flute under the Soviets. During Stalin’s times, music with Uzbek words was banned. They called it ‘dangerous nationalism.’” Alim’s grandfather, Barot, carved a reed flute from the riverside, humming melodies of old tales, each note a small rebellion.

One evening, neighbors heard a tune—the forbidden lullaby of the Silk Road. Barot, behind closed shutters, played for his baby daughter. Listening, María imagined a world where melodies tangled with fear.

Years later, when glasnost came, the flute was played in the open. Barot left his window unshuttered. People gathered below. “He played until the police came,” Alim wrote. “Then, everyone applauded.”

María wrote: “To have voice is to play your song, even when the window is closed, hoping someone will listen outside.”

The Library in Kabul

María’s tale-seeking drew her to Afghanistan. The radio told only of bombs and sorrow, but a blog—the secret diary of Yasamin—painted defiance in petals.

Yasamin, fifteen, dreamed of reading every book. The Taliban banned girls from schools, so her mother hid textbooks beneath flour sacks. By night, Yasamin read by candlelight—poetry, chemistry, a smuggled novel by Sadiq Hedayat.

When she was twelve, Yasamin’s mother founded the smallest library in Kabul: two shelves, a curtain, tea served with whispers. Girls snuck in, giggling behind headscarves, to read aloud—each word a kind of revolution. Names were code, locations shifted, but each time they gathered, they claimed their right to knowledge.

Once, the library was raided. The books were torn, the shelves smashed. Yasamin rebuilt, scribbling verses on walls, binding comics with scrap string.

“Voices can be bound together in books, or scattered in poems,” María wrote. “Speaking is reading aloud in places where silence is enforced.”

The Silent Court in Iran

María read of Niloofar, an Iranian lawyer defending imprisoned activists. Niloofar, draped in her black chador, stood with her daughters at the courthouse gate, shouting case numbers into the indifferent morning.

The court’s gavel did not care for her voice— documents went missing, trials were held in secret. Niloofar persisted, translating testimony, preserving confessions in countless notebooks. She read from them at night, a private litany.

“Sometimes, speaking is writing down what the silenced cannot say,” María reflected. “Sometimes, the right to speak is just the right to keep a record.”

The Park Bench in St. Petersburg

Her stories led her north, beyond the equator, crossing digital bridges to Russia. Sasha, an artist, made protests from yarn and color. In snowy St. Petersburg, she knitted scarves and draped them quietly over statues—rainbows winding up stone arms in a city afraid of dissent.

People called them “Yarn Bombs.” One morning, police found Sasha painting a Tolstoy quote under a bridge: “If you want to be happy, be.”

They arrested her for ‘spreading propaganda.’ The scarf unravelled, bits clinging to the wind. Days later, the phrase reappeared on twenty walls.

María grinned, writing: “Sometimes, the right to speak is translated through colors, woven into scarves, written on walls. Even the smallest acts multiply.”

The Classroom in Johannesburg

In South Africa, her uncle José taught languages in Soweto. He described meeting children who still couldn’t learn in their mother tongues. María imagined the frustration: English lessons that slipped past ears that understood only Zulu or Xhosa.

One boy, Pokane, drew pictures. When asked why he wouldn’t speak, he held out his images—houses, rivers, faces lost in the government’s words. José learned to listen in silence, nodding as Pokane’s drawings grew more elaborate.

Eventually, Pokane whispered a greeting in Zulu, carefully. The classroom shifted; suddenly, children were laughing and talking in the language they’d known since birth. José began to teach in both tongues, defying the curriculum.

“The right to speak is the right to speak in your own language,” María scribbled, a gust of pride racing through her hand. “It’s drawing until words arrive, and trusting they will be understood.”

The Broken Microphone in Poland

Poland’s story came not through news or letters, but via a recording—a scratchy tape, digitized from a dusty attic. The speaker, Marek, was once a radio host in Wrocław during communist rule. Every night, he broadcast hopeful messages: celebrating local poets, sharing foreign music, reading messages from families divided by walls.

His microphone crackled; censors would cut to static. Even so, people tuned in. One bitter winter, when authorities yanked the station’s plug, Marek walked to the square, carrying a megaphone.

Hundreds gathered around the statue of Copernicus, words tumbling through the icy dusk. The police tried to silence him, but for a few brief minutes, the songs soared across rooftops, echoing off tramcars.

“Even a broken microphone can carry a voice,” María decided, looping a sketch of a megaphone on the page. “People find new ways to listen.”

The March in Santiago

María’s final story was closest to home. Students across Latin America, from Chile to Nicaragua, rose up to demand safe schools, honest elections, and their vanished friends. Protest had many faces: painted, hidden, tear-stained.

In Santiago, Camila scrawled her dreams on her arms. She marched after curfew, banging pots. The streets became rivers of voices, surging against riot shields. By day, Camila whispered plans in daughter’s ears. By night, she wept for classmates who never returned. Still, she marched.

“The right to speak,” María wrote, firm, “is hope stitched into banners. Even tears are a kind of speech.”

The Choir at Dawn

Days passed. María’s notebook filled. She realized that each voice, whether shouted with fists or whispered from beneath bedcovers, echoed the same need: the old ache for agua, the hunger for words.

She borrowed stories, but her own voice became clearer. In her city, she saw the quiet ones—cleaners humming songs from the highlands, grandmothers telling stories through food, children drawing joy on cracked sidewalks. The right to speak lived in each of them.

María gathered her tales. At her school’s assembly, she stood—heart trembling, tongue thick—and began to read.

“I have stories from Rangoon and St. Petersburg, Tashkent and Kabul,” she said, her voice gathering. “I want you to remember that the right to speak is not just for some people—it is for us all. It lives in music, in silence, in banners and books, in languages nearly lost and languages yet to be made. Every time someone listens, that right grows stronger.”

The auditorium quieted. For a long moment, it seemed the world itself was listening.

Outside, a rainstorm battered the bus stop. María closed her notebook as the final bell rang. She stepped into the shimmering street, carrying her stories, her voice echoing faintly amidst the thunder—a small light, joining countless others.
