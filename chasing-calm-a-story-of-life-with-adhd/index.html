Chasing Calm: A Story of Life with ADHD


I woke up to the sound of my phone alarm blaring, a shrill noise that felt like it was trying to pierce my skull. I instinctively hit the snooze button, but not before I had already lost count of how many times it went off. The first wave of panic hit me. “Not again,” I thought. I had promised myself I would wake up earlier, be more organized, and start the day calmly. But it seemed like each morning started the same—chaotic, unstructured, and uncontrollable.

I sat up in bed, trying to remember what I was supposed to be doing today. There were tasks—so many tasks—piled in my mind, like a tower of Jenga blocks about to collapse. I had an important work presentation to prepare for, a meeting with a client I couldn’t afford to miss, and a hundred other things that would inevitably fall to the wayside if I didn’t focus.

I took a deep breath and tried to center myself. Focus. Calm. It’s all I needed, right? Just a little calm.

But of course, my mind had other plans. Thoughts were already swirling—like leaves caught in a gust of wind—darting from one to another with no clear direction. Did I feed the dog this morning? What was I supposed to wear to the presentation? Did I reply to that email from yesterday? Why am I thinking about that random song from childhood right now? And just like that, I was lost again.

ADHD had been my constant companion for as long as I could remember. The official diagnosis came in my late twenties, but by that point, I had already developed my own strategies to survive each day. It wasn’t easy, though. Focus felt like a distant concept—something I chased but could never quite catch. Time slipped through my fingers, my thoughts scattered in all directions, and calm seemed like an elusive dream.

Life with ADHD wasn’t always a series of frantic moments, but it often felt that way. I could be incredibly focused for short bursts, hyper-productive even, but the moment my attention was pulled elsewhere, it was like being swept into a tornado. It wasn’t a matter of being lazy or disinterested; it was simply how my brain functioned. Some days, it felt like a victory just to get through the basics—getting dressed, brushing my teeth, making it to a meeting on time.

Yet, there were also moments where everything clicked into place, where I felt like I could conquer the world. Those moments were rare, but when they came, they were intoxicating.

By the time I managed to crawl out of bed, I had already wasted a good 30 minutes of precious morning time. In the bathroom, I stared at myself in the mirror, silently chastising myself. I couldn’t afford to waste any more time.

I grabbed a cup of coffee and slumped into my desk chair, my computer screen glowing with emails, notifications, and endless to-do lists. One email caught my eye, and for a moment, I felt the spark of interest. But before I could read it properly, my phone buzzed. Then I heard the sound of a car honking outside. Then a bird chirped loudly by the window. All of a sudden, the room felt like it was spinning.

I shut my eyes for a second, forcing myself to breathe deeply. I counted to four—hold for four—exhale for four. A simple technique, but one that never quite seemed to work for me. It wasn’t enough to calm the chaos.

I glanced at the clock. I had a meeting in fifteen minutes, but the presentation wasn’t even close to ready. Panic gripped my chest as my thoughts spiraled again.

I had learned a lot about managing ADHD over the years, but there were still so many days when it felt like a constant battle. People often assumed that ADHD was just about being hyperactive or having trouble sitting still, but it was so much more than that. It was the mental fog, the constant internal noise, the inability to regulate emotions, the overwhelming sense of being out of control.

One of the hardest parts was the unpredictability. One day, I could be on top of the world, accomplishing everything I set out to do. But on another day, I could barely summon the energy to open an email. The instability wore me down, but I kept pushing forward. What choice did I have?

Therapists and doctors told me the same thing over and over again: structure your life, build routines, break tasks into small, manageable chunks. It all made sense in theory, but execution was a whole different story. I would start the week with a detailed plan, but by Wednesday, it was all in ruins, and I was scrambling to catch up.

I had tried medication, too. Some days, it helped to quiet my racing thoughts. But the side effects—insomnia, loss of appetite, and the all-too-familiar brain fog—weren’t worth it. I was left with the same dilemma: chase calm, or accept the chaos?

As the clock ticked down to my meeting, I quickly jotted a few notes about my presentation, not caring about the messy handwriting or the disorganized thoughts on the page. There was no time for perfection. There was only time for survival.

I entered the meeting feeling like an imposter. My mind was a jumble of disconnected ideas, and I had no clear structure to follow. But I knew I had to push through. I smiled and spoke confidently, even if I didn’t feel confident at all. My words came out in spurts, like pieces of a puzzle that didn’t quite fit together. Still, I managed to make it through without completely embarrassing myself.

When the meeting was over, I collapsed into my chair, relieved that it was behind me. But as the adrenaline faded, the weight of the day settled in. My brain buzzed, never quite shutting off. The quiet moments—those precious pockets of calm—were few and far between.

I decided to take a walk, hoping that some fresh air would help clear my mind. I had read somewhere that exercise was supposed to be good for ADHD, but I never really saw the results immediately. Still, something about moving—about putting one foot in front of the other—was grounding. As I walked, my thoughts slowed down, just a little. I felt the cool breeze on my skin, and for the briefest moment, everything felt right.

It was during those walks that I began to understand something important about myself: calm wasn’t something I could force. It wasn’t a goal to achieve or a destination to reach. It was a process—something I had to actively cultivate every day.

Chasing calm meant finding small moments of peace within the chaos. It was learning to embrace the disorganization, the unpredictability, and the scattered thoughts, and finding a way to navigate through it without letting it consume me. It was about taking a step back and realizing that I didn’t have to be perfect. Life with ADHD wasn’t about controlling every aspect of my day; it was about finding a way to function within the mess.

Some days, I would win the battle. I would finish tasks, stay focused, and feel like I had my life together. Other days, I would falter. But through it all, I would remind myself that I wasn’t chasing a perfect version of calm—I was chasing a version of myself that could handle the chaos with grace, patience, and understanding.

And so, life with ADHD continued. Some mornings were harder than others, some meetings more challenging, but each day was an opportunity to grow, to adapt, and to find my own version of peace.

I wasn’t going to let the noise define me. Instead, I would learn to find calm in the storm.

End.
