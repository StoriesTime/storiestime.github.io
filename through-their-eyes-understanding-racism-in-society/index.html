Through Their Eyes: Understanding Racism in Society


In a bustling city where glass towers gleamed under the sun and street vendors filled the air with spices and chatter, the pulse of life beat in diverse rhythms. Here, people came from all corners of the world, bringing with them stories, traditions, and dreams. Yet, beneath the surface of this vibrant mosaic, subtle and sometimes glaring fractures of racial prejudice persisted.

The Meeting Place

At the heart of the city, a small community center stood, an unassuming brick building with faded murals depicting hands joined across skin tones. Every Thursday evening, a group gathered there—not just to talk, but to listen, to understand, and to share. They called it “Through Their Eyes,” a forum born out of necessity, aimed at confronting racism in society through stories and lived experiences.

On a late spring evening, the center filled with a mix of people. There was Maria, a Latina mother balancing three jobs; Jamal, a young Black artist seeking acceptance; Priya, an immigrant from India navigating cultural invisibility; and George, a white teacher grappling with his own biases. Each had been touched by racism in its many forms—some overt, some nearly invisible.

Stories Unfold

Jamal spoke first, his voice steady but loaded with a painful history. He described walking home late at night, the tightening grip of fear as police cars approached, the instinct to appear smaller, less threatening.

“It’s like every step I take outside feels like a test I never agreed to,” he said. “I’m not just me; I’m a symbol in their eyes.”

Maria nodded, her eyes damp but resolute. “For me, racism is quieter but no less damaging. At work, people question my English, my ideas dismissed because I don’t look ‘professional’ or ‘American’ enough. It wears you down.”

Priya shared how her name was regularly mispronounced in school, her accent a target for jokes, the subtle exclusion from group conversations. “It’s like this invisible wall between me and everyone else.”

George listened deeply, his mind turning over memories of unintentional microaggressions and ingrained assumptions from his upbringing. He admitted feeling ashamed but motivated to change.

The Roots of Racism

As the evening progressed, discussions delved into the history entwined with these stories. Racism, far from being just isolated acts of prejudice, was deeply woven into laws, education, and institutions that shaped opportunities and life chances.

The group examined how systemic racism manifested—housing discrimination that kept communities segregated, economic inequalities that funneled people of color into underfunded schools and unstable jobs. They recognized the difference between individual bias and structural factors, understanding that tackling racism required more than good intentions.

Empathy as a Bridge

One activity had everyone pair off to retell their partner’s story, fostering empathy in a personal, tangible way. George recounted Jamal’s experience with police encounters, feeling a newfound awareness of the terror hidden behind his friend’s calm delivery.

Maria repeated Priya’s experiences of subtle exclusion. “I never thought about how painful those small moments are,” she said softly.

Through this empathy exercise, the group transcended statistics and headlines, connecting instead with real human lives. It became clear that understanding racism meant stepping into another’s world, confronting discomfort, and acknowledging complex truths.

The Power of Collective Action

Towards the end of the night, the conversation turned hopeful. What could be done, beyond awareness? They agreed that education was a powerful tool—sharing stories in classrooms, workplaces, and online spaces to challenge stereotypes and normalize diversity.

They envisioned community programs supporting marginalized youth, advocacy pushing for fair policies, and allies in privileged positions using their voices to amplify those unheard.

Jamal suggested art as a medium to express pain and resilience, while Maria highlighted the need for economic support for immigrant families to break cycles of poverty linked to discrimination.

The group recognized that change would be gradual but believed that dialogue like theirs—rooted in honesty, respect, and vulnerability—was a crucial step.

Reflection Outside the Center

Long after the meeting ended and the center grew silent, the city carried on. But for those who had gathered, the experience shifted their perspective. They saw the streets differently, attentive to the subtle cues that spoke of invisible barriers.

George walked home contemplating how to bring these conversations into his classroom, determined to create a space where children of all backgrounds felt seen and valued. Maria thought about petitioning her landlord for fairer treatment, inspired by the solidarity within the group.

Jamal sketched a new piece for his art show, weaving faces and stories into a tapestry of resilience and hope. Priya planned to start a blog to share her journey, hoping to reach others feeling isolated.

A New Lens

“Through Their Eyes” was not just a meeting—it was a mirror reflecting society’s fractured image and a window into possibilities for healing. By sharing personal narratives and unpacking the roots of discrimination, the participants learned that racism is not a distant problem but an everyday reality for many.

Understanding racism through lived experience challenged assumptions and sparked a commitment to act—not from guilt or shame, but from empathy and solidarity.

The story of their city, like countless others, was more than numbers or policies. It was a story of people—complicated, diverse, and bound together by the potential for change.

In a world too often divided, seeing through another’s eyes holds the promise of breaking down barriers, one story at a time.
