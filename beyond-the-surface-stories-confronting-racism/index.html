Beyond the Surface: Stories Confronting Racism


The town of Maplewood had always worn a façade of peace and unity, its streets lined with tidy houses, its parks bustling with laughing children from every background. To the casual observer, it was the picture of harmony. But beneath this veneer, the town held stories—buried moments of hurt, resilience, and quiet rebellion—stories that demanded to be heard.

The Whisper of the Old Oak

Jasmine sat beneath the ancient oak tree in Maplewood Park, her sketchbook balanced on her knees. The old tree was a silent witness to generations, its branches sprawling wide as if embracing the history beneath its roots. Today, Jasmine was drawing a portrait—not of a person, but of a feeling, something that weighed on her heart.

Born and raised in Maplewood, Jasmine was all too familiar with the subtle ways racism threaded through the town’s everyday life. It wasn’t the fiery, overt hatred of stories she had heard in history books; it was quieter, more insidious.

Like the time she applied for the summer internship at the town library. Despite excellent grades and glowing recommendations, she was passed over, the position given to someone with fewer qualifications and a well-connected family name. Or the way neighbors crossed the street when she walked by, eyes downcast, as if their discomfort was her burden to carry.

Jasmine’s art was her rebellion. Every line she drew was a refusal to be invisible, a testament to her existence beyond stereotypes.

Marcus and the Broken Window

Not far from Jasmine’s oak tree was the corner bakery, owned by Marcus—a warm-hearted man whose laughter was known to brighten even the grayest mornings.

One chilly October evening, a baseball shattered the storefront window. The glass fragmented across the sidewalk, sparkling dangerously in the streetlight.

Marcus knew the culprit wasn’t a random act. He had heard the whispers in the town: the resentment some felt toward his success— a Black man thriving in a predominantly white neighborhood.

Instead of anger, Marcus felt a call to bridge the gap. He gathered his neighbors for a community meeting, including those who might otherwise stay silent.

“It’s easy to throw stones,” Marcus said, his voice steady, “but harder to build windows—to open lenses through which we understand each other.”

What followed were stories shared—sometimes halting, often painful—of discrimination and missed opportunities. The meeting became a tipping point for Maplewood, peeling back superficial niceties to reveal raw truths.

Grace’s Classroom

Grace, a schoolteacher in Maplewood Elementary, noticed the subtle disparities in her classroom. Students of color often fell behind not for lack of ability but because of unconscious bias that shaped expectations.

She introduced a project called Beyond the Surface, encouraging her students to explore their own identities and those of their classmates through stories, art, and dialogue.

One day, during a sharing session, a shy boy named Luis spoke about his experience being the only Latino kid in his class. “Sometimes, I feel like I have to pretend to be someone else… so I won’t be noticed for the wrong reasons,” he whispered.

Grace saw in that moment the power and necessity of confronting these feelings—not just for Luis, but for all children growing up with invisible walls.

The Town’s Reckoning

Maplewood’s journey was not without resistance. Many found it uncomfortable to confront their own biases, or the legacy of exclusion that had lingered unacknowledged.

But change began in small acts—a heartfelt apology, a community mural painted with faces representing every heritage, a local bakery hosting cultural food nights.

The mayor, inspired by Marcus’s leadership and Jasmine’s art, declared a town day dedicated to stories confronting racism. “To look beyond the surface,” she said in her speech, “is to see one another fully—not as abstractions, but as neighbors and friends.”

Leaving the Surface Behind

Jasmine finished her sketch beneath the old oak—the lines coming together to form a tapestry of faces, hands intertwined, eyes meeting without judgment.

The portrait wasn’t just about pain or struggle; it was about hope forged in the fire of truth.

Marcus replaced the bakery window, now etched with a quote: “Strength grows in the cracks.”

Grace’s students carried forward the lessons, becoming ambassadors of empathy in their growing world.

Maplewood had learned that overcoming racism wasn’t a sudden fix but a lifelong commitment to looking deeper, listening harder, and daring to bridge divides.

This was a town transformed—not because racism vanished overnight, but because its people chose courage over comfort, stories over silence, and understanding beyond the surface.
