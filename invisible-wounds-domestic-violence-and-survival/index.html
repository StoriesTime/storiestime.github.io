Invisible Wounds: Domestic Violence and Survival


Maya watched the delicate curve of steam rising from her coffee. She traced its ephemeral dance, winding into the kitchen’s brittle morning light like a silent symphony—almost beautiful, if she didn’t know that silence, in her world, came at a cost.

The cabinets hummed faintly from the old refrigerator. The clock ticked over to 6:13 a.m. Maya’s shoulders tensed, anticipating the familiar rhythm of footsteps overhead. They would start soon: heavy, decisive, a sound that once belonged to a man—her husband—but now belonged to a storm.

Every morning, as she sat alone at the chipped table, she rehearsed her part in the play that was her life. She reached for the sugar with her left hand—a small, invisible act of survival. Her right arm, covered by a worn cardigan, bore the shape of a bruise not yet faded to yellow. It hurt less than her ribs, and less than the ache she felt somewhere she couldn’t touch.

Sometimes, she almost envied the mug—cracked, patched with glue, and still held together. She sipped, burned her tongue, and stared at the secondhand calendar bolted to the wall. Sunday: parent-teacher conference. Wednesday: grocery shopping if there’s extra money. Saturday: pretend everything is fine for the neighbors.

She tried to recall a time when she hadn’t been so good at pretending. When she laughed simply because something was funny, not because she was terrified of what silence might mean to him. Was it last year? Five years ago? Before the first apology, the first bouquet of flowers, wilted before she could set them in water?

Upstairs, the footsteps moved toward the stairs. She inhaled, trained her face into a smile that never reached her eyes. A second later, the door burst open and Tom entered—six-foot-two, with the paunch of an ex-football player and the impatience of a man perpetually angry at gravity. “Where’s my coffee?”

“It’s on the table, just like you like it,” she replied, warm and practiced.

He grunted, clattered down into the chair with the force of an avalanche. He didn’t look at her, not directly, but she felt his gaze like the crackling heat from a furnace: something that could burn, destroy, if she got too close.

She buttered his toast, careful not to make eye contact, careful to keep her words measured. Conversation was a minefield, and she was tired of losing.

As he ate, she thought of their daughter, Annie, who was still asleep in her room, safe for a few more minutes. Maya’s mind raced through the ritual she performed every day: Did Annie finish her homework? Did she pack her lunch? Was her sweater set out? Every small action a form of armor. Maya remembered Annie’s tiny face, so open, so quick to smile—Maya would do anything to keep it that way. Even if that meant biting her tongue until it bled.

The morning unfurled in fits: the rattle of the newspaper, Tom’s grumbling about the cost of milk, the television’s shriek of morning news. When Annie padded into the kitchen—dragging her unicorn backpack, hair askew—Maya started breathing again. Her daughter, a living, breathing reason to hold fast.

“Morning, Mama,” Annie said, and Maya’s heart leaped with relief.

Tom didn’t look up. He never did when Annie entered—a deliberate decision, or perhaps simply indifference. For years, Maya waited for Tom to notice their child, to show some trace of the tenderness he’d offered in those electric early months of their marriage.

Maya had fallen for him when they were twenty-four: after a broken engagement, a flood of loneliness, an impulsive summer wedding. Tom had flowers and jokes and the confidence she craved. For a while, that was enough to keep her afloat. But confidence became control, and jokes became insults, then slaps, then something worse—a cycle Maya recognized, but couldn’t explain, not even to herself.

She poured cereal, nuzzled Annie’s hair, and packed the lunch. The school bus pulled up, momentary salvation in diesel and daylight. Annie turned, gave her mother a quick hug, and dashed through the door, leaving only the faint scent of strawberry shampoo in her wake.

Behind her, Tom finished his meal and stood. He stared at Maya for a long, baleful moment—as if searching for something that only ever slipped free in the shadows of memory.

“I’m going to be late,” he announced.

She nodded. “I’ll clean up here.”

“Don’t forget to call the electric company. And if that check bounces, you know whose fault it’ll be.”

“I know, Tom.” She kept her voice even, unaffected.

He left—a final slam of the door, a ripple running through the house, and Maya let herself shudder. She wiped the table, her hands raw and trembling. This was her space now, at least for the next few hours. With Tom gone, the air lightened, almost imperceptibly, like the weight of a storm cloud lifted before the first drops fell.

For months, Maya’s world had shrunk. Calls from her sister went unanswered, text messages left to rot in the digital ether. Friends dropped away, confused or frustrated by her distance. She used Annie as an excuse: “So much to do, just busy, you know how it is.”

A familiar pattern—women like her, hemming themselves in with silence. Each bruise, every harsh word, dissolved in the lie that “it’s not so bad.” She’d heard the statistics, years ago—reports about women battered by the people meant to love them most—but thought herself different, immune by virtue of some imagined strength.

Now, she understood: survival wasn’t about strength, but endurance, a willingness to bear invisible wounds while pretending they didn’t exist.

She leaned against the counter, considering the sunlight. How could it cut so sharply through glass and not burn away the darkness inside?

Here in her private hours, Maya mapped secret plans. She’d stashed twenty-dollar bills in the hollow of a broken floorboard, gathered pamphlets from the public library—shelters, legal resources, crisis hotlines. But the idea of escape was terrifying. How would Annie react? Would she forgive her mother for uprooting her life? Could Maya survive the aftermath, the guilt, the poverty, the uncertainty?

Each plan spiraled into fear. Each day she survived, she became more adept at hiding: covering bruises with foundation, laughter with silence, hope with resignation. Was this enough? She wanted to believe it was.

Some days, she phoned her mother’s voicemail and hung up without leaving a message. Some nights, she read fairy tales to Annie—a world where monsters were always defeated, where love was a shield, not a weapon.

“I love you the most,” Annie whispered, snuggling close, and Maya let herself believe it.

One evening, as rain lashed the windows, Maya sensed a change in the air. Tom was late, eyes bloodshot, slurring his anger at some unknown slight. He railed at the television, at the bills, at the cold stew she hurried to reheat. Annie cowered in her room, hugging her knees to her chest.

He turned on Maya. “Why can’t you ever do anything right? Is that too much to ask?”

She absorbed the words, flinched at the plate he hurled into the wall. She snapped into apology mode, a rote performance that sometimes worked, sometimes didn’t.

But something snapped in her. Maybe it was Annie’s frightened gasp, or seeing her own reflection in a shattered plate—a ghost trapped by her own fear. She remembered the money, the bus schedule, the bag she’d secretly packed weeks ago.

The next morning, when Tom left for work, Maya’s hands shook as she stuffed Annie’s favorite sweater into a backpack. She scribbled a note to Annie’s teacher, a slim lie improvised for safety. She pocketed her hidden cash, gripped Annie’s hand, and they slipped into the stormy street.

At the bus stop, Maya pressed the shelter pamphlet’s address into her memory. The ride was long—a blur of exhaust and anxiety—but they arrived at a low-slung, brick building with bars on the windows and a faded blue sign.

A shelter worker greeted them with tea and a smile that didn’t shrink from Maya’s pain.

That night, Maya and Annie huddled together in a narrow twin bed. Annie whimpered once, not quite asleep, and Maya brushed her fingers through the child’s hair.

“Are we going to be okay, Mama?”

Maya swallowed the terror, smoothing it into a promise. “Yes, baby. We’re going to be okay.”

The next weeks unspooled slowly. Maya’s wounds, both visible and invisible, clawed at her in waves. She listened to other women’s stories at the shelter—each as different, and yet as familiar, as her own. Some, like Maribel, had hidden for years. Others, like Lisa, left at the first punch. They spoke in coded language, laughter laced with sorrow. Maya found herself learning to trust again.

In the pale light of early morning, she helped Annie get ready for school. The child’s laughter returned with cautious resilience. Maya found a part-time job at a bakery, her hands kneading dough, slowly remembering the shape of hope.

Some nights, nightmares slithered in: Tom’s voice, the ache in her bones. She would wake with her heart racing. But Annie would reach for her with sleepy warmth, her small arms anchoring Maya to the present.

She began to see a counselor—soft-spoken, unflappable—who never flinched when Maya spoke her fears aloud. Each session peeled away a layer of shame, revealing the battered truth beneath. Maya learned that her wounds were real, that survival was not a weakness, that her story was not a cautionary tale, but a journey.

Months passed. Spring budded at the shelter windows. Maya applied for public housing with the help of her counselor and filled out legal paperwork: a restraining order against Tom. The process was slow, fraught with bureaucracy and reminders of the violence she had endured. But each form signed, each hour spent in waiting rooms, drew her further from the shadow that haunted her.

One afternoon, her phone vibrated with a text from her sister: I’m proud of you. We’re here when you’re ready. Tears sprang, sudden and unwelcome. Maya realized she’d spent so long constructing walls, she’d forgotten how to let anyone in.

She invited her sister for coffee the next week. Sitting in a sunny corner of a quiet café, Maya found her voice. She spoke of suffering, and survival, and Annie. Her sister wept, hugged her, and together they began the slow process of mending the fabric of family.

A year later, Maya and Annie moved into their own apartment. It was small—a single bedroom, orange carpet, pealing paint—but it was theirs. Annie drew a picture of their house, a wild, exuberant sketch taped to the fridge: two smiling stick figures, hand in hand.

Maya’s scars hadn’t faded. Some days, she stumbled. Certain sounds—shouts, slammed doors, thunder—still sent jolts through her veins. But she held on, compelled by the quiet courage she discovered in herself, and in Annie’s eyes.

She began volunteering at the shelter, gently coaxing frightened newcomers out of silence. She told her story, not as a victim, but as proof that even invisible wounds could be healed.

In time, laughter became honest again. The kitchen, once a battleground, was filled with warmth and promise—batches of cookies, Annie’s off-key singing, Maya’s quiet, persistent joy.

And at night, when Maya lay awake—softly brushing away the last tendrils of fear—she no longer saw herself as broken. She was patchwork, stitched together by love, endurance, and the unstoppable will to survive.
