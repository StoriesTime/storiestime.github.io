Rising Tides: Overcoming Challenges in the Crucible of Adolescence


It was no secret that the Horner family was approaching Rockefeller Plaza from the opposite side of sunbathers and selfie-stick wielding tourists. Still, as the waves of the Atlantic crashed ferociously to Delaney’s right and the proud beams of the midtown skyline braved left, she couldn’t help but feel the weight of adolescence tug forcefully at her heartstrings, as if to measure just how willingly she’d cling to the rocky bastion that was the edge of Manhattan.

She shifted her duffel bag, feeling the frayed edges of the worn canvas scream against her palm. They were going to be late to the audition; no doubt about that. But as Delaney took a steadying breath and exhaled fully, a sense of determination percolated through her bones. For Delaney, the audition meant everything—this final stretch of high school was more than just algebraic formulae and honors literature analysis; this was her chance to be heard.

Delaney had always been the quietest of her three siblings. True, she had sisters feisty as the tides of the Atlantic that she’d left frothing behind them that morning. Aisling with her sultry mix of worn leather and thorned roseblossoms, she’d regale tales of high school escapades, conspiring in secret whispers; only leaving Delaney to wonder at the edge of her hearing if her name was ever so much as threaded into the tapestry.

But Eden, sullenly defiant with a nowhere glance—a melancholic crescent moon caught mid-wink—she’d shut herself away in her bathroom, the doorway colored with neon paint and expanded poetry, ceramic masks rotting along the floors. Eden embodied how the Horner family—erratic with secrets and whispered loyalties—tried to hold themselves together, like sand castles staunch against the relentless tide of equally fractured families.

Big cities had never been Delaney’s forte. Lights, sounds, and what felt like miles of concrete jungle suffocating her—he was her escape, in a world of quiet pounding rain on rooftops, sunset conversations, and the dissenting hum of frogs and crickets. His words were a beacon—a horizon of potential and the boundless ocean where she could find solace. And now, asking her to navigate the rain-soaked streets of the city alone felt like departing the safety of her seaside home enticed by sirens luring storm-tossed sailors to their doom.

As the crowds engulfed them, Delaney couldn’t help but look around cautiously, scanning for any sign of him. Hellbent as the city was to swallow her whole, she knew that if she could just find him, everything would fall into place. She had taken chances before, but none quite like the one she’d taken when she’d spoken to him for the first time.

In the backseat of her mother’s car, amidst the smell of damp raincoats and a surfeit of multicolored emoticons and sticky notes scrawled along the skyline of ancient road trips, she’d written: “Do you believe that our hearts flutter like the moth to a flame or are they more like the ocean waves bound by the mountains, persistently pushing against the inevitable?”

It would be days before she received his carefully handwritten response. “Dear Delaney, the ocean may heed the cry of mountains but once a moth is ensnared, it holds its fire.” She couldn’t understand then the depth that just a few words could reach. All she knew was that she’d told a stranger her moonlit musings, and in return, he’d given her the empathy she had so desperately craved.

And somehow, in the chaos of this city, this seaside city—maybe because it was home to countless souls aching for connection or maybe because the air was just thick with the pulsating rhythm of life—she’d started to turn to him amid the youth-strangled summer storms. Less fireside heart-to-heart or late-night pillow talk, but quick message exchanges between streaking stars, and truthful smiley faces pressed on the concrete mosaic of this geodesic city.

“Are we really going to be late, Delaney?” Shauna’s voice eventually pierced through her contemplative stupor.

“It doesn’t matter,” Delaney mumbled, gathering her waning courage. “Because I’ll make them hear me loud and clear.”

The New York stage was deafeningly hot, blinding white, and the ushers seemed no more than shadows skulking in the wings. The stage manager had a voice so cold it iced over the words, but Delaney’s gaze transfixed itself to the rows and rows of anonymous eyes. When they’d hastily introduced her, she’d momentarily lost all words in the static that inundated her senses. But when she opened her mouth, she felt the impossible to be true—a singular voice conquering the silence of the theater.

The lights shimmered in her eyes as she recited the words that whispered to the vulnerability that ebbed and flowed like the high tide. The pain in which she’d carried the weight of her family and the promise she’d made; the sea and sky had molded her in both light and shadow. Her bold voice alone filled every seat with a crescendoing swell, calling upon the essence of the New York skyline to reverberate with the truth she sang with the daunting city skyline as her backdrop.

In this crucible of adolescence, Delaney Horner shone with a luminescence of resilience and strength that belied the tensions she carried in her grasp of the fleeting hours she had left. As she held her breath with each uttered word, she found solace in the beaming faces that watched her. Her stage cries echoed fervently with fire in the clarion call of Shakespeare’s world; she, the rising tide that overcame young life’s challenges. The sanctuary she found within the resonance of her voice, the fierce horizon she dared to dream upon—as ephemeral as the ocean mist—marked her passage into womanhood.

And as she stole a glance at Shauna—her loyal storm-trooper by her side—she knew, as they made their way back into the relentless tide of the city, she’d defy public perception to hold onto the horizon, amidst the crowded cinema of lives unfolding on this concrete carousel.

“Delaney,” the stage manager called, his voice softened, “You were extraordinary tonight.”

But as the lights blinded her entirely, she didn’t need to turn toward the voice of encouragement, but keenly appreciated the distant echo of thunder over the restless sea—a specter of an altogether different conflicting tone; one carried within her from her conversations with him. How apt and synonymous were these phantom voices to each other—the sky and the sea.
