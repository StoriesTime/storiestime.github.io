Friend Requests and Fake Realities: The Cost of Connection


Emma hesitated. She didn’t recall ever meeting an Alice before, but her profile seemed harmless enough—a smiling woman in her late twenties, her bio a blur of clichés: “Love life. Nature enthusiast. Always seeking adventure.” Scrolling through her public posts revealed busy cafes, sweeping mountain landscapes, and the occasional shot of a book or cup of coffee captioned with inspirational quotes. It felt generic, but harmless. The vast digital world was full of strangers, after all, each extending the intangible hand of connection.

Emma accepted.

As soon as she did, Alice reached out via chat: “Thanks for accepting! I found you through a mutual friend and just knew we’d click. How are you?”

The message felt warm, even flattering, as though Alice had put genuine thought into her willingness to reach out. Emma replied cautiously, keeping pleasantries short, but Alice was persistent. Over the following weeks, their exchanges evolved well beyond small talk. Alice seemed to pour her energy into the friendship, regularly sending messages filled with personal anecdotes, funny memes, or links to articles that perfectly fit Emma’s interests. And then there were the compliments—simple validations like, “You’re so insightful,” or “Your taste in music is amazing.” It was tough not to feel seen, especially when conversations with IRL friends had grown increasingly mundane.

Alice started tagging her in posts. Hikes Emma had never joined, recipes she hadn’t made. But when Alice uploaded a picture of a smiling woman sitting across a café table—someone distinctly unfamiliar—she captioned it: “Coffee date with my amazing friend Emma ❤️.”

Emma stared at her screen for several long moments. It felt surreal, watching this narrative of friendship form online, one she hadn’t lived yet. Confused but hesitant to confront, she let it pass. Maybe it was just a joke, or Alice’s way of exaggerating their connection.

But then there were other posts. Alice shared stories of heartfelt conversations they’d supposedly had, inside jokes they’d never made, days they hadn’t spent together. She painted a vibrant portrait of camaraderie, so vivid that friends of friends began reaching out to Emma, commenting on her posts: “Wish I could meet you two one of these days!” or “Your friendship with Alice looks so special.”

Emma wasn’t sure what unsettled her more—the falseness of Alice’s account, or how easily others accepted it as truth.

It didn’t end there. Within a month, Alice’s reality began bleeding into Emma’s own life. Mutual acquaintances appeared, people Emma hadn’t met but who claimed connections to her through Alice. One called her up unexpectedly and asked if she’d like to attend a hiking trip he’d planned. Emma didn’t even like hiking, but she felt pulled into the deepening web of perception Alice had spun.

Part of her wanted to sever the tie, blocking Alice and reclaiming her sense of autonomy. But the heavy guilt of disappearing under the guise of what was, outwardly, just harmless connection held her back. Alice wasn’t malicious—she seemed kind, supportive, and genuinely interested in Emma’s life. It was only the strangeness of it all that unsettled her. Cutting her off might make Emma seem like the irrational one.

Weeks passed, and Emma allowed the friendship to grow, though cautiously. She started probing Alice’s interactions, dissecting her posts, trying to unravel any threads binding the false realities she authored. But every time Emma attempted to unmask Alice’s intentions, the answer eluded her. Alice remained supportive and attentive, yet her alternate version of their lives continued painting a brighter, richer picture than Emma could reconcile.

One night, curiosity finally wrestled its way to victory. With hesitant fingers, Emma searched deeper into Alice’s profile. From the photos to tagged posts, the comments down the threads—all spoke to Alice’s vibrant life filled with dozens of friends, milestones, and connections. But as Emma examined the names one by one, she noted something amiss. Nearly all of Alice’s friends shared eerily polished profiles, brimming with success, adventurous outings, and overly generic anecdotes. No one seemed real—not in the messy, complicated way people actually existed.

Emma pulled up one “friend” by name: Jared, a supposed artist in Chicago. His posts reflected a glamorous lifestyle—the kind that blossomed with a suspicious lack of effort. But tracing back, Emma realized his photos used suspiciously repetitive stock aesthetics, tags linking to profiles that circled back into Alice’s pseudo network.

Her pulse quickened. It was all fabricated. Alice wasn’t just curating Emma’s role in her narratives; she had created a universe entirely out of artificial connection. Each relationship was a thread delicately formed and digitally nurtured, part of a fictional tapestry designed to paint Alice as the center of an intricate and fulfilling community.

Emma felt ill.

When Alice messaged later that evening, her tone as friendly as ever, asking how Emma’s week had been, Emma decided she couldn’t let the charade stretch further. “Alice,” she typed slowly, deliberately, “I need to ask you something. Why do you make things up about our friendship?”

The response came quickly, almost breathlessly, but with calculated warmth: “What do you mean? I love sharing our moments together. It keeps me connected.”

“Connected to what?” Emma pressed, her heart hammering. “Nothing you post seems real.”

A full minute passed without reply. Emma wondered if maybe she’d finally crossed an unspoken line. But the answer came in fragments: “It’s not fake, I swear… I just… I don’t know how else to create bonds in a world where everyone’s already too busy being strangers. Doesn’t everyone exaggerate online anyway? It’s just a more exciting way to live.”

Emma closed the chat without responding. Her hands trembled. Whatever Alice believed didn’t matter anymore. What shook her more was the realization of how much time she had invested in their friendship—built entirely on distortions, on fabrications that blurred the line between reality and what people only wanted others to see.

That night, she deleted Alice and scrubbed her own profile clean. It was a small act of catharsis, but as Emma stared at the blank space left in Alice’s absence, a gnawing suspicion lingered that somewhere, in her fractured digital archives, Alice would persist—posting photos of mythical coffee dates, laughing at jokes Emma never made, and inviting strangers into the world of meticulously curated falsehoods.

The cost of connection had been too great.
