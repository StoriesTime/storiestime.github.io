In Their Shoes: Empathy and the Fight Against Racism


It was a warm spring afternoon in Brookline, and the soft hum of chatter filled the coffee shop on the corner of Cedar Street. Maya sat in her usual spot by the window, her journal open to a blank page. A half-finished latte sat cooling beside her, and the light streaming through the glass painted golden patterns on her brown skin. She had come here to write, as always, but today, the words wouldn’t come.

Across the room, a group of college students chatted animatedly about an upcoming protest. They were planning to march downtown in solidarity with a campaign calling for justice for victims of racial violence. Maya caught snippets of their conversation—their passion was infectious, their determination evident. Yet, despite her own principles, something held her back from joining them. She had been to marches before, felt the rhythm of collective resistance, but lately, the cause itself felt distant. She had grown tired of the arguments, the rancor, and the silence that followed when the world moved on.

Maya glanced back at her journal, her pen resting idle in her hand. Growing up as a Black woman in a predominantly white community, she had experienced prejudice more times than she could count. Sometimes it was overt, like the time a teacher had doubted her math skills despite her straight-A record. More often, though, it was the subtle things—people assuming her opinions came with an agenda, the surprise in someone’s voice when they learned of her accomplishments, or the unease in their eyes when she spoke about her experiences.

But sympathy wasn’t the same as understanding, and lately, Maya had started to wonder if people truly wanted to see through her eyes or if they simply wanted to acknowledge the pain without fully knowing it.

The bell above the door chimed, pulling her from her thoughts. A man stepped in, his lanky frame casting a shadow across the polished floor. He wore a weathered leather jacket and carried an old satchel slung over one shoulder. His presence immediately caught Maya’s attention, not because there was anything particularly striking about him but because he walked with a cautious deliberateness, as if he were sizing up the whole room. She watched as he ordered a coffee, scanned the café, and then, to her surprise, approached her table.

“Excuse me,” he said, his voice deep but gentle. “All the other seats are taken. Mind if I sit here?”

Maya hesitated for half a second before pulling her bag closer to make room. “Sure.”

He set his cup down and sank into the chair opposite her. He introduced himself as Oliver, offering a small, almost reluctant smile. Maya nodded politely and went back to staring at her blank page, certain he would simply drink his coffee in silence. Instead, he leaned forward, his gaze fixed on her journal.

“You’re a writer?” he asked.

“Trying to be,” she replied, making no effort to hide her doubt. “Sometimes, the words don’t cooperate.”

“What are you working on?”

Maya sighed. “That’s the problem. I don’t know. I want to write something meaningful, something that gets people to… I don’t know… feel something. But I’m not sure how.”

Oliver nodded thoughtfully, as if weighing her words. Then, after a beat, he said, “I might have something that could help.”

She raised an eyebrow. “Oh?”

He reached into his satchel and pulled out what appeared to be an old, scuffed pair of sneakers. They were plain—white with black trim—worn down in the way shoes get when they’ve carried someone through years of travel and toil.

Maya frowned. “Shoes?”

“These are no ordinary shoes,” Oliver said, his voice lowering to a conspiratorial whisper. “They let you see the world through someone else’s eyes. You wear them, and suddenly, their memories, experiences, and emotions… they all become yours.”

Maya stared at him, unsure whether to roll her eyes or laugh. “You can’t be serious.”

“I am,” he replied, matter-of-factly. “Call it magic, science, or whatever you want, but they work. And if you’re looking to write something that helps people feel, there’s no better way than walking in someone else’s shoes.”

Despite her skepticism, there was something about his earnestness that intrigued her. She glanced down at the shoes, wondering if this was some elaborate prank. But curiosity got the better of her.

“What’s the catch?” she asked.

“No catch,” Oliver said. “Just choose whose shoes you want to step into. Close your eyes, think about it, and these will take you there.”

Maya reached out tentatively, her fingers brushing against the aged leather. They were warm to the touch, as if they’d been left out in the sun. She looked back at Oliver, who gave her an encouraging nod.

“Alright,” she muttered. “Let’s give this a shot.”

The moment she slipped the shoes on, the world dissolved around her. Maya’s stomach lurched as if she were falling, and then, just as suddenly, she landed—though “landed” wasn’t quite the right word. She wasn’t in her body anymore. She wasn’t even in her own time.

She stood on a dirt road, her feet barefoot and aching. Around her stretched open fields, the air heavy with the scent of earth and sweat. Her hands—though not her own—were calloused, gripping the handles of a plow. Her back burned from hours of labor under the relentless sun.

She realized she was in the body of a young man, likely in his late teens. His skin was dark, his voice silent, but his thoughts screamed within her. He worked on a plantation in the Deep South, sometime in the mid-1800s. His fear and anger were palpable, but so was his determination—to survive, to endure, to find a way to freedom, even against impossible odds.

As Maya moved through his day, she experienced his exhaustion, his longing for the family he had been torn from, and the indignities he suffered under the watchful eyes of overseers. But she also felt his quiet defiance, the small ways he held onto his humanity. A song hummed under his breath, a whispered prayer before bed—these were his acts of resistance.

Before she could process it all, the world shifted again.

This time, she found herself in a cramped apartment in 1940s Harlem. She was a woman now, her hair styled in pin curls, her fingers expertly stitching a hem. She worked as a seamstress, saving every penny to send her son to college. Around her, the air buzzed with the sounds of jazz drifting in from the streets below, mixing with the distant clanging of pots and pans. She was proud, resilient, and fiercely protective. Yet, she carried the weight of knowing that no matter how much she achieved, the world would always see her as less than her white counterparts.

The shoes took her to other lives after that—a factory worker in the 1960s fighting for equal pay, a teenager during the Civil Rights Movement standing face-to-face with armed officers, a teacher in the 1990s navigating veiled racism in the school system. Each experience was vivid, raw, and overwhelming.

When Maya finally returned to herself, she found tears streaming down her face. She kicked off the shoes, staring at them as if they were alive. When she looked up, Oliver was gone.

The next day, Maya attended the protest downtown. She marched alongside the college students she had overheard, her voice blending with theirs as they chanted for justice and change. But this time, something felt different. The stories she had lived through the shoes weren’t just abstract ideas anymore—they were part of her.

And as she marched, Maya realized she had found her words. Not just for her journal, but for the conversations and actions that lay ahead.
