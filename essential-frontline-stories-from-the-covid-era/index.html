Essential: Frontline Stories from the COVID Era


Shawn’s day began before sunrise, hours before the rest of the city even thought to stir. The world outside the hospital’s glass façade was ghostly pale, quieted by a pandemic that stalked the streets. He walked the dim hallway with that strange mixture of exhaustion and heightened awareness, every step sounding twice as loud since the volume of the world had dialed so low. Today’s shift would run twelve hours, same as yesterday and tomorrow.

The hospital cafeteria, now rearranged for “distancing,” still smelled of old coffee and bleach. Shawn poured himself a cup—when had he last tasted coffee that wasn’t burnt or reheated? Someone had left donuts donated by a local bakery, each individually wrapped in plastic. He grabbed one, feeling childish delight. Across the room, a younger nurse, Priya, was reading a text under the glow of her phone. She looked up.

“Hey, Dr. Weaver. Ready to save the world?”

He smiled, only halfway joking. “Depends. Could the world maybe save itself for once and give us the day off?”

She grinned. The joke didn’t hold up well—it was one they’d all made before, but some days, the endurance felt like an act of bravery.

Chapter Two: The Arrival

Eight o’clock brought its usual chaos: an ambulance squealed to a stop outside the ER’s sliding doors. The paramedics signaled frantically as they wheeled in a middle-aged man with shallow breaths and sweating skin. The triage nurse called over her shoulder, “Mr. Losada, 47, oxygen saturation at 81. No significant medical history.”

It was astonishing, just how vulnerable healthy-seeming bodies could be; the virus cared nothing for calendars, diets, or gym memberships. Shawn made quick decisions—order labs, start high-flow oxygen, page respiratory therapy, call for a COVID swab.

Mr. Losada’s wife, Maria, wasn’t allowed inside. From out in the cold, behind two layers of glass, Shawn promised her they would do everything possible. She pressed her palm to the window. For a moment, beneath his PPE—mask, goggles, double gloves—that gesture reached him, a sadness so pungent it was almost physical.

He thought, not for the first time, of his own parents. He hadn’t hugged them in thirteen months.

Chapter Three: Blue Tape on the Floor

The inside edges of the hospital became a patchwork map of colored tape—blue for “safe zones,” red for “contaminated” areas, yellow for “buffer.” The whole building a living, breathing organism now, changing rules by the hour as scientists fed updates from a world scrambling to understand.

In the ICU, machines beeped slow and sorrowful in the dimmed light. Nurses moved quietly, shoulders bowed by stress. Patients lay in narrow beds behind glass, many sedated and so deeply unconscious they wouldn’t remember any of this.

Priya briefed the team. “Mr. Losada just dropped to 75 percent O2. We ramped up to 100 percent, but he’s tachycardic. He keeps asking if he’ll see his wife.”

Even now, in the worst moments, it was the small, human questions that cut deepest: Will I see my family? How long will this last? Who else is here with me?

The physicality of fear lingered in the room like a fog.

Shawn braced himself for another round. He wasn’t sure if the sweat running down his back was from the heavy gear or his own nerves.

Chapter Four: The Numbers Game

Outside, the daily numbers—the ones reported on evening news segments—clicked steadily upwards. Shawn’s phone buzzed with updates; deaths, cases, recoveries, hospitalizations. An abstract scoreboard, but inside these walls, the numbers had faces: Mrs. Simon, 82, sweet as lavender soap; Andre, an orderly, out with symptoms himself and waiting for his test results.

There was frustration, too, from the public—a push and pull between caution and “returning to normal.” Sometimes on his way home, Shawn saw parks full of masked joggers running in wide, looping circles around family picnics, and he wondered how much longer everyone could keep balancing hope against risk.

Laurie, a respiratory therapist with a dry sense of humor and the stamina of a mountain goat, said it best one afternoon: “Numbers are just the outline. But people—they’re the story you fill in.”

Chapter Five: Turning Points

As spring turned to summer, the stories kept changing. New treatments emerged—monoclonal antibodies delivered by nervous hands, remdesivir administered in tense quiet. Still, nothing was certain. Every patient was a new equation.

One afternoon, Priya asked, “Do you remember March? When we were clapping for healthcare workers at 7 p.m.?”

Shawn nodded. “Honestly, I think it was for all of us. For surviving.”

Some nights he lay awake, sifting through the faces. Patients he’d known for just hours whose deaths haunted him more than those after years of routine.

But there were miracles. Mr. Losada made it through: one toe over the line, then another. The first time he called his wife—shaky, breathless, voice crackling over the hospital room’s tablet screen—everyone teared up. It was a small win, a rare one, and the kind that fuelled two more days, three more shifts.

Chapter Six: The Supply Closet

A running joke in the breakroom: “Inventory is just another word for improvisation.” Early in the crisis, Shawn and Priya had learned to ration PPE, find creative uses for plastic wraps, and reuse face shields with the steady professionalism of stagehands in a never-ending play.

Once, the head nurse, setting down a box of donated homemade masks, said, “I keep things, just in case”—and produced a misshapen N95 with pink doodles from her daughter. They all agreed: homemade or not, kindness went a long way in fortifying the soul.

At night, Shawn would see the janitorial staff working long after everyone had gone home, wiping down chairs and fixing window latches. They didn’t wear stethoscopes or white coats, but they, too, belonged to this fabric called “essential.”

Chapter Seven: Worlds Collide

Shawn’s days blurred, his own apartment morphing into little more than a staging area. He kept his distance from neighbors, who nodded politely in hallways, sometimes leaving unsolicited care packages of granola bars and bottled water at his door. He felt a strange, pervasive guilt for retreating into his work, leaving texts from old friends unanswered for weeks at a time.

One evening, he attended a virtual birthday for his nephew. The entire family popped up in digital boxes—laughing, masked, singing a fractured “Happy Birthday” that echoed. From the screen, his nephew (now seven) held up a tiny sign: “Thank you Unky Shawn! Hope you stay healthy!”

Shawn’s chest twisted. There were victories everywhere, but reminders of what they’d lost, too: hugs, handshakes, crowded tables, lazy Sunday afternoons.

Chapter Eight: The Vaccine Arrives

The tone finally shifted that winter—buzzing, nervous anticipation as vials of the vaccine arrived. The air practically shimmered with hope, and the smell of alcohol swabs and band-aid adhesive filled the hallways.

Priya, needling Shawn in the arm as he sat, quipped, “Doctor’s orders: try not to faint on me.”

He grinned. “After this past year? I’ll take my chances.”

Staff gathered for photos—eyes crinkling above masks, thumbs up around plastic syringes. Across the country and the city, headlines flashed: “A Promising Step Forward.” Staff cheered, not as much for the science, but for the prospect of the ordinary: regular dinners; seeing parents; crowded, happy hospital lobbies again.

Still, the work wasn’t finished—the virus taught them humility. New variants seeded anxiety, the surge waxed and waned, and yet, they adapted. Resilience, after all, was part and parcel of the job now.

Chapter Nine: Aftershocks

Months later, the famished city awoke gradually, sleepwalking into something like normalcy. The hospital was fuller—now with patients not just of COVID, but all the other usual suspects. The post-pandemic landscape was different. Priya confessed to Shawn she’d started therapy. “It’s just… we witnessed so much grief and couldn’t do anything for so many people. Never felt so helpless.”

Shawn nodded. “It’s not just grief. It’s all the other stuff we carried—the little fears. The feeling you’re about to break, even when you keep going.”

They agreed: resilience wasn’t just about enduring but recognizing when the weight was too much.

Chapter Ten: The Commons

More than a year after the nightmare began, the hospital gifted staff a new space—a garden terrace. Once a barren roof, it now brimmed with potted sunflowers and hummingbird feeders. Staff were invited, in staggered shifts, to sit among trees and plastic deck chairs, the city skyline stretching behind them.

Shawn looked out over the rooftops, sipping a coffee that almost tasted like real coffee. In that moment, the city was both haunting and beautiful—marked by scarcity, certainly, but also by every act of ordinary courage that filled the last two years.

He pulled out his phone and texted his parents:
Let’s plan dinner. Outside, masks off. I miss you.

Across the terrace, Priya laughed at something Laurie said. Shawn watched the sun move slowly, tracing shadows over everything. It reminded him of how they’d measured time during the worst of it—not only by losses or struggles, but by every story of resilience, friendship, and hope that persisted.

And as the city exhaled in cautious optimism, he understood: the term “essential” was never about a particular job description. It was about - showing up, despite the odds; choosing compassion, even while afraid; weaving strength out of the smallest moments, day after difficult day.

The world didn’t save itself, no. It was saved, piece by piece, by every essential story lived on the front lines—and, perhaps most enduringly, by the way those stories carried forward, into whatever would come next.
