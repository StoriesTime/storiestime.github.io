Beneath the Northern Lights: A Journey Through Arctic Traditions


The wind was biting, a sharp reminder that the Arctic was not a place to be taken lightly. Snowflakes whirled through the air, creating a veil of white that seemed to stretch forever. But beneath this frigid expanse, there was warmth—a warmth born not of fire, but of ancient traditions that had endured through generations, sheltered in the shadow of the mighty northern lights.

Mikael adjusted the fur-lined collar of his parka, feeling the cold nip at his cheeks as he gazed out across the endless expanse of snow. He had traveled far from his home in Stockholm, but he was drawn north by a calling, one that tugged at the very core of his being. It was more than curiosity—it was a deep-seated need to understand a way of life that had survived against the odds.

His journey had brought him to a remote village in the far north of Sweden, nestled in the heart of the Lapland region. Here, the indigenous Sámi people had lived for centuries, their traditions as old as the landscape itself. The village was small, with only a handful of houses scattered around the edge of a frozen lake, but it was alive with stories, wisdom, and a deep connection to the land.

The Northern Lights, known locally as the Aurora Borealis, painted the sky in shimmering waves of green, purple, and blue, as though the heavens themselves were alive with spirit. This was a sight Mikael had only ever seen in photographs, but standing beneath the vast, starry sky, he felt the weight of its beauty and mystery.

It was late in the evening when Mikael met Laila, a Sámi elder who had agreed to show him the rhythms of their world. She was tall and strong, her weathered face a map of the land she had lived on all her life. Her eyes, however, were soft with a knowing kindness, as though she had seen the rise and fall of many seasons. She greeted Mikael with a nod and a smile that made him feel welcome despite the starkness of the surroundings.

“Follow me,” she said, her voice low but filled with authority.

They walked together across the snow-covered landscape, the village fading into the distance behind them. Laila led him toward a traditional Sámi tent, a lavvu, its cone-shaped frame made from wooden poles covered with animal skins. The smoke from a small fire curled up from a chimney atop the structure, filling the cold air with the scent of burning pine. Inside, the warmth of the fire greeted them, casting flickering shadows on the walls.

The lavvu was modest, yet it felt like the center of the world—a place where generations had gathered to share stories, songs, and knowledge. Laila motioned for Mikael to sit beside the fire. He obeyed, settling down on the soft reindeer pelts that lined the floor.

The fire crackled, and Laila began to speak, her voice steady and calm as she told Mikael the story of her people.

The Sámi People: Guardians of the North

The Sámi, Laila explained, were one of the oldest indigenous groups in Europe, with a rich history dating back thousands of years. Their ancestors had lived in the far reaches of the Arctic long before borders were drawn and nations were established. They were the original stewards of the land, caretakers of the tundra, the forests, and the seas.

“They are people of the reindeer,” Laila said, her words carrying a weight of deep reverence. “For us, the reindeer are not just animals. They are a part of our soul, our spirit. Our entire way of life revolves around them. Their migration paths are our paths. Their seasons are our seasons.”

Mikael listened intently as Laila described the complex relationship between the Sámi and the reindeer. It was not just a matter of livelihood, but of spirituality. The reindeer were sacred creatures, integral to the Sámi’s beliefs and traditions. The annual migration of the herds was more than just a physical journey; it was a reflection of the rhythms of nature itself.

“The reindeer are our teachers,” Laila continued. “We learn from them how to adapt to the land, to respect the changing seasons. Their movements guide us, just as the Northern Lights guide us through the dark nights of winter.”

Mikael was fascinated by how the Sámi had woven their lives so seamlessly into the natural world around them. Everything—from their homes and clothing to their rituals and beliefs—was connected to the land. Even their language, with its deep reverence for nature, reflected this bond.

“Do you see the Northern Lights?” Laila asked, looking toward the sky. The shimmering colors had intensified, swirling in mesmerizing patterns. “For us, they are not just beautiful lights. They are the spirits of our ancestors, dancing in the sky. When we pass, we become a part of the lights, and our spirits will join them, guiding future generations.”

Mikael’s eyes followed her gaze to the heavens. He had always known the scientific explanation for the Aurora Borealis, but hearing Laila’s words imbued the spectacle with a deeper, almost sacred meaning.

“The Northern Lights,” Laila continued, “are a symbol of life and death, of the cyclical nature of our existence. Nothing is ever truly gone. It lives on in the sky, in the winds, and in the snow.”

A Day in the Life of the Sámi

The next morning, Mikael was up before the sun, eager to experience more of Sámi life. Laila had invited him to join her family on a reindeer herding expedition. Wrapped in layers of wool and fur, he trudged alongside the Sámi men and women, their faces set with determination as they navigated the snow-covered tundra. The snow was deep, but the reindeer seemed to move effortlessly through it, their hooves finding purchase on the frozen ground.

Mikael marveled at the skill with which the Sámi handled their animals. Each member of the family had their role—some were skilled in guiding the reindeer, while others kept watch for predators or dangers on the horizon. The Sámi had a language that was uniquely suited to their environment, filled with words for the subtle shifts in the weather, the movements of the animals, and the changes in the landscape.

As they worked, Laila explained more about the Sámi’s way of life. “Our traditions are based on respect for nature. We take only what we need, and we give thanks for what the land provides. When a reindeer is slaughtered, we use every part of the animal—nothing is wasted. The meat sustains us, the hides keep us warm, the bones become tools, and the antlers are carved into handles for knives.”

The reindeer herding was more than just a livelihood—it was a ritual, a sacred duty that connected each person to the earth. Mikael was in awe of how seamlessly tradition and survival blended together. He had always thought of modern life as something that had moved far beyond the need for such practices, but here, in the wilds of the Arctic, he was witnessing a living, breathing tradition that had endured for millennia.

The Sámi Festival: A Celebration of Life

That evening, Laila invited Mikael to a gathering in the village, where the Sámi people celebrated the return of the sun after the long, dark winter. The festival was held in honor of the Sami Siida, a traditional community gathering that marked the end of the harshest months and the beginning of the season of renewal.

The air was filled with the sound of drums and chanting as the villagers gathered around a large bonfire. The fire flickered brightly in the darkness, its flames dancing in time with the rhythm of the drums. Mikael was invited to join in, and though he did not fully understand the significance of the dances, he felt the energy of the community—the sense of belonging, of being a part of something far larger than himself.

Laila explained that the festival was not just a celebration of the sun’s return, but also a time to honor the spirits of the land, the animals, and the ancestors who had come before them. As the night wore on, the sky above was again illuminated by the Northern Lights, and the people danced beneath them, their movements a reflection of the ancient rituals that had sustained them through the centuries.

Reflections Under the Northern Lights

The following day, as Mikael prepared to leave the village and continue his journey, he found himself standing on the frozen lake, gazing up at the Northern Lights one last time. The colors danced across the sky, more vivid now than ever before.

Laila had given him a small token—a carved reindeer antler pendant—as a symbol of his journey and his connection to the Sámi people. “The lights will guide you, just as they guide us,” she had said. “May they always show you the way.”

As the cold wind blew across the lake, Mikael felt a deep sense of gratitude. He had come to the Arctic seeking answers, but what he had found was something far more profound: a way of life rooted in respect, resilience, and a deep connection to the earth. Beneath the Northern Lights, he had witnessed not just the beauty of the land, but the enduring spirit of a people whose traditions would continue to guide them for generations to come.

The lights swirled overhead, and for a moment, Mikael felt as though he too had become a part of them—a small but meaningful thread in the tapestry of the Arctic’s timeless rhythms.
