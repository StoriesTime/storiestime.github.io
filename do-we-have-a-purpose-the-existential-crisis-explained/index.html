Do We Have a Purpose? The Existential Crisis Explained


In a modest café nestled between the blur of a bustling city and the quiet embrace of an old park, Julian nursed his lukewarm coffee. He wasn’t here for the coffee; he was here because he needed a moment, a pause, a space to rummage through the tangled threads of his thoughts. The question had been gnawing at him for weeks: Do we have a purpose?

Julian wasn’t a philosopher by trade, nor was he particularly religious. He was a software developer who had, until recently, measured the worth of his days in lines of code, successful projects, and promotion emails. But something inside him shifted, an invisible fracture in the smooth surface of daily routine, and he found himself caught in the grip of an unexpected crisis of meaning.

The Quiet Onset of an Existential Crisis

It began subtly, as these crises often do. A late-night email that went unanswered, an empty congratulatory message at the office, a birthday passed with little fanfare. These small moments whispered a disturbing truth—that something was missing. Not the thrill of success or the cheer of company, but something deeper, something fundamental.

Julian’s mind churned questions tirelessly: Why am I here? What is the point of all this? Is there a reason for the work I do, or the relationships I build, or the dreams I harbor?

He found himself staring out of the café window, watching people pass by—each on their own journeys, each their own universe of thoughts and doubts. Did they question this too? Did they find answers? Or did they simply move forward, day after day, anchored by invisible purposes or blind faith?

The Search for Meaning Across Time and Culture

Julian’s thoughts drifted through history, logic stepping in to illuminate the question from angles he hadn’t considered before. Across centuries and cultures, humanity has grappled with the very same question. The ancient Greeks believed in telos—a purpose or end goal to all things. Aristotle proposed that the highest human good was eudaimonia, sometimes equated to flourishing or happiness, attained by living virtuously.

In Eastern philosophies, the question was queried differently. Buddhism spoke of enlightenment, liberation from suffering and desire. Taoism urged harmony with the flow of the universe. Meanwhile, religious traditions like Christianity, Islam, and Judaism offered narratives where purpose was divinely ordained, a plan set forth by a higher power.

How did Julian’s quest fit within these vast traditions? More importantly—did relying on their answers resolve the unease, or simply reframe it?

The Science of Our Place in the Universe

Science, Julian realized, offered its own perspective. From the grand dance of galaxies to the minute workings of subatomic particles, existence follows no conscious plan discernible through empirical observation. Evolutionary biology explained life as an emergent phenomenon—life forms adapting and surviving through natural selection, not because of predetermined meaning, but due to survival advantages.

The existentialist philosophers of the 19th and 20th centuries painted a stark picture reflecting this scientific view: the universe indifferent to human hopes or ideals. Jean-Paul Sartre famously declared that existence precedes essence, which meant humans don’t come into the world with a built-in purpose; rather, each person must forge their own through free will and choices.

Julian found this simultaneously liberating and terrifying. If there really was no cosmic blueprint, then responsibility for meaning-making rested squarely on his shoulders—or any of ours.

The Psychological Dimensions of the Crisis

Feeling the weight of that freedom, Julian recognized a broader psychological truth. The existential crisis often surfaces when one confronts the finitude of life and the uncertainties of existence. It can lead to anxiety, dread, and depression—or become a transformative process steering people toward authenticity.

Modern psychology suggests this crisis fuels meaning-making, a vital human need. Viktor Frankl, a Holocaust survivor and psychiatrist, illustrated this in his work. He argued that even in the most horrific circumstances, humans could find meaning in suffering, creativity, or love.

Julian reflected on this: could his crisis be an invitation? A chance to peer beneath the surface of his automatic days and discover something truer, deeper?

Crafting a Personal Purpose

Sitting now with his thoughts, Julian understood that the search for purpose isn’t about uncovering some universal, one-size-fits-all truth. It’s about recognizing that meaning is handcrafted, emerging through engagement with life’s experiences.

He began jotting ideas. What energized him? What felt worthwhile? He realized that connection—human connection—mattered deeply to him. Moments when a friend’s laugh silenced his internal chatter, when he helped a junior colleague struggling with code, or when he simply listened without judgment.

Purpose, Julian decided, would be rooted in these moments of presence and care. Not an abstract concept or a monumental goal, but a mosaic of small acts, built day by day.

The Role of Creativity and Contribution

Julian’s mind wandered to the stories he loved—the novels that reshaped his outlook, the music that stirred his soul. The arts have long been recognized as vessels of meaning, channels through which people express, confront, and transcend existence.

He also thought about contribution: the impact one life can have on others and the world. Purpose could be expressed in creative work, acts of kindness, or seeking justice. The ripple effect of these choices defies cosmic indifference, carving significance into the fabric of life.

Accepting Uncertainty and Embracing Freedom

Perhaps the most profound lesson Julian encountered was embracing uncertainty itself. The existential condition is defined by its openness, the absence of clear answers about why. Yet this lack is what allows freedom.

If purpose is not handed down, it becomes a canvas, waiting for each person’s brushstrokes. While daunting, this openness invites authenticity—living life according to one’s values rather than prescribed narratives.

Julian felt a tentative peace settle in. The crisis hadn’t vanished, but it had changed shape. It was no longer a question to paralyze, but a process to live: exploring, creating, learning, and connecting.

A New Beginning

Julian finished his coffee, the cup now cold. He stepped outside into the busy street, carrying less weight and more hope. He didn’t have all the answers, and maybe never would. But in embracing the question itself, he found a new kind of purpose—not a destination, but a journey.

As he walked, he noticed a woman struggling with her stroller. Without hesitation, he offered help. She smiled, gratitude glowing in her eyes. A simple moment, but to Julian, it was as if a spark had ignited.

Do we have a purpose? Perhaps the answer is as varied as human life itself. For some, it’s faith; for others, philosophy, art, science, or connection. For Julian, and for many of us, purpose is crafted, discovered, and lived moment by moment—woven from the threads of choice, freedom, and love.

The existential crisis remains a shadow in the human story, but within it lies the possibility of freedom—a blank page waiting to be filled by every life daring enough to ask, and live, Why?
