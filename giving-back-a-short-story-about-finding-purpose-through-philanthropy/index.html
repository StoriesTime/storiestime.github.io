Giving Back: A Short Story About Finding Purpose Through Philanthropy


Simon Bellini never saw sunrise from the same place twice in one week. The soft click of his black suitcase—zip, snap, zip—marked another conference, or board meeting, or launch event in another city whose luxury hotels he judged by thread count and the way his name rolled off the bellhop’s tongue. Success, Simon often thought, had an odd echo to it. Loud applause in glass-walled meeting rooms, then a whistling silence when the doors slid closed behind him at night.

After selling his software company, Simon expected contentment—maybe even happiness, if only it came with the right label. He found himself on magazine covers, speaking panels, and, often, sitting alone at the window in his Manhattan penthouse, gazing out at a city that didn’t notice him.

It wasn’t until he attended Samantha Tran’s retirement party—a favor to an old venture capitalist friend—that someone finally asked him a question he hadn’t already mastered: “What are you doing for others, Simon?”

Samantha’s voice was gentle, but those words trailed Simon home, as relentless as the city’s nighttime buzz. In the glassy depths of three a.m., between one sip of whiskey and the next, Simon realized he didn’t have an answer.

Passing the Envelope

The very next week, a thick envelope landed on Simon’s marble countertop. Alexa, his assistant, looked sheepish. “A gentleman dropped it at the front desk,” she said, “and insisted it was for you.”

Inside: a handwritten letter from his childhood friend, Marcus Solano, principal of their old neighborhood’s elementary school in Richmond Hill. The words were neat, stoic, but Simon could feel the exhaustion pressed into the paper. Resources were threadbare: the library falling apart, after-school programs dissolving, parents running between two jobs and still behind. A quiet plea—did he remember the playground they’d built together in seventh grade? Would he join an advisory board to breathe life back into the school?

Simon stared at the skyline that night but saw Richmond Hill: chain-link fences rusty as autumn leaves, the gym with its cracked orange basketballs, and Mrs. Dorsey’s laughter echoing through the library stacks.

Lessons from the Library

A week later, Simon arrived at Richmond Hill Elementary with a cup of burnt diner coffee and an ill-fitting sense of nostalgia. Marcus’s office wall was lined with photographs: parents at science fairs, students in cardboard crowns, faded snapshots of the two of them battling in a sandlot baseball game.

“Are you looking for a donation, Marcus?” Simon asked.

Marcus tapped the letter on his desk. “I’m looking for you, Simon. Your head, your hands. The library’s moldy, the heating’s broken, and the students think computers are ten years old—because they are. We need more than just money.”

The first days, Simon felt awkward in the crumbling library. The curious stares of students made him fumble. When Mrs. Dorsey, now a retired volunteer, greeted him with a raspy, familiar laugh, some long-frozen part of Simon thawed.

They planned. Simon sketched digital literacy curriculums between calls with venture capitalists. He flew in tech volunteers, connected the school with free device drives, and called in favors from his network. Sometimes, he read picture books to first graders, his voice rusty, learning how to answer questions like, “Why do buses smell weird?” or “Is it true computers can get the flu?”

The Ripple Effect

The work was uneven, but within six months, a new computer lab took shape. Middle schoolers hacked together simple games, teaching each other basic coding. The library’s carpet was still a faded maroon, but now rows of donated books brightened each shelf, and a projector hung from the low ceiling. Simon started a storytelling club, challenged students to dream up futuristic cities, and watched as even the shyest found confidence at the podium.

The school’s spirit revived, and Simon felt an unfamiliar electricity. His phone rang with invitations to galas and summits, but he made excuses. He began eating cafeteria pizza with the kids and learning their names—Raj, Lila, Jamal, Sara. When one student handed him a thank you card, Simon braced for the generic “You’re the best!” Instead, it read, “All I ever wanted was a place to feel special. Thank you, Mr. Simon.”

New Roads and Old Roots

Philanthropy, Simon realized, was more than cutting checks. It meant sticking around for awkward silences, for moments of confusion and uncomfortable truths. It meant being present when the principal shared that half the students went home to empty kitchens, or when he saw the hunger in a child’s cautious smile.

Simon applied his business mind to new challenges—partnering with local grocery stores to fund weekend food bags, creating after-school robotics classes, setting up a scholarship fund for older students with leadership potential.

Each success made way for deeper challenges, and sometimes heartbreak. But Simon felt himself belong, not in the way magazine features promised, but in a way that stayed long after applause faded. With the students, he learned that the most meaningful gifts weren’t transactional. Sometimes the only thing you had to give was steady hope.

Finding Purpose

Word spread, as it always does. Other alumni from Richmond Hill reached out, bringing networks, resources, expertise. Job training programs blossomed, art supplies arrived in bulk, and local businesses began offering internships to high schoolers. The school’s walls, once chipped and silent, rang with after-hours projects and dances. Even the city noticed: Simon was named to task forces on urban education and hungry kids, panels previously beyond his concern.

In time, Simon’s portfolio expanded in a direction none of his spreadsheets could quantify. His definitions of wealth and status shifted—incrementally, then all at once. The luxury hotels blurred into each other, but in Richmond Hill, every cracked sidewalk and hand-painted mural was real.

One winter afternoon, as Simon stood at the school’s front steps helping organize winter coat donations, a small boy tugged at his sleeve. “Are you the man who fixed the computers?” the boy asked.

Simon, startled, nodded. The boy grinned. “You made it fun to stay late. Everybody wants to come back tomorrow.”

No private jet, watch collection, or silent auction could compare to that.

Lasting Change

Simon’s philanthropy drifted outward, rippling into new communities—urban food banks, literacy clinics, mentorship for first-generation college applicants. But Richmond Hill remained his anchor, a place where purpose wasn’t about headlines or endowments, but showing up, day after day.

In a city where everything changed in the blink of an app update, the school stood as proof that change—real, stubborn, hopeful change—was slow, persistent, and always incomplete. Giving back meant facing problems you couldn’t solve in a single meeting, sticking around for every messy part of the journey.

Simon’s business cards grew lighter. He turned down more speaking gigs, focusing on hands-on work. Sometimes he wondered if he’d wasted those first years chasing empty accomplishments. But as he watched students graduate to high school, and parents run successful bake sales for the first time, he knew the past was just the curriculum he’d needed to tackle the present.

A New Definition

Late one spring, Marcus and Mrs. Dorsey hosted a celebration: new books, a working greenhouse, another tech fair. Simon looked around at the faces—grinning, shouting, some beaming with the same restless energy that once pushed him from conference to conference. He felt, for the first time, fully visible.

Later, after the clean-up, Mrs. Dorsey and Marcus joined Simon outside. The city’s lights blinked on, familiar and distant. Simon turned to them, hands jammed in his pockets. “I used to think purpose was something you chased. Now, I think—it’s something you build, slowly, from wherever you started.”

Mrs. Dorsey smiled. “You found your way home, Simon.”

Maybe that was all philanthropy ever promised: not the ending of every struggle, but the start of a story that made the struggles matter. Of giving back because you were, finally, part of something bigger.

And in that place, Simon Bellini knew—he had found his purpose.
