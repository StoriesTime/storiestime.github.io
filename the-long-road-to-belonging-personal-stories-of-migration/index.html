The Long Road to Belonging: Personal Stories of Migration


Lina sat quietly at the edge of the bus station, watching the headlights of incoming buses cut through the thick fog. The sounds of the city—distant car honks, murmurs of conversation—felt like they belonged to someone else, not her. She had been in this place before, a place where time seemed to stretch into an eternity of uncertainty and waiting. Yet, it was different now, more daunting. Her journey had not begun here, but it was about to take another turn.

She thought of her village back in El Salvador, the one she left behind. The memories of lush green mountains, the soft sounds of children playing in the streets, and the warmth of her family’s embrace all felt distant, as though they belonged to another life entirely. But here, at this bus station in the heart of the United States, she was trying to find her footing, just like so many others around her.

Her journey had started years ago, but it had been one of continuous motion—an endless journey for belonging.

It was a rainy morning when Lina decided to leave. She remembered the rain falling in sheets, as if the heavens themselves were mourning the decision she had just made. At sixteen, she had become a part of the phenomenon known as the migrant caravan—a desperate, dangerous mass movement of people searching for a better future.

Her village had not been a place for dreams anymore. Violence had gripped it, with gangs controlling not just the streets but the very air people breathed. The small convenience store her father ran was no longer a source of income; it was just another point of extortion. She and her mother had often sat together, their hands working silently on the fabric of their lives—patching up old clothes, mending what was broken—but it was clear they were both patching up a larger wound that could never be fully healed.

She had spoken to her mother about leaving many times before. Her mother, always with a quiet tear in her eye, had begged her to stay. “Stay, Lina,” she would say. “Stay and help rebuild our lives.” But Lina knew, deep down, that the life she could rebuild here was not the one she wanted. It wasn’t the life she dreamed of when she was younger, staring up at the stars, wondering about the world beyond the hills.

One morning, the decision was made. She packed a small bag—just the essentials: a change of clothes, a photo of her mother and father, and a small Bible her grandmother had given her. She kissed her mother goodbye in the early hours, before the sun came up, and without another word, left.

Lina’s path wasn’t smooth. The journey was grueling, filled with uncertainty. She crossed the border into Guatemala and then Mexico, dodging authorities, relying on the kindness of strangers, and sometimes, the kindness of complete strangers who turned out to be far from kind. The caravan stretched for miles, winding through countries with no guarantee of safety, every step fraught with peril.

But the promise of a better life, of safety, kept her going. She would not give up. No matter how many times she fell, no matter how tired her body became, Lina kept walking, kept moving forward. She had to.

At one point, when they were in the middle of Mexico, the caravan stopped in a small town near the coast. It was there she met Ricardo. He was a young man, much older than Lina, but his quiet strength and steady gaze gave her a sense of stability that she hadn’t known she needed. They didn’t speak much at first, but they shared the same quiet determination. They were both in search of something—something that only the promise of a new life could offer.

Ricardo told her that his journey had started years before, but he had been stuck in the same cycle—trying to find work, finding none, and trying again. His family had once lived in the bustling streets of Tegucigalpa, Honduras, but he had left after his father was killed. The loss of his father had shattered their family, and Ricardo had hoped that in another country, there might be some chance to start over. But like so many others, he had found that starting over was a complex and often elusive goal. Still, he kept going.

For a brief moment in that coastal town, Lina and Ricardo shared a bond forged in shared experience. Their paths might have been different, but the journey was the same—both were searching for belonging in a world that was increasingly unwilling to offer it. They became traveling companions for a while, supporting each other through the long hours on the road. Eventually, they parted ways, both continuing to chase their dreams, though in different directions. But they would never forget each other, the way their paths had once crossed in a place between borders.

Lina eventually made her way to the United States, crossing the border in the dead of night with a small group of others. The air was cold, the ground uneven, and the sense of uncertainty even more tangible. But there, in the shadows, she felt the weight of the journey beginning to lift. The border was behind her. Now, all she had to do was survive what came next.

The next few months were spent in shelters, receiving aid, and trying to understand the complex web of systems that governed her new life. It was hard—harder than she had imagined. She had assumed that once she crossed into the United States, things would get easier, that the promise of a better life would immediately be waiting for her. But the truth was far more complicated. There were endless forms to fill out, applications to submit, interviews to attend, and above all, the constant fear of being caught and sent back.

Her English wasn’t good, and the people around her often looked at her with suspicion. She felt like an outsider in a world that was so different from everything she knew. The cold streets, the tall buildings, and the faces of strangers—none of them felt like home. She had no family here, no history. She was just one more immigrant, trying to survive in a country that often didn’t want her.

But Lina held on. She found a job as a housekeeper for a kind woman named Clara. Clara was patient with her, and over time, Lina’s English improved. Clara’s small apartment became her safe space, a place where she could take off the mask of survival and just be herself. Yet, even in these moments of safety, the question lingered: Was this enough?

Years passed, and Lina slowly began to build a life for herself. She found more stable work, learned more about the legal systems, and began to connect with others who shared similar stories. Through community centers, she met other migrants who had faced the same struggles—refugees from Venezuela, families from Guatemala, and young adults from Honduras. Together, they formed a small, tight-knit group that provided emotional support and practical help.

But something inside her still felt broken, fragmented. She was in a new place, but it didn’t feel like it was hers. The United States was a land of opportunity, but for Lina, it was a place of perpetual longing. She wanted more than survival. She wanted to belong.

It was in one of those community meetings, months after she had first arrived, that she heard someone speak about the concept of belonging. The speaker was a woman named Isabel, who had been in the country for over a decade. She shared her own story of struggle and resilience, of finding ways to reconcile the parts of her life that were torn between two worlds. Isabel explained that belonging was not just about being accepted by others; it was about accepting oneself, despite the many pieces that didn’t fit.

That night, something clicked for Lina. She realized that belonging wasn’t a destination, a place to arrive at. It wasn’t something granted by others. Belonging was something she had to build for herself, piece by piece. And though her journey had been long and filled with pain, it had also been filled with growth and learning. She had become someone new in the process, someone who could stand in the face of adversity and keep moving forward.

She wasn’t the same Lina who had left El Salvador years ago. She was stronger now, wiser, and more sure of her place in the world. It wasn’t easy, and it wasn’t quick, but it was real.

And for the first time since she had arrived, Lina smiled. She had found the strength to belong—to herself, to the world, and to the people who had walked with her along the way.

The road had been long, but it had led her home.

The long road to belonging is not a singular path. It is a collective journey, one where each individual—no matter their background or their struggles—seeks a place to call their own. Migration is a difficult, challenging journey, but it is also one of hope, resilience, and the ever-present search for a place to finally belong.
