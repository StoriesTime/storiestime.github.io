Laughing Through the Worst Job Interview Ever


I sat nervously in the waiting room, my hands sweating as I fiddled with the edge of my resume. The office around me was stark, with no personal touches or decor. Just plain white walls and fluorescent lights that hummed overhead. My heart was beating a little faster than usual, but I kept telling myself it would be fine. I had been to interviews before, and I could handle this one too.

“Next!” a voice barked from the open doorway.

I stood up immediately, smoothing out my blazer and walking into the interview room. As soon as I crossed the threshold, I felt an odd chill. The room was unnaturally cold, and there was something about the fluorescent lighting that made everything feel more sterile. But I quickly pushed those thoughts aside. This was just another interview, right?

The hiring manager, a man in his mid-40s with slicked-back hair and an impeccably pressed suit, motioned for me to sit. His face was expressionless, and he didn’t offer a handshake. I smiled, trying to keep things light.

“Thank you for coming in, Mr. Fields,” he said, his voice flat, almost robotic. “I trust you found the office with no trouble?”

“Yes, no trouble at all,” I replied, forcing a smile.

He glanced at my resume, barely scanning it, and then back up at me. “So, tell me, Mr. Fields, why do you want to work for this company?”

I had prepared for this question. I’d rehearsed my answer countless times in front of the mirror. “I believe your company’s values align with my own. I’m passionate about innovation and delivering high-quality service, and I think I can contribute to the team.”

He stared at me, unblinking, as if evaluating my every word. The silence stretched on a bit too long. I shifted uncomfortably in my chair, but then the man broke the stillness with a short, dry chuckle.

“Hm. Innovation, you say?” His tone had a strange edge to it, as if he didn’t believe a word I said. “Tell me this: if you were an animal, what would you be and why?”

I blinked. I hadn’t prepared for that.

“Uh…” I stumbled for a moment, not knowing if he was serious. “I guess… I’d be a wolf. Wolves are strong, independent, and work well in a team.”

He didn’t respond right away. Instead, he leaned back in his chair, folded his arms, and stared at me with an inscrutable look. The awkwardness in the room thickened. I could feel the sweat forming on my forehead.

“Interesting,” he said finally. “But I need you to understand something, Mr. Fields. Here, we value efficiency above all else. So, tell me this: if you could only complete one task every day for the rest of your life, what would it be?”

I scratched my head, completely caught off guard by the question. “Well, I suppose… I would answer emails. I think it’s important to keep communication flowing.”

Another strange pause.

“Emails. So you’re telling me that if you were hired, you’d spend your days responding to emails?” His voice was tinged with disbelief, as if the idea was utterly absurd.

“Not… just emails. I mean, I would be managing communications, collaborating with the team, ensuring everything runs smoothly,” I tried to clarify, but it didn’t seem to matter. The more I spoke, the more he seemed disinterested.

He tapped his pen on the desk once, twice, and then leaned forward, narrowing his eyes. “Mr. Fields, tell me. If you could eliminate one thing from the workplace entirely, what would it be?”

I wasn’t sure whether to laugh or run out of the room. The questions were getting weirder and more disconnected from reality.

“Uh… meetings?” I offered. “I think a lot of time is wasted in unnecessary meetings.”

The hiring manager stared at me like I had just suggested banning coffee from the office. “Meetings. Right.” He jotted something down on his notepad. “Tell me this: what is your greatest weakness?”

At this point, I had to remind myself not to lose my composure. This was a question I had anticipated, and I knew how to answer. “I think my biggest weakness is being overly detail-oriented. I sometimes focus too much on getting everything perfect, which can slow me down.”

“Interesting,” he muttered, making another note. But he didn’t let me finish before cutting me off. “Tell me, Mr. Fields, why is it that you think this company would hire someone like you?”

“Well… I believe my skills, experience, and enthusiasm make me a strong candidate. I’m a fast learner, and I’m committed to achieving success in everything I do.”

He leaned back again, clasping his hands in front of him as if contemplating something deeply. For a moment, I thought he might actually say something constructive. But then he spoke, his voice dripping with sarcasm.

“Fast learner, huh? You know, Mr. Fields, I’ve been conducting interviews for years, and I’ve yet to meet anyone who isn’t a ‘fast learner.’ Tell me this: if you had to teach a monkey to juggle, how would you go about it?”

I stared at him. Was this some kind of joke? He had to be testing me, right?

“Uh, well,” I began, trying to match his absurdity with a semblance of reason, “I would start with basic hand-eye coordination exercises, then introduce juggling balls one at a time to help the monkey build up the necessary skills.”

He grunted, almost as if he was disappointed by my answer. “Hmm. You really don’t think outside the box, do you?”

I tried not to laugh. The whole situation had taken such a bizarre turn that I couldn’t help but wonder if he was just messing with me.

But he wasn’t done yet. “Alright, Mr. Fields, we’re almost finished. Tell me: if you could change one thing about your life right now, what would it be?”

This question felt more personal, and I couldn’t help but feel a little defensive. But I decided to answer honestly.

“I think I’d work on finding a better balance between my professional and personal life. Sometimes I get too wrapped up in work, and I forget to take care of myself.”

The hiring manager’s eyes narrowed, and I braced myself for whatever came next.

“Balance,” he repeated slowly. “Interesting. So you believe in work-life balance, huh? You wouldn’t mind working long hours if it meant achieving the company’s goals?”

I froze. Was this a trick question?

“I mean… I’m willing to put in the hours when necessary, but I think it’s important to have a balance for long-term productivity and well-being,” I answered carefully.

He nodded slowly. “Well, that’s a very… typical answer. But here’s the thing, Mr. Fields. We don’t care about balance. We care about results.”

At that moment, I knew this interview had taken a turn for the worst. I had answered every question with as much professionalism as I could muster, but I was certain that nothing I said would matter. This wasn’t a place for balance or creativity. This wasn’t a company that wanted someone like me.

I glanced at the clock and realized we had been talking for over an hour. It felt like a lifetime.

“Alright, Mr. Fields,” the hiring manager said, breaking my reverie. “We’ll be in touch. Don’t call us, we’ll call you.”

I stood up, feeling the weight of the entire conversation crash down on me. “Thank you for your time,” I said, offering a weak smile.

As I walked out of the office, I couldn’t hold it in anymore. I burst into laughter. The absurdity of the entire situation hit me like a wave. From the weird questions to the complete lack of rapport, I had just survived the worst job interview I’d ever been a part of.

It was almost as if the whole thing had been a test, but I wasn’t sure what I had been tested for. Maybe it was to see how well I could handle nonsense. Or perhaps it was just a bizarre rite of passage. Either way, I wasn’t sure if I was relieved or disappointed when I left that office.

One thing was certain: I would never forget the day I laughed through the worst job interview ever.
