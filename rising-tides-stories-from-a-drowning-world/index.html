Rising Tides: Stories from a Drowning World


The sea was slowly consuming the world. It crept across the land like a slow, inevitable death, with each wave erasing more of what once stood proudly. Cities, once bustling with life and activity, now lay abandoned, overtaken by saltwater, with only the faintest whispers of memories left behind. The tides, which had once been a rhythm of nature, had become an unstoppable force, rising higher and higher each year, threatening everything in its path.

In the small town of Altar, perched on the edge of a vast bay, the sea had already claimed most of the streets. The docks, once home to fishermen and traders, had long since disappeared beneath the waves. The only remnants of the bustling marketplace that had been there were the cracked stone foundations, now home to barnacles and seaweed.

Clara stood at the window of her family’s house, looking out at the waves that lapped against the stone. The view used to be beautiful—green hills that stretched as far as the eye could see, meeting the endless blue horizon. Now, the hills were half-submerged, and the ocean stretched further inland than ever before. The waves would sometimes crash violently against the ruins of her childhood home, a cruel reminder of how much had been lost.

For as long as Clara could remember, her family had lived here. The house, though old, had been a place of warmth and laughter. Her father, a fisherman by trade, had once taken her out on his boat, showing her the deep waters and teaching her the names of all the creatures that called it home. But those days were long gone. The fishing boats had been sold, the nets abandoned. The sea had taken what it wanted, and there was no fighting it.

Clara’s mind wandered back to the day she had watched her father walk into the sea for the last time. He had been part of a group of local fishermen who set out on one of their final trips, hoping to salvage what they could before the tides overtook them completely. But the waves had been too strong that day, and Clara’s father had never returned.

She blinked back the tears threatening to form, turning away from the window. The world outside was gone, and there was nothing left to hold on to. But she had to keep going. There were still people who needed help.

Clara had become a volunteer in the few remaining communities that survived in the coastal towns. With the world slowly sinking into the sea, the survivors had banded together to rebuild what they could, though there wasn’t much hope left. Her work had taken her from one flooded city to another, searching for anyone left behind. Every day, the waters would rise, consuming more homes and fields, pushing the survivors into higher ground. The Earth’s temperatures were rising, and the storms were fiercer than ever before. Climate change had led to the collapse of entire ecosystems, and human life, once thriving on the planet, had been left teetering on the brink of extinction.

It had started with the small things, barely noticeable at first. More frequent storms, higher tides, and then the slow submerging of coastal areas. Then, within a few decades, it had spiraled into the catastrophic event that was now reshaping the world.

Despite the rising tides, Clara still held on to a sliver of hope. She had heard stories of the Safe Zones—places where people were being relocated to higher, drier lands, away from the encroaching waters. There were rumors that some of these zones were thriving, even prospering, but Clara had never seen one herself. Most survivors she had encountered were still struggling to adapt to the new world, but the desire for a better life remained. Some still dreamed of reclaiming what was lost, even though they knew in their hearts that it might be too late.

Clara’s journey would take her north, where a community had set up a refugee camp, hoping to escape the flooding. But the journey was dangerous. Rising tides had turned much of the land into a series of disjointed islands. People traveled in small groups, navigating the flooded areas on makeshift rafts or in boats, often fighting off the elements and the creatures that now roamed the waterlogged world.

As Clara packed her things, a knock came at the door.

“Clara, are you ready to go?” It was Liam, her long-time friend and fellow volunteer. His face was etched with exhaustion, but there was determination in his eyes.

“Yes, I’m ready,” she replied, her voice steady despite the chaos inside her. She wasn’t sure if she would ever return to Altar, or if she would be able to help the survivors in the north, but she had to try. She had to keep moving forward.

The two of them made their way through the narrow, flooded streets, where the water had risen so high that it had almost reached the rooftops of some of the smaller homes. The town, once a thriving fishing village, was now a graveyard of broken buildings and abandoned boats. There was no sound but the occasional crash of waves against the buildings.

As they reached the edge of the town, they joined a group of survivors who were preparing to set out on a small raft. The raft was made from salvaged wood, and there were enough people to crowd it, but not enough to make it feel safe. The air was heavy with the scent of salt and rot, the remnants of a world that had once been vibrant and alive.

“We don’t know what’s ahead,” Liam said, his voice barely audible over the sound of the waves. “But we can’t stay here. It’s all we’ve got.”

Clara nodded, her eyes scanning the horizon. The water stretched out before her, dark and endless. There was no turning back now. All that was left was forward, into the unknown.

The raft set off, its wood creaking beneath their weight. The wind was strong, pushing them away from the shore, toward the open water. Clara’s thoughts drifted back to her father, to the way he had once taught her to love the sea. But now, the sea was their enemy. The tides had risen, and with them, the world was drowning.

Days turned into weeks as they traveled north. The landscape, once familiar, had changed beyond recognition. What had once been lush forests and vast fields were now barren, flooded wastelands. The survivors moved in silence, focusing on their journey, their eyes always on the horizon, hoping for a glimpse of something—anything—that would tell them they were on the right path.

But there were no signs. The world they had known was gone, replaced by a new reality where survival was the only goal. The only way to move forward was to adapt, to accept that things would never be the same again.

Eventually, they reached the northern refugee camp, a place built on the highest ground that had survived the rising tides. The camp was surrounded by makeshift walls, a fragile barrier against the storms that were becoming more frequent and violent. People moved about in a haze, going through the motions of survival.

Clara and Liam were greeted by a group of camp leaders who showed them where they could settle. The camp was a lifeline for those who had managed to make it this far, but even here, the sense of hope was fleeting. The floodwaters were relentless, and there was no telling how long the camp would last.

Clara found a small place to sleep, a corner near a campfire where people gathered to share stories of their lost homes and loved ones. As she sat there, she thought about the world she had left behind—her father, the home she had once known, and the life she had once taken for granted.

But now, all that was left was survival. The tides were rising, and the world was drowning. But Clara had made it this far, and she wasn’t ready to give up yet.

For as long as there was breath in her body, she would keep moving forward.

And perhaps, just perhaps, in a world being swallowed by the sea, there could still be hope.

End
