The Great American BBQ Road Trip: Flavors of the South


As I embarked on the Great American BBQ Road Trip, I couldn't help but feel a sensation of excitement and anticipation coursing through my veins. My trusty SUV, loaded with essential barbecue gear and a sense of adventure, awaited me as I prepared to embark on a 3,000-mile journey through the heart of America. The mission: to sample the region's most mouth-watering barbecue and explore the stories behind the pitmasters who crafted each dish. From the Blues to the Low Country My first stop was Memphis, Tennessee, a city steeped in the rich history of dry-rubbed ribs and tangy barbecue sauce. As I strolled down Beale Street, I stopped in at Jim's Place to try their famous Pork Ribs. The fall-off-the-bone tenderness and sweet, smoky flavor transported me to a world of pure bliss. The story behind Jim's signature dry rub, developed by the late Jim Gibson, was just as fascinating as the taste. I listened intently as the owner regaled me with tales of the secret blend's origins, passed down through generations of the Gibson family. From Memphis, I headed to the Low Country of North Carolina, where whole hog barbecue is the norm. I pulled into Scott's Bar-B-Que in eastern North Carolina and ordered the pulled pork, a cultural staple of the region. The tender, slow-cooked meat was piled high on a bun and slathered in a spicy, vinegar-based sauce. I asked the chef about his secret techniques, and he shared the art of using a combination of oak and hickory woods to infuse the flavor. Texas, Where Beef is King After a few days of sampling pork and beef in the Carolinas, I set off for the Lone Star State, the Mecca of brisket and beef. In Austin, I stopped at Franklin Barbecue, one of the most renowned barbecue joints in the country. I was greeted by the heavenly aroma of smoked beef wafting from the pit. The tender, peppery brisket was carefully sourced from local farms, and the high-fat content made it impossible to resist. The pitmaster, Aaron Franklin, shared the secret to his legendary brisket: a combination of post-cooking resting, a careful seasoning process, and the perfect temperature control. Kansas City, the Sweet Spot As I continued westward, I stopped in Kansas City, a metropolis known for its famous slow-smoked Kansas City-style barbecue. At Joe's Kansas City BB-Q, I bit into a mouth-watering burnt ends sandwich, a culinary experience unlike any other. The mixture of smoky, hand-rubbed steak and tangy barbecue sauce was out of this world. Owner-beef rag-m researcher Mildred Fudge revealed the history behind Joe's reputation as a barbecue trailblazer and the proprietary dry rub formula revealed. New Orleans: Where Cajun Meets BBQ A four-and-a-half-hour drive south took me to the vibrant city of New Orleans, where Creole and French influences blend with traditional Southern cuisine. I popped into Coop's Place, a legendary eatery along the infamous Frenchmen Street. Chef Charles Trevino's fried chicken livers, crispy on the outside, juicy on the inside, exploded with fiery flavor.
