A Culinary Safari: Unearthing Hidden Artisans of the Culinary World


As the sun begins to rise over the lush, vibrant landscape of Rwanda, a small group of culinary enthusiasts find themselves at the doorstep of an unforgettable adventure. This is not just any ordinary trip, but a rare opportunity to embark on a culinary safari – a journey that will unearthing the hidden artisans of the culinary world.

The group, led by renowned food critic Agnes Mbewe, sets off to uncover the culinary treasures tucked away in the hinterland of Rwanda. Their first stop is at the bustling Toxeria Market in Kigali, a haven for local farmers and food vendors who’ve dedicated their lives to creating delicious, wholesome dishes using traditional methods.

The air is filled with the tantalizing aromas of grilling plantains and roasting coffee beans. Mbewe leads the group through narrow lanes, weaving through a tapestry of vibrant colors and tantalizing flavors. They explore a kaleidoscope of tastes – fragrant local spices, rich stews, and sweet, syrupy delicacies.

As the market comes to life, the group has the unique opportunity to interact with local artisans who’ve honed their culinary skills over generations. Mbewe takes extra care to introduce the group to local heroes like Cynthia, a young entrepreneur who left a corporate job in Europe to start a social enterprise called “Ughe N’ojos” (Egg on a Plate).

Ughe N’ojos employs women from the nearby Batwa community and teaches them how to cook traditional Rwandan dishes, revitalizing age-old recipes and imparting hospitality skills. Mbewe explains how this has empowered the women and strengthened their sense of identity, helping them hold their ground in the face of deep-rooted cultural barriers.

The group travels further, crossing the national park border, to reach the home of the Mafuga family – real-life culinary guardians preserving the art of Rwandan cooking in their ancestral village. They are welcomed with warm smiles and open arms. Inside their cozy kitchen, the aroma of smoking meat and roasting coffee is overwhelming, and the chatter of family members preparing food resembles a symphony of sounds.

Over a hearty meal, Mbewe reveals an animal most unexpected to be both a dietary mainstay and a guardian of culture – the goat. In every Rwandan homestead, the goat holds a unique place – it is a companion, a symbol of wealth, and an essential ingredient of traditional dishes.

Mbewe explains how different cuts of goat meat are used in the preparation of distinct dishes, while the skin and bones find their way into soups and stews. It’s a lesson not just in cooking, but also in the understanding of a culture so intertwined with food.

As the week draws to a close, Mbewe takes the group to a local brewery – an unlikely but fascinating end to their culinary journey. They meet with Paul Nshimiye, a former chemist, and an award-winning brewmaster, who has successfully blended traditional African brews with modern techniques.

Paul introduces the group to Ina Mbabzi – a traditional Rwandan brew made of fermented sorghum and millet grains with natural flavors from locally sourced fruits. Nshimiye’s unique approach to brewing has not only given a new lease of life to long-forgotten traditional drinks but has helped improve Rwandan beer tourism as well.

The culinary safari comes to an end as the group departs, their minds laden with new knowledge and a renewed appreciation for the art of Rwandan cuisine. As the sun sets over the vast Rwandan landscape, their hearts are full – buzzing with flavors and brimming with the joyous memories of a voyage to the heart of the culinary world.
