Whispers of No Man's Land


No one knew where it began. The maps were all but useless, and even the most seasoned explorers couldn’t agree on its true expanse. Some said it stretched for hundreds of miles, an indeterminate blank spot on cartographers’ works—an enigma, unclaimed, unnamed, and ungoverned. To the villagers at its edges, the expanse was simply called No Man’s Land. But among them, one truth was sacrosanct: no one ventured into it willingly.

The stories… well, the stories varied. They spoke of faint voices at the periphery, strange whispers carried by winds that seemed alive. Local elders swore by their whiskey-sweetened breath that once you heard the whispers, you were as good as lost. But what were the whispers? That was the riddle for brave souls to unravel. And brave souls, like poor little moths, had an uncanny ability to fly toward peril.

When Alder first heard about it, he had been sitting in the knotted shadow of the old ash tree behind his family’s shop. He wasn’t what you’d call brave; in fact, he had earned the reputation of the village’s quiet dreamer. He studied bugs longer than any grown man could justify and had more patience for myths than he cared to admit. This story, though, intrigued him in all the ways it shouldn’t have.

It was Marit who told him. Marit, whose reckless streak burned as bright as her copper-red hair. She towered over Alder as he bent over the remnants of a desiccated beetle, flinging dirt lazily at his boots.

“They say old Brenrick heard the whispers last spring,” she announced as though it were breaking news.

Alder didn’t look up, his fingers busy prying soft flakes of shell from the creature’s brittle frame. “Whispers? You mean the ones from No Man’s Land?”

“Obviously.” She rolled her eyes. “They said he went to investigate the desert’s edge. Just wandered off one evening after hearing something strange. Barely made it back alive.”

That gave him pause. Brenrick was one of those sinewy types, all stubborn fortitude and jagged wrinkles carved into leathery skin. If someone like that faltered, then there was real weight behind the warning. Yet, his curiosity itched; it demanded he scratch at the tale’s edges.

“What did he say they sounded like?” he murmured, more to himself than anyone else.

Marit folded her arms, grinning wide like a jackal. “Some say it wasn’t one voice, but dozens. Quiet, calling his name, speaking in strange tongues. Brenrick wouldn’t talk about it after he came back. He just locked himself up and stopped hunting altogether.” Her face lowered, her grin softening into something darker. “My brother says his eyes don’t focus anymore. Like he left some part of himself out there."

Alder pondered the image in silence. His fingers drifted from the beetle, stilling in the dirt.

The desert had long been the boundary of their village. A wall of endless dunes shifted lazily beyond the tufts of grass marking the end of fertile soil. On rare occasions, some travelers swore they’d glimpsed strange shadows moving against the horizon. But it was always from afar. No one dared breach the unspoken border.

The next week, though, Alder’s resolve cracked. Something inside him—a crescendo of disappointment in his small, forgettable life—drove him to do the unthinkable. He waited until just before dawn, when the world held its breath, and the air shimmered with impossible quiet. Holding his breath, he stepped past the final clutch of green and into the steely sand.

The deeper he ventured, the darker his thoughts became. Every step fell into the silence, until even his pulse seemed muted. The rumors of No Man’s Land played over in his mind. Of those wanderers who claimed the whispers weren’t just voices, but echoes from another world. Some even said the land twisted its victims, reshaping them into hollow shells incapable of return.

By his third day of wandering the dry expanse, he had started to feel it—a nagging sensation at the edge of his consciousness. It was as though something tugged at him, a magnetic pull he couldn’t resist even if he wanted to.

That’s when he heard them for the first time.

Soft at first, the words seemed scattered, like mismatched pieces of a broken melody. Their cadence was uncanny—ever so slightly off, as if skipping over spaces where syllables should rest. They whispered his name, over and over again: “Alder… Alder… Alder…” Then they spoke of his dreams, of his childhood fears, of things he had never shared with anyone, not even Marit.

And yet, they beckoned with neither malice nor kindness. Only knowing.

He clutched at his chest, instinctively veering in the direction of the sound despite every logical nerve in his body urging him to turn back. Most unnerving of all, it seemed the whispers were no longer burdened by the dense, dry air of the desert. Instead, they swirled inside him now—deep, vibrating against his ribcage and settling in the marrow of his bones.

There was no sunrise nor sunset. Time lost meaning when neither shade nor shadow would tell it true. He pressed onward, though he was no longer sure where “onward” meant to go. Days stretched on like years; fatigue couldn’t claim him, for hunger and thirst seemed to have been abandoned at the boundary. And yet, the whispers grew louder.

Until he found the wall.

It wasn’t stone or wood or anything you might expect. No, the wall was made of light—an iridescent shimmer rising vertically from the sand, stretching far beyond the scope of Alder’s vision. At first, it flickered like impossibly warm firelight before stabilizing into a cascade of shifting hues. The colors were almost intoxicating, each one unfamiliar to the human eye yet deeply resonant with the soul.

He marveled for hours, no longer hearing the whispers but feeling them pouring forth from the wall’s glowing surface. It was alive. It was asking him to step closer.

He complied.

When they found Alder, nine months later, he was wandering at the edge of the village—unharmed, but forever changed. Marit recognized him instantly despite the ghostly pallor that clung to his gaunt face. His eyes held a strange, hollow sheen, as though he had seen something unimaginable.

But Alder didn’t speak for weeks. Not to the villagers, not to Marit, not even to himself. He simply waited, staring at the horizon as if expecting it to blink back at him.

When the first whispers came to the village itself, others began to listen. Whatever Alder had seen, whatever secrets No Man’s Land had given him, it wasn’t finished yet.
