Existential Crisis: A Debate Between Two Souls


Night in the in-between was neither dark nor comforting. It was more a space than a time: a breadth of velvet uncertainty, fraying at the edges, barely stitched to reality. In this paradoxical dusk, two souls hovered, each flickering with the faint memory of bodies once belonged to, words once spoken, fears once half-confessed to bathrooms and empty subway cars.

Yva drifted close to the border between what had been and what might be. Her shape was suggestion and memory—cheekbones, perhaps, or the memory of a hand that had known the weight of rain. Her thoughts moved in tides, rarely resting in language.

Across from her, Orin was sharper, as though drawn with deliberate lines, impatient even in eternity. He projected himself as a figure just on the verge of solidifying: boots unpolished, laughter almost audible. There was a hint of cigarette smoke, of accidental epiphanies at 2 a.m., of sleepless ambition.

They found each other not by chance, but because the in-between loved a paradox, and nothing created one like a shared existential crisis.

I. Reckoning

Yva broke the quiet. Not with words, initially—just an intake of breath. Here, in the hush, it was enough to start a storm.

Yva: “Do you think there’s a reason? For any of this—soul, body, in-between?”

Orin scoffed. Not out of disrespect, but as a reflex—admiring and exasperated at how efficiently Yva dug under the surface.

Orin: “I tried to ask, once. Figured I’d find an answer tucked somewhere obvious, like the fold behind my ears. All I found was the shape of forgetting.”

Yva smiled, a ripple through memory. “It’s the same for me. Some days I thought I could touch it—a purpose, a structure. But the closer I got, the emptier it seemed.”

Orin floated closer, leering into the expanse that replaced their physical world. “We’re here. Or, at least, we’re not anywhere else. That should mean something, right?”

Beneath their debate, the in-between hummed with infinite possibility and equal emptiness.

II. The Argument of Meaning

Yva: “Let’s define meaning. Is it something found? Or built, brick by hesitant brick?”

Orin shrugged. “I spent life building—careers, relationships, even disappointments. But every structure eventually collapsed. Sometimes I think meaning is what survives the collapse.”

“Then what about suffering? Or failure?”

Orin paused, considering. “Collateral damage, maybe. But also… evidence you cared enough to try. Isn’t that worth something?”

Yva smiled again. “Suppose that’s true. But what if you care, and build, and the world just… forgets anyway? Even now, shrouded by death and this endless in-between, I can feel myself being forgotten. Like the last echo of a song that never quite caught on.”

Orin’s gaze sharpened, his form thickening in the invisible gravity of the moment.

Orin: “If we’re debating what matters, we have to agree where the stakes lie. Legacy? Impact? Or just the texture of the hours as they pass?”

Yva: “All of it, maybe none of it. I remember once, in life, watching snowfall through a dirty window. It was silent, pointless, and profoundly beautiful. Not because anyone saw it, but because I did. Is that meaning? The act of witness?”

Orin let the silence swirl, then nodded. “That’s a start. But what does it mean now, when even the act of witnessing is untethered? Here, we’re outside context—no consequences, no observers but us.”

III. The Failure of Philosophy

Each soul conjured fragments from their prior lives, old philosophy books dusted and thumbed through at 3 a.m. during bouts of despair.

Yva attempted a Socratic method: “If meaning must be external—must touch someone, change something—does that mean we’re meaningless now?”

Orin huffed. “I hated that thought even when alive. The idea that you exist only because others confirm you. Hive validation—it always felt hollow.”

“Maybe it’s both,” Yva mused. “You generate meaning for yourself, but it solidifies—or dissolves—in the gaze of others. Like Schrödinger opening a box.”

Orin barked a laugh. “You think we’re alive or dead inside the box?”

Yva: “Something in-between, evidently.”

Orin: “And what if there’s no box, only a hallway and echoes?”

They drifted, thoughts knotting together and unspooling.

Yva: “Sometimes I think consciousness is a trick we play on ourselves. We convince ourselves it’s valuable until we’re empty—and then we look for more value. Like a gambler always betting tomorrow’s fortune.”

Orin: “But that need… isn’t that proof of something? We crave meaning because we’re wired for it. Evolutionary leftover or not, it shapes what we do.”

Yva: “But then we’re just chasing ghosts. Are we happy with that?”

Orin: “Maybe the chase is all there is. The process, the hunger—it’s what kept me alive. In love with questions I couldn’t answer.”

IV. Interlude in Memory

The in-between allowed them visions—not quite memories, not quite hallucinations. They walked together in a city not their own, constructed from hundreds of half-recalled streets. Dim lamplight, the smell of wet pavement, the hush after midnight.

Orin, recalling a night from his youth, gestured: “I used to talk myself out of terror. Afraid of death, yes, but also of living badly. You know that feeling?”

Yva: “More than terror, it was loneliness. Wanting to be seen exactly as I was, but worried there was no exact me—just layers, forever.”

Orin: “Did you ever find what you were looking for?”

Yva shook her not-quite-head. “I found moments. They flickered, never stayed.”

Orin: “Maybe that’s enough. A flicker per lifetime, a taste of certainty.”

They walked on under imaginary stars.

V. The Debate Heats Up

Yva: “But what if we missed some greater plan? What if we were meant for something, and we’re failing even after death to understand it?”

Orin: “I always doubted meant for. Always seemed arrogant, thinking the universe had time to write a script for a couple of lost souls.”

Yva: “Maybe. But—what if humility is just fear in disguise? A way to dodge responsibility?”

Orin: “Responsibility to what? Cosmic justice?”

Yva: “Or to ourselves. To live bravely. To love, even knowing it hurts. To try, knowing we fail.”

Orin: “That, I’ll grant you. Bravery feels more real than fate. But ‘bravely’ also means facing the void, doesn’t it?”

Yva: “Yes. That’s what we’re doing now. Talking instead of fading. Insisting on our own names, even here.”

Orin: “So the act of questioning, even in nothingness, is itself meaning?”

Yva: “I think so. Or at least, it’s defiance. And maybe that’s enough to be called a soul.”

VI. On Suffering and Worth

Silence buzzed around them, the audible thickening of space left empty by answers.

Orin squared his spectral jaw. “What about pain? All that pointless agony so many endure. Can that be justified, even by meaning?”

Yva: “Justified is a hard word. In my worst moments, I wanted there to be a reason. Not just random, cruel luck.”

Orin: “Do you believe suffering builds character?”

Yva considered, drawing a pattern of shadow in the air. “Not always. Sometimes it just breaks you. Sometimes it passes through and leaves you changed, but not stronger.”

Orin: “The philosophers fall short there. They wanted suffering to redeem, to teach. But sometimes it’s just a fact. A storm that shreds your house.”

Yva: “But then what? Curl up in the wreckage, or keep building, knowing you’ll lose it again?”

Orin: “I think both are honest. Survival isn’t mandatory. The only logic is the one you choose.”

Yva: “So we’re free, but it’s a cruel freedom. You set your own value, knowing it could collapse at any moment.”

Orin: “But isn’t that what makes it precious? If meaning came pre-packaged, we’d discard it. But to strain for it, to puzzle and ache for it—it’s ugly, but it’s ours.”

VII. The Final Movement: Acceptance?

They stood, or floated, side by side on the threshold of some potential conclusion.

Yva: “So we agree: meaning’s built and destroyed. Sometimes it fails. Sometimes it flares up just because we insist it must.”

Orin: “And neither of us ever found a higher authority to confirm or deny. Maybe that’s the tragedy, or the joke.”

Yva: “Or both. Isn’t that what Camus said? The only serious question is whether to keep going. To embrace the absurdity.”

Orin: “He said to imagine Sisyphus happy. Pushing the endless stone, knowing it never rests.”

Yva: “So what’s our rock, then?”

Orin: “I think it’s this—the arguing. The relentless refusal to accept nothingness without protest.”

Yva: “And the hope that even our little debate matters, because it’s what keeps us here, in the in-between.”

Orin, with the trace of a grin: “Maybe one more question, then. What if, after all this talking, we faded away, but the argument echoed—just long enough for another wandering soul to hear it?”

Yva: “Then we’ve left something behind. Not a monument, but a murmur. A reason to linger, even if just for a moment.”

The in-between glowed faintly, as if pleased with their conclusion—or at least, with their refusal to stop wrestling. The paradox persisted, shimmering in the dusk between life and forgetting.

Orin reached out, and Yva took his hand. They stepped forward, not toward an answer but toward the next question, armoring themselves in curiosity, in the sacred, stubborn act of seeking.

And so they walked on, joined by the resonance of their debate, two souls refusing silence, forever.
