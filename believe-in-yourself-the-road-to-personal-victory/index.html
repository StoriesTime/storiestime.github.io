Believe in Yourself: The Road to Personal Victory


The first time Aria Travers returned to Cedar Run in eighteen years, the town hadn’t changed at all. The haze of late summer coated the same labyrinth of narrow ice-cream colored houses, and the river still chattered to itself under the sycamores. Though the world had marched into a new decade, with all its restless urgency, Cedar Run clung to its old rhythms, as if waiting for her to pick up where she’d left off.

She walked past her childhood home, its porch sagging resolutely beneath hanging baskets of fuchsia, and for a moment, time obeyed the old rules. She felt twelve again, sneakers flicking dust from Main Street as she ran errands for her mother, dreams trailing her like a determined shadow. Back then, she believed in everything: the power of a wish, the justice of hard work, and her place at the center of a world that had not yet learned to doubt her.

Today, belief was harder work.

Aria paused in front of Marston’s Store, the bell tinkling overhead as she pushed open the door. The cool, familiar scent—part penny candy, part licorice, all memory—washed over her. Behind the counter, Mr. Marston himself, old as the stained wood of the shelves, peered at her.

“You’re the Travers girl,” he said, without preamble. “Left for the city. Bet you learned fancy things out there.”

She smiled, the corners of her mouth unsteady. “Trying to remember the not-so-fancy things, lately.”

He nodded as if this were wisdom. “You here for the reunion?”

“Just passing through.”

“Funny—most folks only come ‘back through’ when they’re running away or looking for something.” Marston turned back to his register, letting her ponder which she was.

Aria left with a bag of penny candy and a head full of ghosts. It was almost reassuring—the way the people of Cedar Run regarded her, the prodigal girl, as though she carried both questions and answers stitched along her seams.

It was nearly dusk when she sat by the river in the park, knees hugged to her chest, watching kids toss skipping stones. Not far away, the town’s old gymnasium brooded in the growing darkness, windows flashing the orange-pink memory of sunset.

The gym was a relic, its bleachers scarred by decades of restless feet. All through middle school, Aria had spent her afternoons there, running laps with her sneakers a blur, lungs burning, each circuit a fragile promise to her younger self: You can do this. Eventually, no one will doubt it.

She remembered her coach, Mrs. Holcomb, and the way the woman’s voice was forever gravelly with encouragement and an undertow of expectation: “First step’s the hardest, Travers.” Back then, it never occurred to Aria that someday the hardest step would be coming back at all.

As the lanterns clicked on along Main Street one by one, Aria found herself drifting toward the gym. She nudged open the heavy door; it gave a familiar groan, as though irked at the intrusion.

Inside, the air was warm with the memory of effort. She crossed the bleachers—boards creaking familiarly—until she sat at the very top, the vantage point she’d always chosen to convince herself she saw more than she did.

Her fingers traced names carved into the wood, legends of fleeting victories. T.A., 1981—County Champions, Bel, 1996—First Place. Her gaze snagged on a jagged scrawl: Aria T, 2006—Run Faster.

She grinned despite herself and let her mind tumble back to the spring of eighth grade, and the day she almost didn’t run at all.

That year, Aria’s confidence—shiny as a new coin at the beginning of the season—had dulled quickly. The world suddenly seemed an audience waiting for her to trip. She botched one race, then another, each mistake playing loud in her head. By the finals, she was a bundle of nerves, palms slick as she laced her shoes.

In the locker room, Mrs. Holcomb knelt in front of her, hands cupping Aria’s shoulders. “You’ve got a body built for this,” she’d said, “but the truth is here—” one hand tapping her brow, “—and here.” She pressed a hand over Aria’s heart.

“Everyone loses,” Coach said, “but it’s believing you can win that gets you anywhere near the starting line. Nothing else—no fancy theories, no pep talk, just that small little voice. You don’t have to silence your doubts. You just have to turn up your belief a little bit louder.”

Aria had nodded, blinking back tears, and in the strange way memories grow, that speech became a talisman in the years that followed—even when, as an adult, belief felt like trying to keep water in her fists.

She sat on the bleachers now, older, supposedly wiser, but still fighting to believe. The city had offered opportunities in glossy wrappers, but each job, each relationship, each new beginning seemed to require more belief than she ever had in reserve. There were deadlines missed, rejections smiling through emails, friendships splintered without warning. Her self-assuredness became brittle, then invisible.

Coming home wasn’t about nostalgia; it was a retreat, a last gasp before giving up.

A voice jarred her back. “You’d better not be vandalizing my bleachers.”

Aria startled; Mrs. Holcomb (older, smaller, yet somehow larger at the same time) stood at the bottom of the stands, keys jangling loudly.

“Just reading,” Aria said.

“Well, you never could spell,” the old coach huffed, but she was smiling.

They sat together, silence stretching comfortably between them, the way only old connections can stretch without breaking.

“Back for good?” Mrs. Holcomb asked.

Aria shook her head, “I don’t know. Things got hard. Out there, I mean. It used to be that if I worked hard enough, believed enough, things would work. Lately, it feels like believing isn’t doing much good.”

The older woman regarded her for a long minute. “You think victory means never stumbling?”

“I guess I thought I would’ve figured it out by now,” Aria admitted.

Holcomb shook her head, a low chuckle rumbling from her chest. “You think the victories I talk about are about being the best? Victory’s showing up again, even after falling on your face. You ever take a good look at yourself? Really look?”

Aria didn’t answer.

Mrs. Holcomb went on, “The hardest thing to do is keep listening to that quiet voice, the one that tells you to try again. That voice gets quieter, sure, but it never goes all the way away unless you let it.”

Aria let the words sit. In the gym’s half-light, silence pressed in, filled with the ghosts of old games and last chances.

They stayed there, parallel in thought, while outside, the crickets started their evening chorus.

The next morning, Aria woke early, unable to shake last night’s conversation. She laced up her running shoes—stiff from disuse—and jogged to the old trail beside the river, lungs dragging in the dirt-wet air, heart keeping time with her pounding feet.

As she ran, every worry from the city seemed to chase her and, little by little, fall away. She remembered what Mrs. Holcomb used to say: “You keep your eyes forward, one foot after the other.”

The trail was familiar and strange, like tracing the edges of a dream. She slowed and finally stopped, hunched to catch her breath, staring out over the water. Ducks landed in lazy ripples. Her chest burned, but for the first time in years, there was a spark of pride, quiet and persistent.

Back at the house, her mother stood in the kitchen, flipping pancakes that sizzled and spat in the pan.

“Haven’t seen you smile like that in a while,” her mother said, a gentle challenge in her tone.

Aria hesitated, then offered the bare truth: “I thought I came home because I’d failed. But maybe…I just needed to remember how to start again.”

Her mother nodded, eyes kind. “Starts are always waiting, if you let yourself try.”

That week, Aria lingered in Cedar Run. She visited old places: the library, where her card still hung on the rack, dusty and disused; the bakery, now run by Mia Lopez—a girl she once beat in a three-legged race.

Everywhere, people greeted her with a smile; not the brittle politeness she’d learned to expect in the city, but a warmth that offered space for her to stumble, to restart. They asked about her life, sure, but didn’t press for success stories; they seemed content to let the question rest, a gentle invitation rather than an interrogation.

At the Saturday morning farmer’s market, Aria bumped into Mrs. Holcomb again, this time in line for fresh peaches.

“Well, fancy seeing you here, Travers.”

“Couldn’t stay away from your wisdom,” Aria teased.

Mrs. Holcomb grunted. “Mostly, I just like peaches.”

They walked together through the booths, sampling jams and exchanging news.

Eventually, Holcomb said, “You know, I’m coaching the relay for the middle school these days. Could use someone to help wrangle kids who think running is an excuse to chase each other in circles. You available?”

Aria hesitated. She had job prospects in the city (tentative, theoretical), an apartment with half-packed boxes, and an inbox lined with HR responses. But here was a chance, small but real, not to win or prove, but to start again.

She nodded. “I’d love to.”

Over the next weeks, Aria threw herself into coaching. She remembered each trick Mrs. Holcomb had used: tricky warm-ups disguised as games, kind words that came only when most needed. The kids—clumsy, shy, bursting with impossible energy—looked at her with the same mixture of doubt and hope she’d once had.

One evening, after a particularly disastrous practice, a boy named Marshall lingered behind.

“I’m not good at this,” he muttered, staring at his feet.

Aria knelt. “You think I was born good at it?”

His eyes asked questions he was too worried to voice.

“I was the slowest in third grade. Tripped, fell, the works,” she said. “But you know what I did?”

“What?” he whispered.

“Put my shoes on the next day and tried again. Trying is believing you might get it right. But even if you don’t…” She smiled, ruffling his hair. “You try again tomorrow.”

He smiled a little. “Will you be here tomorrow?”

She nodded. “Every time you show up, I will.”

For the first time in years, Aria felt unburdened—not just by victories, major or minor, but by possibility. The joy of teaching, of passing on belief, eclipsed her own disappointed ambitions. Slowly, she saw that belief in oneself wasn’t an anthem you shouted when things went well. It was a presence you chose to bring, no matter how many times you’d been proven wrong by circumstance.

She applied for a local teaching job, not because she’d failed elsewhere, but because she realized she belonged here, in Cedar Run, where failure and success felt less like opposites and more like phases in a cycle that never stopped turning.

Her city friends sent messages: So you’re giving up? Isn’t that quitting?

She answered them honestly: “No. I’m starting again. Sometimes believing means trusting yourself enough to choose the life that fits. Sometimes the greatest victories are the ones no one else sees: the morning run, the scared kid who keeps trying, the chance to live without needing to prove anything.”

Years later, as Aria watched a new generation of runners dash around the faded track, she thought again of the small carved inscription on the bleachers: Run Faster. It once meant competition, always more, always better. Now, she translated it another way: keep moving, keep hoping, keep believing—if only for one more day.

At the end of another practice, the newest team member, out of breath but hopeful, asked, “Coach, do you think I can win next time?”

Smiling, Aria replied, “I believe you can. But more than that, I believe you’ll get up and try again, no matter what. That’s the real victory.”

And in the echo of those words, Aria found her own belief, steady and sure, winding alongside Cedar Run’s river—quiet, persistent, and endlessly returning.
