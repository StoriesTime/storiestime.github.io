Zoom Fatigue and Forgotten Dreams


It had been months since Clara had seen the sun rise in any meaningful way. Each morning bled into the next, with her bedroom becoming more of a holding cell than a sanctuary. She awoke, groggy, her alarm blaring a reminder that the day ahead would be much like the one before. Her eyes, still adjusting to the dim light filtering through the blinds, couldn’t shake the thought that something was missing. But what?

The answer eluded her every time, like a dream fading upon waking. It wasn’t just the monotony that gnawed at her spirit. No, there was something deeper at play, something that felt like a distant memory, a fleeting sense of purpose she once had but now couldn’t place.

She rolled out of bed with the mechanical motions of someone accustomed to routine. The same old rituals. The same old tasks. Coffee, stretching, checking her emails, and then firing up the computer, hoping for a spark of energy to ignite a sense of progress in her day.

Zoom meetings. Every day. For hours.

The screen flickered to life, displaying her colleagues’ faces — some muted, some smiling awkwardly, some staring off into the void. Clara’s reflection stared back at her from the small window in the corner. Her face had grown tired. Her eyes, once bright and full of curiosity, now looked hollow. She wondered if anyone else could see it.

A notification popped up on the screen. Another meeting request. She sighed, clicked accept, and joined the latest digital gathering.

The Digital Disconnect

The meetings started to blur together, like watching the same scene unfold in different colors. She would nod in agreement, make her usual small talk, and contribute to discussions that felt increasingly detached from the life she once envisioned. The disconnection wasn’t just professional. It was personal.

Each Zoom session, while filled with voices and faces, left her feeling more alone. Conversations that used to be rich with interaction had turned into scripted routines. She missed the days of spontaneous brainstorming sessions, the laughter shared during lunch breaks, and the casual exchanges of ideas while walking between offices. Now, it was just a series of screens and static.

But the fatigue wasn’t just mental. It was physical too. Her neck ached, her eyes strained, and a dull headache had become a permanent fixture in the background of her thoughts. It wasn’t just the back-to-back meetings. It was the sheer emotional drain of being constantly “on,” of having to put on a face, even when she didn’t feel like it. The weight of expectations, even from a distance, was heavier than she had imagined.

The term “Zoom fatigue” had been floating around for months now, but Clara had never understood it until now. It wasn’t just about the screen time or the constant flow of digital interaction. It was about the way these meetings demanded so much of her, mentally and emotionally, without ever truly fulfilling her.

She realized that it was as though every Zoom call she joined stripped away a little piece of who she was. Each minute she spent staring at her own face in that little box was another moment spent wondering who she had become.

The Forgotten Dreams

It wasn’t always like this.

Clara remembered a time when she had dreams — vivid, full of color and excitement. She had once believed in a future full of possibility. She had imagined herself traveling the world, writing stories, working on projects that felt important and meaningful. There had been a spark in her chest when she thought of those things. She had once stayed up late, sketching out plans for the book she would write or the art exhibit she would curate. The world was full of potential, and she was determined to seize it.

But now, the dreams seemed like a distant memory, something from another life. It wasn’t that she didn’t care about her work, but she had come to realize that the work she was doing now felt so far removed from the life she had envisioned for herself. The Zoom meetings, the endless emails, the projects that seemed to lose their meaning the more she worked on them — they had all become a blur.

It wasn’t just the fatigue. It was the fact that the very act of existing in this virtual world, of living a life so disconnected from the physical, had caused her to forget who she truly was. She had forgotten her passions, the things that once lit a fire inside her. She had forgotten the dreams that once felt so vivid and close, like they could be grasped at any moment.

As Clara scrolled through the day’s to-do list, she paused on a task that caught her attention. “Write reflection piece for next quarter’s presentation.” She frowned. She didn’t even remember why that was a priority. Hadn’t she been working on something else? Something more meaningful?

Her mind wandered back to the days before all of this — before the pandemic, before the endless hours spent staring at a screen. There was a time when she had believed in herself more than she had ever believed in a PowerPoint presentation or quarterly metrics. But now, all those dreams felt like fragments of a forgotten reality.

The Breaking Point

It wasn’t until the meeting with her boss that Clara reached her breaking point.

It had started like any other meeting. Her manager, Alex, began the session with pleasantries, asking how everyone was doing, then immediately diving into the topic at hand. Clara found herself zoning out, her eyes flicking from one person’s face to another on the screen. As the conversation continued, her mind began to drift, and she felt that familiar sense of disconnection creeping back in.

When it was her turn to speak, Clara found herself at a loss for words. Her usual prepared responses weren’t there. The ideas she had been so eager to share earlier seemed distant and hollow now. She stumbled through her comments, feeling the pressure of every eye on her, even though she knew no one was truly paying attention.

“Clara,” Alex’s voice broke through the fog. “Is everything okay? You seem… distracted.”

The question lingered in the air, an uncomfortable silence filling the digital space. Clara blinked, realizing she had been caught. She didn’t know how to respond. How could she explain the overwhelming fatigue, the sense of disillusionment, the forgotten dreams that once seemed so important? How could she tell Alex that she had lost herself in the grind of endless meetings and virtual check-ins?

“I… I don’t know,” Clara said, her voice quieter than she intended. “I just feel… disconnected, I guess.”

The admission hung in the air, a vulnerability she hadn’t intended to share. For a moment, Alex seemed taken aback, but he softened.

“I understand,” he said slowly. “This has been hard on all of us. You’re not alone.”

A New Perspective

After that meeting, Clara found herself reflecting on what Alex had said. She wasn’t alone. This was a shared experience. Everyone, it seemed, was struggling with the same overwhelming fatigue and loss of direction. Maybe it wasn’t just her. Maybe it wasn’t just the work or the Zoom calls that had drained her. Maybe it was the fact that, in this digital world, they had all lost something — something important.

Clara decided that it was time to take a step back, to reconnect with the things that once inspired her. She couldn’t change the fact that Zoom meetings were now a part of her life, but she could change how she approached them. She could set boundaries. She could carve out time for herself, even if it meant disconnecting from the digital world for a while.

She dug out her old sketchbook, the one she had abandoned months ago, and started to draw. The pencil felt strange in her hand, but the more she drew, the more she remembered who she used to be — the person who once saw the world through a lens of creativity and possibility. She didn’t know if she could get back to the dreams she had forgotten, but she knew she had to try.

Clara closed her eyes for a moment, imagining a future where she was in control of her time, where Zoom calls didn’t dominate her day. She imagined a life where she could pursue her dreams again, even if they were different from what she had once imagined.

And for the first time in a long while, Clara felt a flicker of hope.

Maybe the dreams weren’t completely gone. Maybe they just needed a little time to resurface.
