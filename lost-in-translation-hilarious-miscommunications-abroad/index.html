Lost in Translation: Hilarious Miscommunications Abroad


It was a crisp autumn morning when Sarah arrived in Japan. She had been planning this trip for months — the allure of cherry blossoms, sushi, and a rich cultural experience had lured her to the Land of the Rising Sun. She had taken a few weeks of Japanese language lessons, armed with a handful of phrases like “arigato” (thank you) and “sumimasen” (excuse me). But nothing could prepare her for the series of hilarious misunderstandings that awaited her.

The first of these occurred when she stepped off the plane at Narita Airport. Sarah had studied her Japanese phrases meticulously, even watching YouTube tutorials to perfect her pronunciation. She was ready to impress the locals with her newly acquired language skills. As she walked up to the counter, the attendant greeted her with a polite bow and a warm “Irasshaimase!” (Welcome).

Sarah beamed, took a deep breath, and replied with the phrase she had rehearsed in her mind: “Arigato gozaimasu!” (Thank you very much).

The attendant looked at her, slightly puzzled. “Excuse me?” he asked.

“Arigato gozaimasu,” Sarah repeated, a bit unsure. “Thank you very much?”

The attendant tilted his head, and with a soft smile, he said, “You are very welcome, but that’s not the response I was expecting. I said ‘Irasshaimase,’ which means ‘Welcome.’”

“Oh!” Sarah’s face turned a little red. She had responded to a greeting with a thank you, not realizing the cultural difference. She quickly apologized in English, but the attendant was already laughing gently. “No problem,” he said, still bowing. “Enjoy your stay!”

Sarah couldn’t help but laugh at herself too. Her first attempt at speaking Japanese had already gone awry, but it was all part of the experience, she thought.

Chapter 2: The Train Conundrum

Sarah’s second miscommunication happened on her first day exploring Tokyo. She had set out to visit the famous Shibuya Crossing, known for its chaotic pedestrian traffic and neon lights. She was a bit nervous about navigating the city’s famous train system, but how hard could it be? She had studied the map, and her smartphone had a translation app — she was prepared.

Upon entering the train station, she was immediately greeted by a wall of signs, all in Japanese. Sarah stood frozen, scanning the platform for any signs of an English translation. It was rush hour, and the station was bustling. Trying to appear calm, she stepped up to a nearby attendant, hoping to get some guidance.

The attendant, a middle-aged man in a crisp uniform, was busy assisting another passenger. Sarah waited patiently, and when he finished, she greeted him with a hesitant, “Sumimasen,” (Excuse me). The attendant turned, and with a smile, he said, “How can I help you?”

“I’m looking for the Shibuya Line,” Sarah said, confidently, though she wasn’t completely sure about her pronunciation. “Could you tell me where it is?”

The attendant, who didn’t speak much English, looked at her quizzically and then motioned for her to follow. Sarah followed him eagerly, ready to be led to her destination. However, the attendant didn’t take her to the usual platform where the Shibuya Line trains departed. Instead, he led her down a long hallway and up several flights of stairs, towards an entirely different section of the station.

At the top of the stairs, Sarah looked around and found herself in front of a small, empty room. There were no signs of trains — just a few benches and vending machines. The attendant nodded and pointed to the empty room, saying, “Shibuya Line.”

Perplexed, Sarah glanced around, then back at him. “Is this where the train departs from?” she asked. The attendant nodded, gave her a polite bow, and left.

Still unsure, Sarah stepped inside the room. It was quiet, almost eerily so. But before she could decide what to do, a door on the opposite side of the room opened, and a man in a business suit hurriedly stepped through. “Ah!” he said in a thick Japanese accent, clearly surprised to see Sarah standing in the room. “You’re looking for the Shibuya Line, yes?”

Sarah nodded enthusiastically, thankful that someone spoke English. “Yes, yes! This is where the train departs, right?”

The man smiled widely. “No, no! This is the restroom,” he said, chuckling.

A flood of embarrassment rushed through Sarah. She had mistakenly followed the attendant to a restroom, thinking it was the platform. She quickly apologized, bowing repeatedly. The man laughed and pointed her back toward the correct platform.

Chapter 3: The Sushi Dilemma

Later that evening, Sarah decided to try one of Tokyo’s most famous dishes: sushi. She found a cozy little sushi restaurant tucked away on a quiet street. The place had a simple yet elegant interior, and the chef greeted her with a warm smile as she entered.

Feeling confident now that she had recovered from the train fiasco, Sarah took a seat at the counter. She glanced at the menu, which was entirely in Japanese. But there was one section that seemed to have pictures of the sushi. She pointed to one of the images, hoping to order it.

The chef nodded, smiled, and quickly prepared the dish. When he placed it in front of her, Sarah could tell it was a roll of some sort, but she couldn’t quite identify the ingredients. She looked at it suspiciously, but didn’t want to seem rude.

“Thank you,” she said, attempting to keep her cool as she reached for her chopsticks.

Sarah took a bite. The taste was unlike anything she had ever encountered. There was a crunch, a softness, and a strange, unfamiliar flavor that she couldn’t quite place. Her mind raced. Was it raw fish? Was it seaweed? Maybe it was… was that wasabi mixed in with something?

Her stomach churned a little, but she didn’t want to disappoint the chef. She forced herself to chew and swallow. The chef watched her closely, likely expecting a reaction. She smiled weakly and gave a thumbs-up.

“Delicious!” she said, trying to be polite. But in her head, she was already regretting it. It was definitely not the sushi experience she had imagined.

The chef smiled broadly, clearly pleased. “Thank you! You like?”

“Y-yes,” she said, trying to fight down the gag reflex. “I love it.”

The next bite, however, was even more perplexing. It tasted overwhelmingly salty, almost like a mouthful of brine. Sarah was unsure if she could finish it, but she didn’t want to make a scene.

Just as she was about to swallow, the chef came over to check on her. He pointed at the plate, and then at his own mouth, mimicking the act of chewing. “Good, yes? You like?” he asked, clearly enthusiastic.

Sarah, not wanting to offend him, nodded again and said, “Yes, yes! Very good!”

It wasn’t until after she had finished the entire plate that she realized what she had eaten: a dish made of pickled fermented seaweed. It was an acquired taste, to say the least.

As she left the restaurant, the chef waved enthusiastically, and she waved back, still feeling somewhat queasy but trying to hide it behind a polite smile.

Chapter 4: The Hotel Mix-Up

The following day, Sarah decided to take a day trip to the nearby Mount Fuji area. She had booked a hotel near the base of the mountain, but as she arrived at the address, she found herself at a small, nondescript building with no sign. A small man in a suit greeted her at the door.

“Hello!” he said, bowing deeply. “Welcome!”

Sarah returned the bow politely, trying not to let her confusion show. “I think I have a reservation for a room,” she said in her best Japanese, though she wasn’t entirely sure it was understood.

The man smiled and nodded, gesturing for her to follow him. He led her up a flight of stairs and opened a door to a modest but clean-looking room. There was no desk, just a futon on the floor and a low table.

“This is it,” he said, “your room.”

Sarah looked around, confused. This wasn’t quite what she had imagined. The room was small, and there were no amenities that she had expected, like a television or Wi-Fi. She began to wonder if there had been a mistake. “Um… excuse me,” she began, “I thought I had reserved a… hotel room?”

The man smiled and nodded, as if that was exactly what this was. “Yes, yes. Hotel,” he said proudly.

Sarah wasn’t sure how to explain it, but she realized that the place wasn’t a hotel at all — it was someone’s private home converted into a tiny guest house. The whole situation felt strange, but she didn’t want to seem rude.

She smiled, thanked him, and took a seat on the futon.

Her stay at Mount Fuji turned out to be one of the most peaceful, albeit unexpected, moments of her trip. She learned to embrace the simplicity of her surroundings and let go of any preconceived notions about hotels. The owner even served her a delicious homemade meal of miso soup and rice, which she later realized was more authentic than anything she could’ve had in a fancy restaurant.

Chapter 5: The Departure

As Sarah boarded her flight back home, she reflected on her entire journey. Japan had been filled with hilarious miscommunications and embarrassing moments, but it had also been an adventure of a lifetime. The language barrier, the cultural differences, and the moments of confusion had all contributed to her understanding of the country in a way no guidebook ever could.

Her time in Japan had not been perfect. It had been awkward, hilarious, and at times, downright confusing. But in those moments of lost in translation, she had found the true spirit of travel: learning from mistakes, embracing the unexpected, and laughing at herself.

And as the plane lifted off from Tokyo, Sarah couldn’t help but smile. She couldn’t wait for her next adventure — though she’d definitely be brushing up on her language skills first.

The End.
