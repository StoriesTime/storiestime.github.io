Emerging from the woods, Harriet arrived at a clearing—the first meeting point of the Underground Railroad. We have to ensure their passage to Philadelphia safely.” “Then we must prepare,” she responded, her voice steady and resolute. “We must show them that freedom is not merely a dream, but a path they can walk.” While the sun climbed higher, casting dappled light through the leaves above, Harriet divided the tasks. Some gathered provisions—food and blankets for the weary travelers; others set about reinforcing the old barn as a safe haven. They whispered in hushed tones, sharing stories of courage and survival, weaving a tapestry of hope for those who would soon arrive. As they worked, Harriet caught snippets of their conversations. Stories of families separated, of journeys endured, of the overwhelming fear and the relentless pursuit by slave catchers. Each narrative fueled her growing resolve. Harriet felt a familiar pang of empathy wash over her, knowing that these were not merely strangers; they were an extension of herself—fragments of a shared history. “Welcome, friends,” she greeted, her voice carrying warmth that cut through the fear. “You are safe here. Days turned into weeks, and the sacrifice of those around her painted a vivid picture in Harriet’s mind.
