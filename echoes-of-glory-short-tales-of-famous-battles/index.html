Echoes of Glory: Short Tales of Famous Battles


The sun dipped below the horizon as Thomas Paine looked out over the battlefield, Schoenfeld’s farm. The fields had been ingloriously bathed in the red light of sunset, burnt orange stains against the yellowing deadline of November’s closing days. Crows cawed from lofty branches above, scavengers thirsty for righteousness and victory. Paine closed his eyes for a moment, trying to hear the harmonies that only he could. But the night had stolen the songs of the day, leaving only the somber cry of death and of ‘faults righted’. Battling with one hand clutching his trembling heart, he held his quill in the other, his ink still splotched and dark.

All around him, a remorseful assembly of officers began to gather, their visages cast in the shadows of chilling terror or grim triumph. Henry Knox leaned against a nearby tree, his eyes fixed on his hands as if pondering a great weight. Nathanael Greene, a soldier with certain substantial tattoos, looked as if he had something to prove. For these men and many others, the fight at Lexington and Concord had represented the call to arms in pursuit of a greater truth.

Paine had provided the words - words insinuating themselves into minds and souls as if he had written an ode to the obscure, the blurred, or, perhaps, the divine. Those words burning in the hearts of brave men and women echo with rebellious cries. Paine sighed. It’s his reflections that are being called forth to bring these war-torn soldiers some perspective, given the intensity and magnitude of the upcoming battle.

The muskets lay glinting in the fading light. Those who are alive remember their father’s musket, which was said to have been used at Lexington. In its remains, dreams of bygone battles still simmered, although the wood is now worm-eaten and cracked with age. These images motivated them, late into their lives.

And so did Paine know that writing sometimes means channeling some mightier pen? Beneath the vast expanse draped in the red/orange hues of daylight, amidst creaking grass and a soundtrack of shifting cicadas, these thoughts crashed into waves against the shores of Paine’s mind. His every word drawn from his heart had their own torch-lit presence.

The thump of a horse’s hooves on the ground snapped Paine from his reverie. An officer on horseback arrived, one of the many to pass his sentry duty. Paine looked over and saw Washington astride, his face painted with shadows and the solemn burden of leadership.

“My general,” Paine addressed him, careful not to appear as impertinent or desperate, “I have penned these words for your perusal: ‘For the support of this declaration, a respectable army, befriended by the gracious favor of heaven, must maintain its virtuous habits and become generally respectable to the world, let it raise the wretch from the dust and the humble seeker after peace from the ranks of obscure life.’” His voice trailed off as he handed Washington his manuscript, a tangible testament to the hope, the legacy they carried into this momentous Pennsylvanian winter.

Washington read the words slowly, his chin darting from side to side as if trying to gnaw through the lines. His eyes paused momentarily, reflecting on the complacency he’d fought so hard to escape. He knew within his heart, and in his dreams, the thorny pricking of inaction. Only through action could the cruel “knavery and falsehood” that sought to bury the revolution be rooted out. It was in the bloodcurdling reverie that he glimpsed the raw and hidden dormant that remained buried within his soldiers.

The words resonated within Washington, who mulled the message over and felt himself become anew. They gripped in his chest, radiating a determined chill from his quaking heart to his fingers, where they traced the brittle words. With that icy determination, he knew they would march on toward what could be victory or ruin.

As the soldiers settled into position, Paine’s words took flight, bearing with them the weight of righteousness. Patriots sweltered in the hard rains and brave men found solace in his words and in each other’s company. With a profound finality, they were pushed toward the fate their diverse hearts had summoning.

For in this music of their hearts, America sang.

This memoir, chronicling the reflections of Thomas Paine, carries the narrative of the American Revolution that was frequently obscured by brutal realities and the necessity of war. It unveils not only the movement of history, but also the interweaving struggle to bring dreams and fallen hopes back to life - scriptural murmurings behind the tumult.

It was indeed in the transition from closed, unassuming lives that the downtrodden commoners sprouted new talents. Pushed by every nerve, every dream, they recognized the immensity of this prophecy. Not just Paine’s words, but every dream, every memory of their deceased children fought with them in this great battle.

As Paine scribbled down the haunting melodies of the battle, he spoke about the binds of freedom and humanity that sealed the fate of infamous battles. Yet, in this potent atmosphere, an intriguing question emerges: How did ordinary people breath new life into dreams and legacy? Can an artist bring to life these fallen hopes, can an artist parse the dreams of common folk? And yet, behind that, what did history remember?

Writing is but a solitary dogma etched in time. These words that Thomas Paine thrust into the temping paradigms shaped profound shifts within individuals. For in the battle against misconceptions and the untrue, our players found a quiet strength and faith in their resilience.

Always remember the true revolution is what imagining greater things could look like - a concept both gripping and terrifying. And the unfortunate and ruthless price of war turned this dream into a raging inferno of hope.

In some ways, Paine had his quill carve out paradoxical moments in American history. In other ways, outside of creating poignant verses, he could not delve into the entirety of soldierly fortitude that lays buried within. For history needs an artist, a songwriter that provides to the phrase, “the righting of faults,” a deeper understanding.

That is one compelling reason that these echoes of histories’ familiar worn pages will persist as a confirmation of memory and mysterious life. Unraveling dreams and diversities’ inherent yesterdays, they seek to reveal hidden aspects and subtle fabrics within the annals of history’s tragic lament. And the space into which our dreams unfold creates a perfect interplay with manifest destiny’s power.

Ultimately, each and every individual’s intricate dreams revealed a command heroism that captured heart-felt declaration points within, melding all of this freely to forge ahead to their unfinshed destiny.
