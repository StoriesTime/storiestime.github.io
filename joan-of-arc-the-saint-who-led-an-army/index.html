Joan of Arc: The Saint Who Led an Army


In the quiet meadows of Domrémy, a small village along the Meuse, Joan’s world began in the gentle rhythms of rural life. The crackle of hearth-fires, the bleat of sheep, the ancient timber of her father’s house—these surrounded Joan as she grew, as did the ceaseless talk of battles and the feuding between Armagnacs and Burgundians that echoed along the winds from distant towns. The Hundred Years’ War, a conflict both endless and vague to the villagers, was simply part of the air Joan breathed.

Joan was the youngest daughter, strong-limbed and sharp-eyed, spinning tales and wool with equal ease. She loved the church of Saints Margaret and Catherine, finding solace among the stone saints and the flickering candles. The world beyond Domrémy seemed immense, incomprehensible, and often dangerous—especially as voices of war grew closer. Yet, Joan felt a serenity in her faith, a conviction that illuminated her more deeply than the sun-dappled fields. Unknown to her neighbors, her devotion would one day enkindle hope in a nation battered by invaders.

At thirteen, her peace unraveled with a whisper. While working in her father’s garden, there came a sudden, brilliant light. She fell to her knees as, amid singing bells, a voice unlike any she had known before spoke out from the radiance. When the light faded, it left behind an echo: “Joan, daughter of God, save France.” Saints spoke to her: Michael, Margaret, Catherine—holy protectors, clear as sunlight, commanding her to seek the Dauphin, Charles VII.

Part II: Called by Voices

Through the seasons, the voices persisted. Days grew tense. Villages were burned by marauding soldiers. Rumors passed like shadows—Orléans, once unconquered, was besieged. Joan could not ignore her divine mission. Though her father and mother begged her to stay, to ignore what they assumed was a fever of imagination, she felt the call to action.

With stubborn resolve, Joan approached the local garrison commander, Robert de Baudricourt. At first, he laughed at the young girl in simple dress, but she refused to leave, declaring, “I must go to the Dauphin. I will raise the siege at Orléans.” Her persistence, uncommon for a girl of her station, amused some and troubled others. Soon, whispers of miracles circulated—how she predicted a French defeat outside Orléans, how she never lost her strange composure.

Baudricourt, weighing suspicion and hope, finally relented. In a scarlet dawn, Joan cut her dark hair, donned the tunic and trousers of a soldier, and set out for Chinon, accompanied by loyal men and a growing legend.

The journey was perilous—enemy patrols roamed the wintry woods, and the roads were thick with thieves and the dispossessed. But Joan pressed forward, heartsick yet determined, sometimes praying aloud as she slept on hard ground beneath the shifting sky.

Part III: Chinon and the Crownless King

News of the peasant girl who heard voices arrived at Chinon before Joan herself. Charles VII, the Dauphin—uncrowned and haunted—received her with curiosity and skepticism. To test her, he dressed as a courtier and mingled among his nobles, yet Joan walked straight to him and knelt, addressing him by name.

The court watched in awe. Ministers raged; generals scoffed; the clergy considered her a heretic, even a witch. But as she answered questions about her visions and repeated secrets known only to Charles, his mistrust faded. “Gentle Dauphin,” she said, “I come from the King of Heaven to tell you that you will be crowned and consecrated at Reims.”

Before sending her to lead any army, Charles had Joan examined by theologians at Poitiers. They grilled her with traps and riddles. Joan, illiterate yet fiery, confounded them with blunt honesty. The priests found no heresy, only conviction.

At dawn, Charles handed her a sword and a banner of lilies. News spread rapidly: an illiterate girl was to lead France’s beleaguered soldiers.

Part IV: The Siege of Orléans

Orléans, its walls battered by English cannons and famine inside, became a symbol for all France. When Joan entered the city astride a white horse, clad in shining armor, crowds surged after her, touching her banner as if it could heal the sick and renew courage in the weak.

The generals, wary of a peasant leading their men, soon recognized her effect: soldiers rallied to fight with tenacity unseen in years. She led from the front, rushing into battle with little regard for her own safety. She carried her banner high, unbloodied, encouraging men to press on.

As the sun rose over bloodied ramparts, Joan took an arrow to the shoulder. She pulled it out, undeterred, rallying the troops from the breach. Her confidence poured into weary men until, at last, the English fell back. Within a week, the siege was broken.

Songs and tales sprang up like wildflowers—Joan the Deliverer, Joan the Angel. For the first time in decades, France tasted hope.

Part V: The Road to Reims

Orléans freed, Joan urged the army onward, refusing counsel to rest or wait for winter’s end. Her goal never wavered: Charles’ coronation at Reims, a city guarded by English and Burgundian garrisons.

Joan’s banner swept ahead, cutting through enemy lines as towns fell one by one. Auxerre, Troyes, Châlons—each surrendered or were won with such speed that rumors began: perhaps God truly did walk with this young woman.

In July, the battered company arrived at Reims. With trembling hands, Joan led Charles into the grand cathedral. There, amid smoke and echoes of hymns, as golden oil was poured and the crown lowered onto Charles’ brow, she wept in awe.

“Kneel, for you are consecrated King,” the archbishop intoned. The gathered crowds roared, and at the altar’s edge, Joan collapsed onto her knees, whispering prayers of gratitude and relief.

Part VI: The Turning of the Tide

Joan’s victories could not erase the tangled feuds among French lords or the relentless ambitions of the English. Newly crowned, Charles hesitated, unwilling to follow Joan into further battle. She pleaded with him: “Strike while your enemies tremble.” Yet, inertia prevailed.

Despite setbacks, Joan never shrank from battle. In skirmish after skirmish, she rallied fighters, raised spirits, and led assaults, sometimes armed only with her unwavering faith.

Then, in the spring of 1430, while attempting to relieve Compiègne, Joan was unhorsed and captured by Burgundian soldiers. She fought savagely, refusing to surrender, dragged through mud and flame as her followers fell or fled.

Handed to the English, she was escorted to Rouen in chains.

Part VII: Trial and Martyrdom

In a chill winter chamber, the trial began. The inquisitors, experts in twisting words, sneered at the peasant girl who had embarrassed their armies. They pressed her for weeks on theology, prophecy, obedience, and the nature of her voices.

Joan, battered but unbroken, stood her ground. “If I am not in the grace of God, I hope He will set me there. If I am, may He keep me.” They tried to trip her, to catch her in heresy or pride, but she answered with direct, often astonishing clarity.

Torture was threatened. Threats alternated with promises of mercy, yet she remained steadfast, refusing to renounce her visions.

Eventually, the verdict was sealed. Joan of Arc was condemned for heresy and witchcraft, a verdict that fluttered on scraps of paper already torched by fear and politics.

On May 30, 1431, at nineteen, Joan was led to the marketplace of Rouen. As flames devoured the pyre and smoke choked the air, Joan called on God and forgave her accusers. French soldiers who had mocked her wept openly. An English captain muttered, “We are lost; we have burned a saint.”

Part VIII: Legacy of the Maid

Though her body was reduced to ashes, Joan’s legend proved unquenchable. Tales of her courage traveled the breadth of Christendom. Within decades, her name became a rallying cry for resistance. Eventually, even her enemies repented; a new trial declared Joan innocent, a martyr, and pure in faith.

For centuries, Joan of Arc has stood as a symbol of unshakable conviction, uncompromising courage, and hope in the face of overwhelming odds. Her visions, battles, and death have been recounted in the whispers of churches, the pages of history, and the dreams of all who fight for something greater than themselves.

What remained at Domrémy, where the great rivers flow and the grass still grows beneath the village church, was a memory—of a girl who followed voices, defied kings and conquerors, and became the saint who led an army.

The story of Joan of Arc endures not just because of her victories or her death, but because her unwavering faith changed the course of history, leaving an echo that still inspires all who dare to answer the call of the impossible.
