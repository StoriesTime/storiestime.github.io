The Life and Legacy of Harriet Tubman: Freedom Fighter


In the dense, misty swamps of Maryland, a young woman named Harriet Tubman was born into bondage in the early 1820s. From the moment she took her first breath, the chains of slavery sought to define her existence. Yet, through sheer courage, resilience, and an unyielding spirit, Harriet Tubman would carve a path not only for her own freedom but for hundreds of others. Her legacy as a fearless abolitionist and freedom fighter endures, embodying the relentless pursuit of liberty and justice.

Early Life Under Tyranny

Harriet Tubman entered the world as Araminta Ross, the daughter of Harriet and Ben Ross. Her childhood was marked by hardship, labor, and cruelty endemic to the system of American slavery. Working on a plantation, her days melted away under the oppressive heat and the demands of constant toil. Despite the physical and emotional toll, Harriet’s spirit remained unbroken.

At an early age, Harriet experienced a severe head injury when a overseer threw a heavy metal weight aimed at another slave. The strike changed her life irrevocably—she suffered from seizures, intense headaches, and visions that many would later believe were spiritual gifts, guiding her path toward freedom.

From these visions and dreams, Harriet’s faith blossomed. She became deeply religious, believing that God would lead her to liberation, not only for herself but for others who languished in slavery.

The Great Escape

By the mid-1840s, Harriet’s determination to escape the chains of slavery had grown unbearable. In 1849, seizing an opportunity while her owner was away, she fled the plantation under cover of darkness. She navigated dozens of miles through forest and swamp, guided by the North Star and her steadfast belief in freedom.

Harriet’s journey to the North was fraught with danger. Slave catchers roamed, laws like the Fugitive Slave Act of 1850 made escape even more perilous, and betrayal lurked in every shadow. Yet her keen instincts and unwavering resolve saw her cross into Pennsylvania, a free state, securing her liberty.

But Harriet’s story did not end with her own freedom.

Conductor on the Underground Railroad

Rather than settle into a quiet life, Harriet Tubman transformed into “Moses,” a conductor on the Underground Railroad—an intricate and perilous network of secret routes and safe houses designed to shepherd enslaved people to freedom.

Between 1850 and 1860, Harriet made at least thirteen daring trips back into the slave states, guiding nearly seventy enslaved men, women, and children out of bondage. She never lost a single passenger, a testament to her cunning, courage, and strategic brilliance.

Her success hinged on careful planning, trust in allies across states, and her extraordinary knowledge of terrain. Harriet’s intuitive understanding of human nature enabled her to identify safe havens along the way and avoid capture.

Each rescue was a calculated risk. Harriet carried a pistol—not only to defend herself but to maintain order among her group, demonstrating her decisive and authoritative leadership. Her courage inspired others to believe that escape was possible.

Resistance in the Civil War

When the American Civil War erupted in 1861, Harriet Tubman found new ways to fight for freedom. She volunteered as a cook, nurse, and spy for the Union Army, using her intimate knowledge of southern geography and slave communities to gather intelligence.

Her role as a scout was historic. In 1863, Harriet helped lead the Combahee River Raid in South Carolina, an operation that liberated over 700 enslaved people. This mission marked the first time a woman led an armed expedition in the war, underscoring her significance as a military strategist and symbol of resistance.

Her tireless efforts extended beyond the battlefield. Harriet cared for freed slaves, providing nursing support and advocating for their integration into society. She understood that abolition was just the first step towards true liberation.

Life After the War

After the war, Harriet Tubman dedicated herself to civil rights and humanitarian work. She settled in Auburn, New York, where she purchased land and established a home for elderly African Americans, embodying her lifelong commitment to community welfare.

Harriet remained a vocal advocate for women’s suffrage, aligning her fight for racial justice with the broader struggle for gender equality. She spoke publicly, urging the nation to extend voting rights to all citizens, regardless of race or sex.

Despite her monumental contributions, Harriet lived in relative poverty during her later years. The government struggled to compensate her adequately for her war service, reflecting broader societal neglect of Black women’s sacrifices.

The Enduring Impact of Harriet Tubman

Harriet Tubman’s legacy resonates profoundly in American history. She shattered barriers of fear and oppression, providing a beacon of hope in the darkest of times. Her life story teaches lessons of bravery, selflessness, and unyielding faith.

Her name graces schools, streets, and national monuments, immortalizing her impact. The Harriet Tubman Underground Railroad National Historical Park preserves her memory and educates future generations about the fight against slavery.

In 2016, the United States Treasury announced plans to feature Harriet Tubman’s image on the $20 bill, symbolically recognizing her role as one of the nation’s greatest heroes.

Reflecting on a Life of Courage

Harriet Tubman’s journey from enslaved child to freedom fighter reveals the strength of the human spirit when fueled by hope and justice. She defied the cruel institution of slavery, risking life and limb to guide others into liberty.

Her story is not just about history; it is a call to action, reminding us that the pursuit of freedom often demands extraordinary courage.

As society continues to grapple with issues of racial inequality and social justice, Harriet Tubman remains a symbol of resistance and resilience—a beacon encouraging each individual to stand against oppression in all its forms.

Her legacy is alive in the countless lives she touched and the enduring inspiration she provides to those who strive for a more equitable world. Harriet Tubman’s life and legacy demonstrate that the fight for freedom is both a personal journey and a collective mission, forever etched in the annals of history.
