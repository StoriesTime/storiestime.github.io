The Siege of Vicksburg: A Turning Point in American History


In the sweltering summer of 1863, the city of Vicksburg, Mississippi, was the focus of a Union army's relentless assault on the last major stronghold of the Confederacy on the Mississippi River. The Union's ultimate goal was to cut off the Confederacy from the Trans-Mississippi, severing a vital transportation route and crippling the nation's ability to wage war. The defenders of Vicksburg, led by General Ulysses S. Grant, were determined to hold their ground. The Siege Begins Grant's army of approximately 48,000 soldiers encircled the city, once a bustling port and industrial center, cutting off all escape routes and supplies. The Union forces, bolstered by the contributions of African American units, poured artillery fire down on the city, causing widespread destruction and stifling the defenders' attempts to launch sorties against them. The once-thriving trading center had become a seedy network of makeshift camps and bewildered refugees. The Last Remnants of the Confederate Army General Joseph E.
