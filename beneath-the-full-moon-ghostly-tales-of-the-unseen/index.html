Beneath the Full Moon: Ghostly Tales of the Unseen


Under the full moon, the village of Silverwood lay still, as though frozen in time. The night air carried a chill, yet the villagers continued their routines, their lives intertwined with the haunting stories passed down through generations. Tales of spirits, shadows, and forgotten souls whispered in every corner of the village. But few dared to speak of the true darkness that stirred beneath the light of the full moon, when the veil between the living and the dead thinned, and the unseen could cross into the world of the living.

The Arrival of the Traveler

It was on one such night that a traveler arrived in Silverwood, his face obscured by the shadows of his hood, his steps quiet and deliberate. He had no name, no destination, and no story to tell—at least, not to the villagers who watched him from behind their windows. The full moon had risen high in the sky, casting an eerie glow over the ancient cobblestones, and with it, the stories of the past began to stir.

The traveler sought shelter in the inn at the edge of town, a rickety old building with sagging beams and the smell of wet earth. Inside, the innkeeper, a burly man named Thomas, watched the stranger warily as he entered. “No one travels this late, especially not on nights like this,” he muttered to himself as he wiped his hands on his apron. But he said nothing aloud to the traveler. Instead, he led him to a small room at the back of the inn, its single window facing the dense forest that bordered the village.

As the stranger settled in, he couldn’t shake the feeling that he was being watched. The hairs on the back of his neck prickled, and a strange, cold presence seemed to hang in the air. He turned to the window, peering into the inky blackness of the forest, where the trees swayed gently in the wind, their branches twisting like skeletal fingers.

“Just the wind,” he whispered to himself, though a gnawing feeling in his gut told him otherwise.

The Old Woman’s Warning

The next morning, the traveler ventured out into the village. He was drawn to the ancient church at the center of town, its stone walls weathered by centuries of storms and time. The door creaked open as he entered, the scent of incense and old wood filling his nostrils. Inside, an old woman sat by the altar, her gnarled hands clutching a wooden rosary. Her eyes, though clouded by age, seemed to pierce through him.

“You seek something,” she said in a voice that rasped like dry leaves. “But beware, for the full moon brings more than just light. It brings the unseen.”

The traveler hesitated, then nodded slowly. “What do you mean?”

The old woman smiled, her teeth yellowed and sharp. “The moon has a power. It calls to the restless, the souls who wander between worlds. And on nights like this, when the moon is full and bright, they cross over. They seek the living, drawn by their fear, their desires… their regrets.”

The traveler stiffened, the hair on his neck standing on end. “Is there a way to stop them?”

“Perhaps,” she replied cryptically. “But you must be careful what you wish for. Not all ghosts are the same, and not all spirits are harmless.”

Before he could ask any more questions, the old woman stood and shuffled toward the door. “Be gone before dusk,” she called over her shoulder. “The full moon will rise soon, and with it, the dangers you cannot see.”

The Restless Spirits

That evening, as the sun dipped below the horizon, a sense of unease settled over Silverwood. The villagers hurried about their business, closing shutters and locking doors, preparing for the night ahead. For them, the full moon was more than a celestial event—it was a warning.

At the inn, the traveler sat by the fire, the crackling flames casting dancing shadows on the walls. His thoughts lingered on the old woman’s words. He had heard stories of restless spirits before—of those who had died violently, of souls bound to this world by unfulfilled desires or unfinished business. But something about the way she spoke… something about the way the air felt that night… it made him uneasy.

Suddenly, a soft knock at the door broke his train of thought. He opened it to find a young girl standing on the threshold, her face pale and gaunt, her eyes wide with fear.

“Please,” she whispered, her voice trembling, “you must help us. They are coming.”

The traveler frowned, stepping aside to let her in. “Who is coming?”

“The ghosts,” she said, her breath quickening. “They come on the full moon, drawn to those who are alone, those who are afraid. We… we don’t know how to stop them. They… they take us.”

The traveler’s heart raced as he looked at the girl, her trembling hands clutching the edges of her cloak. He had heard the rumors, of course, but he had always dismissed them as superstition. Now, standing in the dim light of the inn, with the girl’s fearful eyes locked on his, he began to wonder if there might be some truth to the stories after all.

“I will help you,” he said, more to calm her than anything else.

But the girl shook her head, her face twisting in anguish. “You cannot. No one can. Once the moon is full, they come. And they will not leave until they have taken what they want.”

The Night of the Full Moon

As night fell and the full moon rose higher in the sky, the village of Silverwood grew eerily silent. The air was thick with anticipation, a tension that seemed to hang in every corner of the town. The traveler, now fully aware of the danger that lurked beneath the moonlight, made his way to the church where the old woman had warned him about the spirits.

The doors to the church creaked open, and he stepped inside, the flickering light of candles casting long shadows across the stone floor. The old woman was gone, but her warning echoed in his mind. He knew the ghosts were coming. But what could he do? How could one man stand against the forces of the unseen?

Suddenly, the door slammed shut behind him. He spun around, his heart pounding in his chest. The temperature in the church dropped, and a low, eerie hum filled the air, as though something was stirring just beneath the surface.

Then, through the darkness, they appeared. Ghostly figures, translucent and pale, their faces twisted in agony, their bodies hovering above the ground. They moved with a slow, deliberate grace, their eyes fixed on the traveler as they circled him like vultures.

He could feel their presence in his bones, their cold fingers brushing against his skin. The air seemed to hum with their whispers, voices from the past, crying out for release. The traveler tried to move, but his legs felt heavy, as though the weight of the ghosts was pressing down on him.

“Leave this place!” he shouted, his voice trembling. “I won’t let you take anyone else.”

The spirits paused, their eyes flickering with a mix of sorrow and hunger. For a moment, there was silence, and the traveler felt a flicker of hope. Perhaps he could fight them off, perhaps he could stop the curse that had haunted the village for so long.

But then, the ground beneath him cracked open, and from the depths of the earth rose a shadow far darker than any of the ghosts. A figure, tall and cloaked in a swirling vortex of darkness, emerged, its eyes glowing with an unnatural light.

“You are too late,” the shadow whispered, its voice like a thousand voices speaking in unison. “The full moon has risen, and now the unseen shall claim what is theirs.”

The traveler stood frozen, his heart racing as the shadow reached out toward him. But just as it was about to engulf him, the church door burst open, and a bright, blinding light flooded the room.

The ghosts recoiled, shrieking in terror, as the shadow writhed in pain. The traveler turned to see the old woman standing in the doorway, her hands raised high, her rosary glowing with an ethereal light.

“Begone!” she cried, her voice strong and commanding. “The spirits of the past cannot rule the living.”

With a final, agonized howl, the shadow disappeared into the darkness, and the ghosts vanished with it, leaving only silence behind.

The Aftermath

When the dawn finally broke over Silverwood, the village was quiet. The full moon had set, and the spirits had retreated back into the shadows from which they came. The traveler stood at the edge of the churchyard, watching as the villagers emerged from their homes, as though nothing had happened.

But he knew better. The village had been saved, for now, but the unseen would always be there, lurking just beneath the surface, waiting for the next full moon.

The old woman was gone, her warnings now a part of the village’s history. And the traveler, forever changed by his encounter with the unseen, would continue on his journey, knowing that beneath every full moon, there were ghostly tales waiting to be told—stories of the dead, the forgotten, and the restless souls who never truly left.

And so, beneath the full moon, the ghosts of Silverwood slept—for now.

The end.
