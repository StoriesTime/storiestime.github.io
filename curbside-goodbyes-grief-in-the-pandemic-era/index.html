Curbside Goodbyes: Grief in the Pandemic Era


The pandemic arrived without warning, like a shadow creeping into a well-lit room. In the beginning, there was confusion. The world had never known such widespread isolation, such sudden disruption. As countries locked down and cities shut their doors, it became clear that this would be a time of unimaginable loss. Not just the loss of life, but the loss of human connection, of ritual, of touch. Among all the changes that came with the COVID-19 era, the one that haunted people most was how grief had to be navigated differently.

Lena sat on the edge of her bed, staring out of the window, watching the gray clouds swirl in the sky. It had been two weeks since her mother died. Two weeks since she’d stood at the curb, in a facemask, holding her phone to her ear as her mother’s funeral procession drove away. That had been the last goodbye.

Her mind wandered back to the day she had received the call. Her mother’s voice, already weak from years of health complications, had faded completely in those final moments, replaced by the sound of a doctor’s voice on the other end of the line. The doctor explained the situation, the deteriorating health, the hospital’s overrun capacity, and the inability to allow family members into the building.

For years, Lena had imagined a different kind of goodbye. She imagined holding her mother’s hand, offering one final squeeze, whispering words of love, perhaps shedding a few tears, maybe laughing over shared memories. But the pandemic took that option from her and countless others.

Instead, Lena had watched her mother’s condition worsen over FaceTime, hoping her presence through a screen would offer some solace. Yet it never felt enough. The delay between the words they shared and the video feed—clumsy, disconnected—was a reminder of just how far apart they were, even in the same room, separated by walls of glass and the world outside.

The day her mother passed, Lena had been alone in her apartment, just like everyone else, in lockdown. The nurse who called her from the hospital had been polite but distant, a stranger recounting events she couldn’t truly understand. There were no comforting arms to cradle her, no shoulder to cry on. The pandemic had made sure of that. Grief, in this new era, felt different—lonelier.

Lena had to make decisions she never thought she’d make alone. Funeral arrangements, decisions on what to do with her mother’s belongings—these things had once been a shared burden, passed from one person to the next. But not now. Not in a time of distancing and mask-wearing, when family gatherings could be no larger than ten people and only a few were allowed to gather at the graveside.

She hadn’t even been allowed to touch her mother’s body, a fact that haunted her in the quiet moments, when she was alone with her thoughts. How could one grieve without the sacred act of touching the person they had lost? How could she say goodbye to the woman who had brought her into the world when all she had was a screen?

The funeral itself was a somber affair. No packed church or heartfelt eulogies from family members who’d traveled from distant states. Instead, a small group of mourners stood distanced, dressed in their finest masks, six feet apart, speaking in hushed tones.

Lena had never felt so disconnected from the people she loved. The distant faces on the Zoom call, the awkward pauses as the funeral director read out her mother’s name, the sterile, mechanical feel of it all—it was as if her mother’s death had happened in a vacuum. No one could comfort anyone else. No one could hug, hold hands, or share a tear.

A week later, Lena sat on her couch, scrolling through the many messages and social media posts from friends and family offering their condolences. A part of her felt grateful for their words—many of them heartfelt, others more formal, awkward, as people tried to navigate the unfamiliar terrain of pandemic grief. But the messages, though kind, didn’t fill the emptiness she felt.

How could they? They couldn’t replace the physical touch, the deep connection of simply being there for someone. The comfort of human presence—no matter how well-intentioned, no matter how sincere the words—was simply not the same. She felt the weight of her grief in every corner of her home. The absence of the familiar was palpable: the phone calls that would never come again, the absence of the woman who had once been the heart of her family.

The next few weeks passed in a blur of emails, virtual meetings, and quiet nights spent sifting through her mother’s belongings. The small apartment had once been filled with laughter, with her mother’s gentle reminders to call her more often, to slow down, to eat better. Now, it echoed with silence.

Lena would sit in her mother’s old armchair, running her fingers across the fabric, trying to find some trace of her, some small piece of her essence. She looked through old photo albums, listening to old voicemails left on her phone, desperate to hold onto the sound of her mother’s voice.

It was the curbside goodbyes that stayed with Lena, though. The image of standing there, masked and distanced, watching the hearse drive away, was something she couldn’t shake. The ceremony had been brief—no long speeches, no music to carry the weight of her grief. Just a quick exchange of flowers at a distance, a hurried wave from a safe distance, and then, the long, lonely drive back to an empty apartment.

She was not the only one. Around the world, people were experiencing the same isolation. People were saying goodbye without the comfort of a hug, without the strength of a familiar hand to hold. The pandemic had redefined grief, forcing it to be experienced in isolation, forcing people to mourn in ways that felt incomplete.

Lena’s grief came in waves. Some days, it was a dull ache, a longing for something that felt unreachable. Other days, it was a fierce, raw emotion that threatened to drown her. There were moments when she felt like she couldn’t breathe, like the weight of everything—the death, the isolation, the uncertainty—was too much to bear.

But there were also small moments of peace. They were fleeting, but they came nonetheless. Like when she sat in her mother’s chair and remembered the quiet nights they had spent together, watching old movies, talking about life, and laughing over shared memories.

A few months later, as life began to return to a new kind of normal, Lena found herself going through her mother’s belongings. She came across a box of letters her mother had written over the years. Some were to her, others to distant relatives, friends, and even old lovers. Each letter was a glimpse into her mother’s life—a life that Lena had never fully understood until now.

It was in those letters that Lena began to find some kind of solace. She could feel her mother’s presence again, not in the physical sense, but in the words she had left behind. And though it wasn’t the same as holding her hand or hearing her voice, it was a reminder that the love between them had not been erased by distance or by the pandemic.

Lena smiled softly as she read one final letter, written just a few months before her mother passed. It was simple but meaningful: “Don’t be afraid of the goodbyes. Love never fades, not even in times of separation.”

Grief in the pandemic era wasn’t easy. It was jagged, fractured, and often lonely. But it was also a shared experience. Around the world, people were learning to say goodbye in new ways—at curbside, in waiting rooms, over video calls, and in moments of quiet solitude. They were finding strength not in the traditional rituals of mourning, but in the resilience of their love and their memories.

Lena knew that her mother would always be with her, not just in the spaces they had once shared, but in the way she had shaped Lena into who she was today. And though the pandemic had stolen so many of the comforts of mourning, it could never take away the connection they had. That, Lena realized, was something that would never be lost, no matter the distance, no matter the circumstances.
