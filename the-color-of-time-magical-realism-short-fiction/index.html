The Color of Time: Magical Realism Short Fiction


The first time Mateo noticed the town’s peculiar relationship with time was on a quiet Wednesday afternoon. Sunlight filtered through the sugar maples lining the narrow streets of Valdéz, a place where ages seemed to weave together like threads in an ancient tapestry. But in Valdéz, time wasn’t just something to measure—it had a color.

Mateo was eight when he discovered the color of time. His abuela, the town’s unofficial storyteller, had always insisted that everyone sees time differently. She called it el color del tiempo — the color of time. “You’ll understand when it chooses to show you,” she said, rocking her chair on the porch, a knowing smile beneath her weathered eyes.

At first, Mateo thought she was teasing. Time was either past or future, measured by a clock or the calendar. But then, at the local market, amid the jumble of fresh fruits and woven crafts, he saw it: a shimmering hue pulsing faintly against the worn wooden stalls, shifting from gold to indigo in a fluid dance.

It wasn’t just a visual quirk. Time here stretched and compressed like the rubber bands he played with. Yesterday’s light merged into tomorrow’s shadow, and moments spilled over with unexpected emotion.

Near the fountain in the town square, where water gushed in endless loops, Mateo felt the colors deepen. The water’s spray caught iridescent light, painting the air in soft blues and luminous tangerines. He blinked, and the scene slipped back to normal—but inside, a seed of wonder had taken root.

The Arrival of Luna

Years passed, and Mateo grew into a young man curious enough to chase the mysteries swirling around Valdéz. His abuela had passed, but her stories lingered like the scent of marigold tea. It was during the town’s autumn festival that Luna arrived.

She came dressed in a cloak the color of twilight, her eyes reflecting a kaleidoscope of memories. Luna wasn’t from Valdéz—or from any place Mateo recognized. Yet, like him, she was drawn to the town’s strange temporal aura.

“I hear you see the colors too,” Luna said softly after they bumped into each other near the marigold stalls.

Mateo nodded, uncertain but intrigued. “It’s like… time isn’t just minutes and hours. It has feeling—a color.”

She smiled. “Exactly. And I’m here because I’ve lost my time.”

Mateo’s brow furrowed. “Lost your time? How can someone lose time?”

“It’s not simple,” she replied, brushing a strand of violet-tinted hair from her face. “Some moments vanish from us, slipping into the folds of time’s spectrum. I need to reclaim mine.”

The festival lights flickered as laughter and music swirled around the plaza. Mateo offered to help, captivated by the unfolding enigma.

Chasing the Past in Color

The next morning, Mateo and Luna wandered beyond the familiar borders of Valdéz, following streaks of iridescent light that only they seemed able to perceive.

They crossed the old bridge over the river, where the currents hummed with soft silver hues. “Time here bends,” Luna observed. “You can see every choice you’ve ever made, like threads of silk stretched across a loom.”

Mateo reached out, touching the glowing air, and suddenly a scene played out—himself at seven, running barefoot under a summer rainstorm, laughing with abandon. The color was a warm amber, full of youthful innocence.

“It’s beautiful,” Mateo breathed. “But why can’t I see everyone else’s colors?”

Luna shook her head. “Because time reveals itself only when you’re truly present. And it hides when fear or regret cloud your heart.”

As the sun dipped lower, casting the town in a deep violet twilight, they found themselves at the base of an ancient ceiba tree—roots sprawling like veins into the earth.

“This is the heart of Valdéz,” Luna said. She closed her eyes, whispering words Mateo couldn’t understand. The colors around them shifted violently, swirling from crimson to jade to cobalt.

Suddenly, a figure emerged—a younger Luna, trembling and lost, trapped in a liminal space between moments. Mateo reached out, touching her shoulder, anchoring her in the here and now.

Restoring What Was Lost

Helping Luna reclaim her scattered moments took days, each a journey through fragmented memories, each memory painted with a unique shade of time.

They revisited her childhood home bathed in soft rose-colored light, reliving laughter mixed with the ache of early loss. They walked the fields beneath a golden sunset where Luna had first dreamed of a life beyond limits imposed by circumstance.

With every restored memory, the colors brightened, as if time itself was healing.

But during their travels, Mateo began to notice his own time flickering unsteadily. Memories he once cherished blurred, moments he held dear losing their vividness.

“Am I losing my own time?” Mateo asked one evening, his voice small.

Luna nodded, her own regained memories shimmering brightly. “We’ve connected deeply, and that means our times are intertwined. But sometimes, to save one’s time, another must surrender pieces of theirs.”

Mateo faced a choice: to keep chasing Luna’s lost time and risk fading, or to let her complete her journey alone.

The night was ink-black, alive with faint stars, when Mateo made his decision. He pressed his hands over his heart and let go, surrendering the brighter shades of his past—the glowing ambers and sunlit golds—to Luna.

The New Color of Time

Time in Valdéz never settled into linearity again. Instead, moments lingered, stretched and shared, colors blending within the town’s residents like a communal canvas.

Luna, now whole, stayed on as its quiet guardian, weaving the threads of the community’s histories into a vibrant mosaic.

Mateo, though the colors within him were softer now, carried a different kind of light—a gentle, steady glow that bound the present and future.

One evening, standing once more beside the ceiba tree, Mateo watched the sky bloom in an unusual hue—soft lavender edged with streaks of emerald.

Time had become a dance of colors, infinite and fluid.

And in Valdéz, every person carried a piece of that magic, knowing that moments, just like colors, are more alive than anyone could have imagined.

The color of time was no longer a mystery in Valdéz—it was a living story, rippling through hearts and memories, illuminating the unseen, and inviting all who looked to see beyond the ordinary.
