The Colors of India: A Train Journey Across the Subcontinent


The sun began its ascent over the horizon, brushing the edges of the sky with hues of saffron and crimson, as Meera stood on the crowded platform of Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus in Mumbai. A faint mist curled through the morning air, carrying with it the scent of freshly brewed chai and fried samosas from the vendor stalls lining the platform. The announcement blared through the speakers, marking the arrival of the Konkan Express, the first leg of her audacious journey across India by train.

With a large leather duffel bag slung over her shoulder and a small diary clutched in her palm, Meera felt both exhilarated and slightly overwhelmed. She had spent years living abroad, her roots in India a distant memory. Now, she had returned—not just to reconnect with her homeland but to rediscover its soul. She hoped India’s expansive railway network, stretching over tens of thousands of miles and traversing deserts, forests, mountains, and plains, would serve as the thread that wove her back into the tapestry of her heritage.

The Coastline Blushes: Mumbai to Goa

As the train lurched forward, Meera watched the sprawling chaos of Mumbai recede, giving way to quiet fishing villages dotting the Arabian Sea. The Konkan Railway was a marvel of engineering cutting through verdant green cliffs, past rivers and waterfalls that spilled from rocky ledges. The morning rush of a monsoon breeze flooded the train compartment, carrying the fragrant aroma of wet earth.

Her compartment-mates were a microcosm of India itself: a young couple heading to the beaches of Goa for their honeymoon; an elderly man with a kind, wrinkled face wrapped in a white kurta who regaled them with ancient legends about the Konkan coast; and a family of four, the children delighting in their packed tiffin boxes of puris and mango pickle.

By noon, the train pulled into Madgaon station. Meera stepped off briefly to catch her breath amid the lively chaos. Vendors called out, offering packets of cashews grown in Goa’s orchards, bottles of local feni, and strips of jaggery. The platform buzzed with energy as passengers disembarked and boarded before the departure whistle cut through the commotion.

As the train pressed southward, the landscape seemed to become imbued with the essence of Goa itself: palm-fringed fields, shimmering backwaters, and the vibrant colors of Portuguese-style homes tucked into the greenery. It was as though life had spilled every color in its palette across the western coastline.

Spice and Gold: Mangalore to Kochi

Switching trains at Mangalore, Meera boarded the Malabar Express. A leisurely calm seemed to settle over the journey as the train traversed Kerala’s backwaters. Coconut palms lined the edges of placid lagoons, forming shadows that danced on the surface. The scent of freshly cooked fish curry from nearby villages wafted through the open windows. The rhythmic clatter of the wheels on the tracks, almost like a heartbeat, lulled Meera into a momentary reverie.

At the next station, a pair of Kathakali artists dressed in traditional green and red makeup boarded, their vibrant costumes resembling something otherworldly. They explained they were heading to a performance in Kochi, their voices animated as they described the folklore and mythology that inspired their dance. Meera listened, fascinated, as one of them traced his roots back to a line of temple dancers who performed for the gods along this very coast.

By the time the train sinuously approached Kochi, the setting sun seemed to paint gold along the waterways. Meera could scarcely imagine a more picturesque arrival. The sight of Chinese fishing nets swaying gently against the backdrop of the twilight sky made her exit with a sense of awe.

Into the Hills: Kochi to Madurai

From Kochi, Meera boarded the Palaruvi Express headed to Madurai, her next destination. The landscape began to morph as the train climbed into the Western Ghats, a mountain range beloved for its biodiversity. The air grew cooler, carrying with it the scent of tea plantations and wildflowers.

In the dining car, Meera struck up a conversation with a tea estate worker who had boarded at a small station en route. He handed her a pouch of fresh tea leaves, their aroma intoxicating. As they chatted, he spoke of the hills’ quiet beauty, the ritual of tea picking, and the folklore of the Nilgiri Hills.

By the time she arrived in Madurai in the early evening, the chaos of the city was a stark contrast to the serenity of the hills. Yet this ancient temple town, glowing in the warm light of countless oil lamps on the streets, exuded an entirely different kind of magic. The sound of temple bells ringing in the distance was a reminder that in India, the gods were never far away.

Across Plains into Heartland: Chennai to Varanasi

After a brief but rich exploration of Tamil Nadu, Meera caught an overnight train—this time, the iconic Tamil Nadu Express—to Chennai. From there, she switched lines onto a northbound train toward Varanasi. This stretch of India was vast and sprawling, crossing the Deccan Plateau and then opening onto the fertile Indo-Gangetic plains. The scenery rolled endlessly in shades of amber, yellow, and green. Fields of sunflowers stretched to the horizon, interspersed with temple domes peeking through treetops.

The compartment was quieter this time, populated by pilgrims and sages heading toward the sacred city of Varanasi. One woman, draped in a yellow saree, told Meera she had been traveling from Kanyakumari—the southernmost tip of India—to wash away her sins in the Ganges. Meera smiled, once again struck by how journeys on India’s trains often seemed to weave spiritual undertakings with physical movement.

By the time the train arrived at Varanasi, the cacophonous sounds of life on the Ganges had already reached her ears. Meera spent the evening watching diyas—small, flickering oil lamps—float down the river during the Ganga Aarti ceremony, blending with the thousands of stars above. Colors reflected in the dark, sacred waters like a painter’s masterpiece.

The Roof of India: Varanasi to Darjeeling

From Varanasi, Meera journeyed eastward, eventually boarding the narrow-gauge Darjeeling Himalayan Railway, famously known as the Toy Train. This time, the train snaked upward through pine forests and gorges, wrapping around the Himalayas like a determined vine. The crisp mountain air invigorated her as sunlight filtered through wisps of clouds enveloping the peaks.

The vistas here were unlike anything she had seen before: snow-capped mountains towering above tea gardens that blanketed the slopes in emerald shades. She stopped at Kurseong station and sipped on a freshly brewed cup of Darjeeling tea, marveling at how it tasted of the mountains—strong, earthy, and pure.

When she finally reached Darjeeling, an orange-gold sunrise greeted her from Tiger Hill. The Kanchenjunga mountain range glimmered in the distance, bathed in the first light of day. It felt like an apt conclusion to her incredible journey: India, a land of endless horizons and shifting palettes, offering each traveler something new to carry in their soul.

Home in a Million Hues

By the end of her trip, Meera’s diary was filled with sketches, pressed flowers, train tickets, and hastily scribbled lines about memories she vowed never to forget. Each corner of India she had traversed told its own story, painted in its own vibrant colors. And yet, every thread was interwoven into the same rich tapestry—a vast, ancient, ever-changing subcontinent teeming with life and diversity.

As she boarded her final train back to Mumbai, Meera realized that the journey had not only taken her across the length and breadth of India but had brought her closer to herself. Like the country, she, too, was many colors—complex, multifaceted, and beautiful in her contradictions.
