Faces of Prejudice: Short Tales on Racism and Its Impact


Jasmine walked into the playground, the familiar buzz of children playing settling around her. But there was a subtle shift in the air—something she could never quite place. It was as though she could hear whispers between the wind and the rustling of trees, voices that weren’t meant for her ears but reached her with a clarity that cut through the noise.

“Why is she even here?”

A group of girls stood by the swings, their eyes flicking toward her with a sort of calculated indifference that made Jasmine’s stomach twist. It wasn’t a question born out of curiosity but out of a deep-rooted belief that she didn’t belong. Her skin, the color of dark chocolate, and her features, shaped by a heritage she was proud of, made her a target in ways she didn’t fully understand.

Jasmine was new to the school. She had tried to tell herself that it would be different this time, that she could start fresh, but that voice, so subtle yet so strong, always seemed to follow her. No matter how many times she told herself she belonged, the feeling of being an outsider remained.

It wasn’t the first time Jasmine had experienced it. Her mother had tried to prepare her for it when they moved from their hometown. “People will see you differently,” her mother had warned gently, her dark eyes filled with a quiet sadness. “It’s not your fault, Jasmine. It’s just how some people are.”

But no amount of preparation could shield her from the sting of those words. The school was supposed to be a place of learning, of making friends, of sharing moments of joy. But instead, every classroom felt like a minefield, every hallway an obstacle to cross.

On her first day, she had been placed in a corner of the cafeteria where there was plenty of space—too much space. The empty seat next to her felt like an accusation, a reminder that her skin was a barrier too high to cross. The whispers, always too close, made her aware of the way she ate her lunch, the way she walked, the way she spoke, as if everything about her was being scrutinized under a microscope.

“Are you sure she belongs here?” A girl with blonde hair and a smug grin had asked her during science class, just loud enough for the entire row to hear.

Jasmine had tried to ignore it, to keep her focus on the lesson, but the words clung to her like a weight she couldn’t shake.

It wasn’t just the kids in her class, either. Sometimes it was the teachers—subtle, in the way they would offer a little extra praise to her classmates while passing over Jasmine’s efforts. It was as though the color of her skin dictated her worth, her potential, and the value of her thoughts.

That afternoon, as she sat alone at recess, staring at the backs of children who laughed without a care in the world, she felt a sharp tug of anger rise in her chest. Why was it so hard for them to see her for who she was—just another girl with hopes, dreams, and a mind of her own? She wasn’t an anomaly, a “token,” or an outsider. She was Jasmine, and she was here to stay.

Tale 2: The Job Interview

Carlos sat in the sterile conference room, the hum of the fluorescent lights above his head reminding him of how far he had come. He had worked hard to get here, had put in the hours at university, sacrificed weekends and free time to sharpen his skills, and now, after years of struggle, he was on the cusp of landing his dream job.

The interviewer, a middle-aged man with an air of self-importance, flipped through Carlos’s resume with practiced indifference. His name was Mr. Davidson, and he had been hired to evaluate the candidates for a high-ranking position at the company. As he looked up at Carlos, there was a brief moment of hesitation—a flicker that disappeared as quickly as it had arrived.

“So, Carlos,” Mr. Davidson began, his voice thick with an accent Carlos couldn’t quite place. “You’ve worked at some impressive companies, but I have to ask, what makes you stand out from the others?”

Carlos felt his pulse quicken. This was the moment. He had prepared for this question for weeks, analyzing what made him unique, what he could bring to the table. He opened his mouth to speak, but the words caught in his throat when he saw the subtle shift in Mr. Davidson’s expression.

It was as if the room had become heavier, colder, and Carlos felt the invisible weight of something unspoken press on his shoulders. He had seen it before—this unacknowledged suspicion, the doubt that lingered in people’s minds when they saw his name or his face. It was the same feeling he had gotten throughout his career, even in high school and college, when his classmates or professors would glance at him as though his success was an anomaly, as though his achievements weren’t earned but somehow granted.

“Your name,” Mr. Davidson said, not looking up from the resume, “it’s… different. Carlos. Is that Latin American?”

“Yes,” Carlos replied, his voice steady despite the knot in his stomach. “My parents are from Puerto Rico.”

“Ah, I see. And do you think your background will help you relate to clients in, uh, certain markets?” Mr. Davidson asked, his words clumsy but calculated.

Carlos felt a flush of frustration. Why was his heritage always a question? Why wasn’t his experience, his qualifications, the focus of this conversation? He had never been asked about his “background” in previous interviews, but here it was, a glaring question on the table, as though the mere fact of his ethnicity could define his worth as an employee.

“It helps me relate to people from all walks of life,” Carlos replied, keeping his tone even. “I believe diversity strengthens any team.”

Mr. Davidson nodded, but Carlos could tell that the conversation had shifted. It wasn’t about his skills or potential anymore. It was about something else.

The interview continued with more questions, each one a subtle variation of the same theme. The implications were clear—Carlos wasn’t being considered for his merit alone. He was a “diversity candidate,” someone who might offer the company a sense of social responsibility rather than an asset to its goals.

As the interview ended, Carlos felt deflated. The weight of his ethnicity had overshadowed his accomplishments once again. He stood up, shook Mr. Davidson’s hand, and left the room with a hollow feeling in his chest.

The job would eventually be offered to someone else. The company’s diversity program had met its quota, and Carlos’s resume—despite being impressive—was just a checkbox to be ticked.

Tale 3: The Neighborhood

Amina had always loved her neighborhood. It was a small, tight-knit community nestled on the outskirts of the city. Everyone knew everyone, and there was a sense of pride that came with living there, a shared history passed down through generations. But lately, things had started to change.

It began when a new family moved in next door. They were white, from a neighboring suburb, and while they were polite, something felt different. The way they looked at Amina and her family when they passed on the street, the way they would quickly cross to the other side of the road when walking their dog, made Amina feel like an intruder in her own home.

It wasn’t overt racism—it wasn’t the kind of blatant hostility one might expect in a story of prejudice. It was subtler, more insidious. It was the way the neighbors would avoid eye contact at the mailbox, the way they’d tighten their grip on their bags when Amina and her younger brother walked by. It was the feeling of being watched, even when no one was looking.

One afternoon, as Amina was walking home from the local store, she overheard a conversation between the new neighbors. They didn’t see her, but she heard every word.

“I just don’t know about this neighborhood,” one of them said, a hint of concern in their voice. “It’s… different now. I heard they’re building a new mosque down the street. That’s just going to change the whole dynamic around here.”

Amina’s heart sank. She had heard similar whispers before, from other neighbors and friends. It wasn’t about the mosque or the changes to the neighborhood—it was about the people. People like her, who were “different,” who didn’t belong.

She had always known that some people would never see her as an equal. No matter how much she tried to blend in, to be a part of the community, there would always be someone who would see her as an outsider, someone who didn’t belong.

That night, Amina sat on her front porch, her eyes tracing the stars. The world felt smaller, harsher, in moments like this, but she reminded herself that she wasn’t defined by the prejudices of others. She was strong. And no matter what, she would fight to make her mark in this world, even if others couldn’t see her for who she truly was.

Conclusion

Racism doesn’t always show itself in obvious ways. It can appear as a whisper in the schoolyard, a subtle look in the office, or a comment made when one believes no one is listening. But even in its most insidious forms, its impact is profound. It shapes how we see ourselves, how we navigate the world, and how we relate to others.

For every person who is made to feel “other,” the scars left by prejudice are deep. Yet, in the face of such adversity, there are countless stories of resilience, of standing tall despite the weight of societal expectations and biases. These stories remind us that no matter the obstacles, we must continue to push back against the forces that seek to divide us.
