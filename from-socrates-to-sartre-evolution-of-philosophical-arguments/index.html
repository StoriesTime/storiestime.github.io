From Socrates to Sartre: Evolution of Philosophical Arguments


The sun had barely risen, casting golden rays over the city of Athens. The air was cool, and the bustling sounds of the city gradually began to stir, filling the streets with life. Among the usual morning noise, one voice stood distinct, carrying ideas that would resonate through centuries to come. Socrates, sitting at his favorite corner of the Agora, was preparing to engage with yet another group of curious minds. He did not speak in grand proclamations or sermon-like speeches, but in the form of questions — leading his interlocutors down a path of self-discovery. This was his method: dialectic, a way of examining the world that would lay the foundation for the philosophical discourse that followed.

Socrates’ main concern was the pursuit of truth. He questioned everything. From the nature of virtue and justice to the existence of the gods, no belief was immune from his scrutiny. His relentless questioning exposed the inconsistencies and contradictions in his fellow citizens’ beliefs, leaving them uncertain and, at times, angry. Yet, Socrates did not seek to answer every question. Instead, he aimed to guide others to recognize their own ignorance, as he famously said, “The only true wisdom is in knowing you know nothing.”

Socrates’ focus on ethics and virtue became the cornerstone of Western philosophy. But it was not until his student Plato took the ideas of his mentor and systematized them that the concepts of reason, virtue, and knowledge truly took shape. Plato sought to answer questions about the nature of reality and the ideal state. In his dialogues, he argued that the physical world was an imperfect reflection of an ideal realm of Forms — perfect, eternal, and unchanging concepts. These Forms existed beyond our sensory experience, and the role of philosophy was to help one’s soul reconnect with these perfect ideals.

Through Plato, the idea of the philosopher-king emerged — the idea that a just ruler would be one who was not only wise but had direct knowledge of the Forms, and by extension, truth itself. Plato’s vision of a just society was one that was ordered according to these ideals, where each class of society would be led by those most capable of understanding and embodying the Forms.

Yet, even as Plato sought to bridge the material world and the divine, not all philosophers would follow his idealistic path. Plato’s student, Aristotle, took a more grounded approach. While Plato sought transcendent truths, Aristotle turned his attention to the empirical world. Where Plato focused on universals, Aristotle focused on particulars. For Aristotle, knowledge came not from abstract forms but from the study of the natural world and its causes.

Aristotle’s emphasis on observation and categorization led to what would become the scientific method. His works on logic, ethics, politics, and metaphysics laid the foundations for much of Western thought. He believed in the potential of human reason but also in the importance of virtue as a means to achieve eudaimonia — a flourishing life. In Aristotle’s view, the best life was one lived in accordance with reason, where the individual aimed to achieve the highest good through the pursuit of knowledge and moral virtue.

As centuries passed and civilizations rose and fell, the philosophical conversation did not end with Aristotle. The Middle Ages saw a blending of Greco-Roman philosophy with religious thought, as thinkers like Augustine and Aquinas sought to reconcile faith with reason. The focus shifted from the nature of the physical world to the divine and the nature of existence itself. Augustine, drawing heavily from Plato, emphasized the importance of God and the soul in achieving truth. Thomas Aquinas, on the other hand, sought to integrate Aristotelian philosophy with Christian theology, arguing that reason and faith were not in conflict but rather complemented one another in the search for truth.

But as the medieval period waned and the Renaissance ushered in a new era, so too did the philosophical questions shift. With the rise of humanism, the focus of philosophy moved away from the divine and back toward the human condition. Thinkers like Machiavelli and Montaigne began to question traditional notions of power, virtue, and society. The Renaissance was a time of exploration, not just of the world but of the self. The individual became the focal point, and philosophy began to explore not only the nature of reality but also the nature of the self in relation to the world.

The rise of modern philosophy came with the profound impact of the Enlightenment. Thinkers like René Descartes, John Locke, and Immanuel Kant began to question the role of reason, experience, and perception in constructing knowledge. Descartes famously declared, “Cogito, ergo sum” (“I think, therefore I am”), grounding the existence of the self in thought itself. Descartes’ work centered on establishing a secure foundation for knowledge, based on the indubitability of the thinking subject. This shift from external objects to the thinking subject set the stage for the modern emphasis on subjectivity.

Kant, meanwhile, advanced a complex theory of knowledge. He proposed that while our perceptions of the world are shaped by our senses, the mind also plays an active role in organizing and structuring that experience. Kant’s “Critique of Pure Reason” argued that while we can never know things-in-themselves (noumena), we can understand the way in which our mind shapes the phenomena we encounter. This dual approach — combining empirical reality with the structures of the mind — created a bridge between the rationalism of Descartes and the empiricism of Locke.

As modernity progressed, so did the critiques of reason and the development of new philosophical ideas. With the advent of existentialism, the focus turned from reason and knowledge to the individual experience of life itself. Figures like Søren Kierkegaard and Friedrich Nietzsche began to examine the challenges of existence, particularly in a world where traditional values and systems of meaning were in decline.

Kierkegaard’s existentialism emphasized the individual’s relationship with God and the leap of faith required to believe in a world beyond reason. Nietzsche, on the other hand, rejected the idea of a transcendent meaning, declaring that “God is dead” and that human beings must create their own values in the absence of divine authority. Nietzsche’s concept of the “Übermensch” (superman) was not a figure of brute strength but one who could transcend societal norms and create new values through sheer willpower.

By the time Jean-Paul Sartre emerged in the 20th century, existentialism had fully matured into a comprehensive philosophical system. Sartre’s existentialism emphasized radical freedom and responsibility. For Sartre, existence preceded essence: humans are born without a predefined nature, and it is through their choices that they define who they are. This freedom, however, comes with the burden of responsibility. Every action, every decision, creates meaning, and in doing so, one is responsible not only for their own existence but for the existence of humanity itself.

Sartre’s notion of “bad faith” explored how people deceive themselves to avoid the anxiety that comes with absolute freedom. In bad faith, individuals refuse to accept their freedom and instead act in accordance with societal roles or expectations, pretending that their identity is something fixed. This tension between freedom and responsibility became one of the central themes in existential philosophy, leading to new discussions about the nature of the self, identity, and authenticity.

Yet, while Sartre’s existentialism offered a profound liberation, it also cast a shadow of despair. The burden of freedom could feel overwhelming, and the constant demand for authenticity left little room for comfort or certainty. It was a far cry from the certainty that Plato sought in his Forms or the virtue ethics of Aristotle. But it also paved the way for a deeper exploration of the individual’s role in shaping their own destiny in a world that appeared increasingly indifferent.

Philosophical arguments, as they evolved from Socrates to Sartre, were not merely academic debates but reflections of the changing worldviews and challenges faced by individuals and societies. The shift from objective truths to subjective experiences mirrored the transformations in culture, science, and politics. The search for wisdom and truth, which began with the dialogue of Socrates and the pursuit of virtue, was slowly replaced by the search for meaning in a world where reason alone could no longer provide all the answers.

The road from Socrates to Sartre is not a linear one, but a journey of constant transformation. Each philosopher, in their own way, sought to address the fundamental question: what does it mean to live a good life? From the ethics of virtue and the search for ideal forms to the individual’s struggle for meaning and freedom, philosophy has evolved in response to the complexities of human existence. And while the debates may shift and change, the underlying pursuit remains the same — to understand the nature of our existence, our relationships with others, and our place in the world.

In the end, the journey from Socrates to Sartre reflects the evolution of human thought, from the search for absolute truth to the recognition that meaning is something we must create for ourselves. The philosophical arguments may change, but the quest for understanding continues — a journey without an end, but one that pushes us ever forward.
