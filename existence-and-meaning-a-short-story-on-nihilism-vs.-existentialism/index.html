Existence and Meaning: A Short Story on Nihilism vs. Existentialism


In a city veiled under the constant hum of neon lights and unanswered questions, two strangers found themselves sitting on an abandoned bench in a forgotten park. The night was thick with the scent of rain and a restless energy that barely masked the emptiness coursing through the streets. Their encounter was neither planned nor forced, just two minds intersecting at the crossroads of meaning and existence.

Lucas was a man who had surrendered to nihilism. His eyes, tired and unshaken, reflected years spent navigating a world he saw as fundamentally devoid of purpose. The bustling city, full of ambition and hope, seemed to him a grand farce, each individual actor reciting lines in a script written by chaos—without direction, without meaning.

Across from him sat Evelyn, a philosopher of the existential kind. Her gaze held the flame of defiance against the void, an acknowledgment that while life could be intrinsically meaningless, humans possessed the unique capacity to forge their own essence and significance. She believed meaning wasn’t handed to us but sculpted by the choices we make and the authenticity we embrace.

“Why bother?” Lucas finally spoke, breaking the silence that had stretched between them like a gulf. “If the universe is indifferent, if mortality is the end of everything, why strive, why hope, why create?”

Evelyn smiled, the gentle kind born not of certainty but of acceptance. “Because the struggle itself is where meaning is found. Not in some cosmic decree, but in your rebellion against the void.”

Lucas shook his head. “That sounds like delusion. An escape from the truth. The truth is—there is no grand design. No inherent value. Life is a brief flicker in an infinite darkness.”

Her eyes softened, yet her voice remained steady. “And in that flicker, you have freedom. Freedom to choose how to live, freedom to define what matters. It’s terrifying, yes, but exhilarating too. The absence of predetermined meaning means you are the author of your story.”

He looked away, pondering, as the city’s distant noises morphed into a melodic background to their conversation. The tension between nihilism and existentialism was not just philosophical—it was personal, an existential crossroads. Both philosophies offered responses to the same existential void but in profoundly different ways.

Lucas’s nihilism was a shield, protecting him from disappointment by lowering expectations to zero. In contrast, Evelyn’s existentialism was a call to action, a challenge to confront the absurd and yet leap anyway.

“But what if I fail at creating meaning?” Lucas finally asked. “What if my choices are meaningless because they have no impact beyond my death?”

Evelyn’s voice dropped to a whisper, as if sharing a secret with the night. “Then you still lived authentically. Meaning is not about permanence or cosmic importance. It’s about presence and engagement. It’s about the courage to face nothingness and still choose to care.”

The rain began to fall lightly, dotting the cracked pavement and pooling around their feet. Lucas felt an unfamiliar stirring — not hope, but acknowledgment. Maybe the absence of meaning was not an ending but a beginning.

“Maybe,” he murmured, “meaning isn’t found—it’s made.”

Evelyn nodded, her smile broadening. The park, the city, the universe—they were indifferent, but together, sitting quietly on a forgotten bench, two souls dared to carve meaning from the void.

The night deepened, but their conversation lingered, a testament to humanity’s quest: not for answers handed down by the stars, but for significance born in choice, struggle, and ultimately, existence itself.
