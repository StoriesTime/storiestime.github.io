Finding Belonging: Stories of Migrants in a New Land


The ship’s horn bellowed like a restless giant, echoing across the harbor. Hassan stood on the deck, his hands gripping the cold metal railing, his eyes fixed on the endless row of cranes silhouetted against the skyline. This was not home. Nothing about the sight before him was familiar: neither the imposing steel structures nor the sharp salt tang in the air. The port city felt as if it were carved out of stone and ambition—a far cry from the sun-warmed fields and labyrinthine markets of his village.

For thirty-six days, the vessel had carried its passengers across the roiling, unpredictable seas. Each day, the passengers shared little more than wordless glances, occasional nods, and whispered prayers. But as the ship docked, a collective hush fell over its occupants. A new land awaited them. A land whispered of opportunity but shadowed by uncertainty.

Hassan adjusted the strap of his satchel, the only possession he carried besides the clothes on his back. Inside was a single faded photograph of his family, folded so many times the faces within were barely discernible. He had kissed it one last time before stepping onto the ship weeks ago, making a silent promise to his mother and sisters. I will send for you. I will build us a life there.

The gangway rattled as passengers shuffled down, one by one. Hassan hesitated. The fear of failure, of losing himself in a place where he knew neither the language nor the customs, coiled in his chest like a tight knot. But the fear of staying behind—of succumbing to the hopelessness that consumed so many he knew—was even fiercer. With a deep breath, he descended into the unknown.

The gritty streets of the industrial district greeted him with an unvarnished frankness. Row after row of brick tenements stood like silent sentinels, their windows grimy and occasionally patched with cardboard. As he made his way through the tangled alleys, the sounds of a new life began to fold around him: the screech of a tram rolling by, the distant laughter of children playing soccer in a gravel lot, the guttural calls of street vendors. The city felt alive, albeit in a manner that was indifferent to its newest arrivals.

Hassan walked until the soles of his shoes blistered, clutching the address given to him by the recruitment officer back home. After what felt like hours, he finally found himself at a narrow building squeezed between an old bakery and a dimly lit repair shop. A small sign above the door read “Fremont Rooming House.”

Inside, the air was dense with the smell of frying onions and boiled cabbage. A burly man handed Hassan a key without asking questions or offering a smile. “Room 19, up the stairs,” he grunted. Hassan managed a polite nod before heading up.

Room 19 was little more than a box with a bed and a rickety desk. The single window offered a view of crumbling rooftops and a sliver of the distant harbor. Hassan sat on the edge of the bed, the springs creaking beneath him. Exhaustion coursed through his body, but sleep would not come until long after the streetlights outside flickered on.

Days turned into weeks. Each morning, Hassan joined a crowd of men at the gates of the manufacturing plant, hoping to be among the few picked for daily labor. At first, the foremen regarded him with suspicion. His accent marked him as an outsider, one unfamiliar with their ways, and that made him expendable. But Hassan was persistent. He learned quickly, staying after hours to observe the machinery and offer help where he could. Over time, the wary glances softened into brief nods of approval. It wasn’t much, but it was enough for now.

Though the days at the factory were grueling, the nights were lonelier still. The other migrants at the rooming house spoke a cacophony of languages: Slavic, Italian, Chinese, Yiddish. Conversations were a patchwork of hand gestures, smiles, and laughter over shared plates of food. Hassan often sat on the outskirts of these gatherings, hesitant to step into their circle. He longed for the easy camaraderie he once had back home, but the words for it eluded him here.

One evening, as Hassan quietly ate a dinner of bread and lentils in the communal kitchen, an older man approached him. His name was Mr. Novak, a carpenter from Poland who had been in the city for nearly two decades. “You are new here,” he said in heavily accented English, pulling out the chair across from Hassan.

Hassan nodded. “Yes. Just a few weeks.”

“You remind me of myself, many years ago,” Mr. Novak said, his lined face softening with a small smile. “It is not easy. But you will find your way, little by little.”

That conversation was the first of many. Under Mr. Novak’s guidance, Hassan learned not just words, but the rhythm of the city. Where to buy spices, how to catch the bus, which foremen to avoid, and which might offer a fairer deal. Sometimes, he helped Mr. Novak in his tiny workshop in exchange for lessons in English. Their bond became a lifeline, a bridge that pulled Hassan from the margins into something resembling community.

Months later, on a crisp spring morning, Hassan stood in line at the post office. The envelope in his hands contained the small savings he had managed to gather, along with a letter he had painstakingly written in his faltering Arabic script. As he handed it to the clerk, he felt a weight lift from him. His mother and sisters were still far away, but this act made their absence bearable. It was the first step in keeping his promise.

Outside the post office, a wave of voices caught his attention. A group of children, bright-eyed and full of energy, crowded around an old man playing the accordion. When the melody shifted to a tune Hassan recognized—a folk song he hadn’t heard since leaving home—his throat tightened. Without thinking, he began to hum along.

“Hey, you know this one?” one of the older boys asked, wide-eyed.

“I do,” Hassan replied, smiling despite himself.

“Where are you from?” the boy pressed, curiosity replacing the usual skepticism that greeted newcomers.

Hassan hesitated, then spoke with a quiet pride. “A place very far from here—but I remember it well.”

The boy nodded as if considering this deeply, then extended an open hand. “Come teach us the words.”

Hassan laughed, the sound startling even himself. And for the first time since arriving in this strange, sprawling city, he felt something break free inside him—a tentative, fragile sense of belonging.

Perhaps it would never be like home. But perhaps that, too, was the point.
