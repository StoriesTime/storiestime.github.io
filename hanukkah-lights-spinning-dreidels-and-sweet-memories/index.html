Hanukkah Lights: Spinning Dreidels and Sweet Memories


The cold winter night descended over the small town like a blanket, but inside the Cohen family home, warmth and light filled every corner. The soft flicker of the candles on the menorah cast long shadows on the walls as Sarah Cohen carefully adjusted the wicks, ensuring they burned brightly and steadily. The aroma of freshly baked latkes filled the air, mixing with the scent of her mother’s famous sufganiyot, fried doughnuts stuffed with jelly, that were cooling on the counter.

It was the first night of Hanukkah, and the excitement was palpable. Every year, Sarah’s family gathered together to celebrate the Festival of Lights, a tradition that had been passed down through generations. Her grandmother, who sat in her favorite chair by the window, smiled as she watched Sarah light the candles. Her eyes twinkled, reflecting the warm glow of the menorah, as she hummed an old Hanukkah song under her breath. Sarah’s mind wandered back to her childhood, to the way her grandmother would tell stories about the miracles of Hanukkah—the oil that lasted for eight days, the bravery of the Maccabees, and, most of all, the memories of her own childhood celebrations.

As the candles flickered and the room settled into a peaceful silence, Sarah’s brother, Noah, entered the room carrying a small wooden box. His eyes sparkled with mischief, and Sarah knew instantly what he had in mind. Noah was never one to miss the opportunity for a game of dreidel, the spinning top that was as much a part of Hanukkah as the menorah itself.

“Ready to lose again, Sarah?” he teased, his voice full of playful confidence.

Sarah rolled her eyes but couldn’t suppress the smile that tugged at the corners of her mouth. “We’ll see about that, Noah,” she replied, her competitive spirit rising.

Noah set the box down on the coffee table and opened it to reveal a set of colorful wooden dreidels, each inscribed with Hebrew letters—nun, gimel, hey, and shin—representing the phrase “Nes Gadol Hayah Sham,” meaning “A great miracle happened there.” The dreidels had been passed down from their great-grandfather, and to Sarah, they were a symbol of family and tradition, a link to the past that carried with it the joy and laughter of generations long gone.

As Noah began to spin the first dreidel, their mother, Miriam, appeared from the kitchen, wiping her hands on a dish towel. “What’s all this noise about?” she asked with a grin, her eyes soft with affection for her children.

“We’re about to play a serious game of dreidel,” Sarah said, her tone playful yet determined. “I plan to win this year.”

Miriam chuckled. “I’m sure you will, dear. But first, let me bring the latkes in. No Hanukkah celebration is complete without them.”

As their mother left the room, Noah grabbed a handful of chocolate gelt from the box and handed a few pieces to Sarah. The foil-wrapped coins shimmered in the dim light, promising sweet rewards for the winner of the game. Sarah glanced at her grandmother, who was now settled beside the fire, a content smile on her face as she watched the family come together.

The dreidel game began with eager hands and hopeful hearts. Sarah’s first spin was a triumphant “gimel,” which meant she took the entire pot. Noah groaned, but his smile was never far behind. It was all part of the fun. As the game continued, Sarah was reminded of the countless nights she had spent playing dreidel with her siblings when they were children, the same feeling of excitement bubbling up inside her. The sound of the dreidels spinning, the laughter echoing through the house—it was the soundtrack of her childhood, a sound that brought her comfort and joy.

Each year, as the dreidel spun and the candles flickered, Sarah felt closer to her ancestors, to the generations that had celebrated Hanukkah before her. Her grandmother often told stories about the hardships their family had endured, how they had fled from Europe to start anew in America, bringing their faith, traditions, and love of family with them. Those stories, passed down from one generation to the next, were as much a part of Hanukkah as the latkes and the dreidels themselves.

As the night wore on, the menorah’s candles burned lower, casting a gentle glow across the room. The dreidel game had become a blur of spinning tops and laughter, and the air was filled with the sweet scent of sufganiyot and latkes. Noah had won a few rounds, but Sarah was determined to catch up. As her dreidel spun one last time, her grandmother’s voice broke through the laughter.

“I remember my first Hanukkah in America,” Grandma Miriam said, her voice soft and reflective. “It was just after we arrived from Poland. The lights, the food, the music—it was all so different from what we knew. But the most important thing was the sense of home, of community. Even in a new land, we found ways to celebrate together.”

Sarah stopped spinning her dreidel, listening intently as her grandmother continued. “I was a little girl, just about your age, when I first held a dreidel in my hands. My mother told me that the dreidel wasn’t just a game—it was a way to remember our faith, our resilience. When we spun the dreidel, we were reminded that even when things seemed dark, there was always light. There was always hope.”

Sarah felt a lump form in her throat as she gazed at her grandmother, who was now looking at the menorah with a wistful expression. The words her grandmother spoke resonated deeply within her, stirring something in her soul. Hanukkah was more than just a celebration of the miracles of the past—it was a celebration of family, of the bonds that tied them together, and the unbreakable thread of tradition that had been passed down through the generations.

As Sarah turned her attention back to the dreidel, she felt a sense of peace settle over her. The spinning top, the warm glow of the candles, and the sweet taste of sufganiyot were all part of something much larger than any individual. They were part of a story that had been unfolding for thousands of years, a story of faith, resilience, and the unshakable belief in the power of light to overcome darkness.

The night continued with more laughter, more spins of the dreidel, and more stories shared between bites of delicious food. The menorah’s candles slowly burned down, each flicker a reminder of the miracles that had shaped their history and the love that bound them as a family. When the game finally came to an end, with Sarah emerging victorious, the family gathered around the table, hands filled with chocolate gelt and hearts filled with gratitude.

Grandmother Miriam leaned back in her chair, her eyes shining with love and pride. “You know,” she said softly, “the most important thing about Hanukkah is not just the lights or the games. It’s the memories we create together. It’s the love we share and the traditions we pass on. That’s what makes the miracles live on.”

Sarah nodded, feeling a deep sense of connection to her past, her present, and the future she would one day share with her own children. As the last candle of the menorah flickered out, she knew that the memories of this Hanukkah—of spinning dreidels, of sweet sufganiyot, and of shared moments with family—would stay with her forever, just as they had stayed with her grandmother.

The lights of Hanukkah may have dimmed for the night, but the warmth they had created would last far beyond the Festival of Lights.
