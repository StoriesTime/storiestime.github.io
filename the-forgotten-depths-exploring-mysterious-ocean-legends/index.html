The Forgotten Depths: Exploring Mysterious Ocean Legends


All endings are beginnings, a truth Jonas Marrow learned only after he saw the impossible light moving below the black skin of the Atlantic, alone on a deck where nothing should glimmer.

The storm had not been forecast. Weather satellites had missed it—perhaps purposefully—Jonas considered as he stood in his battered raincoat, knuckles white on the trawler’s rail. His small research vessel, the SV Penumbra, rocked atop waves that gleamed with an unnatural sheen beneath flashes of midnight lightning. Jonas tapped his radio: nothing but static, then, ghostlike, a whisper in a language he didn’t recognize.

He had come chasing legends, not expecting to find one.

Jonas was a modern oceanographer, but his heart had been steered by the tales of his grandmother, who’d grown up on the Orkney cliffs, watching the restless sea. She spoke of selkies and drowned cities, of the Lusca and the Carcharodon megalodon lurking beneath the waves; stories to frighten children or to comfort widows. At university, Jonas learned the difference between myth and reality. Still, some myths had teeth. That was why he’d risked his grant—and his marriage—on this lonely voyage. There were stories that didn’t yield to sonar or scientific survey. He was looking for one in particular: the Hollow Light.

They called it fatuus abyssum in old mariners’ charts—“the foolish abyssal light.” Hidden in abyssal valleys too deep for sunlight, seen only by those on the edge of disaster. To some, it meant hallucination. To others, a portal.

Tonight, Jonas saw it.

A burst of rose and azure pulsed below the ship, not from bioluminescent algae—which drifted by, green and wan—but from a single point, deep and steady. The lure of a colossal anglerfish, perhaps? No: it was too vast, too symmetrical. Jonas, heart pounding, dropped the hydrophone into the water.

Ethel, his grandmother’s ring on a chain around his neck, felt hot.

He recorded the sound and strained to listen. Through the coil, he caught not the creak of hull plates, but music—a slow, tolling melody, as if sung by a choir of whales. But lower than any whale, lower than any human-made note. The sound bored into his bones, made his veins ache.

He should have turned the boat and sailed for shore, but old stories had already wound their spell.

Six hours later, the Penumbra drifted in the eye of a stillness heavy as velvet. No wind. No rain. Jonas, sleep-starved and trembling, prepared the submersible drone—a boxy, barnacle-flecked thing he’d leased from a Norwegian company, a machine that had seen the wrecks of two world wars. He double-checked his tether, adjusted the camera, and lowered the drone.

His eyes locked to the monitor. For a long moment, there was only murk—plankton, silt, the frantic dart of a lanternfish. But as the drone descended, an outline appeared. Geometry. Ruins? No one had mapped ruins here—nothing but a lost container ship, and those sank deeper still.

Jonas gripped his mug so tightly the ceramic cracked.

He recognized the pattern: interlocking rings, too regular for randomness, an architecture of immense age. Cyan light seeped from chasms in the stone, pulsing in time with the distant song. The Hollow Light. It existed.

Tiny motes flickered around the ruins, conscious, almost curious. Jonas half-suspected they would assemble into forms—sea-seraphs, writhing kraken, the collected ghosts of all who went down with their ships. He squinted, leaning closer.

Shapes moved through the light. Fins, yes, but with arms—no, tentacles—no, appendages he could not name. As each shadow approached the drone’s lens, it shimmered out of view. Still, the camera caught glimpses: skin reflecting rainbows, eyes like pale pearls. Jonas called to them, forgetting himself, voice hoarse: “Who are you?”

The lights responded. The melody changed—a new refrain, urgent and inviting.

He knew, irrational but certain, that the legends were welcoming him.

The moment did not last. The drone’s feed crackled and died, the last image seared into his memory: an empress-shape among the ruins, wearing fronds of amethyst coral, eyes sorrowful, a hand (was it a hand?) raised.

Then the storm returned, sudden and savage.

Jonas barely regained control. The mast screamed in the wind’s grip; the Penumbra spun. He blacked out between lighting strikes, clutching Ethel’s ring, praying to any god—or any deep-sea goddess—that he would see daylight again.

He awoke to silence. The sensors blinked the wrong color. His logbook, miraculously dry, sat open beside him, the last entry unfinished:

“Beyond Atlantis. Not hallucination. Not alone.”

The Penumbra was dead in the water, adrift beneath cloudless sky. Over the side, the sea reflected the morning like a tarnished mirror. He peered in, half-expecting the shining ruins to reappear; half-dreading they might.

Instead, he saw only an immense emptiness. The light was gone.

He manually fetched the drone, finding its exterior scoured but intact, salt deposits eaten into cryptic patterns. The memory card was missing. Only a tiny object remained—an iridescent scale, shaped like a seven-pointed star, impossibly cold to the touch.

A gift, or a warning.

Jonas drifted for two more days before a French fishing trawler found him, sunburnt but resolute. He told the Coast Guard what they needed to hear—a navigational error, an electrical fire, the panic of a man who’d spent too long alone with the sea. Reports were filed; the Penumbra was towed to port. The professor’s wife met him at the dock, not certain whether to cry or to slap him.

He didn’t tell anyone about the ruins, the light, the song. Not yet.

But his journal thickened: he wrote by night, sketching the shapes he’d seen, cross-referencing with legends from Polynesia to Iceland. Some stories, he discovered, described the same ruins, the same “guardians”: every culture’s version of the subaqueous city, just old enough to erase Atlantis, just real enough to rewrite the world. Their names: Mu, Lyonesse, Thule, the Nameless Depths. Always the damaged survivors—sailors with white hair and haunted eyes. “Don’t seek the light,” they warned. It sees you, too.

Jonas stared at the scale every morning. The metal refracted oceanic colors he couldn’t find in any mineral database. Sometimes it seemed to hum, a frequency too low to hear except with the marrow.

Months passed, and Jonas’s reputation grew and faltered—he refused to say why his last voyage produced no data, why his drone’s records were blank. His former students called him “the mad explorer.” He let them laugh. Through it all, the song lingered in dreams—a distant harmony rising from fathomless dark.

One night, the melody sharpened. Jonas awoke gasping, the scale in his hand, pressing into his skin. The sense of invitation was stronger.

He made preparations. The Penumbra was gone—scrapped or sold, records lost. Instead, he joined a consortium bound for the Mid-Atlantic Ridge, persuading the ship’s captain (a skeptical Welshwoman with more pirate than scientist in her) to let him deploy a new, experimental drone.

He waited for the storm.

Far from land, wind howling, he saw the Hollow Light once again. This time, the shape coalesced not as ruins, but as a doorway. The drone’s camera captured everything until, at the threshold of the glowing arch, the feed shimmered, distorted, and then displayed symbols Jonas recognized: not quite letters, not quite math, but something in between.

A message, meant for him.

He understood, just enough.

We are still here. And we remember.

As the feed cut, Jonas closed his eyes and heard the underwater song more clearly than he ever had upon the surface—a lament, yes, but also a promise.

Back on deck, he turned the scale over in his hand, finally comprehending. The mysterious denizens of the depths were not lost—they were patient. Their legends lived on, shared through sunken melodies and impossible luminescence, stories woven into the minds of dreamers and explorers ever since humans first gazed at the ocean.

Jonas knew he would never see the light again. The guardians had let him come close, offering a warning and a wonder: there are worlds beneath our own, memories older than myth, and we are only ever visitors; our legends needed to respect that sanctity.

He published nothing. But late at night, at a coastal tavern or in the soft hush of a university library, he spoke of the Hollow Light to those few willing to listen—young oceanographers with starlight in their eyes and salt in their blood. He taught them the value of the unexplained, the relevance of ancient tales, and the humility required to descend into the forgotten depths.

And sometimes, when the wind was right and the moon was new, Jonas flashed the scale in a shaft of lamplight, smiling as the colors shifted—a tiny, silent symphony echoing from the uncharted ocean where legends sleep and wait to be found.

In the end, the ocean keeps her secrets. But those who listen, truly listen, might sometimes hear her whisper what lies beneath, in the forgotten depths where myth and memory become indistinguishable from the truth.
