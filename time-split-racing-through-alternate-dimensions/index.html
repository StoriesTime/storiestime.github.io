Time Split: Racing Through Alternate Dimensions


The first siren ripped through the neon dusk. I flinched, jaw grinding tight around the rubber mouthguard, hands slick on the bumpless wheel of my Mark V Slipstreamer. Parallel spotlights shimmered off the prism-colored track, painting my face with shifting hues. Around me, ten racers bobbed in their G-lift harnesses, eyes half-lidded with that pre-race trance, hearts pulsing to the same drumbeat: The Rift was opening.

I slid my visor down, the HUD flooding with telemetry. Every marker pulsed chaotic and glitch-bright. Predictive trajectories, stamina guesstimates, dimensional shear: nothing made sense beyond ‘survive.’ The dome overhead lit up as fibers in the Slipway thinned, shivering between worlds. The grand prize blinked above the start arch—Temporal Access: Five Years. A shot at rewriting your past or cashing it for clean slates, whatever tickled your regrets.

My name’s Corin Serrano, and tonight, I had reason to bet everything.

The klaxon howled; grip fields dropped. My world went sideways as the Slipstreamer slung forward, borderless speed and blurred atmosphere. The track blinked, glitched, changed—a sapphire canyon morphing into a metallic ribbon floating above a shattered cityscape, then a tunnel of vines crushing and sliding with prehistoric force.

Every quarter-mile, the race course snapped into a different universe—a wild gamble where only the ruthless, adaptable, or surgically skilled could stay in the slipstream. I navigated on instinct and training, fingertips flying to the G-burst toggles and temporal anchor probes.

A ghost car spun out four lanes ahead—one of the new gen-mods, all teeth and AI reflexes. The multidimensional shift kept chewing the field down. The track twisted, becoming a faceted crystal river over an inverted desert. Through the windshield, I glimpsed Tal Marl, the infamous “Dim-Jumper” out of Akitsu Quadrant, carving a line on gravity waves that didn’t exist in half these universes.

Sweat stung my eyes, but I kept pushing, spooling out the Mark V’s chrono-injectors. My edge wasn’t speed or nerve. It was the memory anchor, synchronizing micro-loops of my own timeline. I could lag behind myself by a tenth of a second, peek through possibilities, then snap back into the main run.

On the next split, the Slipway dumped us into a dimension of rain, the track ethereal blue, the air syrup-thick. I saw Tal and the others—ghosts in other realities—each skewed by their own path through the split. Braxton, the brute in the pulse tank, pinged my left flank, trying to shunt me. Regular physics barely applied; I let the track’s undulation roll me away, sliced under a flaring reality rift, and leaned into the next acceleration window.

Here, the world stuttered—cathedrals growing like roots from an endless ocean. Every reference was off. I trusted feedback loops, not sight, as the Slipstreamer’s engine howled in spacetime agony. My proximity alarm triggered—another racer, Shailene Voss, creeping in tight.

“Move, Serrano!” her voice snarled into my comms feed, all static and wrath.

“See you in the next timeline, Voss,” I fired back, wrenching hard when the Slipway blinked to its mid-race split.

Here, reality crumbled. The track chunked into floating islands, torn from a timeline where the laws of motion had been rewritten just for sadism. The announcer’s voice, unruffled, flickered in my helmet: Mid-race Interchange. Causal Integrity at 78 percent.

I flipped the emergency stabilizer, the Mark V barely keeping grip. Every move now compounded risk—if I crashed in this universe, maybe I made the winner’s podium in another, but you only collected prizes in the timeline where your flesh made it across the finish line.

Voss surged on my right, sideswiping. A thunderous boom split the track. Braxton went down, Slipstreamer shredded by a dimensional mismatch, his avatar flickering out from the common channel—he’d have no shot at coming back through the Spectator Run.

Three laps, each crazier than the last. Phantom forests whipped past the cockpit, then an infinite urban skyline where skyscrapers spiraled like DNA strands in impossible colors. Weird gravity, hazardous temporal jumps, reality fractures that erased whole curves from under us—no two racers ever took the same route.

But the lead was tightening. I saw Tal and Voss fighting in and out of phase, her ride glitching with quantum afterimages. My gut screamed at me to drop back, but the memory anchor told me this was the moment—Tal would overshoot the next split, and a narrow window three seconds away would let me take both their lines.

I took a deep breath, reached for the chronopulse lever, and slid memory ahead of intent. The Mark V blurred. My sense of self did that ugly rubber-band jerk, as I experienced myself hitting the switch in the near-future, then lived every muscle memory in reverse to match.

The split hit—a flaring white. The track became a tight helix among black monoliths floating above a molten field. I surged left, right, then up—Tal missed the first turn, Voss lost traction, and I shot into open track, the finish line beaconing, a long thread shimmering through impossible realities.

Last universe: the race route swirled into a city made of glass and clouds. Nothing solid except the line painted ahead. Twenty seconds left. I pulsed the Mark V’s final reserves. Sensed the old pressure behind me—Tal’s last-ditch dive, Voss coming in from a slide-share jump.

Every possibility flickered. The Slipstreamer groaned, light bleeding from seams as engines overloaded, permanence slipping at the molecular level. I’d never felt more alive. I could see, ahead, thousands of versions of myself, almost winning, almost losing, erased by one slip of will. I locked into the memory anchor, cut every distraction—just me, the track, and three feet of quantum inevitability.

The finish arch was a storm of golden light. I hurled the Mark V forward, saw Tal’s Splitwing vaporize in a dimensional backwash, Voss’s pulseweb snap mid-jerk. The world flickered once, twice.

I crossed.

Then there was only stillness and a long, slow return. The Slipway spun down, spitting the survivors—me, Voss (in defeat), and three others—back into singular reality. The roars of the crowd unwound through the dome. I sat, helmet in hands, trembling.

I’d won. Five years of time granted. Five years to erase my mistakes, or make new ones, or run from the chance. But as I looked at the prize, I realized something strange—every possible version of me had crossed that line a little bit different, wanting something new.

But this me, the one who survived, just wanted to run again. To split time, skip between realities, and find the one in which winning meant nothing except the freedom of the fight.

In the shadows beyond the track, the Rift pulsed. The next race was already calling.

End.
