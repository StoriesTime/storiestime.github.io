Curses of the Pyramids of Giza


The desert air was dense, with grains of golden sand swirling in the light of the setting sun. Dr. Miriam El-Badry adjusted her scarf against the sudden gust, her dark eyes scanning the jagged silhouette of the Great Pyramid of Giza in the distance. For as long as she could remember, the colossal monuments of the Giza Plateau had loomed in her thoughts like specters, beckoning her with whispers of antiquity and secrets buried deep beneath their stone facades. But those whispers came with warnings, echoed time and again through folklore, legends, and history.

“Curses,” Abdul, her assistant and childhood friend, said as he handed her a flask of water. His lips curled into a nervous grin, but his eyes betrayed unease. “Do you think they’re real?”

Miriam chuckled softly, brushing a strand of her curly black hair away from her face. “Curses are a tool of deterrence,” she replied. “A security system of sorts for the ancient world—meant to keep tomb robbers away.”

He hesitated, squinting at the horizon as if expecting the dunes to shift and swallow them whole. “Still,” he muttered, “they say some things shouldn’t be disturbed.”

Miriam didn’t reply. She didn’t need to; skepticism and wonder were two sides of the same coin, and each had guided her through her career as an archaeologist. What Abdul didn’t know was the lingering sense of foreboding she herself had wrestled with since she first received word of the discovery.

The find hadn’t been publicized yet. A team of Egyptian researchers had stumbled upon an unmarked chamber near the base of the Great Pyramid during a routine survey using advanced ground-penetrating radar. What puzzled them was its depth—it appeared to plunge far deeper than any known passageway. No official records had ever hinted at its existence, but local nomads and elders spoke of it in hushed tones, referring to it only as “Al-Bab al-Mafqood”—the Lost Door.

Miriam had been called in to lead the expedition. She was one of Egypt’s foremost experts on the Old Kingdom, with years of fieldwork under her belt. It was an honor, and a risk, to spearhead an operation laced with so much enigma.

As the group arrived at the base of the pyramid, she examined the entrance to the newly exposed shaft. There was something unsettling about its symmetry—it was unnaturally perfect, even compared to the precision the builders of the pyramids were known for. The tunnel yawned like a great chasm, its interior chiseled into the rock with an expertise that defied the tools of its time.

The team descended one by one, their lanterns casting elongated shadows along the smooth walls. Ancient petroglyphs lined the passage, glowing faintly as the light passed over them. Some depicted familiar symbols—Anubis, the jackal-headed guide of the afterlife; Ra, the sun god with his radiant disk. But others were less recognizable, twisted in form and carved with a precision that made them appear as if they were staring directly at the onlookers. Miriam found herself transfixed by a particular glyph—a serpent devouring its own tail, its eyes embedded with tiny chips of obsidian.

“Dr. El-Badry,” called one of the researchers from ahead, snapping her out of her reverie. Miriam quickened her pace until she reached the front of the group, where Abdul and three others stood around a massive stone door, partially ajar. It was etched with hieroglyphs, worn but legible.

She ran her fingers over the inscription, deciphering aloud. “He who treads beyond this threshold shall wake what was meant to sleep. None shall escape judgment; none shall escape the shadow of Set.”

The group exchanged uneasy glances. Set—the god of chaos and disorder, a deity oft-feared for his volatile wrath—was seldom associated with the pyramids. His presence here was odd, unsettling, out of place amidst the usual reverence for Ma’at, the principle of balance and cosmic order.

“Should we stop, Dr. El-Badry?” Abdul asked quietly.

Miriam hesitated, but only briefly. “It’s likely a scare tactic,” she said at last, though her voice rang hollow. “We press on.”

With great effort, the team pried the door open further, releasing a gust of stale air. Beyond lay another tunnel, this one descending at an even sharper angle. It grew colder as they ventured deeper, their breaths visible in the dim torchlight. The group fell silent, their footsteps echoing against the walls.

Eventually, they entered a vast chamber. At its center rested a sarcophagus unlike any Miriam had seen. Its black granite surface was intricately carved with patterns that seemed to shift under the light, giving the eerie impression that the images were moving when they were not being directly observed.

“It’s magnificent,” she murmured, her fingers lingering just above the lid, which was slightly ajar. Ancient amulets were scattered around it—protective symbols, smashed to pieces. The air seemed heavier here, almost viscous. Miriam’s chest tightened as if invisible hands were pressing against her ribs.

One of the researchers, Amina, stepped forward, peering into the coffin. Her face turned ashen almost instantly.

“What is it?” Miriam asked, moving to her side. She leaned over and gasped.

The sarcophagus was empty.

Before anyone could speak, a low rumble reverberated through the chamber. The ground shook beneath their feet, sending grains of sand cascading from the walls. Abdul shouted something, but the noise drowned him out. The torches flickered violently before plunging them into darkness.

And then, the voices began.

They were faint at first, like whispers carried on a breeze. But as the seconds passed, they grew louder—discordant, dissonant, a cacophony of tongues both human and inhuman. The air became suffocating, thick with the smell of burning resin and decay. Miriam stumbled backward, her lantern flickering to life just long enough to cast a fleeting glow on the walls.

The petroglyphs had changed.

What were once static images now moved in unison, their angular forms twisting together in grotesque choreography. Eyes blinked, mouths opened and closed, and serpentine shapes slithered through the intricate maze of carvings.

Abdul grabbed Miriam’s arm, his face pale with terror. “We have to leave!” he shouted.

But the way out had vanished. The tunnel through which they had entered was no longer visible—replaced by smooth, unbroken walls of polished stone. Panic set in as the group scattered, searching for any sign of escape.

Miriam tried to keep calm, grounding herself in the discipline that had carried her through years of perilous digs. Her mind raced through the possibilities—was this an illusion? A trap of some kind? But even as she clung to rationality, a cold dread nestled in the pit of her stomach.

They were not alone.

From the far end of the chamber, a low growl emerged, guttural and bone-chilling. The shadows seemed to coalesce into a massive figure, its form indistinct but undeniably menacing. Its red eyes glowed like embers, locking onto Miriam with predatory intent.

The whispers reached a feverish crescendo, and Miriam realized the entity was speaking, though its voice was fractured, like the grinding of stone against stone.

“You brought the light where only darkness should dwell,” it intoned. “And so, you shall carry the burden of its wrath.”

A sudden blast of wind extinguished the lanterns once more, and Miriam’s vision dissolved into a swirl of chaos and sand. A scream pierced the blackness—whether it was her own or someone else’s, she could not tell.

When Miriam awoke, she was lying in the sand beneath a starless sky. The Pyramids of Giza loomed in the distance, silent and unmoving. Her team was gone without a trace. Her mind felt fractured, haunted by the echoes of words she couldn’t forget.

“You shall carry the burden.”

The next morning, the authorities found her wandering near the plateau, incoherent and trembling. She spoke of shifting walls, empty tombs, and something ancient stirring deep beneath the pyramids. Though she tried, she could no longer decipher the true from the imagined.

Miriam returned to her home in Cairo, her career and reputation in ruins. But she knew the entity’s curse was real—for every night, without fail, the shadows in her room would deepen, and the faint sound of whispers would begin anew.
