Home Away from Home: Stories of Adaptation and Belonging


When Priya stepped off the airplane and into the biting chill of Chicago’s autumn, the wind felt like a greeting—one sharp enough to remind her she had truly left behind the blistering sun of her hometown, Chennai. The announcement about “inclement weather” on the flight had mystified her, but now she understood. Her scarf offered little protection, and her coat—borrowed from a cousin—felt like paper armor. Yet, in spite of the discomfort, her heart thudded with a peculiar mix of dread and exhilaration. This wasn’t just a visit; it was a leap she had prepared herself for months to take: moving halfway across the world in pursuit of ambition, education, and the promise of a better future.

The taxi driver kept the heat cranked up, a kind gesture she hadn’t needed to ask for. Priya stared out the window as skyscrapers loomed in the distance; they were works of art to her, monumental sculptures that seemed almost too fantastical to be real. Chennai had its bustling urban sprawl, but there was something different about how Chicago sprawled—it wore its pride in glass-and-steel façades that glittered even under the gray clouds.

The apartment was on the tenth floor of a building that Priya didn’t think she would ever be able to afford on her own. Thankfully, it belonged to Meera, a distant connection who’d enthusiastically agreed to let her stay until Priya found her footing. Meera welcomed her at the door with arms wide open, her warm smile as comforting as the spice-laden aroma dancing from the kitchen.

“I made chole,” Meera said, pulling Priya in. “I figured you could use a little taste of home on your first day.”

Priya’s stomach growled in response, and she flushed in embarrassment. She hadn’t realized how hungry she was—jet lag, nerves, and excitement had drowned out her sense of hunger. It always fascinated her how food was the universal anchor amongst immigrants, as if a bowl of curry could soften the edges of displacement.

That evening, the two women sat cross-legged on the couch, bowls of rich chole cupped in their hands, and leaned into conversation. Meera, more seasoned than Priya in navigating life overseas, wasted no time narrating her own tales of adaptation.

“At first, I hated this city,” Meera confessed, with a rueful smile. “All I wanted to do was soak in its quirks, but it felt too big. Too lonely. I missed the sound of street hawkers, the relentless buzz of Chennai’s chaos… But slowly, slowly, you learn to celebrate the quiet spaces here.”

Priya listened intently, wondering if the same fate awaited her. Would this strange city ever feel less foreign? Could she truly belong here when everything—from the biting cold to the food that lacked the tang of tamarind—seemed out of sync with everything she had known?

Her first battle was the language. Despite years of schooling in English, conversations moved too quickly, slang muddled her comprehension, and local accents threw her entirely off course. She found herself rehearsing sentences before speaking, scrutinizing every phrase she planned to say. On some days, those sentences evaporated when someone asked a question she hadn’t prepared for.

But Priya was determined. Every evening, she journaled her frustrations, trying to remind herself that mistakes weren’t failures—they were part of learning. She downloaded language apps, practiced with YouTube videos, and started small conversations at coffee shops. The baristas were almost unbearably cheerful, but their casual “How’s your day going so far?” felt like manageable terrain. She replied awkwardly, but by her third week, she responded without stuttering.

As routines began to settle in, Priya found herself craving connection. Meera’s work schedule kept them from seeing each other as often as Priya would have liked, and no one at her new university seemed inclined to befriend a shy international student. Loneliness became her shadow, trailing her into quiet lecture halls and busy grocery stores.

One afternoon, as she wandered through the sprawling aisles of Patel Brothers, a popular Indian grocery store, her senses erupted with nostalgia. The scent of roasted spices was almost intoxicating, tugging her straight back to her mother’s kitchen. She found herself lingering at every aisle—from masoor dal to carefully curated spice blends—trying to hold onto fragments of home. The cashier, a middle-aged man with silver-flecked hair, asked if she was new in town.

“Yes,” Priya said, surprised at the attention. “I just moved here.”

Without missing a beat, he handed her a flyer. “We’re holding a Diwali party next weekend. You should come. It’s a good way to meet new people, you know?”

Her chest tightened at the thought of barging into a room full of strangers. Yet, the ache of solitude was powerful enough to dismantle her fears.

That Saturday evening, Priya arrived at the community center, clutching the invitation. The hum of laughter and the clink of chai glasses greeted her. She exhaled deeply before stepping inside. Strangers offered her warm smiles, pulled her into conversations, and peppered her with questions about her arrival. She discovered alumni from her university, neighbors from adjoining apartments, and even fellow newcomers who shared the same glazed expression of longing beneath the surface of polite chatter.

As the night stretched on, Priya found herself laughing, eating too many samosas, and even joining in on the garba dance circle despite her two left feet. The weight of isolation eased, and something unexpected bloomed—a flicker of belonging. For the first time since arriving, she allowed herself to hope that maybe this home away from home wasn’t quite as unattainable as she had feared.

Months passed. Seasons swept through Chicago in a dizzying whirl—snowstorms, blossoming tulips, and muggy summer evenings. Priya adapted more than she thought possible. She began to learn the shortcuts between towering buildings, began favoring deep-dish pizza, and even started layering without grumbling about the cold. Her professors praised her growing confidence during class discussions. Friendships—tentative at first—grew into study groups, weekend hangouts, and secret sharing over midnight coffee.

But Priya never replaced her roots. Her fridge was always stocked with mango pickle, her apartment filled with the smell of dosa batter fermenting overnight. She shared recipes with new friends, explaining how to temper mustard seeds and curry leaves, delighting when they showed interest. Slowly, her identity became a bridge—a way to invite others into her world rather than isolating herself in either.

Years later, as Priya stood near the shore of Lake Michigan, staring at the shimmering skyline of her adopted city, she reflected on the journey that had brought her here. She missed Chennai still, but it no longer felt so far. Her life was tethered to more than one place now, a collection of homes in her heart that coexisted despite the distance.

Adaptation had been her greatest teacher. In learning to belong, Priya discovered that home wasn’t a single location but a feeling she could craft out of connection, resilience, and moments that reminded her she was never truly alone. Chicago, for all its stark differences, had become hers, a home away from the home she thought she’d never leave behind.
