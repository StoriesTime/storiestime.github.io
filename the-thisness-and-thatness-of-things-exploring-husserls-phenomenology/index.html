The Thisness and Thatness of Things: Exploring Husserl's Phenomenology


Once upon a time, in a quaint little village nestled among the rolling hills, there lived a curious young woman named Elise. She spent her days exploring the lush forests, observing the intricate details of nature, and pondering the essence of things.

Elise had always been fascinated by the complexities of existence and the way the world intertwined with our perception. As she walked through the woods, she discovered an old book nestled between the roots of a grand oak tree. The book was weathered, with its leather cover now a deep brown color and its pages brittle and yellowed from age. The title read, “The Thisness and Thatness of Things: Exploring Husserl’s Phenomenology.”

Elise could not resist the opportunity to delve into the wisdom contained within the pages of this ancient text. As she read, she became more enamored with the concepts of ‘Thisness’ and ‘Thatness,’ describing the unique qualities and identities of objects and experiences. She found herself pondering the deeper meaning of these seemingly simple yet profound ideas.

Guided by the words of the philosopher, Elise began to observe the world around her with new eyes. As she sat by the gently flowing river, she noticed the intricate patterns the water created as it caressed the smooth pebbles at the bottom. She contemplated the various qualities of these pebbles, such as their size, shape, and texture, and realized that these were the very characteristics that defined their ‘Thisness.’

For hours, Elise would sit and observe the changing of the seasons, the ripening of fruits, and the simple elegance of a butterfly catching a gentle breeze, all while embracing the ‘Thisness’ of each fleeting moment. Each experience brought her closer to understanding the complex interplay between the world and her own consciousness.

Intriguingly, Elise discovered that the ‘Thatness’ of a thing, its classification as such, was inextricably linked to her own perceptions. The moment her consciousness became aware of an object, it was imbued with a sense of ‘Thatness,’ thus becoming a part of her experience. In this way, the boundaries between subject and object blurred, and she began to understand the deeply interconnected nature of all things.

Elise shared her newfound wisdom with her friends and neighbors, sparking curiosity and delight in their simple lives. As they took up the mantle of phenomenology, their perspective of the world transformed. They learned to appreciate the infinite variety and irrefutable uniqueness of each thing in their environment, and through this, a profound sense of interconnectedness emerged.

The transformation of the village was not limited to the intellectual realm; it also affected the tangible aspects of their lives. The villagers began working together, leveraging their distinct strengths and unique perspectives to build a more harmonious and sustainable community. Their once trivial disagreements and misunderstandings dissolved, replaced by a deep understanding of each other’s Thisness and Thatness.

As the years passed, Elise’s wisdom continued to spread far beyond the village, eventually reaching across the hills and through the forests, inspiring others to embrace the intricate beauty of the Thisness and Thatness of things in their own lives. Together, they rekindled a sense of wonder and appreciation for the world around them, cultivating a deeper connection with all facets of existence.

Through the wisdom of Husserl’s phenomenology, Elise and those who followed in her footsteps discovered the beauty and significance of each fleeting moment. They learned that the world was not composed of mere things, but rather were experiences, imbued with a unique ‘Thisness’ and ‘Thatness’ shaped by the intricate dance of perception and existence.
