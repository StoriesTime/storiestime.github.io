The Invisible Cry: A Tale of Oppression


The rain fell softly, soaking the cobblestone streets of Lokshire, a city once renowned for its vibrancy but now cloaked in an oppressive silence. The damp air wrapped itself around the buildings, their windows darkened as though the souls within dared not let the light escape. In the heart of a crumbling house at the outskirts of the city, a lone candle flickered, its faint glow reflecting the fragility of resistance.

Amara sat near the dim light, her hands trembling as she leafed through a worn journal that once belonged to her mother. The pages, faded by time, told stories of Lokshire before everything changed—a time when music echoed in the streets, when neighbors gathered for feasts, and when the mere idea of oppression seemed an alien specter from distant lands. But now, Lokshire’s people carried an invisible weight, one that pressed down on their souls—not with chains, but with watchful gazes and whispered threats.

The Regime arrived five years ago. There was no war, no fiery conquest, no explosion signaling their takeover. They came with promises, words woven with silver threads of hope. They declared that the stagnant city would flourish. Roads would be repaired, schools revitalized, poverty eradicated. But beneath their assurances lay an intent far darker than anyone could have foreseen.

It began subtly. The street vendors, who once greeted their customers with hearty laughter, grew quiet. Public gatherings were curtailed under the guise of “efficiency.” Then, the statues were erected—figures cast in black iron, their hollow eyes staring down from every corner of the city. Amara remembered her father’s face tightening with unease each time they passed one, though he said little. He didn’t need to. The entire city felt the same creeping dread settle in their hearts.

What scared Amara most was the silence. It was the kind of silence that festered until it suffocated every breath, every word of defiance. People disappeared—not in broad daylight, but in the stillness of night. Rooms were emptied of their inhabitants as quickly as clouds dissipated after a storm. Each time someone vanished, there was no outcry. Families would close their doors and pretend nothing had happened. It was safer that way—safer to let the invisible cry remain unheard.

Amara’s family was one of the few that endured the changes relatively untouched, though that in itself felt like a heavy curse. Her father was a potter, his craft modest yet steady. The Regime didn’t care much for potters, aside from ensuring there was a supply of plain, undecorated ceramics for their needs. Her mother, a teacher, had been dismissed when they restructured the education system. Yet, miraculously, the family remained together—until they weren’t.

One rainy evening, much like tonight, Amara’s father didn’t come home. His absence wasn’t unusual, as he often delivered his wares late into the night, but something felt different this time. The following morning, his empty cart was found near Fountain Square, the handles still moist from the rain. Amara’s mother searched for him in hushed desperation, asking the neighbors, seeking clues. But every door was shut, every ear turned deaf. No one helped. No one dared to.

Weeks passed, and her mother’s once-unyielding resolve began to crumble. The woman who had taught Amara that courage was the remedy for fear now stared blankly out of their window, her shoulders hunched under the weight of helplessness. “Your father,” she whispered once, her voice breaking, “Your father must want us to survive.” And so they did. They survived through silence, through submission, through an invisible cry that echoed in an indifferent world.

But survival wasn’t living. Each day since her father’s disappearance had been a steady march of monotony, the walls of their home closing in tighter as the years dragged on. Amara’s mother became a shadow of herself, eventually succumbing to an illness no doctor would treat. The funeral was small, just Amara—now nineteen—and the priest who muttered prayers with averted eyes. By the time the earth swallowed her mother, Amara was alone.

It was then that Amara decided she could no longer endure the silence.

Lokshire’s library was one of the few public spaces that remained open, though its use was monitored. Rows of books stood like sentinels, layered with dust, their stories untold for fear of suspicion. Amara frequented the library not for the books—it was too risky to read anything that wasn’t explicitly approved—but to observe. She began to notice who came and went, who wore their fear too plainly, and who seemed to carry fragments of defiance hidden in their gaze.

That was where she met Elias.

He was a plain man, unassuming at first glance, dressed in clothes that bore the marks of a laborer. Yet he had a quickness to his movements, a steadiness in his expression that piqued her curiosity. She hadn’t intended to approach him, but one day they reached for the same plain brown book on a shelf—a book so innocuous, she almost laughed at the absurdity of precaution. Their eyes met, and in that instant, she recognized the spark of something she hadn’t seen in years: resistance.

They didn’t speak then, not openly, but over weeks, small exchanges grew into cautious conversations. Elias revealed that he was part of a group—no more than eight individuals—who sought to reclaim Lokshire’s soul. Their acts were small and symbolic: scattering scraps of poetry in the streets, defacing the iron statues with streaks of paint, leaving messages scrawled in chalk on alleyway walls. Most would brush these acts off as juvenile pranks, but to those who noticed, these gestures were declarations of existence.

Amara joined them. Quietly at first, she listened during their gatherings, held in the back room of an abandoned bakery. Members spoke hesitantly, their voices barely above whispers. They feared betrayal, for the Regime had instilled paranoia even amongst those who resisted. But with time, Amara proved her loyalty. For the first time since her family’s fracture, she felt connected to something larger than herself—a fragile, flickering hope that might one day burn away the all-encompassing oppression.

One night, the group decided to stage their most daring act yet. Elias had learned of a secret Regime meeting to be held in the City Hall. Their plan wasn’t to attack but to illuminate—literally. They had acquired fireworks, a substance banned in Lokshire years ago under the pretense of public safety. When the meeting commenced, they would launch the fireworks over the building, painting the sky with light and forcing the city to look beyond their fear, if only for a moment.

Amara volunteered to be the one to light the fuse.

When the night came, her hands shook—not from fear, but from the enormity of the task. She crouched in the shadow of a crumbling wall, the firework launcher aimed toward the hall’s spire. The others waited in hiding, their nervous breaths filling the air. When the clock struck nine, Amara struck the match.

The fireworks rose like phoenixes, bright and defiant. Explosions of red, gold, and blue illuminated the city, casting the statues in a new, ethereal light. For the first time in years, Lokshire’s silence was broken—not by oppression, but by the awe of something beautiful. Faces appeared in windows, peering out cautiously. People stepped onto their balconies, strangers sharing glances of bewildered recognition.

But the beauty was short-lived. The Regime responded swiftly, their patrols flooding the streets like shadows drowning the light. Amara ran, her heart pounding as the cries of her comrades echoed behind her. She didn’t look back—she couldn’t. The invisible cry had taken form, and for the first time, Lokshire’s citizens saw it for what it was.

Amara was captured that night, along with Elias and three others. They were paraded through the streets as a warning, their heads held high despite the jeers of the indoctrinated few. Yet as she was led past the crowds, Amara saw something else. A man clutched his daughter’s hand tightly, a woman held her head a little higher, and a child stared at her with wide, unflinching eyes. They didn’t cheer. They didn’t shout. But their presence was enough.

As Amara was thrown into the cell that would become her new home, she smiled. The fireworks had lit more than just the sky. They had ignited a spark in the people of Lokshire, one that could never truly be extinguished.

And so, even as the darkness closed around her, Amara knew: the invisible cry had become a roar.
