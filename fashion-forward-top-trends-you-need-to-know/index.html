Fashion Forward: Top Trends You Need to Know


By the time the first autumn leaves fluttered past the mirrored windows of House Oshima’s Paris flagship, Lila Jain knew that change wasn’t just in the air. It was striding down the pavements in neon boots, whispering conversations in thrifted sweater vests, lurking in the corners of TikTok videos starring girls who remembered when Delia’s ruled the mall. It was just before fashion week—if you could call it that anymore, given that half the major cities were hosting pop-up “slow runs” in community gardens rather than on catwalks.

Things weren’t the way they used to be, and Lila—trend forecaster, doom scroller, general observer of sartorial chaos—stood at the edge of her favorite café’s outdoor patio to see: what were the top trends this season, and why was everyone so eager to be noticed after two years hiding behind screens?

The Return of the Maximalists

A month before, Pantone released a color forecast that’d make anyone’s retinas wince: plasticky pinks, lemon-lime, cerulean so wild it begged attention. Lila, with her coil-bound notebook, watched their effect ripple: denim jackets scrawled with graffiti, skirts cut from mesh and vinyl, earrings so baroque and cumbersome that wearing them seemed to be an act of gymnastic prowess.

The new wave washed away minimalism’s soft whisper, drowning out classic beige and camel with a symphony of prints and textures shouting for space in one look. “It’s too much,” her friend Anton had complained, still clutching his favorite black turtleneck, “But maybe that’s the point. We all want to be seen.”

He was right. After endless lockdowns, the world’s appetite for self-expression returned ravenous and unrestrained. Street style was chaos, but it was joyful. Retro-futurism mingled with Y2K nostalgia and utility-core. Tie-dye ran riot alongside upcycled couture. It wasn’t about fitting in. It was about being out there.

Sustainability Steps Out of the Shadows

This season, being ‘in’ also meant being ethical. Cafes buzzed about more than oat milk: talk of circular fashion, capsule wardrobes, and rental subscriptions heated the air. “Zero Waste Club,” read tote bags slung over shoulders otherwise covered in tailored blazers made from recycled ocean plastic. Lila noticed QR codes sewn into jacket hems. Scanning them revealed sprawling supply chain stories—raw silk from Assam, dyes from Italian artists using only vegetable pigments.

Some scoffed. “It’s just a trend, like always,” said an older editor at a downtown launch party. But Lila spotted real changes: young shoppers grilled designers about carbon footprints, proud of knowing who made their clothes. Labels like GINA, which boasted ‘closed loop’ manufacturing, became pledges as much as brands. Thrift stores boomed. The red carpet saw more vintage. Even fast fashion had to slow down or risk turning invisible.

Under the ruckus, a quiet revolution marched forward: the idea that what you wore could say—without speaking—“I don’t just dress up. I give a damn.”

Tech-Inspired Textiles Are the New Black

On the slick runways of Seoul and the digital avatars parading through the metaverse, another movement unfurled: clothing that literally responded to you. Lila’s first brush with smart textiles came via a hoodie that monitored heart rate and humidity, its fabric going from navy to neon when the wearer overheated.

But this wasn’t just athleisure. She watched designers embed LED piping into pleated skirts, sweaters woven from antimicrobial copper, and dresses that unfurled into bold prints when bathed in blue light—great for clubs, lousy for laundry.

Physical/digital crossovers blurred boundaries further. Augmented reality (AR) accessories—headbands, handbags, eyelashes—flickered into existence through phone screens. “If you don’t post it, did you really wear it?” joked one influencer, spinning in a dress that was plainer in reality than on her viral video.

While some dismissed it as gimmickry, Lila wrote in her notebook: Fashion is a playground again. And the slides just got a software update.

Power Suits, Rebooted

Right when everyone expected the death of tailoring, a strange thing happened. Suits returned, but not the corporate armor of yesteryear. The new suits were slouchy, shoulder-padded, in juicy colors or pixelated houndstooth. Paired with chunky sneakers, micro bags, or nothing underneath at all (cheeky!), the message wasn’t “ready for business,” but “ready for anything.”

Young creatives wore them to gallery openings, queer collectives rocked them at protests. Gender didn’t have a monopoly on lapels and cuffs anymore. “It’s about utility and play, not conformity,” said a designer at a Brooklyn pop-up, showing off a blazer with zip-off sleeves and a built-in phone charger.

Lila realized these weren’t just clothes—they were shields, tattoos, a loud handshake with the world that said, “Watch me. I’m not invisible.”

Cottagecore and Escapism Blossoms

Beyond the clamor, romance persisted. If maximalism shouted, then cottagecore sang. Eyelet lace dresses fluttered at farmer’s markets; billowy shirts with Peter Pan collars spilled into Instagram photoshoots on picnic blankets. Straw hats, embroidered cardigans, and hand-knitted socks made power moves of their own—dainty on surface, radical in their celebration of rest, care, and ‘slow living.’

The look spoke to a generation burned out yet hopeful, longing for bits of untouched calm amid the chaos. It was more than prairie cosplay—it was protest against grind culture, a daily rebellion stitched in soft cotton and foraged wildflowers.

The Unlikely Influence of Gen Z

On the Metro ride home, Lila scrolled through her FYP. The real tastemakers weren’t magazine editors but teenagers with ring lights and opinions. They revived low-rise jeans with blissful disregard for millennial fashion PTSD. Filtered through Gen Z’s lens, old rules crumbled: socks with heels, Crocs as party shoes, power clashing patterns so violently it somehow worked.

They hunted authenticity, craved self-reference, posted their fits with self-aware memes—“Can I pull this off, or will my grandma disown me?” The power of ‘core’ subcultures exploded: balletcore, goblincore, blokecore, you-name-it-core. Anything could be a trend if you wore it with conviction and tagged three friends. Lila mused: “Maybe the only constant trend is change—and not caring who approves.”

Upcycled Stories and the Art of Personalization

Curating, not consuming, defined the real forward-thinkers now. Lila’s neighbor stitched together old college T-shirts into a quilted jacket. On a Tuesday night, friends swapped closets, patching moth-eaten sleeves with bandanas and painting denim with borrowed acrylics. It wasn’t just about saving the environment; it was about telling a story.

“People want their clothes to be alive,” said Fran, the local alterations genius who’d gone viral for her mismatched patchwork jeans. “If you buy everything new, what’s your story?” Buttons mixed from old uniforms, zippers rescued from military tents—tails wagged the dog and the dog wagged back. Lila started seeing tags reading “unique item—never to be made again.”

Fashion Week Reimagined

October’s rain glistened on the Place Vendôme as House Oshima’s models took to the garden runways—not with icy glares, but grins, twirling capes and ponchos in thunderous wind. The audience lounged on picnic blankets, apple cider in mittened hands, phones raised to stream the impossible: a show for the world—anyone could watch, tag, remix, and critique in real-time.

Off stage, Lila peered through the crowd. Style wasn’t limited to models: students, retirees, families—everyone tried, everyone watched. She scribbled: “Democracy has landed in fashion. Influence is up for grabs.”

What’s Next for Fashion Forward?

At midnight, Lila’s phone buzzed. A new collection dropped: repurposed fishing nets turned into sleek raincoats; the waitlist exploded within seconds. She grinned, thinking of all she’d seen—trend cycles spinning at breakneck pace, the past and future colliding in closets everywhere.

Her job, once about predicting what’s “in,” now demanded shepherding a more unpredictable terrain: taste, protest, joy, anxiety, hope, rebellion. Fashion, once top-down, now bubbled everywhere. She realized the only real trend was personal agency. Wear what you wish—even if it’s half pajamas, half disco. Make a statement, or make it up as you go. Make it sustainable, make it silly, make it your own.

Lila reached for her own neon puffer—upcycled, mismatched, not quite in vogue—and walked into the buzzing Parisian night. Behind her, the city shimmered in a thousand shades, each striding a different tomorrow. Trends, she thought, don’t shape people. People shape trends. And right now, the world was in love—with itself, its freedom, and its utterly forward fashion.
