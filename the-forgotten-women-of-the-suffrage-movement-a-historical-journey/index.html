The Forgotten Women of the Suffrage Movement: A Historical Journey


In the early days of the suffrage movement, the battle for women’s right to vote was loud, intense, and filled with names that remain etched in history: Susan B. Anthony, Elizabeth Cady Stanton, and Alice Paul. Their contributions were pivotal, but in the shadows of their victories stood a group of women whose roles in the fight for equality were overshadowed, their names often forgotten by the pages of history. This is the story of those women—the unsung heroes who fought fiercely for the same cause but were often overlooked in the mainstream narrative of the suffrage movement.

The Roots of Discontent

In the late 19th century, as the world grappled with industrialization, social upheaval, and the changing role of women, a quiet storm was brewing. Women, once confined to the domestic sphere, were beginning to realize their potential outside the home. Yet, despite the growing awareness of their rights, the legal and social systems continued to marginalize them.

The suffrage movement had already gained momentum in places like the United States and the United Kingdom. Well-known figures like Susan B. Anthony and Emmeline Pankhurst were starting to rally large crowds. However, they did not do so alone. Behind the scenes were women from a variety of backgrounds, often facing the same battles but without the same recognition.

One of the earliest forgotten suffragists was Frances Ellen Watkins Harper, an African American woman whose contributions to the movement are often overshadowed by her white counterparts. Born into slavery in 1825, Harper became a powerful speaker and writer. She was involved in both the abolitionist and suffrage movements, and while her advocacy for women’s rights was unwavering, her identity as a Black woman often led to her work being sidelined.

Harper’s most notable contribution came in 1866 when she delivered a stirring speech at the National Women’s Rights Convention in New York. She emphasized the importance of securing voting rights for Black women and pointed out the hypocrisy in the suffrage movement that ignored the needs of women of color. While figures like Stanton and Anthony were pushing for the enfranchisement of all women, they often minimized the importance of including Black women in the discussion. Harper, however, never wavered in her commitment to securing justice for all women, regardless of race.

The Intersecting Struggles of Race and Gender

While some suffragists fought primarily for women’s rights, others saw the fight for gender equality as inextricably linked to the struggle for racial justice. These women did not merely seek the right to vote for their gender but sought to tear down the oppressive systems that were keeping all marginalized people—women, people of color, and the poor—disenfranchised.

One such woman was Ida B. Wells-Barnett, another African American suffragist who is often relegated to the margins of history. Wells-Barnett’s efforts to bring attention to the epidemic of lynching in the United States were deeply connected to her advocacy for women’s rights. Despite facing fierce opposition from both White society and some White suffragists, Wells-Barnett stood firm in her belief that the right to vote was an essential tool in the fight against injustice.

In her 1910 speech, Wells-Barnett called for women of all races to unite in their fight for suffrage, stressing that the vote would be a critical instrument in achieving social and political equality for Black Americans. However, her appeal was largely ignored by the mainstream suffrage organizations, which had their own racially exclusive agenda. Despite this, Wells-Barnett’s courage to stand up against racism in both the political and suffrage arenas made her a crucial figure in the broader civil rights movement.

The Radical and the Quiet Rebels

Though many suffragists were making waves in the public eye, others took quieter, more grassroots approaches to change. Martha Gruening, for instance, was a suffragist and social reformer who fought tirelessly for women’s rights in the United States but never gained the recognition she deserved. Born into a wealthy family, Gruening used her privilege to mobilize women from all walks of life. She was instrumental in organizing campaigns, fundraisers, and rallies in New York State and advocated for working-class women’s rights, particularly their access to education and healthcare.

Gruening’s story, though, was overshadowed by the more outspoken figures of the movement, whose dramatic protests captured the public’s attention. While others such as Alice Paul were chaining themselves to gates and engaging in hunger strikes, Gruening’s work was more subdued but no less important. She believed that lasting change would come not from confrontation but from the careful work of building alliances and mobilizing women from every sector of society.

Equally quiet but no less revolutionary were the women in the National Woman’s Party, an organization founded by Alice Paul. While Paul is often credited with radicalizing the suffrage movement through her hunger strikes and public protests, the National Woman’s Party was a collective effort, with women like Lucy Burns, Paul’s close ally, playing an essential role in the Party’s success. Burns, a former social worker, was a key strategist and organizer, and her tireless work behind the scenes was a crucial part of the campaign that ultimately led to the passage of the 19th Amendment in 1920.

Burns and other women in the National Woman’s Party not only pushed for women’s suffrage but also sought the passage of the Equal Rights Amendment, which would guarantee equal rights under the law for women. While the amendment was never passed, Burns’s activism laid the groundwork for future feminist movements in the United States.

The Struggle Beyond the Vote

The battle for women’s rights did not end with the passage of the 19th Amendment. For many women, particularly those in marginalized communities, the victory was hollow. While the vote was granted to many women, it was far from universal. The disenfranchisement of Black women, Native American women, and immigrant women continued long after 1920. And even for those who did gain the right to vote, the struggle for equal rights in all other areas of life remained.

In the years following the 19th Amendment’s passage, Mary Church Terrell emerged as a key figure in advocating for the rights of Black women. Terrell, an educator and activist, worked to challenge racial discrimination, particularly in the South, where Black women were still subjected to Jim Crow laws and were often excluded from the vote despite their legal rights. Terrell founded the National Association of Colored Women and campaigned for federal anti-lynching legislation, all while continuing to advocate for women’s suffrage.

Terrell’s work was another example of the intersectionality of the suffrage movement. For her, the struggle was never just about voting; it was about securing full citizenship for Black women. While White suffragists celebrated their victory in 1920, Terrell and others like her understood that true equality could not be achieved without a more comprehensive fight for civil rights.

The Legacy of the Forgotten Women

The history of the suffrage movement is rich and complex, filled with struggles, sacrifices, and victories. But in the pages of mainstream history, the stories of many women—particularly women of color and those from working-class backgrounds—remain untold. These women not only fought for the right to vote but also for the broader recognition of women as full, equal citizens.

Their activism laid the groundwork for the feminist movements of the 20th century, movements that would continue the fight for equal rights in education, employment, and beyond. The work of these forgotten suffragists reminds us that the fight for equality is ongoing and that those who are overlooked in history often play the most crucial roles in shaping the world around us.

As we look back on the suffrage movement, it’s important to remember that the victory was not the result of a single leader or a handful of women but a collective effort—one that included women of different races, classes, and backgrounds, each contributing in their own way to the cause of women’s rights. Their stories, though often forgotten, are an essential part of the broader narrative of justice, equality, and empowerment.

In honoring the forgotten women of the suffrage movement, we not only give them the recognition they deserve but also continue their legacy of advocacy for women’s rights around the world.
