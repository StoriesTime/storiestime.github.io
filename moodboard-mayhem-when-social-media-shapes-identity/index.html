Moodboard Mayhem: When Social Media Shapes Identity


Lila sat cross-legged on her bedroom floor, the sharp white light of her laptop illuminating the dim space. Her walls, once bare, were now plastered with clippings from magazines, printed screenshots, and hand-sketched designs. It was a mess of colors, aesthetics, and ideals — a physical manifestation of her online moodboard. For hours every evening, she combed through apps like Pinterest, Tumblr, and Instagram, hunting for inspiration, for clues that would help her answer the question that always hung, heavy and unspoken: Who am I supposed to be?

At eighteen, Lila was someone else every month. Last fall, she’d plunged headfirst into cottagecore: floral dresses, a fascination with the romanticized countryside, and posts of her attempts at baking pies from scratch. Winter had brought a shift into dark academia, with thrifted turtlenecks, leather-bound journals, and blurry photos of rain sliding down her bedroom window. Now, spring was in full bloom, and her aesthetic had drastically pivoted to something entirely different—what the internet dubbed “clean girl minimalism.”

Lila tied her chestnut hair back into a sleek bun, the signature of her current identity. Her latest addition to the wall was an aspirational photo—soft lighting, a minimalist beige room, and a girl sipping matcha while lounging in perfectly ironed loungewear. Lila didn’t even like matcha. But that didn’t matter.

Her friends used to tease her about these constant overhauls. “Are we meeting art-girl Lila today, or is it boho-chic Lila again?” they’d joke during lunch. Yet she brushed it off with a laugh because deep down, their ribbing struck a nerve. Each identity felt real in the moment—but fleeting, like an outfit she couldn’t bring herself to wear twice.

One night, after scrolling endlessly through her phone, she decided to make her own moodboard. Not just for her style or her room—she wanted one for… herself. Who could she become? What parts of her personality could she curate for the world? For a brief moment, her head spun with excitement. A perfect digital Lila awaited, if only she could figure out the right combination of Pinterest images and hashtags to define her.

She called her moodboard “Project Me.”

Over the next few weeks, Lila became obsessed. The board grew rapidly—filled with motivational quotes, aesthetic photos of organized workspaces, fitness goals, and dreamy coastal towns for imagined weekends away. She spent hours meticulously pinning and saving, transforming her identity into a well-oiled algorithm-friendly collection of ideals.

Her online following began to grow, too. On Instagram, she launched herself as an “aspirational content creator,” sharing everything she’d so carefully curated. Her captions were positive and polished: how she meditated every morning, how she was trying her hand at digital journaling, how her life had begun to feel full of meaning ever since she let go of chaos and adopted her newfound identity.

Of course, none of it was entirely true.

Sure, she tried meditating once, but she found it boring after three minutes. Journaling? That only really happened when she remembered to steal ten seconds between snaps of her “perfect morning rituals.” And the chaos? It was everywhere. Her desk was buried under clutter she cropped out of photos, and the soft beige backdrop everyone loved on her feed was courtesy of clever editing.

But the likes rolled in. People started commenting, asking questions about how Lila had “transformed her life,” begging for advice on how they, too, could find this kind of fulfillment.

“I think I’m figuring it out,” she told herself one afternoon, sitting back in her chair. Her inbox was filled with DMs from strangers who’d stumbled across her profile, thanking her for her inspiration or asking her what products to buy. The praise flooded in so quickly that she felt addicted to it. It buoyed her confidence in a way nothing else had before.

For a moment, she actually believed her own façade.

It all began to change when Arjun entered her life.

They’d met in class during an uncomfortable group project pairing, and while Arjun was outspoken and full of ideas, Lila stayed quiet, her answers subdued and rehearsed. After class one day, Arjun turned to her completely unprompted and asked, “Do you always say what you think people want to hear?”

The question stunned her, and she laughed awkwardly. “What?”

“I mean, it’s fine,” Arjun clarified, “but people can tell when you’re not being honest about who you are. It’s like you’re running on a script.”

Lila’s first instinct was to be defensive, but something in his words lingered, and she couldn’t shake the seed of doubt they’d planted. When she got home that night, she stared at her moodboard. It was perfect, organized, and seemingly aspirational. Yet, did it reflect her? Or what she thought the world wanted?

In a rare burst of vulnerability, she texted Arjun a link to her Instagram.

His reply came a few minutes later: “It’s pretty, but who’s this person supposed to be?”

Her chest tightened. She spent hours flicking between her phone and laptop, rereading his messages, talking herself into and out of indignation. But somewhere deep inside, she knew he was right. Over time, Lila began paying more attention to her posts, captions, and moodboard, trying to chart the gap between the person she was online and the person she was privately. The gap turned out to be larger than she cared to admit.

A week later, as Lila scrolled through her Instagram feed, she noticed a sharp trend. The creators she admired were all iterations of the same things: perfect lighting, perfect motives, perfect lives. All of it packaged for consumption, slipping neatly into aesthetics she recognized so well because she was pulling the same strings. She asked herself what her followers—total strangers—really knew about her.

Nothing, she realized. Not really.

That night, she gave herself permission to dismantle “Project Me.” She tore down the magazine cutouts, dumped the perfectly curated moodboard, and unfollowed accounts that tugged her in directions that didn’t feel authentic.

It wasn’t easy. There was a hollowness at first, a void where validation used to flow with every heart-shaped like. She forced herself to sit with it, unsure of where this experiment would lead.

Arjun noticed the subtle shift in her demeanor before anyone else. She laughed more, sometimes at jokes that weren’t rehearsed, her voice faltering in ways that felt real. At lunch, she brought up topics she actually cared about, whether or not they fit an aesthetic. For the first time in what felt like forever, she was on social media less.

Online, she posted less filtered, more honest moments: her messy desk covered in journals, the cracked ceramic mug she refused to throw out, the way her dog seemed to fall asleep in ridiculous positions. She lost some followers for it—but she gained others. The conversations shifted too. Strangers still asked her questions, but now they were about real things: how to balance chaos with peace, how to embrace imperfection, how to feel okay with not always fitting in.

Lila didn’t have all the answers—she barely had her own. But that felt okay, for once.

What mattered most was that she began to inhabit her own skin in the way she’d always hoped to: unfiltered, unpolished, and a little messy, but undeniably her.
