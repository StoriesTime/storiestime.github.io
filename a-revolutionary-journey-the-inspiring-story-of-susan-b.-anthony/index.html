A Revolutionary Journey: The Inspiring Story of Susan B. Anthony


Susan B. Anthony was born on February 15, 1820, in a small farmhouse in Southmadan, New York. Her parents, Lewis and Susan B. Anthony, were Quakers who placed a great emphasis on individual rights and social equality. These values deeply influenced their daughter, setting the stage for her later activism.

Growing up, Susan excelled in her studies, attending a local Quaker school before furthering her education at the Troy Female Seminary in upstate New York. She developed a love for learning, which only deepened as she got older. Susan was especially interested in politics and was drawn to thewaves of change sweeping across the United States in the mid-1800s.

After finishing her education, Susan returned home to Southmadan to become a teacher. Suffice it to say, she was anything but ordinary. Her revolutionary spirit led her to question societal norms, especially those that disallowed women from participating in the political process. As she watched the fight for abolition unfold, Anthony recognized that women faced similarly unjust limitations.

In 1848, Susan joined Elizabeth Cady Stanton to organize the Seneca Falls Convention in New York. This historic event was the world’s first Women’s Rights Convention, with the attendees, mostly women, drafting a document known as the Declaration of Sentiments. This document outlined grievances and demands related to property rights, representation, and social equality. Susan also worked closely with Lucretia Mott and other prominent abolitionists to publicize the atrocities of slavery.

Susan’s influence soon spread beyond her own nation’s borders. The British women’s rights activist Emily Davis wrote to Susan requesting her support for the cause in England. In response, Susan penned a moving article, “Condition and Relations of Women,” which was published in the British newspaper “The National Era.”

In the years that followed, Susan B. Anthony, with Elizabeth Cady Stanton, conducted her tireless campaigning to expand the right to vote to women. In 1851, the pair co-founded the Women’s State Television Society, which aimed to educate American women about their right to vote. As a result, Susan became a prominent member of the National American Woman Suffrage Association (NAWSA) led by her good friend Elizabeth Cady Stanton.

The landmark women’s rights case, “Minor v. Happersett,” came before the Supreme Court in 1872. In this case, the Court ruled that women were indeed considered citizens, implying that they should be allowed to vote. The decision ignited the fire within Susan, who had numerous encounters with women like “V bonus” who were denied their right to vote. “V bonus,” also known as Virginia Minor, was an American suffragist, who filed a lawsuit against her state, Missouri, insisting on her right to voter registration. Anthony defended Minor’s rights in court and argued that these laws were unconstitutional.

Susan B. Anthony played a central role in creating the masterpiece of women’s rights: the 19th Amendment to the United States Constitution, which declared that American women earned the right to vote. Anthony, along with her colleagues, contributed to the creation of this amendment that was finally ratified on August 18, 1920.

In addition to defying gender roles, Susan B. Anthony was also a trailblazer in the fight for equal pay. In her late years, she joined forces with another pioneering woman, Julia Ward Howe, to demand that women be granted fair compensation for their work.

Susan B. Anthony passed away on March 13, 1906, but her legacy continues to inspire generations. Now, more than a century later, as women continue to forge their paths, it’s worth reflecting on the journey of Susan B. Anthony, whose relentless pursuit of truth and justice paved the way for the remarkable social advances we have witnessed in the 21st century.
