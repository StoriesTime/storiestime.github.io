The Weight of Color: Personal Narratives on Racism


It was a late afternoon when Naomi stood at the corner of Elm Street, the sun casting long, golden shadows across the sidewalks. She had just come from a job interview at a small graphic design firm. The conversation had gone well; she felt confident about her skills, her portfolio, her ability to contribute. But despite this, a lingering discomfort settled in her chest. She couldn’t shake the feeling that the real reason she hadn’t received the offer wasn’t her talent, but something else: the weight of her skin.

Naomi was used to it by now—the way people looked at her differently, the way conversations subtly shifted when she entered the room. She had grown up in a predominantly white neighborhood, and as a child, she didn’t fully grasp the complexities of race. But over the years, the world had taught her. She had learned to see the difference in the way people treated her, the subtle and overt ways in which her skin color impacted how she was perceived and treated.

Her mind wandered back to the interview. The woman behind the desk had been pleasant enough, but Naomi had noticed how her eyes flickered nervously when she spoke of diversity. There had been a moment, just a second, when Naomi had seen the briefest hesitation in her expression. It was small, imperceptible to most, but it was there.

Naomi had learned long ago not to take offense to these moments, but rather to be acutely aware of them. The truth was, racism often didn’t come in the form of explicit words or actions. It was there in the silences, the awkward pauses, the unspoken judgments. And Naomi knew that this weight—this weight of color—was something she had to carry everywhere, every day, even in spaces where she should feel welcome.

Growing up, Naomi had learned to navigate the complexities of identity early on. Her mother, a Black woman with a deep sense of pride, had taught her about the beauty of their culture, the strength of their ancestors, and the resilience that ran through their blood. But Naomi’s father, who was white, had always struggled to find his place in their family. His discomfort with the cultural nuances of their Black identity had sometimes left Naomi feeling torn between two worlds. It wasn’t that he was overtly racist; rather, he simply couldn’t understand the full weight of what it meant to be Black in America.

Naomi’s school years had been a mixture of discomfort and confusion. She had attended a mostly white school, and the few Black students who shared her classrooms were often scattered, isolated in small groups, trying to blend in while remaining true to themselves. Naomi had felt the tension between wanting to fit in with her white classmates and wanting to honor the richness of her heritage. The cultural references her classmates casually tossed around were unfamiliar, and she often found herself laughing along without truly understanding the jokes. At the same time, her Black classmates, though welcoming, had sometimes viewed her as someone who didn’t quite belong. She was too light-skinned to be considered “really Black” by some, but too Black to be fully accepted into the world of her white peers.

These moments of isolation were the silent kind, the kind that crept up on her when she was least expecting them. The whispers of “You’re pretty… for a Black girl” or the uncomfortable way someone might avoid sitting next to her during lunch had become the backdrop of her high school years. They weren’t malicious, but they were constant reminders of the subtle ways race impacted her life.

Now, as an adult, Naomi understood that racism wasn’t just an individual problem; it was embedded into the very systems that governed society. From education to healthcare, from job opportunities to the justice system, the impact of race shaped every aspect of her life. But it was in the small, everyday interactions that the weight of color was most apparent. Naomi had learned to take the little cuts in stride, but they still hurt, still lingered like a bruise that never fully healed.

Walking through the city on her way home, Naomi was reminded once again of the weight of her skin. She had just passed a group of young men standing on the corner. They had eyed her as she walked by, their gazes lingering a little too long. Naomi didn’t flinch; she had grown used to the scrutiny. But it was still unsettling, still a reminder that her presence in certain spaces would never be fully accepted.

She entered her apartment building and took the elevator to her floor. As the doors closed, Naomi caught her reflection in the polished metal. The woman staring back at her was strong, confident, but tired. She had spent years fighting against the weight of color, but it was an exhausting battle. Every day, she had to prove that her worth wasn’t defined by the color of her skin.

Inside her apartment, Naomi set her bag down and collapsed onto the couch. She closed her eyes for a moment, letting the stillness wash over her. It was moments like these, moments when she was alone with her thoughts, that the weight of color felt the heaviest. It was in these quiet spaces that the questions lingered: Would she ever be truly seen for who she was, for her talents, her intelligence, her creativity? Or would she always be seen first through the lens of her skin?

Naomi’s journey wasn’t an easy one. Over the years, she had learned to advocate for herself, to speak up when she faced microaggressions or discrimination. She had found her voice, but it had taken time. It had taken years of confronting the uncomfortable truth that the world didn’t always see her as equal, didn’t always see her as worthy of the same opportunities and respect as those with lighter skin. The burden of racism was not hers to carry, but it was a burden she had to shoulder nonetheless.

She thought back to the stories her mother had told her as a child—stories of Black women who had fought for their place in a world that had tried to silence them. Naomi’s mother had always emphasized the importance of resilience, of standing firm in the face of adversity. But as Naomi had grown older, she had come to realize that resilience wasn’t just about enduring; it was about pushing back, about demanding the space and recognition she deserved.

Naomi didn’t want to be just a survivor of the weight of color; she wanted to thrive despite it. She wanted to live in a world where the weight of her skin didn’t define her worth, where her talents were seen as the sum of her experiences and abilities, not reduced to the color of her complexion.

She had learned that racism was not just an external force; it was internalized, too. The world tried to shape her, to tell her where she belonged and what she could achieve. But Naomi had made a conscious choice not to let the weight of color determine her path. She would not let it define her story.

A week later, Naomi received the call. The job offer had come through. She was both relieved and cautious. The victory felt sweet, but it was bittersweet. She knew that, in some ways, her success wasn’t just a reflection of her talent—it was a reflection of the system that allowed her to succeed despite the weight she carried. The weight of color.

As she hung up the phone, Naomi stood at her window and looked out at the city skyline. The sun was setting, and the sky was painted in hues of pink and orange. In that moment, she realized that while she might always carry the weight of color, it didn’t define her. She was not her skin. She was the sum of her dreams, her struggles, and her resilience. And that was a weight she could carry with pride.

She smiled to herself, a sense of quiet strength settling over her. No matter how heavy the world tried to make her feel, she would continue to rise.

This was Naomi’s narrative, one of countless stories of resilience in the face of racism. It was a story of moments big and small, of challenges and triumphs, of a woman carrying the weight of color but refusing to let it weigh her down.
