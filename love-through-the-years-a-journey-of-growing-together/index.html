Love Through the Years: A Journey of Growing Together


On a windswept autumn day in 1977, Lucy Perrault met James Dunford on the corner of Maple Street and Sixth Avenue, where the bus was perpetually late and the city lingered somewhere between warm and cold. Lucy was heading to her first job at a small law firm, nervous in her pressed trousers; James, then a scruffy literature student at the university, had slept late in a pile of books and missed his ride.

She dropped her mint tin, and he, noticing the tiny silver box roll away, chased it with surprising speed, returning it to her, breathless and grinning over his own bad haircut. Lucy thought he looked like a young bird—awkward and bright-eyed—as he stammered some half-baked joke about “minty destiny.” Their conversation flickered, bright and silly, until the bus finally wheezed up. They sat together, shared nervous smiles, and exchanged numbers scrawled in ballpoint on the inside of Lucy’s palm.

2

They began in the rough and tender ways of young people who know nothing more about love than what movies and mistakes have taught them. Their first date happened two days later, a cheap pizza joint with vinyl booths greasy with the stories of hundreds before them. Lucy asked about books, hungry for cleverness, while James asked about her job, sincerely interested. They got sauce on their shirts, laughed until the old man at the counter shot them a look, and walked home under city lights feeling the world tilt, just a little.

Through the heady first weeks, Lucy cared for James’s relentless optimism. She admired how he could be so unguarded, how he saw possibility behind every defeat. James, for his part, was bewitched by Lucy’s steadiness, her capacity to listen without judgment. They fought sometimes—lightly at first, then more fiercely as they learned each other’s sore spots. But it never lingered. The makeups were as essential as the quarrels.

3

In 1981, James graduated, took a teaching job, and bought a cracked leather couch for the apartment he and Lucy now shared. When it rained, they pressed their feet to the radiator and read poetry aloud, losing track of time. Books spilled over into every room, dog-eared and annotated. They ran out of money more than once. James tried writing, Lucy worked longer hours. Sometimes frustration snapped, arguments rose. But there was always the simple, daily acts—notes slipped into lunch bags, Lucy’s hand squeezing his before a job interview, James balancing rent with freelance assignments.

Their friends started marrying, buying cars. Lucy felt the pull, the panic that perhaps she was “behind.” James, too, sometimes bristled at his slow-to-arrive success. But they grew together, learning how to hold each other through uncertainties. Saturday mornings were rituals: making pancakes, reading the paper, tending to their little windowsill herb garden. Every now and then, Lucy would catch James looking at her with an awe that time hadn’t blunted.

Years went by. They saw the city’s skyline grow, watched their favorite bakery close, made new friends and lost old ones. In 1987, Lucy’s mother fell ill, the weeks thick with hospital visits and whispered hopes. James was her rock—he cooked, managed the home, let her rage and grieve without trying to fix it. She loved him most in those moments: fiercely, with gratitude woven into every silent embrace.

4

In 1992, they moved to a house with a garden. There were cracks in the walls and a stubborn furnace, but Lucy loved the morning light in the kitchen and James was enchanted by a massive old maple outside. They hosted dinners, filled the rooms with music, and sometimes argued about chores or money or whose turn it was to call the plumber. But for every argument, there was a reconciliation: a soft apology, a late-night tea, a shared laugh over something only they found funny.

The years taught them patience, sometimes the hard way. There were miscarriages and work layoffs, health scares and the grit of daily disappointment. They learned to say sorry sooner, to ask for what they needed, to tell the truth kindly. They traveled when they could—once to Paris where James got lost and Lucy found him chatting in broken French with a shopkeeper. Their photos featured squinting smiles, mismatched jackets, and the gleam of adventure.

They aged, together and separately. James’s hair receded, Lucy fretted over laugh lines. But when Lucy turned fifty, James threw her a party and told everyone she was the bravest person he knew. She kissed him in front of their friends, less embarrassed than surprised to find herself still so in love.

5

When they became grandparents, life circled again, this time gentler and slower. Their granddaughter, Sophie, spent weekends in the garden, digging up worms and asking endless questions. Lucy and James took pleasure in the small things—morning walks, crossword puzzles, holding hands in the grocery store. They became the couple others pointed to: steady, happy, enduring.

What those onlookers didn’t see was the work beneath. The talks after midnight, the therapy sessions in their sixties when distance crept in, the choice every day to see each other fully. There were nights of silence, and mornings of starting over. Lucy sometimes wondered if anyone else loved the way she and James did—not in grand gestures, but in the dailiness of showing up.

6

Now, in the present—a house quieter than it once was, a garden fuller—Lucy wakes to the sound of James humming as he makes coffee. She smiles, recalling the decades of learning, stumbling, forgiving, and choosing each other over and over. Their love is not what it was, but richer, seasoned by time and hardship and laughter. Looking at James, Lucy realizes that the secret wasn’t luck or passion, but the slow work of tending, the willingness to grow together, however awkwardly.

She steps into the kitchen and James—older, gentler, still with that bad haircut—turns and greets her like she’s the first morning. They talk, they laugh, they touch, as if starting again, and in some ways, they are. Their journey—bookended by chance and choice—is made of the tiny, unremarkable moments that, woven together, become the story of a life built, side by side, through every year that comes.

7

Love is less about the lightning than about weathering every season side by side: learning who you were, who you are, and who you become, always surprising each other anew. For Lucy and James, growing together was never a single journey but a thousand small ones—each day the chance to begin again.

And that, in the end, is what mattered most.
