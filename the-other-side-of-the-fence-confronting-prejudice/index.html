The Other Side of the Fence: Confronting Prejudice


It was the sort of fence that tells a story as surely as any signpost. Each slat was painted white, once; now weather and time had carved runes into wood and peeled back the finish, letting the world peek through in streaks of gray and tan. It ran like a scar across the neighborhood, dividing Oakwood Lane from Sycamore Row—a ten-foot stretch separating tidy lawns and flowerbeds on one side from untamed grass and wild sunflowers on the other.

Most people hardly noticed. For them, it was just a boundary, an echo of city zoning decisions and property lines. But Eleanor noticed. She noticed everything.

Eleanor was eight, with sharp eyes the color of spruce needles and a restless need to ask questions. She lived with her mother in the small, blue house on Oakwood Lane, two doors down from the fence. Every morning she walked past it on her way to school, and every morning she wondered about the other side.

Oakwood Lane felt like a place she understood—the houses with perfect lawns, the mailbox flags tipped upright, the quiet drone of sprinkler systems at dawn. The families who lived there shopped at the same grocery, picked up at the same pizza place, and filled the neighborhood with the thick aroma of charcoal and barbecue on weekends. Sycamore Row, though, was a world she heard of only in murmurs and warnings.

“Don’t wander past the fence,” her mother would say, locking the door behind her after dusk. “People keep to their own side here, Eleanor. It’s better that way.”

She never explained what she meant. The other parents said similar things: that Sycamore Row was different, that the kids there were rowdy and rough, that they didn’t look after their gardens and left their trash cans out. They said it, assumed it, passed it along like salt at the dinner table.

But one Tuesday in June, the day widened open like a promise.

Eleanor’s best friend Maya was sick, and so her usual walk to school—a giggling parade with secret jokes and toe kicks—became a lonely trudge. As she passed the fence, she heard a laugh from the wild yard beyond. Peering through a knothole, she saw a boy standing atop a tilted skateboard ramp, arms stretched for balance, grinning at the sky. He was maybe a year older, his hair a thick crown of tight black curls, his shirt marked with paint splatters.

He spotted her immediately and waved. Caught, Eleanor considered running, but curiosity had anchored her feet.

“Hey,” the boy called, his voice bright as coins in a fountain. “Are you spying on me?”

Cheeks flushing, Eleanor shook her head. “No! I was just…” She searched for a reason and found none. “I don’t know. I walk past here every day.”

He hopped down and crossed the dandelion-strewn lawn, stopping a few feet from the fence. “I’m Jamal. You’re from Oakwood, right? You wanna try the ramp?”

For a long moment, silence sat between them like a spider in the webbed wood. Eleanor thought of what her mother would say, thought of the warnings, but her hands curled around the picket. “I’ve never skated before.”

Jamal shrugged. “I can show you. It’s just balance, really. You coming over, or what?”

She hesitated. The fence was taller than she was, but the gate—rarely used, coated with a decade of rust—sat a few feet away. She walked to it, lifting the latch; it squealed like a summer cicada as she pushed it open. She stepped onto Sycamore Row for the first time in her life.

Jamal showed her how to stand on the skateboard, guiding her feet and clapping when she managed to stay upright for more than a few seconds. They took turns careening down the wobbly ramp, both howling with laughter at each other’s wipeouts. The yard was wild, yes, but it was beautiful—a mess of purple thistles and sunflowers with bees meandering from bloom to bloom.

Her world had always been maps: neat borders, labeled roads, boundaries she could rely upon. She saw now that maps did not account for the warmth of a flower-filled breeze or the breathless fun of skating with someone new.

When the sun had climbed toward noon, Eleanor realized she was late for school. She started to say goodbye, but Jamal caught her hand.

“Come back after school, okay? I’ll show you how to ollie.”

Eleanor smiled. “I will.”

She slipped back through the gate and hurried home, heart thumping in bursts. That afternoon, the talk at dinner echoed in her ear.

“Sycamore Row, they just let their kids run wild,” her mother had said a week ago. “No respect for rules.”

That night, Eleanor asked, “Why don’t we ever visit Sycamore Row?

Her mother’s mouth puckered around the words. “It’s better to avoid trouble, sweetheart. People over there are…different.”

Eleanor thought of Jamal, with his paint-streaked T-shirt and fearless grin. Different didn’t seem so bad.

She went back the next afternoon, and the next, drawn by the thrill of the unknown and a friendship blooming like the sunflowers in Jamal’s yard. They traded stories—Eleanor’s fascination with birds, Jamal’s love of painting murals on his uncle’s garage. He showed her how some weeds were actually edible herbs; she taught him to whistle like a chickadee.

The fence that had always seemed so solid, so final, turned invisible every time she crossed it. The world was not so easily split into pieces.

The weeks rolled by, and her life took on new colors. She no longer saw only the neatness of Oakwood or the wildness of Sycamore; she saw her neighbors as people, not as whispers or warnings. Some of her friends grew curious; Maya came with her one day, giggling as she balanced on Jamal’s skateboard, seeing at last what all the fear had hidden.

But not everyone was pleased. Eleanor overheard her neighbor Mrs. Finch grumbling as she walked past: “That’s the Oakwood girl, always sneaking off to the other side. Her mother should know better.”

One afternoon, as Eleanor and Jamal painted their names across the ramp in a riot of bright green and blue, she heard shouting near the fence. Mrs. Finch stood on the Oakwood side, hands on her hips, face twisted like an overripe apple.

“What are you doing there, Eleanor? Get back this instant!”

Jamal stepped back, clutching his paintbrush. Eleanor’s heart hammered.

“Why?” she called, mustering courage she didn’t know she had.

Mrs. Finch’s answer was as expected as it was infuriating—a litany of too-loud, too-wild, too-rough. She threatened to call Eleanor’s mother, claimed she was worried for her safety, and insisted the fence was there for a reason.

Eleanor bit her lip. She wanted to scream that it wasn’t fair, that Jamal was her friend, that there was nothing dangerous about a skateboard or a yard full of sunflowers. Instead, she gathered her courage and said, “We’re just playing, Mrs. Finch. Are you mad because he’s different, or because you don’t know him?”

Mrs. Finch sputtered, but did not answer. Eleanor could see the uncertainty flicker across her face—the recipe for fear, after all, was ignorance and distance mixed together.

That night, Eleanor braced herself for trouble at dinner, but her mother surprised her. Instead of scolding, she asked, “You’ve been spending time on Sycamore Row, haven’t you?”

“Yes,” Eleanor said. “With Jamal. He’s nice. He’s teaching me to skate.”

Her mother was quiet a long moment. “I just want you to be safe. That’s all.”

“I am safe. Safer than if I never made a friend because someone told me not to.”

Her mother considered this, then smiled—a tight smile, but a real one.

Change crept in softly, quiet as dusk. Jamal and Eleanor became a fixture on both sides of the fence: painting, laughing, trading ideas for gardens and games. Some neighbors turned their heads, unwilling to see. But slowly, others took note. Maya’s mother began talking to Jamal’s aunt about sunflowers. A boy from Oakwood borrowed Jamal’s skateboard. The invisible dread that had lived in the boundary lost its grip.

The fence stayed standing, but in places, flowers began to creep beneath it, roots slipping under borders deeper than any city planner had intended. The wild and the tidy blurred together; lawns spilled over into tangles of color and life.

One day, as summer waned and the sky burned orange at the edges, Eleanor and Jamal hosted a picnic at the foot of the fence gate. They invited friends from both streets. Some adults lingered nearby, uncertain but curious. Sprinklers sent arcs of water into air, unattended children ran laughing between the houses, and the laughter drifted, easy and sweet as birdsong.

Eleanor sat beneath the sunflowers, paint smeared across her knees, skateboard beside her. She gazed at the fence, seeing it not as a wall but as a relic. Fences might keep out stray dogs, but they could not withstand the determined curiosity of a child, nor the gentle, persistent work of making friends.

She would remember that, always: the other side of any fence was only ever a step away.

It became, in time, a faded memory—the boundary that wasn’t, the line that love and stubbornness erased. Eleanor grew up, Jamal moved away, but the lesson rooted deep. When others told her to keep out, to not ask, to not reach, she thought of peeling paint and wild sunflowers, of skateboards and laughter that wouldn’t stay confined.

For Eleanor, there would never be only one side of the fence again.
