Likes, Followers, and the Quest for Validation


It was just past noon when Ella sat at her desk, staring at the glow of her phone. Her thumb hovered over the screen, unsure whether to post the picture of her lunch or wait for the right moment to post a more “inspiring” photo from her morning hike. Both would likely earn her a few dozen likes, but which one would stir the most reaction?

She had been thinking a lot about the reaction. About the number. The likes, the followers, the comments. Each notification that popped up on her screen was a small burst of endorphins, a fleeting moment of satisfaction. Her phone was more than just a device; it was a mirror reflecting her self-worth in numbers, images, and emojis.

Ella wasn’t alone. Most of her friends, colleagues, and even strangers she had never met, lived in this same feedback loop. The daily grind had evolved into something more insidious: the endless pursuit of validation.

A month ago, Ella had crossed an invisible line. At 10,000 followers, she had thought she’d finally “made it.” It was an unspoken milestone, one where the world seemed to offer you a platform to speak, to share, to be seen. She had celebrated it, of course, sharing a post with a “Thank you for the love!” caption and a picture of her standing proudly in front of a graffiti wall, where vibrant colors seemed to swirl around her.

The likes poured in. Comments flowed. Ella felt a rush of excitement. She replied to every comment, not wanting to miss a single opportunity to interact, to create a moment where someone might feel like they had truly been heard. But it didn’t stop there. As the days went by, the follower count grew. But something started to feel off.

At first, it was subtle. A like here, a comment there, but nothing quite as impactful as before. The posts were becoming a blur—sometimes, she couldn’t remember what she had shared a few days ago. She found herself posting more often, hoping that maybe, just maybe, the next post would spark something bigger. Maybe it would finally give her the attention she craved.

When her follower count hit 50,000, she thought, “This is it. I’ve arrived.”

But she didn’t feel any different. The same void still hung in the air, growing slowly and steadily. The likes still came, but they didn’t feel as fulfilling anymore. The comments, though frequent, lacked depth. She started noticing patterns, as if people were merely going through the motions of liking and commenting because that was what was expected of them. It wasn’t about her, not really. It was about the game they were all playing.

She had read somewhere that influencers—those who lived and breathed off social media validation—could feel the emptiness as well. But the void wasn’t just in her own heart. It was in everyone. All the influencers, the content creators, the brand ambassadors—they all played the game, showing curated glimpses of their lives, feeding their egos with every post. But behind those sparkling selfies and sponsored products, there was a silent battle. The battle for real validation.

One day, as Ella scrolled through her feed, she came across a post by Zara, a fellow influencer who had reached 500,000 followers in record time. Zara’s page was filled with perfect photos, flawless makeup, and extravagant trips to exotic destinations. Her followers adored her, and the likes were endless.

But this post was different. Zara had shared an image of herself, no makeup, no filters, sitting in a messy apartment, staring into the camera. The caption read: “I’m struggling. I don’t know who I am outside of this screen.”

The comment section exploded. Support poured in from followers who never expected to see such vulnerability. They related. They empathized. But more than that, they respected Zara more in that moment than ever before. It wasn’t about the brand deals or the perfectly curated life anymore. It was about realness, rawness, and truth. For the first time, it seemed like Zara wasn’t just chasing validation. She was sharing a part of herself with her followers, without a filter, without a script.

Ella couldn’t shake the feeling of envy as she read through the comments. She thought about her own posts—how carefully they were planned, how meticulously every image was chosen to reflect the “right” image of herself. It was exhausting. And yet, no matter how much she worked, no matter how much she posted, it never felt quite like enough.

That night, she sat in bed, her phone resting on her lap. She had posted something earlier that day—a picture from the hike, her face glowing with a fake, filtered smile. It had gotten the usual amount of likes and comments, but she couldn’t ignore the gnawing feeling in her chest. She was tired of pretending.

She started to type. At first, it was just a few words, but they came pouring out, almost like they had been waiting for the right moment to be shared.

I’ve been thinking a lot lately. About how we share these little pieces of ourselves, but somehow, it’s never enough. We chase likes, followers, validation… But I wonder, are we really seeing each other? Or are we just pretending to be happy?

She hesitated for a moment, looking at the screen. Would people think she was weak? Would she lose followers? But then she realized, maybe she didn’t need those followers if they only cared about the curated version of her. Maybe it was time to post something real. Something that wasn’t polished, something that didn’t fit the mold of what she thought people wanted to see.

She pressed “post” and felt her heart race.

The response was immediate. Comments poured in—faster than she could keep up with. Some were supportive, others puzzled. A few questioned her motives. But among the thousands of comments, one stood out:

I’ve been feeling the same way. Thank you for saying it out loud.

It was a small comment, but it meant more to Ella than any like could. It was validation, yes—but it wasn’t the kind she was used to. It wasn’t based on a curated image or an idealized life. It was real. It was human. She wasn’t just feeding into a system. She was connecting with someone, genuinely.

That night, as she closed her phone and placed it face down on the nightstand, Ella realized something important: the number of likes, the amount of followers—it didn’t matter in the end. What mattered was the connection. The real, honest connection.

Days passed, and Ella’s mindset began to shift. She no longer felt the need to post constantly, to chase after an unattainable level of perfection. Instead, she began sharing things that mattered to her, even the messy parts. It wasn’t always pretty, and it wasn’t always polished, but it felt true. And over time, she noticed something remarkable: her followers began to engage in a new way. They weren’t just liking her posts—they were sharing their own stories, their own struggles. Her feed, once filled with perfectly crafted photos, became a place of shared experiences, raw emotions, and support.

Ella had found something even more valuable than likes. She had found authenticity. And in that space, she discovered that the pursuit of validation wasn’t about the number on the screen—it was about showing up, being real, and letting others do the same.

And for the first time in a long while, Ella felt at peace.
