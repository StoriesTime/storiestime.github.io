Under the Northern Lights of the Aurora Sky Station


The frosted air carried with it a haunting silence. Sofie Lund adjusted the fur-lined hood of her parka and glanced up as her breaths became little puffs of white in the freezing night. Above her, the Northern Lights shimmered in a slow, unearthly dance, casting an emerald glow over the snowy tundra surrounding the Aurora Sky Station. The vibrant ribbons of green, purple, and gold arced across the heavens like a celestial symphony, captivating her as they always did.

She wasn’t new to this. As a local guide stationed in Abisko, Sweden, she had spent countless nights under the Aurora Borealis. Tourists came here from around the world, yearning for a glimpse of the lights that whispered secrets to the stars. But tonight, something felt different. The air held an almost electric charge, a reverence that made Sofie’s thoughts wander and her heart quicken with unease.

“Are you okay, Sofie?” a voice broke her trance.

She turned to Marcus, an astronomer from Lund University, who had joined her group for an ongoing research project. His glasses fogged when he spoke, his gloved hands clutching a silver thermos. The small group of tourists behind him murmured in awe, their heads tilted skyward as they snapped photographs with frozen fingers.

“I’m fine,” she murmured, though uncertainty lingered in her chest. “It’s just… intense tonight.”

Marcus smiled, his youthful enthusiasm bleeding through his otherwise scholarly demeanor. “Intense is good. The solar activity is off the charts. They’ll be talking about this aurora for decades.”

Sofie nodded but couldn’t shake the feeling that there was more to the lights tonight than science could explain. She didn’t share her unease with Marcus or any of the tourists; after all, they had come hundreds, even thousands, of miles to see this spectacle. Nor did she mention the old Sámi legends that sometimes whispered through her memory, tales of spirits and portals that opened under the vivid glow of the aurora.

The group moved to the higher observation platform at Aurora Sky Station. Built on Mount Nuolja, the structure provided an unparalleled vantage point for watching the ethereal lights. The wind was biting, but people didn’t complain. Wrapped in layers of wool, thermal gear, and borrowed excitement, they braved the subzero temperatures to witness nature’s most magnificent display.

As Sofie led them to a good viewing spot, her radio crackled. She paused and adjusted the volume. “This is Sofie,” she said, her voice steady despite the strange heaviness in her chest.

Static. Then—

“—are you reading—storm—coming your way—”

The cryptic message dissolved into white noise. She frowned and tapped the device, knowing weather interference wasn’t uncommon this far north. Marcus noticed her unease again but refrained from prying. Instead, he handed her a cup of hot lingonberry juice from his thermos, a gesture that she appreciated more than she admitted.

Around them, laughter and exclamations rippled as the auroras intensified. Some tourists threw their arms wide as if trying to catch the light; others stood side by side, silently overwhelmed. A faint hum reached Sofie’s ears—not from the crowd but from above. It was barely perceptible, more like a feeling than a sound. She glanced at Marcus.

“Do you hear that?”

“Hear what?” he asked, eyebrows furrowing.

She hesitated. “Nothing. Probably just the wind.”

But it wasn’t.

The hum deepened into a low-frequency pulse that seemed to vibrate through her ribcage. Sofie’s pulse quickened as the aurora began to shift in strange patterns. The ribbons of light suddenly stretched toward the ground, forming translucent columns that flickered like ghosts in the snow. Gasps rose from the group.

“What’s going on?” someone asked nervously.

“Is this normal?” another chimed in, their voice trembling with a mix of fear and wonder.

“Stay calm,” Sofie said, though she didn’t feel calm herself. “Unusual activity happens sometimes. Just enjoy the display.”

The words tasted like ash. Marcus stood as still as stone, his mouth slightly open as if he might speak but had forgotten how. The aurora now encased the observation platform in a glowing, otherworldly dome. The colors swirled faster, brighter—an intensity Sofie had never witnessed before.

And then it happened.

The hum became a thunderous roar, though not one tied to the earth or sky. It seemed to come from within. A sharp gust of wind knocked Sofie off balance, and she stumbled forward. Around her, the group clung to railings and each other for stability. The snow beneath their boots sparkled unnaturally, as if infused with liquid starlight.

“Sofie!” Marcus shouted, his voice finally rising above the chaos. “Something’s not right—”

Before he could finish, a blinding flash forced everyone to shield their eyes. When Sofie blinked her vision back, the scene had changed.

The sky was no longer black. It was a swirling canvas of color, like stepping into the aurora itself. The air felt thick, almost breathable in an uncanny way. And most alarming of all, the snow-laden ground was no longer beneath their feet. Instead, they stood on a crystalline surface that refracted the light into endless rainbows.

“What… where are we?” someone whispered.

Sofie’s heart pounded. The platform, the tourists, Marcus—they were all still there, but everything else had shifted. She turned toward Marcus, who was now fiddling with his handheld spectrometer, muttering to himself frantically.

“This is impossible,” he said, shaking his head. “The readings—I don’t even— Are we still in Abisko?”

Sofie didn’t answer. Her senses screamed that they were no longer in Sweden, no longer anywhere she recognized. A figure appeared in the distance, its shape indistinct but radiating a soft, bluish light. It moved slowly, almost curiously, toward the group.

“Hello?” Sofie called out, her voice trembling despite her best effort to maintain control.

The figure stopped. For a moment, it glowed even brighter, and then its form coalesced into something humanoid yet ethereal. It didn’t speak, at least not in words they could understand. Instead, a series of images and emotions flooded Sofie’s mind: vast arctic landscapes, ancient stone circles, and the unmistakable pull of the Northern Lights.

“It’s communicating,” Marcus said in awe, his scientific curiosity overpowering his fear. “It’s… showing us something.”

The tourists huddled closer together, their earlier excitement replaced with hushed murmurs of apprehension. Sofie stepped forward, drawn by an unseen force. The entity raised an insubstantial hand, as though inviting her closer.

“I don’t think it means us harm,” she said, more to herself than anyone else.

“Sofie, wait!” Marcus tried to stop her, but his voice was distant, like an echo fading in a cavern.

She walked toward the being, the crystalline ground beneath her boots thrumming in resonance with her footsteps. As she reached out to touch the figure, a surge of warmth engulfed her. Suddenly, she understood. The aurora wasn’t just light; it was a bridge, a connection between worlds, a living stream of energy that carried the memories of Earth and beyond.

When she opened her eyes again, she was back on the observation platform. The tourists were huddled together, disoriented but unharmed. Marcus was shaking her shoulder, his face frantic.

“What happened? Are you okay?”

The auroras above had calmed, their wild dance returning to its usual, mesmerizing flow. The crystalline dome was gone, and the familiar snowy landscape of Abisko stretched out below them. Sofie looked around, unsure if what had just happened was a vision, a dream—or something far more real.

“I think…” she began, her voice unsteady but certain, “I think we just glimpsed something we’re not meant to understand.”

Marcus stared at her, then looked back up at the sky, where the Northern Lights shimmered as if laughing. Nearby, a tourist nervously took a photo, breaking the taut silence.

Sofie glanced up once more, and the auroras seemed to ripple in acknowledgment. Whatever had happened, it would stay with her forever—as elusive and inexplicable as the lights themselves.

And somehow, she knew this night was only the beginning.
