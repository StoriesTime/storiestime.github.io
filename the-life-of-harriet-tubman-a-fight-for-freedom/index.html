The Life of Harriet Tubman: A Fight for Freedom


Harriet Tubman was born in the early 1820s, under the name Araminta Ross, in the sprawling woods of Dorchester County, Maryland. Her birthplace was a small, modest cabin nestled on the plantation of the Brodess family. From a young age, Harriet experienced the harsh realities of slavery. Her family was torn apart by the system that sought to dehumanize them, and her own childhood was a turbulent struggle for survival.

At the age of five, she was sent to work as a house servant, but her responsibilities would grow far beyond that. Harriet was strong-willed, and the rough treatment she received only hardened her resolve. She would later recall her early days of work in the fields, performing backbreaking labor for the masters who owned her.

As a young girl, she witnessed firsthand the toll slavery took on both the body and the spirit. Her mother, Harriet Greene, often spoke of the importance of faith, and it was that faith that would eventually guide Harriet through her struggles. Despite the hardships, Harriet remained determined to escape the oppression that bound her.

Her chance came when she was about 27 years old, after her first marriage to a free Black man named John Tubman. Harriet had learned that her master intended to sell her, and that fate would likely tear her away from her loved ones forever. Filled with desperation and the desire for freedom, she made the life-altering decision to escape.

In the dead of night, with nothing but the stars to guide her, Harriet fled. She traveled by foot through the dense woods, relying on her wits and instincts to guide her on a perilous journey to freedom. After a series of dangerous encounters, she made her way to the northern state of Pennsylvania, where slavery had been abolished. There, Harriet found solace in the fact that she was free, but she was far from content.

Harriet’s newfound freedom did not lead her to forget those she left behind. In fact, it only fueled her desire to return and help others escape the clutches of slavery. She knew that many of her friends and family members were still enslaved in the south, and she felt a deep calling to return and lead them to safety.

Tubman became part of the Underground Railroad, a secret network of abolitionists who helped enslaved people escape to the northern states and Canada. The Underground Railroad was neither underground nor a railroad, but it was a series of safe houses, sympathetic people, and secret paths leading to freedom.

Over the next decade, Harriet Tubman made numerous dangerous trips back to the South. She led more than 300 enslaved people to freedom, including her own family members. With each mission, her courage grew, and her reputation as a fearless leader spread. Harriet was often referred to as “Moses” because of her role in leading people to freedom, just as the biblical Moses led the Israelites out of slavery.

But Harriet’s bravery did not go unnoticed. Slave owners offered a hefty reward for her capture, but Harriet was never caught. Her knowledge of the terrain and her ability to evade capture became legendary. She was often forced to rely on a network of abolitionists, who provided food, shelter, and information. Many of these individuals, who risked their own lives to help Harriet, were committed to the cause of freedom.

Harriet’s work with the Underground Railroad became so successful that she soon became a target of the law. But Harriet was not deterred. She understood the risks, but her desire to free as many people as possible overpowered any fear of danger.

Her work in the Underground Railroad was not her only contribution to the fight for freedom. During the Civil War, Harriet Tubman joined the Union Army, where she worked as a nurse, cook, and scout. Her extensive knowledge of the land and her experience with espionage made her an invaluable asset to the Union forces.

In 1863, Tubman became the first woman to lead an armed expedition during the Civil War. She guided a raid in South Carolina that resulted in the liberation of more than 750 enslaved people. This mission demonstrated her courage and leadership, as well as her deep commitment to the cause of freedom.

Despite her many successes, Harriet faced significant challenges. She had to battle not only the physical dangers of her work but also the emotional toll of witnessing so much suffering. Yet, she remained steadfast in her determination to fight for the liberation of others.

After the Civil War, Harriet continued her activism, advocating for women’s suffrage and working to improve the lives of newly freed Black people. She used her fame and influence to call attention to the continued struggles of Black Americans. Harriet was deeply committed to the idea that freedom was not just a political issue but a moral one that affected every aspect of life, from education to equality.

In her later years, Harriet struggled with financial difficulties and health problems, but she remained active in the fight for social justice. She spent her last years in Auburn, New York, where she opened her home to the needy and continued her advocacy for freedom and equality.

Harriet Tubman passed away in 1913 at the age of 91. Though she faced tremendous adversity throughout her life, her legacy as one of the greatest freedom fighters in American history lives on. She is remembered not just for her daring escape and her work with the Underground Railroad but for her tireless commitment to justice and equality.

Harriet Tubman’s life is a testament to the power of courage, determination, and an unwavering belief in the value of freedom. Her journey was not just about escaping slavery but about fighting to ensure that all people—regardless of their race or background—could live in freedom and dignity. Today, Harriet Tubman’s name remains synonymous with the fight for freedom, and her story continues to inspire generations of individuals who believe in justice, equality, and the power of resilience.

Her legacy serves as a reminder that one person, no matter how humble or small, can make a profound difference in the world. Harriet Tubman’s story is not just one of personal triumph but also a collective victory for the many lives she touched, liberated, and saved. Her life’s work continues to inspire those who are fighting for justice and equality, not just in the United States but around the world.
