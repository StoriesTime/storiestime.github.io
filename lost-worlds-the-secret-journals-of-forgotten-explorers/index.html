Lost Worlds: The Secret Journals of Forgotten Explorers


The battered trunk had languished in the attic for decades, unremarkable among the shifting shadows of old coats and moth-eaten drapes. It was only when a summer storm rattled the roofboards and sent rain trickling through the eaves that the water finally soaked through and awakened a slumbering legacy. The journal inside was wrapped in oilskin, yellowed, brittle; yet the ink was dark as midnight, and the story it told—utterly irresistible.

Entry I: The Mapmaker’s Dream

April 14, 1879: Lisbon

“To those who come after me: I am Erico Duarte, once cartographer to kings, now little more than a ghost at the edge of the page. There are lands our charts have never dared to name. I have seen them. Today, I leave for the coast of Guinea.”

Duarte’s script was crabbed and looping, a hurried scrawl as if he scribbled against time itself. He spoke of rumors: sailors in taverns whispering of a continent within the continent, rivers that ran red in moonlight, civilizations untouched by Roman or Moor. The journal chronicled his days aboard the clipper Argonaut, days lashed by storms and haunted by visions—until finally, her bow crunched aground in tangled mangroves, and the air grew thick with the perfume of alien flowers.

According to Duarte, the expedition hacked through veils of verdure for days. Fallen pillars, glinting with mica, rose from the jungle floor: remnants of a people whose artistry had warped stone into impossible arabesques. The night he wrote of torchlit caves, gouged deep into basaltic hills, and fetishes carved like stars. Somewhere, a river forked—one current running toward oblivion, the other toward revelation. The last pages were waterstained, smeared with the words, “I have seen the gates of Etralia. The maps are lies. Our world is vaster than any king knows.”

Entry II: Alone Among the Ruins

May 3, 1882: Siwa Oasis

“A dagger of light sears through the wall at dawn—each morning I expect to hear the voice of the gods. Instead, only the whispers of the sands.”

Helen Lyell was among the first women to cross the Sahara with all-female company. Her prose glimmered with sharp clarity: sand so fine it slipped through cloth, heat wavering above salt pans, loneliness stalking like a jackal. Her second night at the oasis unearthed a curiosity: a circle of standing stones, quartz-veined and etched with enigmatic glyphs, where the Bedouin set offerings.

While her companions sought shelter, Lyell traced the markings with charcoal and wonder: one spiral repeated across every stone face. “The symbol recurs in dreams—coiled inside every living thing,” she wrote. “Locals claim these are the Columns of the Fallen Sky, left by a race who mapped the stars with naked eyes.” She attempted to reconstruct the story hidden within those sigils, copying the patterns with studious care; yet, as sand devils tore through the camp, her narrative took a disquieting turn. She described shadowy figures drifting between moonlit palms, song-like voices luring her from sleep, and the unsettled conviction that their discovery would not forgive their intrusion.

Lyell faded from the records in 1883. The stones, and her drawings, remained beyond the reach of scholars for another century.

Entry III: Under the Storm Glass Isles

August 17, 1901: South Pacific, Latitude Unknown

“Today the sky was not blue, but an endless violet, heavy and swollen with glassy rain. Our ship, the Menelaus, is becalmed. The air grows thick with prescience; the sea boils with luminous things.”

So began the account of Marius Choi, a zoologist whose fascination with cephalopods drew him from Tokyo to the farthest reaches of the ocean. Menelaus struck a hidden reef on a night when the aurora flickered tropical green across the horizon. Choi wrote of chartless islets rising from fathomless deeps—a volcanic caldera sheltering creatures lost to time.

He catalogued towering ferns, ghost crabs with bladed claws, lizards with opal eyes. The crew, beset by fever and delirium, mutinied when one mate vanished, leaving only a smear of ink-black blood. Choi’s last lucid entries described crystalline structures jutting from the surf—deafening harmonics shivering the air. Beneath the constant hum, he deciphered a pattern: the spires each reflected constellations unseen from any Mediterranean deck.

The journal trailed off as his hand decayed to irregular script, then childish scrawls. Whether Choi ever returned, or whether the storm glass isles receded into fog, remains unsettled.

Entry IV: The Temple Below Cobalt Peaks

September 11, 1925: Eastern Himalayas

“No modern soul has ever come this far, nor are we welcome. Yet the wind guides us; the peaks glow, at night, as if lit from within by ancient fires.”

Gérard Valois’s expedition into the Himalayas was neither sanctioned nor accompanied by fanfare. He was a linguist drawn by rumors of a language older than Sanskrit, carried by hermit shepherds among forgotten valleys. Valois ventured into luminous ice caverns and forests of petrified lilacs, describing a silence so deep “you can feel your own heartbeat in the bones of the world.”

In a high plateau, he discovered an edifice half-swallowed by permafrost—a temple left to the mercy of landslides and wild lilac. Its walls were frescoed with multicolored figures: men with three faces, women crowned in flames, beasts with scrollwork hides. No visitor’s boot marred the dust. Valois attempted to speak with the last inhabitants, a blind priest and two children. They answered in a tongue he believed predated human civilization by millennia. He called it the “Root Speech” and included facsimiles of the script, noting their words somehow sung both time and meaning at once.

A marginal note, written trembling, revealed that the mountain itself hummed in harmony with the priests’ chant. “This place,” Valois mused, “is the navel of lost memory—it will swallow us, and spit tomorrow forth.” When search parties arrived weeks later, only footprints remained, vanishing at the lip of a glacier split by blue fire.

Entry V: The River of Vanishing Echoes

June 29, 1932: Amazon Headwaters

“It is the water that deceives. Here, reflections cast futures, not pasts, on the silver surface—a maze where sound and light are both undone.”

Pablo Suárez, an ethnologist from Colombia, devoted years to documenting tribes along the upper Amazon. In handwritten Spanish, he recorded a journey upriver that diverged from any mapped stream. The forest closed in; sunlight grew rare as gold. Elders in the last village told legends of the Yaku-Iná, a city veiled by mist, whose people lived beyond time.

Suárez’s dugout slipped through overgrown channels so narrow that every paddle stroke echoed until it tangled in itself. He described the ceaseless symphony—cicadas, monkey screams, rush of wings—yet sometimes, all noise would abruptly halt. In those silences, he claimed, he glimpsed shadowed figures at the riverbank, their faces mirrored and distorted. Fish with translucent bones leapt, and in their arcs, Suárez began to believe in a place where origin and destiny converge.

On the final page, he confessed, “To find the beginning, you must forget the end. The river loops—always forward, always returning.” Suárez himself vanished; locals say the jungle keeps those it loves.

Epilogue: The Trunk in the Attic

The journal was a composite, stitched together from disparate hands, penned in at least four alphabets and annotated in the margins by someone with the surname de Valera. The trunk yielded other relics: a Navajo talisman, a broken spyglass, clay tablets daubed with ochre, and a feather that shimmered turquoise even under dust.

Pieced together, these accounts formed a map with no coordinates; a testament to the allure and peril of the secret world. There were lands that swallowed those who trespassed, where memory and myth cavorted; where explorers vanished but their stories lingered, inked on fragile leaves drifting through the centuries.

For every known world, ten more remain hidden behind mist, lost to time and the oblivion of tides. Anyone who reads the journals glimpses, if only for a moment, the grandeur—and danger—of forgotten frontiers. Each explorer’s words, echoing faintly in a language half-remembered, invite the daring to look beyond the horizon, and to try, however briefly, to walk the uncharted margin of human knowing.

They are gone. The world is not empty. The journals endure, waiting for the next reader to lose themselves in the wild, unending possibility of discovery.
