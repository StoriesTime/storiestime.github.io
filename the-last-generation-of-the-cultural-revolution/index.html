The Last Generation of the Cultural Revolution


Every morning, the old bell at the end of Jiefang Street rang out across the city, as reliable as the sunrise, as persistent as memory. In the mornings, the city woke to a haze of coal smoke and the chatter of radios tuned only to the approved frequency, and for a moment, it was easy to forget that beyond the faded banners and empty factories, history had already moved on.

Chen Qiguang rose before dawn. His hair, white and thinning, was combed with habitual neatness. His apartment, mended and patched, overlooked an intersection now choked with mopeds and market tarps, a city changing under his watch. He buttoned his shirt, the same drab green he had worn as a young man, and lifted the photographs from his writing desk.

He flipped through them like a deck of worn cards: young faces in armbands, the wide-eyed idealism of seventeen-year-olds with fists raised at dawn parade, the city crowds in blurred black and white behind them. Qiguang and his friends—Wei, Liu, Lin, Xue—shouting slogans, raising red books to the sky, trampling on the relics of an old world as though history could be torn apart with paper and ink.

And now the world outside was louder, faster, greedy with ambitions he only half understood.

He placed the last photograph on the stack. It was from summer 1968: his parents’ apartment, the plaster peeling, evidence of a struggle just out of frame. In the corner, his mother stared at the camera with an expression he’d learned to recognize: defiance tinged with fear. “We are all just waiting for the storm to pass,” she had whispered, breath warm with desperation.

He never told anyone what happened that night.

On Thursdays, Qiguang joined a weekly gathering at the library on Heping Road, not far from the river. The group called themselves the Last Generation, only half in jest. There were always eight or nine of them, sometimes more if the rail tickets weren’t outrageously expensive.

The faces changed, but some presences were as constant as the bell outside. Ms. Han, once a chemistry prodigy sent to the rice paddies of Guangxi, sat near the window. Uncle Lu, whose hands had been ruined by years of forced labor in the mountains, brought oranges from his son’s shop. Even the librarian seemed to move softly around the tables, as if mindful of the memory buried in their words.

“I tried to explain to my grandson about the campaign,” Liu Zheng said, his voice quiet and formal. He looked far younger than his sixty-seven years, hair still black, skin weathered but strong. “He listens—but it’s like describing a fever he’s never felt. Too strange, too distant.”

Ms. Han nodded. “He will never believe what hunger feels like. What it’s like to crawl for leftover grain at a thresher, or fight for a decent shirt.” She coughed, and silence filled the room. “Even my daughter pretends it never happened.”

They drifted into stories. The ration book that never seemed to last. Poems recited in basements, copying lines from banned books by candlelight. The time a neighbor was denounced for owning a dictionary. They revisited the same memories each week as if by moving them around, they might find a different arrangement, a way to reconcile what happened with what they’d become.

“Do you regret it?” asked Xue Min, the group’s youngest member, a former teacher in the countryside who had walked back to the city at the end of the decade.

Her question fell into the hush like a stone.

Qiguang hesitated. “Regret? Before I knew better, I believed—truly believed—that we were remaking the world. Bread, books, respect for those without privilege. That was the promise.”

He turned the photo in his hands. “The price…no one counted until too late.”

That evening, Qiguang walked under the plane trees, shoulders hunched, listening to the city pulse with electronic music and car horns. Change pressed in on him at every intersection; neon lights had replaced banners, queues for smartphones instead of rice.

But in his mind, the city’s old ghosts still walked: the teachers marched through crowds with dunce caps and inked slogans, the temples burned in the mornings, the laughter of boys trying on their father’s army jackets and dreaming of becoming heroes, unafraid, untested.

He passed a bar where young men in Western clothes spilled into the street, laughing, watching a soccer game on their phones. He stared at them, at their shining shoes and easy confidence. For a moment, the hope that had driven him at seventeen returned: the desire to be part of something larger than himself, to give shape and purpose to life.

Was that desire still dangerous?

There were things he could say, and things he could not. In his youth, he had shouted down professors, stripped souls of love for the pleasure of righteousness. Now, he wanted to tell these boys—and his own grandson—that truth was slippery, that ideals contorted themselves as the world spun on, that violence in the name of virtue left only ruins behind.

But there was no language for warnings that sounded like blame.

In every home across the district, television sets flickered to life at seven. News from Beijing, from Shenzhen, from the world—the story of a China hungry and clamorous, reaching beyond itself. The camera panned to schoolchildren reciting the national anthem, to skyscrapers climbing into the smog. It would be easy to believe the past was a scrim, a faded backdrop beneath the new script of progress.

Yet some days, history’s residue pressed its hand through the grit and ambition. In the alleys, old slogans flaked from grey walls: “Smash the Four Olds!” “Revolution is not a dinner party!” Others had been painted over, their outlines still visible where the sun had peeled away new paint.

Tourists sometimes stopped to photograph these relics, giggling, treating them like souvenirs from another planet. Qiguang watched them sometimes from his balcony, feeling both envy and relief. For them, the banners were novelties. For him, they were scars.

The next Thursday, Qiguang arrived late to the library; rain had turned the streets into rivers, and the buses had stalled on the Ring Road. Inside, the group was smaller than usual. Uncle Lu was gone—the others said his son’s permit to work in Australia had finally come through. The absence left the table off-kilter, the rhythm of their conversation awkward.

“We’re getting smaller,” Xue remarked quietly.

Qiguang answered with a shrug. “Perhaps that’s how history washes clean. Fewer witnesses mean fewer contradictions.”

Ms. Han smiled. “Or just that the ink runs out.”

They laughed, gentle and sad.

Liu Zheng produced a manuscript: yellowed, typed on brittle onionskin. They gathered close as he read a poem, unsigned, written during long nights in a mountain dormitory. The poem spoke of longing, hope, of homesickness too dangerous to utter aloud.

Qiguang recognized the lines, though he had not heard them in forty years.

Afterward, Ms. Han, who never cried, dabbed at her eyes. “Does anyone write such things anymore?”

“Who would read them?” Liu replied. “They’d rather read about the new tech parks and the university graduates making a fortune in Shanghai.”

A silence fell again, closer this time.

Walking home, Qiguang thought of his grandson. A boy of thirteen years—digital native, hair always brushed forward, eyes full of mistrust for anything slower than a screen flicker. On rare visits, Qiguang sometimes attempted to evoke the world of his own boyhood, to explain the razor-thin edge on which they all balanced: faith and fear, progress and destruction.

He tried once more.

That weekend, he took a bus to the city’s modern center, where steel towers rose over the dirty canal. His daughter held the door open, ushering her family into a spotless white-tiled apartment above a bustling shopping mall.

“You look tired,” she said, only half-concerned.

“Just the rain,” Qiguang lied.

At dinner, he mentioned the group at the library. His grandson, busy at a tablet, looked up, no trace of curiosity.

“Why do you meet?” the boy asked, frowning as if it were suspicious.

“We talk about old times,” Qiguang answered, simple as that.

“Were they good?”

There it was: the secret question, direct as a blow.

“They were filled with hope,” he said. “But also with loss. Both are true, just as you and I are both here tonight.”

His grandson frowned, then returned to his game. The child’s indifference stung; after all he’d risked, after all he’d mourned.

Yet it was also a kind of mercy.

Over the months, the group at the library shrank further. Ms. Han’s health faded, though she persisted, arriving each week bundled in scarves. Liu Zheng’s son moved him to another city, promising better care, less pollution. Xue Min stopped coming after her apartment block was razed, daily life now too complicated to make the journey.

For a time, Qiguang sat alone at the table near the window. He brought his photographs and spread them carefully, one by one, under the library’s hard white lights.

Each face had a story: who was vanished, who was redeemed, who made peace with their memories, who was haunted still. Sometimes a stranger paused to ask about them. He would tell a fragment, never the whole.

He feared, increasingly, that history would die with them—the contradiction and complexity of it, the defiance and self-doubt, the way the world could collapse between one speech and the next.

There were books now, of course. Scholarly debates on the suffering and gains, statistics and anecdotes flattened by careful language. They did not contain the smell of coal smoke on the river, the yelp of a teacher struck by her own students, the moment late at night when hope turned suddenly to exhaustion.

One evening, a student approached while Qiguang arranged his photographs. She was researching a thesis, she said, eager-eyed and clumsy.

“May I record your account?” she asked.

He studied her, noting the smartphone already poised in her hand, the intentness with which she leaned forward, unafraid.

“What do you want to know?” he replied, wary but not unkind.

“Everything,” she said. “Anything you can share. I want to know what it was really like.”

He thought a moment.

“Do you want the truth as I saw it then, or the truth as I see it now?”

The student hesitated, unsure which to choose.

Qiguang offered her the photo from 1968: the night his mother stared past the lens, dignified and terrified.

“Tell me about this,” she said.

He began, and the words led him unexpectedly back—through the hunger and the songs, the betrayals and reconciliations, the countless ways hope could breed cruelty. He described his youth in a language he had not used in decades. He watched the student’s face shift: revulsion, awe, amazement, sadness.

“I believed,” he said at last. “We all did. It’s how we survived it, and why we failed so often.”

When he finished, dusk had fallen outside. The student sat quietly, not sure what to say.

She stood, bowed in thanks, and left, her footsteps quick down the marble hall.

He sat, surrounded by his photographs—a silent revolution of memory, more fragile than any banner or statue.

In the years that followed, Qiguang visited the library less. Age closed over him, gentle as dust, and he took comfort in routine. Sometimes, late at night, he sketched besides the window: fragments of a vanished city, silhouettes of the friends he could not name aloud.

One spring morning, his grandson came to visit—no longer a child, but a man on the edge of escape. They talked of new countries, open visas, the long reach of possibility.

Before his grandson left, Qiguang pressed the old photos into his hands. “When you have time, look at these. Not for me—for yourself. They are all that’s left.”

His grandson accepted them, uncertain, but did not refuse.

Above the city, the bell tolled again, stubborn against the onslaught of change. Qiguang watched the world rebuild itself each year—faster, richer, ever-divorced from the past.

But sometimes, in certain light, he could see their shadows: the last generation—solemn, reckless, and unfinished—walking the streets beneath banners both red and invisible, whispering to the careless world that followed, urging it to listen.

Even if nobody ever did.
