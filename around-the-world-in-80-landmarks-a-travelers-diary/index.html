Around the World in 80 Landmarks: A Traveler's Diary


The cracked leather spine of the diary called to me from the dusty shelves of a Parisian flea market. In silver-embossed letters, faded by generations of eager hands, the cover read: “Around the World in 80 Landmarks: A Traveler’s Diary.” Beneath it, the name “Arthur Bellamy” was carefully inscribed, along with the date — 1922.

I flipped open the cover, drawn in by the faded, looping handwriting. Within moments, I was transported, swept along by a current of wanderlust that carried Arthur from the shadowed alleys of Europe to the dizzying heights of South America, across scorched African deserts, through lush jungles of Asia, and onward into the beating heart of the world. His story—fragmented, passionate, vividly observant—unfolded through my fingers.

Below are pages selected from Arthur’s diary, a tapestry of wonder fashioned across continents.

Entry 1: Paris – Against the Wind of Change

March 17, 1922

I began, naturally, in Paris; the city of light and croissants, where history clings to cobblestones and laughter spills from corner cafés. My boots echoed along the Champs-Élysées beneath the brooding figure of the Arc de Triomphe.

From its summit, I gazed down at the twelve radiating avenues, thinking of Napoleon’s armies marching outwards, boots and banners swirling in the wind. Tourists shuffled below—old men, babies in prams, street vendors hawking miniature Eiffel Towers. I scribbled a sketch into my diary, capturing the arc’s ornate reliefs and the way the sun split the spring clouds.

Later, at midnight, I stood under the iron latticework of the Eiffel Tower, its girders humming in the wind. Couples waltzed along the Seine. I pressed my hand against the cold iron, promising the city (and myself) that I’d return.

Entry 4: Machu Picchu – Mist and Memory

April 12, 1922

The climb to Machu Picchu drained the air from my lungs and left my mind sharp, focused only on the vision rising through the morning mist. Every stone of the citadel was luminous, reclaimed by green moss and time. The silence here thundered, save for the occasional cry of some unseen bird or the fleeting rustle of llama hooves.

I ran a hand along ancient terraces, feeling the notched scars of Incan engineering—the stonework more precise than any mortar-bound wall of Europe. I scribbled furiously—the way the morning fog spun gold between the gray stones and how the world below looked impossibly far away.

A young guide, Martín, spoke of ancestors, of gods dwelling in the cold peaks, and of secrets hidden in shadowed corners. For a moment, with the world stretched below, I too felt the old magic stirring.

Entry 9: Istanbul – Whispers in the Blue Mosque

May 3, 1922

The city bridges continents as deftly as it weaves history: Istanbul, crossroads of empires, where every stone hums with stories. I wandered through the chaotic bazaar, cinnamon and cumin twisting on the breeze, and pressed forward until the twin minarets of the Blue Mosque rose before me, blue-tiled domes glinting in the sun.

Inside, the silence draped itself over a thousand felt slippers, as if centuries of prayers still hung in the air. The walls, lined with more than 20,000 handmade İznik tiles, shimmered with distant stars, and the mihrab glowed, catching the noon sun.

An old man sat nearby, sketching. He nodded at me, gave a gap-toothed smile, and returned to his drawing—a mosaic of blue and gold. I wondered how many travelers had knelt in this same wonder as I did.

Entry 18: Petra – Echoes in the Rose-Red Rock

June 1, 1922

Petra appeared as a slit of shadow between stone cliffs, a wound in the desert, hiding one of the world’s most astonishing secrets. I wound through the Siq as crimson dawn crept along the walls. Each step was muffled, as though the gorge itself was holding its breath, waiting.

And then—the Treasury, el-Khazneh, bathed in rose and morning light, carved with impossible grace into the façade of living rock. My heart hammered at the sight. Camels dozed in the shade, their long lashes dusted with ochre. The silence cracked only by distant laughter and the slow, shuffling steps of Bedouin guides.

While others snapped photographs, I tried instead to listen—to hear voices buried within the stone, and the secrets of lost kings. Petra’s corridors unfolded into tombs, temples, and a Roman-style theater, each more enigmatic than the last.

Entry 25: Venice – Labyrinth of Water and Time

June 17, 1922

The morning fog shrouded Venice in soft veils, swallowing the sharp cries of gondoliers and the slap of water against stone. The city fluttered at the edge of dream, hovering between memory and myth. St. Mark’s Basilica loomed, its façade a kaleidoscope of gold mosaics reflecting light like a mirage.

I roamed alleys narrower than my shoulders, bridges arching over green canals. The Doge’s Palace greeted me with pink marble serenity. At the Bridge of Sighs, I paused, picturing prisoners—one final glance at Venice before darkness. A slow sadness lingered amid the melodious clang of bells and laughter echoing from the Piazza San Marco.

In the evening, I watched the city glow with lanterns as a gondolier sang Puccini arias, carrying my thoughts out onto the lagoon.

Entry 29: Great Wall of China – Stones Across the Sky

July 2, 1922

I looked back over my shoulder at the long snake of stone, winding away toward a horizon blurred by haze and distance. Each brick was shaped by hands long-buried, and every watchtower stood as a testament to fear, determination, and endurance.

Climbing the Great Wall was a trial: relentless staircases, uneven stones digging at my feet, knees aching. Yet with each new vantage, the imagination soared higher—armies in distant centuries, diplomats carrying treaties, merchants leading trains of silk-laden camels. Now, only the wind and birds kept watch.

I sat at a lookout, legs dangling over a precipice, chewing on a piece of dried persimmon as the countryside bloomed with the green of early summer. I inscribed a poem in the margin: “Where wall meets sky, the world unfolds—still, and infinite.”

Entry 33: Taj Mahal – At the Edge of Eternity

July 15, 1922

Agra greeted me in a blur of swirling dust, market chatter, and the far-off clang of temple bells. Nothing, not even the postcards or tales from other travelers, prepared me for the spectral beauty of the Taj Mahal, riven out of marble by Shah Jahan’s impossible grief.

I crossed the long reflecting pool as the monument shimmered, pure as a dream in the dawn’s pale fire. Its inlaid stones spun out peacocks, arabesques, and flowers, each petal a labor of sorrow transformed into beauty. I sat and tried to sketch it, but the building itself refused capture—always shifting with the light and sky, dissolving away at the edges.

I met a guide who recounted the old legend: how the builders’ hands were cut, so such a marvel could never be replicated. It seemed a cruel story, yet in the stillness, some strange, piercing joy lingered.

Entry 41: Victoria Falls – Smoke That Thunders

August 6, 1922

The Zambezi River whispered through jungles toward the precipice, where it exploded in a million white threads of spray and rainbows. Locals called it “Mosi-oa-Tunya”—the smoke that thunders. Even at a distance, every stone, every leaf trembled with the waterfall’s power.

Standing at the edge, I felt the roar vibrating through my bones, drenching me to the skin. Rainbows flickered in the spray, ephemeral promises. The falls stretched across the chasm, a sheet of silver tearing at the seam of two continents. Even with my ears ringing, I longed to leap into the vapor, to dissolve into its brightness.

I crossed the rickety iron bridge to Zimbabwe, laughing at the shudder beneath my feet, alive in every cell.

Entry 48: Sydney Opera House – Sails on the Pacific

August 28, 1922

From the deck of a steamship entering Sydney Harbor, the white sails of the Opera House gleamed as if lifted from the ocean’s surface. Seagulls whirled overhead, and the city skyline jostled behind, cranes and domes locked in silent argument.

Inside, the air shimmered with the ghost of music—a rehearsal hummed along the walkways, cellos and sopranos, rising and falling like the surf itself. At sunset, the shells of the building flared against a sea of gold. I sat with locals on the steps, sharing cold beer and tales of the sea, and marveled at the way this singular building captured the restless energy of an entire nation.

Entry 56: Alhambra – Gardens of Blood, Gardens of Song

September 30, 1922

Granada held heat beneath its skin, glowing from the embers of Moorish empires and Christian conquest. Atop the hill, the Alhambra sprawled—an ensemble of palaces, fortress walls, and fountains singing their secret, ancient songs.

Courtyards whispered with the drip of water and the rustle of wind in cypress trees. I traced the lacy arches of the Palace of the Lions, marveling at the calligraphy carving stories into every wall. There was both blood and beauty here; old wounds dragged under the perfection of tilework and gardens fragrant with orange.

In dusk’s hush, the Nasrid Palaces blushed and lanterns flickered in the Generalife Gardens, blurring the line between poetry and reality.

Entry 63: Chichen Itza – Shadows on the Stone

October 15, 1922

The heat soaked itself into my bones as I trod the cracked paths between broad plazas and looming pyramids. At the heart of Chichen Itza, El Castillo rose like the memory of a lost world, shadow-drenched and stubborn.

A guide showed me the snake shadow—the way, at the equinox, an unbroken serpent writhes down the staircase, joining earth and sky. He described sacrifice and astronomy in hushed tones, filling the air with an electric tension. The cenote yawned nearby—water dark and secret, rimmed with flowers.

The clangor of the ancient ball court rang, carried by tourist guides and Maya descendants alike. I ran my hands along glyphs roughened by centuries, feeling the pulse of a people who mapped stars, measured time, and vanished into history.

Entry 71: Angkor Wat – Kingdom of Morning Light

November 12, 1922

A world of stone lay sunken in jungle, towers drawn in lotus-bud spires. Angkor Wat, dreamed by Khmer kings and abandoned to the strangling grip of roots and vines.

I entered before dawn, wading through thigh-deep grass, watched by impassive Apsaras carved on the lintels. As the sun broke, beams spilled between columns, igniting the bas-reliefs in fire and shadow. The Churning of the Ocean of Milk danced before my eyes; gods and demons locked in cosmic tug-of-war.

In the stillness, monks in saffron robes passed, their chanting drawing the birds into morning flight. The air rang with the echo of elephants, half-imagined, tramping avenues no longer there.

Entry 80: Stonehenge – Circles of Silence

December 31, 1922

Endings and beginnings blend here, where the sun sinks below Salisbury’s rolling hills and the ancient stones of Stonehenge cast long, cryptic shadows. The ring was ringed by frost, the fields silvered in winter mist.

I walked among the megaliths, trailing my hand along the cold, pitted sarsen. No one spoke as the last daylight faded, and silence filled the circle, humming in a pitch older than language.

A fox darted past, vanishing into darkness. Above, the stars wheeled—the same constellations that shone on nameless builders, priests, and wanderers. For a moment, everything seemed joined—a world spun around ancient hopes, clever hands, and quiet awe.

Epilogue: Return

Across eighty landmarks, I let myself be changed: by cities of glass and dust, by rivers, jungles, deserts, by the kindness of strangers and the marvel of ancient stones. In every ruin, I found stories not just of the past, but of home.

I close this diary now with gratitude—footprints left across the world’s wild tapestry, and a promise to always journey forward, wherever the next path leads.

In ninety years’ time, perhaps someone else will pick up this diary, leaf through its pages, and find themselves tempted by the call of distant places. If so: travel well. The landmarks wait, eager to tell you their secrets.
