Rise Above: A Tale of Overcoming Adversity


Andre Romero never liked rain. It reminded him too much of the years before anything changed—years spent looking through fogged up windows, counting the drops racing down glass as if every tiny rivulet carried his hope away. For eighteen years, Andre’s world had been small: the crumbling second-floor apartment he shared with his mother, the battered library on the corner, and the narrow alleyways that wound between his existence and everyone else’s.

But rain also meant work.

He stepped outside, the plastic poncho barely keeping his torso dry, and unfolded his last remaining umbrella. It snapped open, the metal slightly bent from too many storms. He gripped the handle, turning into the wind as he hurried down to Elias Street.

Deliveries didn’t wait—not in the city, not on a Saturday. He’d been cycling groceries for two years. Some days, it felt like battling the sky itself. As he guided his bike through a pothole, Andre silently repeated his mantra: Every mile earned.

His phone buzzed in his pocket. Another order, this time all the way across town, up Byler Hill. The incline was infamous, a short, brutal rise Andre usually avoided. Today, there was no choice. Rent was overdue. The lights flickered every evening now, promising their own ultimatum.

He glanced longingly at the old guitar cases in the pawn shop window as he pedaled by—reminders of music lessons he’d begged for as a child and sacrifices he’d watched his mother make. Ashira Romero worked night shifts cleaning the city courthouse. Andre tried to carry his disappointments lightly; she bore the heavier load. Sometimes, when she was too tired to hide it, he’d catch her staring at bills or the fading family photo by the stove, a rare admission of defeat.

He pushed harder up Byler Hill, the weight of the groceries threatening to pull him backward. Tires slipped. Rain stung his face. For a single moment, he wanted to stop—just give up, let the groceries roll to the gutter, tell the app he wouldn’t finish today. But behind his eyelids: rent, the hush of the repo man’s voice last winter, his mother’s feverish cough. Andre gritted his teeth and climbed.

Just before the peak, he spotted another figure at the curb—a girl about his age, sopping wet, clutching a torn backpack to her chest. She looked lost, her hair stuck to her cheeks in thick coils.

“You okay?” Andre called out between breaths.

She hesitated. “Missed the bus. My phone’s dead.” Her shivering seemed bigger than the rain, as if she was lost in more than mere weather.

Without a second thought, Andre handed her his umbrella. “Take this. I’ll be fine.” He’d survived worse storms.

She blinked, as though she didn’t trust kindness any more than he once had. “You too?” she asked. “Trying to make it through?”

Andre smiled, brief but genuine. “Aren’t we all?”

The exchange was fleeting but electric, a small charge against the city’s pervasive gray. He pedaled away, heart pounding from more than the incline. When he reached his destination, breathless but victorious, the customer barely glanced up.

“About time,” she sniffed, snatching the paper bags.

Andre nodded, winded. He reminded himself: every mile earned.

Back home, Andre shucked off his soaked clothes, wiped his glasses, and pulled out the cracked laptop Ashira had salvaged at a yard sale. While she snored on the couch, he opened the website he visited every night—a scholarship portal from Langley University. Guilt gnawed at him with every click; he knew applying could mean leaving his mother, leaving their fragile balance. But the alternative was staying, growing more transparent with every shift, everything unfulfilled.

He filled out his application, typing up the essay question: Describe a challenge you have overcome.

At first, his mind blanked. Every challenge felt woven so tightly into his life, it was impossible to extract just one. He tried to imagine another version of himself—confident, planning a future instead of surviving day by day. The words, halting at first, began to spill out.

He wrote about the cold winters with no heat, the lunches skipped for rent, the heavy bag of groceries up Byler Hill. He described wanting more, not just for himself, but for the mother who had sacrificed so much she rarely smiled with her eyes anymore. He wrote about music, and the ache in his fingers for chords and melodies and performances that seemed centuries away.

Tears surprised him—not from sorrow, but from the relief of finally admitting the struggle out loud.

For weeks, he checked his email obsessively. The city rolled on: blaring sirens, the grind of bus brakes, news of layoffs at the plant. Andre filled every spare minute with side gigs, and butted up against every closed door—jobs that vanished, deliveries that turned out to be scams. Once, he was mugged for his tips; another night, his bike was stolen outside the corner bodega.

“My luck died years ago,” he muttered, chain grease staining his palms as he limped home on foot, groceries dragging at his side.

But beneath every broken day, the memory of the uphill climb lingered. Andre learned how to patch his own inner tube, how to cut through back alleys to avoid the worst traffic. He found another lost soul sleeping behind the library—a runaway with bruises and nowhere to go—and split his sandwich without thinking. The city was hard, but he refused to be harder.

Ashira noticed the change, too. One night, she woke abruptly while Andre played his old taped-up guitar, the chords rough but hopeful.

“That was your father’s song,” she murmured, smile finally touching her eyes.

Andre played on, confidence blooming as quietly as the spring outside.

March arrived, bringing a slow thaw. Andre received no word from the scholarship office. He tried not to care, tried to invest purely in the present—delivering coffee to elderly neighbors, studying late at the library. His life wasn’t glamorous, but it was honest. Still, the longing crawled along his spine like the chill that never left him.

One afternoon at the library, he ran into the girl from Byler Hill again. She was stacking books at the front desk, hair tied up, hands swift and sure.

“You found your bus,” he joked, smiling.

She gave him a bemused look. “Yeah. And I found your umbrella, too.” She held it up, duct tape expertly coiled around the bent ribs.

They exchanged stories—her name was Lydia, she’d been kicked out of her home for reasons she didn’t want to explain. She was working at the library for meal vouchers. The bond was immediate; hardship recognized itself. Over the next weeks, they shared lunches, swapped stories, and trampled old fears into new soil.

“You ever think life gets tired of knocking us down and gives up?” Andre asked one evening as they watched the city lights.

Lydia grinned. “Maybe. Or maybe it just makes us tougher. Like, eventually you punch the rain back.”

Andre laughed, and it was a relief—a sound he’d missed.

April bloomed into sunlight through dust. Andre’s phone buzzed—this time with a subject line he’d only dreamed of: Langley University Scholarship Decision. He almost didn’t open it, afraid that hope was an indulgence he couldn’t afford.

Congratulations. We are pleased to offer you the Connor Harris Scholarship for First-Generation Students. Welcome to Langley University.

Andre sat down hard. The moment wasn’t cinematic; it was small and real. Ashira, waking to his quiet sob, drew him into her tired arms.

“You did it, mijo,” she whispered. “You did it.”

Later, on the cracked library steps with Lydia, Andre confessed his fears. “If I go, I leave everything—Mom, the jobs, this city that taught me everything about fighting. I’m not sure if I know how to rise any higher.”

Lydia squeezed his hand. “That’s the secret, Andre. You don’t rise alone. You pull others up with you.”

He shook his head, half-smiling. “What if I fall?”

Lydia nudged him. “Then you get up. That’s what rising above means.”

In the fall, Andre boarded a bus with Lydia waving from the curb, his battered guitar at his side and the knowledge that heartbreak and adversity did not render him lesser—they refined his will. He carried the stories of everyone who’d traveled Byler Hill, of every customer who handed him a crumpled dollar, of every night his mother had worked while he dreamed of music and light.

Langley was nothing like his neighborhood—sprawling lawns, sunlit libraries, classrooms echoing with laughter and inquiry. For a while, Andre felt like an impostor, his thrift store clothes a badge of survival others might never see. But his professors marveled at his tenacity; peers gathered to hear him play guitar in the quad. A strange, unexpected peace stole over him every morning—rain or shine.

He sent half his stipend home every month. He worked the campus café, studied until his eyes burned, and played gigs at local coffee shops to send ashira videos of her song—her smile, now, a permanent grace in his life.

Sophomore year, Lydia joined him at Langley, emboldened by her own scholarship. They found a rhythm, two storm veterans who wouldn’t let the world wear down their edges.

Whenever rain returned, Andre grabbed an umbrella—no longer afraid of storms, but welcoming the cleansing. He knew now that every adversity was a season, every setback a rise in progress.

And always, beneath the downpour and above the clatter of the city, Andre Romero climbed: every mile earned, every moment a testament to the quiet strength of those who rise above.
