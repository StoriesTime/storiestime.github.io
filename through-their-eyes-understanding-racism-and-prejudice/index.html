Through Their Eyes: Understanding Racism and Prejudice


The small town of Havenbrook was, by all accounts, a peaceful place. Nestled in a valley between towering mountains, it had a slow, rhythmic pace. Most of the residents were friendly, but the town wasn’t without its complexities. Havenbrook had long been a homogenous community, where everyone knew everyone, and the past seemed to cast a long shadow. Despite its charm, the town was not immune to the kind of issues that plagued larger cities—issues like racism, prejudice, and the fear of the unknown.

It was here that Mariam, a young woman of color, found herself after moving to Havenbrook for a teaching job. Mariam had grown up in a bustling city far away, where diversity was a way of life. She had seen racism and prejudice firsthand, but she believed that change was possible, that education and empathy could break down walls. Little did she know, her arrival in Havenbrook would lead her down a path that would challenge her ideals, forcing her to confront the depth of misunderstanding and fear that existed in places she least expected.

Mariam moved into a modest apartment on the edge of town. It was quiet, serene, and far from the noise of the city she had once known. She had high hopes for her new role as a history teacher at Havenbrook High School. As the first person of color to join the faculty, Mariam was eager to teach her students not only about history but about empathy, tolerance, and understanding.

Her first few days at the school were met with polite smiles and cautious glances. She noticed the subtle differences—how some teachers lingered just a little too long on their greetings, how some students whispered as they walked past her, and how others kept their distance, hesitant to make eye contact. It wasn’t overt racism, not the kind she had seen in more blatant forms in the past, but it was there, lurking beneath the surface.

In her classroom, Mariam made it a point to incorporate diverse perspectives. She shared stories of leaders from various cultures and backgrounds—stories of perseverance, resilience, and courage. Her students, a mix of different races and backgrounds, seemed intrigued. But there were a few students who remained distant, disconnected from the lessons she was trying to impart. Among them was Ethan, a quiet boy with sharp features and a hardened expression. He rarely spoke in class, but Mariam noticed him scribbling furiously in his notebook, his brow furrowed, as if he were struggling with something inside himself.

One afternoon, after class, Mariam noticed Ethan lingering behind, hesitant to leave. She approached him, her footsteps light and measured.

“Ethan, is everything okay?” she asked gently, her voice soft but firm.

Ethan looked up at her, his eyes briefly meeting hers before he lowered them. “I don’t understand it,” he muttered.

“Understand what?” Mariam asked, her tone warm, inviting him to speak freely.

“All this… all these different people, their stories, their cultures,” Ethan continued, his voice barely above a whisper. “Why does it matter?”

Mariam took a deep breath, choosing her words carefully. “Because understanding different cultures, different people, helps us build a more compassionate world. It helps us see the world through their eyes, not just our own.”

Ethan’s eyes hardened again. “But that’s the problem, isn’t it? People don’t care about what we go through. They only care about what they go through.”

Mariam could see the pain in his eyes. He wasn’t angry at her; he was angry at the world. He had felt left out, unheard, perhaps even forgotten. And Mariam realized that his resistance wasn’t just about racial prejudice—it was about his own struggles with identity, with being misunderstood.

“I understand that,” Mariam said quietly. “But we can’t let that make us blind to other people’s struggles.”

Ethan looked at her, his expression torn between skepticism and curiosity. He was far from convinced, but Mariam could see a flicker of something—perhaps a spark of hope, or a longing for something more.

The next few weeks were a delicate dance for Mariam. She continued to teach her students, pushing them to think beyond the textbook, to challenge their assumptions. She incorporated stories of racism and prejudice from various historical periods, showing how even small acts of bias could snowball into something much larger. She wanted her students to see that racism wasn’t just an issue for “other people” but something that affected everyone, regardless of race or background.

But despite her best efforts, Mariam couldn’t shake the feeling that something was still missing. She wasn’t sure if her students were truly understanding the lessons she was trying to teach or if they were just going through the motions, pretending to get it. The divide between them seemed to grow wider each day.

It wasn’t until one rainy afternoon in late October that Mariam realized what had been missing. She was grading papers in her classroom when Ethan entered, his usual scowl replaced with a weary expression. He had been absent for several days, and she had been wondering where he had gone.

“I need to talk to you,” he said, his voice rough.

Mariam set her papers down and gestured to the empty seat in front of her. “Of course. What’s on your mind?”

Ethan hesitated before sitting down, his hands fidgeting nervously. “I don’t know how to say this,” he began, his eyes flicking around the room, avoiding her gaze. “But I think I’ve been wrong about a lot of things. About you. About… people.”

Mariam waited, her heart beating slightly faster. She could sense that something was shifting in Ethan, something deep and significant.

“You’re right,” he continued, his voice gaining strength. “I never thought about how other people see the world. I never tried to understand them. I just thought everyone was supposed to see things the way I do. But that’s not the case, is it?”

Mariam smiled softly, grateful that he was opening up to her. “No, it’s not. We all have different perspectives. And it’s through those different perspectives that we can learn and grow.”

Ethan nodded slowly, his expression contemplative. “I’ve been angry for a long time. Angry about things that happened to me, things I couldn’t control. But I think I’ve been using that anger to shut people out. I didn’t want to see the world through their eyes, because I was too focused on my own pain.”

Mariam’s heart swelled with empathy. She had seen the anger in Ethan before, but now she understood where it was coming from. It wasn’t just about race; it was about the universal human experience of pain, of feeling misunderstood, of feeling invisible.

“It’s okay to be angry,” Mariam said gently. “But don’t let it blind you. The world is much bigger than our own experiences. And when we open ourselves up to other people’s stories, we begin to understand them—and maybe, just maybe, we begin to understand ourselves a little better.”

Ethan sat in silence for a moment, processing her words. Then he looked up at her, his expression softening. “I think I get it now.”

From that day on, things began to change in Havenbrook. Ethan became more engaged in class, asking questions, challenging ideas, and sharing his own experiences. Slowly but surely, the other students began to follow his lead. The classroom became a space for open dialogue, where difficult conversations about race, identity, and prejudice could be had without fear of judgment.

Mariam knew that the road ahead would not be easy. Racism and prejudice ran deep, not just in Havenbrook but in society as a whole. But she also knew that the first step to understanding—truly understanding—was seeing the world through the eyes of others.

As for Ethan, he had taken the first step toward healing, and Mariam was determined to help him—and all her students—continue on that path. Because in the end, it wasn’t just about teaching history; it was about teaching empathy, compassion, and the power of understanding.

Through their eyes, the world could change. And it was up to them to make that change a reality.
