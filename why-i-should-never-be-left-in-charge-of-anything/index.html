Why I Should Never Be Left in Charge of Anything


It started on a deceptively simple Monday morning. The office was buzzing with the usual frantic energy, phones ringing off the hook, and the faint smell of burnt coffee lingering in the break room. Our manager, Mr. Thompson, had a sudden emergency and needed to step out for the afternoon. Without hesitation—or perhaps without much thought—he entrusted me to oversee the entire department.

Now, I’m well aware that the title “being left in charge” often suggests trust and responsibility, but it also often foreshadows chaos when assigned to someone like me. This story isn’t just about a day in charge; it’s about a symphony of mishaps that together compose a hard-learned lesson—a testament to why I should never be left in charge.

The Early Decision: Setting the Stage for Disaster

My first task was straightforward on paper: handle the scheduling for the week. Seems easy, right? But here’s the problem—my idea of “handling scheduling” is akin to playing a game of Tetris with people’s availability.

Not all the employees submitted their availability on time, and instead of asking for clarification, I took a creative approach. I arranged shifts based on whoever I thought would be free, occasionally eyeballing the spreadsheets and guessing. Immediately, disaster was brewing. I booked Sarah for both the morning and evening shifts on Tuesday and scheduled two people for the same client meeting, oblivious to the fact that these were mutually exclusive events.

A flurry of panicked phone calls followed. “Am I supposed to be here or at the meeting?” Sarah texted frantically, while Tom called to clarify why he was scheduled at two places at once.

The scheduling chaos was the first ripple on what would soon become a tidal wave of managerial missteps.

The Forgotten Task: Missing the Critical Deadline

About an hour after tackling the schedule, I realized Mr. Thompson had mentioned something crucial about submitting a monthly report by 4 pm. The report was a compilation of sales numbers, client feedback, and staffing summaries. Under normal circumstances, this wouldn’t be a big deal.

Except I didn’t ask where to find the files and assumed they were all updated in the shared folder named “Monthly Reports” — which, as it turns out, did not include the latest client feedback. In fact, that feedback remained in an unattended email thread in Mr. Thompson’s inbox.

Despite my frantic clicking, shuffling, and searching, I couldn’t locate the client data. The deadline came and went, and I had to submit the “best possible” version of the report — minus the most important feedback segment.

The email that came minutes later from the regional manager made it clear the report was thoroughly incomplete. Somehow, I had failed not just to uphold standards but to even understand the critical content necessary for successful reporting.

The Meeting Catastrophe: Talking Without Knowing

Next on the list was stepping into a video conference with a key client. This was perhaps the most significant mistake of the day.

Mr. Thompson had assured me the client meeting was “just a casual check-in” and that I’d only need to take notes. However, the instructions vanished in the morning chaos, and I arrived wholly unprepared—and, worse, completely uninformed about the client’s current project status.

As soon as the meeting began, I realized I didn’t know a single update, hadn’t reviewed the relevant file, and couldn’t answer the client’s direct question: “How are we progressing with the system integration?”

In a desperate attempt to stall, I asked the client if they could send over a summary report after the call. Unsurprisingly, this didn’t inspire confidence. The client’s politely strained patience was exhausting, and by the time the meeting adjourned, I was crunching on the bitter feeling of professional embarrassment.

The Coffee Machine Incident: Small Disaster, Big Mess

After the disaster on the conference call, I needed a pick-me-up. The break room was deserted, and it seemed like a perfect moment to refresh with a cup of coffee.

Unluckily for everyone, I had no idea how to operate the increasingly complex office coffee machine—one equipped with a touchscreen, multiple brew options, and a propensity for errors when touched incorrectly.

I opted for the simple “Black Coffee” button and waited a few moments. Suddenly, the machine started hissing loudly and sparking slightly near the water compartment.

Panicking, I unplugged it. Unfortunately, coffee grounds, water, and steam sprayed out, flooding the counter and the floor—creating a sticky, hazardous mess. While scrambling for paper towels, I accidentally knocked over a potted plant, which crashed near the litter of wet wires.

Some coworkers came in mid-cleanup and gave me looks that ranged from sympathetic to outright amused—more or less as if I’d found a new way to break an already fractured day.

The Email Fiasco: Sending Before Thinking

In an effort to regain some semblance of control, I decided to send a reassuring email to the team, acknowledging the day’s confusion and expressing confidence in finishing strong.

The problem? I hit “Reply All” on a forwarded email containing some not-so-flattering comments about the client’s last project update. Meant for a private vent to a friend, the snarky remark about “how could they possibly miss so many key points?” suddenly broadcast to not only the team but the client’s entire organization.

Within minutes, I got a notification about the email being recalled, followed by a wave of worried internal messages. Damage control was necessary—and it fell squarely on my shoulders.

The Aftermath: Reflecting on Why I Should Never Be Left in Charge

By the time Mr. Thompson returned late afternoon, it was clear the department had been through a chaotic day of scheduling errors, missed deadlines, shaky client relations, messy coffee breaches, and ill-advised communications.

He took one look at the chronicles I sheepishly narrated and sighed—not in anger, but in the resigned exasperation of someone who’s seen it all before.

“I think we’ve learned one thing today,” he said hesitantly, “You should never be left in charge.”

At first, I felt defeated and ashamed, but with a little distance, I recognized the unexpected gift in the day’s disasters. Managing isn’t just about being responsible; it’s about understanding context, prior knowledge, communication, and a bit of grace under pressure.

Most important, it’s about knowing your limits.

Closing Thoughts: Embracing My Role (with Boundaries)

This story, a personal account of a day in charge gone wildly off track, isn’t meant as a confession of incompetence but rather as a laugh-and-learn lesson for anyone who ever found themselves unexpectedly responsible.

Whether you’re managing a team, organizing a project, or even just leading a coffee break, understanding the importance—and pitfalls—of responsibility can save not only the day but your sanity.

As for me? I’m more than happy to be a valued team member, contributor, and occasional snack-provider. But being left in charge? That’s an adventure best left unopened.

If you ever find yourself in charge suddenly—take a breath, ask for help, and maybe keep a fire extinguisher close. Because, as I learned the hard way, some people really should never be left in charge of anything.
