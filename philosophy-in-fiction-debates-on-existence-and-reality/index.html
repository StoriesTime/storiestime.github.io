Philosophy in Fiction: Debates on Existence and Reality


The rain tapped lightly against the window, a soft rhythm that seemed almost too perfect for the silence of the room. The university library was always quiet at this hour, a sanctuary for the few students who still spent their nights buried in books. Among them sat Arthur Gale, a philosopher by training but a storyteller by passion. His fingers drummed restlessly against the worn pages of a thick tome on metaphysics, his mind too occupied to focus on the text.

For days, he had been wrestling with a question that had plagued him for as long as he could remember: What is reality?

He had spent years in academic halls discussing the great philosophers—Descartes, Kant, Hume—and yet none of their answers seemed to capture the true essence of what reality was. He had entertained the possibility of solipsism, the idea that only the self could be known to exist. Perhaps everything around him—the books, the people, the world—was just a figment of his imagination. But there were consequences to thinking this way. The more he considered it, the more his thoughts began to spiral. The line between what was real and what was imagined blurred, and it frightened him.

Arthur wasn’t the only one who had pondered this question. All throughout history, the question of existence and reality had been a central theme in philosophy and literature. From Plato’s allegory of the cave to modern works like The Matrix, humanity had continually questioned the nature of the world it inhabited. Was the world a mere illusion? Was it constructed by our perceptions? Or did something more fundamental underpin everything we experience?

Tonight, however, Arthur was on the brink of something new. Something personal. Something that might not just change his academic career but his life.

Part 2: A Stranger’s Proposition

Just as Arthur’s mind seemed to reach a breaking point, the door to the library opened, and a figure stepped in. The man was tall, dressed in a long black coat and a fedora, his face partially obscured by shadows. He approached Arthur’s table with an unusual confidence, the soft sound of his boots on the wooden floor cutting through the silence.

“You look lost in thought,” the man said, his voice smooth, almost melodic. “May I?”

Arthur looked up, startled. The stranger’s presence felt… strange, out of place even. He nodded slowly, unsure of why he didn’t feel inclined to ask the man to leave.

“You’re a philosopher, aren’t you?” the man asked, sitting across from him without waiting for a response. “I can tell by the book you’re holding. You have that look—intense, yet disconnected from the world.”

Arthur set the book down, intrigued despite himself. “Yes, I am. And you…?”

The man smiled slightly, his lips curling into something almost like an enigmatic grin. “Let’s just say I’m someone who knows more about your question than you think.”

Arthur raised an eyebrow. “What question?”

The stranger leaned forward, his eyes locking with Arthur’s. “What is real? What is existence? You’ve been asking it for years, haven’t you?”

Arthur felt a shiver run down his spine. How could this stranger know so much about him? It was as if he had been reading his thoughts.

“I—” Arthur stammered. “I’ve been reading… and thinking, yes. I can’t seem to find the right answer.”

The man’s eyes glinted with something like amusement. “Perhaps that’s because you’re asking the wrong question.”

Arthur leaned back, more confused than ever. “What do you mean?”

The man’s smile deepened. “Reality is not a question to be answered. It is something to be experienced, something to be created. The very fact that you are debating it means that you’re already living in a world that is malleable. In your mind, everything is fluid.”

Arthur frowned. This was getting more abstract by the second. “Are you saying that reality isn’t real?”

The man nodded slowly, his voice dropping to a near whisper. “Reality is exactly as real as you believe it to be. You can shape it, bend it, and change it. The only thing that truly exists is the perception of it. The moment you realize this, you can transcend the limits of existence itself.”

Arthur’s head spun. “Are you telling me that everything is just… an illusion?”

“Not an illusion,” the man corrected gently. “An interpretation. The world is a canvas, and your mind is the brush. How you see it, how you experience it, is entirely up to you.”

Arthur’s mind raced. “But… but how can that be? If everyone sees the world differently, doesn’t that mean no one can agree on what’s real? Does that mean we’re all trapped in our own subjective realities?”

The man’s eyes gleamed with excitement. “Exactly. That is the key. Your reality is shaped by your experiences, your perceptions, your beliefs. But it doesn’t end there. What if I told you that you could enter someone else’s reality? Live in their world for a time? What if I told you that the very boundaries between individual perceptions could be crossed?”

Arthur’s heart skipped a beat. “How… how could that be possible?”

The man smiled, his gaze unwavering. “Come with me, and I’ll show you.”

Part 3: Into the Otherworld

Arthur followed the man out of the library, feeling like he was walking in a dream. They exited the university and moved down the winding streets, the rain now falling harder, blurring the lights of the city into a watery haze. They stopped in front of an old, unmarked building. The man pushed open the door, revealing a dimly lit room filled with books, strange objects, and a large, intricate machine in the center of the space.

“What is this?” Arthur asked, his voice trembling with curiosity.

The man gestured toward the machine. “This is where perception becomes reality. This is the place where the boundaries of the mind can be crossed.”

Arthur’s eyes widened as he stared at the machine. “Are you saying this can change reality?”

“In a way,” the man said, his tone still cryptic. “It can connect you to another person’s consciousness. You will experience the world through their eyes, live their life, see their version of reality. But be warned—once you step inside, you might never want to leave. You will lose yourself in someone else’s reality.”

Arthur hesitated. This was absurd, impossible. And yet… something about the man’s words felt true. “What’s the catch?”

The stranger smiled again, that same knowing smile. “The catch is that you may not return the same. Reality, once experienced through another’s mind, can alter your own perception forever. Are you willing to take that risk?”

Arthur stared at the machine, the hum of its energy growing louder in his ears. The temptation was overwhelming. The idea of stepping outside his own perception of reality, of understanding the world from someone else’s point of view—it was too compelling to ignore.

“Yes,” Arthur said, his voice steady, though his heart raced. “I’ll do it.”

Part 4: A New Reality

The machine’s hum became a roar as Arthur stepped into the chamber, the stranger standing just outside the door, watching him intently. The walls seemed to pulse with light as he was strapped into the seat, the machine’s various components whirring into life around him.

“This is the moment of transformation,” the man’s voice echoed in his ears. “Open your mind, and let it unfold.”

And then, the world vanished.

Arthur found himself in a different place entirely. The surroundings were unfamiliar—a sprawling meadow under a deep blue sky, mountains in the distance, a soft breeze rustling the grass. He could feel the ground beneath him, the warmth of the sun on his face. He turned, and there, standing in front of him, was a young woman, her eyes wide with surprise.

She smiled gently, as if she had been waiting for him. “You’re here,” she said softly, her voice comforting.

Arthur blinked, confused. “Where am I?”

The woman chuckled, her voice echoing through his mind. “You’re in my world. My reality.”

His breath caught in his throat. “You… you are me?”

She nodded. “In a way. But also, I am not. I am someone else’s perception of reality. Just as you are mine. We are connected now. For as long as you remain here, you will experience life as I do. You will see what I see, feel what I feel.”

Arthur’s mind raced as he tried to process everything. The sun. The wind. The woman. None of it made sense. And yet, it all felt so… real.

But was it real? Was this just another illusion? Or was this a truth that had eluded him for so long?

He didn’t know the answer. But in that moment, standing in the middle of an unfamiliar world, he realized something important: Reality was not just a question of existence, but of experience.

Part 5: The Boundaries of Mind

As the days passed, Arthur lived within the woman’s world, seeing through her eyes, experiencing her reality. He learned what it was like to live as she did, to feel the things she felt. It was both illuminating and disorienting, the borders of his own identity slowly dissolving.

But as he grew more entwined with her mind, he began to sense a deeper truth. Reality was fluid. It was a construct, a product of perception. And in the end, there was no one true reality. There were only the realities that individuals crafted for themselves.

When the time came for him to return, the machine’s hum faded, and the world around him shifted. He stood once again in the library, the rain still falling softly outside.

The stranger was gone.

Arthur sat in silence for a long time, the weight of his experience settling in. He knew now that reality was not something to be debated, but something to be lived, to be experienced. Each person, each mind, shaped their own world, their own truth. And perhaps, just perhaps, that was the most profound answer to the question that had haunted him for so long.

In the end, Arthur realized that to live was to create. To exist was to perceive. And in that understanding, reality was no longer a puzzle to be solved, but a mystery to be embraced.
