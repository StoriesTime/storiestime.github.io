Sculpting Silence: The Art of Finding Your Voice

Ask me to describe the sound of silence, and I'll tell you it's like a whispered secret only the wind knows. But, one day, I found myself stood at the edge of that secret, hands trembling with anticipation, before pen scratched against paper, releasing a murmur into existence. My name is Akira, and I'm a luthier of sorts, a craftsman of silence. Not the kind who shuns sound, but the kind who weaves it into existence. My workshop is a modest space, tucked away in a quiet neighborhood where the air is thick with the scent of freshly brewed coffee and the distant hum of traffic. It may seem an odd place for a musician, but for me, it's a sanctuary. Here, amidst wood shavings and abandoned instruments, I carefully craft each piece, coaxing the essence of silence from the material. My focus isn't on the volume of sound, but on its texture, its feel, its essence. Six seasons had passed since I lost my voice. No, not the voice you might think of – a spoken word or a loud scream. Mine was different. It was the silence that accompanied me to bed at night, stayed by my side throughout the day, and watched me struggle to create anything meaningful. I'd try to gather words, but they would falter and fade away like sandcastles in the rain. It was as if my words had been hijacked by some mischievous spirit, taking turns with me, just to watch me squirm. One day, a friend suggested I try instrumental music, hoping it would help me find my voice again. She handed me an old lute, worn from history, its wooden parts abused by many fingers long ago. At first, I laughed – how could an instrument restore my ability to speak? But something within me – maybe the stillness of the day, the beat of the luthier's hands behind me as I rested against the workbench – nudged me to give it a try. I strummed the lute, allowing its notes to hang in the air like whispers between lovers. Those sounds sent tremors through the quiet, reminding me of my original love, almost lost. That simple act set me thinking: Silence, like my lost voice, could be manipulated, shaped, and creator. I learned to listen, really listen – to silence. The way it transports you to faraway places, gently turning your head, and abandoning your thoughts. I realized that my voice wasn't just hearing or speaking; it was tactile. It was in the sensation of the lute humming below my fingertips, coaxing subtle vibrations into harmony. My eyes were opened to the music hidden within the stillness – dissonant whispers, comforting softspoken messages carried by every unit of time. Essentially, I discovered that I could feel the spoken words I wanted, the very ones that had been playing hide and seek in my mind for years. My style took a drastic turn; then, instruments started reminding people of shapes and shades of the unspoken melodies exhaling silence into wider echoes. She began to incorporate them into her art, weaving together the disparate threads of history, mythology, and personal experience.
