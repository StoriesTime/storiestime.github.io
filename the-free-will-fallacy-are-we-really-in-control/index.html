The Free Will Fallacy: Are We Really In Control?


Evelyn sat at her kitchen table, watching the steam rise from her morning coffee. The world outside was quiet, as it always was before the city awoke fully. The clock on the wall ticked in steady rhythm, its second hand moving with a precision that matched the way the rest of her life seemed to unfold. Every morning felt like the last one. Every action felt like it had been rehearsed a thousand times before.

But today was different. Today, Evelyn was going to question something that had always been certain in her mind: free will.

She had spent years in a job that was not quite fulfilling, but not quite terrible either. She had followed the expected steps—graduating college, climbing the career ladder, and finding herself in a stable, yet uninspiring life. But was she really in control of any of it? The more she thought about it, the more it seemed as though each choice was influenced by something beyond her understanding.

The thought nagged her for hours. Was her decision to go to college her own? Or had it been subtly guided by her parents’ expectations? Was the career she had chosen a result of her own passion or merely the product of societal pressures? Did she have the power to truly choose, or was she simply a puppet controlled by invisible strings?

Chapter 2: The Illusion of Choice

That evening, Evelyn met with Tom, an old friend from college. They had kept in touch over the years, often chatting about life and the paths they had taken. Tom was a philosopher, someone who seemed to always be questioning the world around him. His office was filled with books on existentialism, determinism, and metaphysics, topics that Evelyn had never fully understood. But tonight, she was determined to get answers.

“Tom,” Evelyn began, stirring her drink absently, “do you think we really have free will?”

Tom looked up from his notebook, where he had been scribbling something about causality. “That’s a big question,” he said, setting the pen down. “But I think it depends on how you define free will.”

Evelyn raised an eyebrow. “What do you mean?”

“I mean, most people think of free will as the ability to make choices independent of any external influences. But is that really possible? Every choice we make is influenced by something, whether it’s our upbringing, our genetics, or even the environment we’re in. What we think of as ‘free’ might not be free at all.”

Evelyn leaned forward, intrigued. “So you’re saying that everything we do is predetermined?”

“Not exactly,” Tom replied, “but think about it this way: every decision you make is the result of a series of causes that you may not even be aware of. Your desires, your fears, your thoughts—they all stem from things outside your control. And those things shape the decisions you make. It’s like a river flowing downhill. You might feel like you’re steering the boat, but the current is doing most of the work.”

Evelyn sat back, processing this. She had always believed in the power of choice, that she was the captain of her own ship. But now, that belief felt flimsy, like a bubble about to burst.

Chapter 3: The Ghosts of Influence

The following week, Evelyn decided to experiment. She had been on autopilot for so long, following the same routines day after day, that she wanted to see if she could make a choice that wasn’t shaped by external forces.

She woke up early one morning, before the sun had fully risen, and made a decision: she was going to change her route to work. Instead of the usual highway, she took the side streets. It was a small act, one that seemed insignificant, but as she drove through unfamiliar neighborhoods, she noticed something strange. The world around her seemed to feel different. The houses, the trees, even the air—everything seemed to be influencing her thoughts, pulling her in subtle directions.

When she arrived at work, she felt oddly out of place. The decision to change her route had felt empowering in theory, but in practice, it had only heightened her sense of being controlled. The world had adjusted, reshaping her thoughts and actions in ways she couldn’t quite articulate. It was as though she was still following an invisible map, a map that she had no hand in creating.

Later that day, Evelyn had a conversation with her coworker, Rachel. They were talking about an upcoming project, one that Evelyn had been eagerly anticipating for weeks. But when Rachel brought up a potential issue with the project’s timeline, Evelyn realized something unsettling: she didn’t care anymore. What had once been an exciting challenge now felt like an obligation. She hadn’t chosen this path—it had chosen her.

“Do you ever feel like you’re just following a script?” Evelyn asked, her voice quieter than usual.

Rachel chuckled, but Evelyn could hear the hint of discomfort in her tone. “Yeah, I think we all do. But what can we do about it? We have to keep moving forward, right?”

Evelyn nodded, but inside, a deep uncertainty was stirring. Was this all she was capable of? Moving from one predetermined task to the next, with no real agency of her own?

Chapter 4: The Science of Control

Evelyn’s curiosity about free will grew into an obsession. She started reading articles, watching documentaries, and even attending lectures on the subject. The more she learned, the more the idea of free will seemed like an illusion, a comforting fiction we told ourselves to feel in control.

Neuroscientists, she discovered, had been studying the brain for years and found that many of the decisions we believe we make consciously are actually initiated by unconscious processes. Brain scans showed that our brains begin to activate and form intentions before we’re even aware of them. In fact, some studies suggested that the feeling of making a conscious choice was simply the brain’s way of rationalizing decisions that had already been made.

Evelyn watched a TED talk by a neuroscientist who explained that the human brain was constantly processing information, filtering out stimuli, and making decisions on a subconscious level. What we perceived as “free” choices were, in reality, the result of complex patterns in our brain that were shaped by our environment, experiences, and biology. The idea of pure autonomy, the ability to make a decision completely free of influence, seemed more and more unlikely.

“The notion of free will is comforting,” the neuroscientist said in the talk. “But it may be little more than a coping mechanism. A way for us to believe that we’re in control when, in fact, we’re not.”

Evelyn sat in silence, the weight of the words sinking in. What if everything she had done in her life, every decision, every moment of success or failure, had been driven by factors she couldn’t even comprehend? What if the person she thought she was—this individual with autonomy and purpose—was nothing more than a construct?

Chapter 5: The Acceptance

In the weeks that followed, Evelyn began to see her life in a new light. She became more aware of the subtle forces at play in her decisions. The expectations of her family, the influence of her friends, the cultural norms that shaped her actions—all of it became clear. She was not as in control as she had once believed.

Yet, this realization did not bring despair. In fact, it had the opposite effect. For the first time, Evelyn felt a sense of peace. She was no longer burdened by the idea that she had to control everything. Life, with all its unpredictability and complexity, had its own rhythm. She could stop trying to force outcomes, stop trying to make everything fit into her narrow vision of success.

Instead, Evelyn began to embrace the flow of life, accepting that while she may not have control over every detail, she could still choose how to respond to the circumstances around her. In that sense, she had power—power to adapt, to learn, and to experience life as it came.

The fallacy of free will had not broken her. It had liberated her.

Chapter 6: The Question Lives On

Even as Evelyn found peace with her new understanding, the question of free will remained. It was a question that might never have a definitive answer. But the journey toward understanding had been transformative.

She no longer sought to control every aspect of her life. Instead, she welcomed the unknown, trusting that even if she wasn’t always in control, she could still make meaning in the choices that truly mattered. And in that, perhaps, lay the truest form of freedom.

The end.
