The Last Stroke: Wrestling with Self-Doubt as an Artist


Each morning, Eloise sat in the corner of her cramped studio, staring at the half-painted canvas that leaned against her easel like a bruised promise. The outline of a woman’s face stared back at her, trapped in unfinished strokes. Her brush sat stiff in her hand, an inert thing unwilling to move, unwilling to bring her vision to life. It had been weeks since Eloise completed anything, months since she felt proud of her work, and years since she felt like any of it mattered.

The studio was dim, the light filtering through the narrow window casting streaks of gray across the scuffed wooden floor. The smell of turpentine lingered, mingling with a faint hint of cigarette smoke from the tiny café below her apartment. Eloise had once loved that aroma, associating it with nights of creative fervor when her imagination poured forth like an unstoppable torrent. Now, it only reminded her of the abyss she seemed to be circling.

The woman on the canvas—a nebulous idea, not yet a person—was meant to represent strength. The curve of her jaw and the intensity of her eyes were supposed to radiate resilience. Eloise had envisioned her as a mirror of her own inner struggles, a declaration of survival etched in pigment. But as Eloise glanced at the incomplete portrait, all she could see was betrayal: by herself, her talent, and her increasingly tortured dreams.

It hadn’t always been like this. Eloise had, for as long as she could remember, been obsessed with color, texture, and the intoxicating freedom of creation. As a child, she used broken crayons to turn the walls of her bedroom into swirling murals. In university, she was the star of every gallery showcase, her pieces leaving people mesmerized. Critics described her work as raw, electrifying, sometimes unsettling—but always unforgettable. She felt invincible then, as though she could pull galaxies out of her own fingertips.

But the world ground her down with the slow weight of expectation. After her third solo exhibition, her agent had urged her to move away from her usual themes. “Darker pieces don’t sell as well,” she had said, her voice clipped and businesslike. “Clients want beauty, not bleakness.” Eloise acquiesced, replacing her jagged edges with softer lines, trading her shadows for paler hues. Her paintings sold, but her satisfaction waned as her art started feeling less and less like her own.

The pressure to meet deadlines, to sell, and to please others grew into something insidious. Bit by bit, self-doubt began gnawing at the foundation of Eloise’s confidence. It started as a whisper—Are you really any good?—and grew into a roar—Everything you make is a sham. Her broken nights stretched into years of tortured perfectionism. She would start a piece with hope and abandon it halfway through, paralyzed by the imaginary judgment of critics and collectors who hadn’t yet seen it. Eloise had lost count of how many canvases lingered in her studio, haunting her like forgotten ghosts.

This was how Eloise felt as she stared at the woman on the canvas. She shouldn’t be here, she thought bitterly, stuffing her unused brush into the cup filled with murky water. Maybe the studio itself was the problem. She had once found solace and creativity in its crammed space, but now it felt suffocating, cluttered with fragments of dreams she barely remembered. Eloise decided she needed air.

She stepped into the narrow street outside her building, her sneakers slapping lightly against the cobblestone. The early morning smelled of rain that hadn’t yet come, and strangers drifted past her on bicycles with absent smiles. Eloise didn’t know where she was going. Her feet led her aimlessly away, as if they had intentions she couldn’t quite discern.

It was by accident that Eloise stumbled upon the gallery. A chalkboard outside advertised a new exhibition entitled Untamed Edges. The title caught her attention. Through the window, she could see bold swirls of color spread across the walls, brushing up against jagged geometric shapes. Her curiosity tugged at her, and before she could think twice, she found herself inside.

The gallery was small and quiet, but alive with emotion. Every painting vibrated with raw energy—anguish, joy, chaos—all of it spilling out unrelentingly. Eloise approached a piece that stopped her cold. It was messy, almost defiantly so: an explosion of thick, uneven strokes and violent splashes of red and black. The artist’s bio described them as someone “dedicated to painting the truth they’ve never been able to speak.”

Staring at that painting, Eloise felt something stir. It wasn’t jealousy; that would have been an easy, familiar feeling. It was recognition, deep and unyielding. These pieces hadn’t been created for an audience; they weren’t polished with the intention to sell. They had been forged in the crucible of raw emotion, stripped bare of pretense. Eloise felt herself swallowed by the bravery of it.

When she returned to her studio, Eloise didn’t sit in her usual corner. Instead, she stood in the center of the room, letting her gaze travel over every unfinished painting littered across the space. She felt ashamed, but not of the work itself—ashamed of the fear that had tethered her hands and twisted her purpose. The fear of disappointing others, of failing, of not being enough. Slowly, she walked to the easel holding the half-painted canvas of the woman.

Eloise picked up her brush and dipped it into a bright yellow, letting the bristles absorb the color. She lifted her arm, and in one motion, she placed a blazing stroke across the woman’s face, dragging it from forehead to chin. That single gesture set her free. It didn’t matter now, Eloise realized, whether the painting was beautiful or sellable; what mattered was that she had made the choice to keep going.

Moving with renewed purpose, Eloise abandoned her caution. She spat defiance into every gesture, throwing colors and textures together like reckless declarations. Her strokes were bold, toppling the precision she’d once clung to. The woman’s face transformed before her, becoming fragmented and intense, her strength spilling out in jagged lines and bursts of color.

For hours, Eloise painted. The final stroke came at dusk, when the light was too dim for her to see clearly anymore. She cleaned her brush with steady hands and took a step back. Instead of doubt, she felt gratitude—a fierce appreciation for every misstep that had led her here. The canvas felt alive. The woman stared back at her, no longer a nebulous idea but a force. She embodied not perfection, but perseverance.

Eloise wasn’t magically healed of self-doubt that day, of course—those feelings are persistent, creeping into even the most triumphal moments. But she learned something valuable: art wasn’t about erasing insecurity, but about creating in spite of it. Creativity is a constant battle. And sometimes, victory lies in the courage to make that last, imperfect stroke.
