The Price of Bias: Confronting Everyday Racism


In the heart of a bustling city, amidst the clatter of trains and the hum of conversations, there was a small café that felt like a world of its own. It was the kind of place where people came to escape the chaos of their lives, sip their coffee, and lose themselves in their thoughts. Tucked in a corner of the café was a woman named Amina, her fingers nervously tracing the rim of her mug. Amina had lived in this city her whole life, but today, as she sat alone, she felt more like an outsider than ever before.

It wasn’t the first time she had felt this way. Amina had long been accustomed to the subtle glances, the quiet whispers that followed her as she walked down the street, the feeling that she wasn’t quite enough—not quite like everyone else. She had grown up in a neighborhood where the majority of people were from her ethnic background. But as she moved to the city for work, she encountered something she hadn’t expected: a kind of casual racism that seeped into every interaction, every glance.

In the workplace, it was the questions about her “exotic” heritage. “Where are you really from?” they asked, as though her answer could never be the city she had been born and raised in. In the grocery store, it was the slightly too long look from the cashier, who barely smiled at her and quickly moved on to the next customer. At the café, it was the woman behind the counter, who hesitated before taking Amina’s order, giving her an awkward smile that didn’t quite reach her eyes. It was all so small, so subtle—but it was real. And it hurt.

But Amina wasn’t the type to let things slide. She had grown up with strong values from her family, who had taught her to fight for what she believed in, to stand up for herself and others. And today, she had decided she would confront it. She wasn’t sure how or where to begin, but she knew that it was time.

Her moment arrived sooner than expected.

As Amina was getting up to leave the café, she noticed a young man standing at the counter, his head lowered, eyes focused on his phone. He looked familiar. It was James, a colleague from her office. He worked in the marketing department and was one of the few people who had never made her feel like she didn’t belong. Amina appreciated him for that, though she had never said so.

She hesitated for a moment, wondering if it was too forward to speak to him, but then, almost instinctively, she walked over. “James?” she said, offering a small smile.

He looked up, his face lighting up with recognition. “Amina! Hey, how’s it going?”

“I’m good, thank you. But, um, I wanted to ask you something,” she said, feeling the familiar nervousness creep in. “Have you ever noticed… that sometimes people treat us differently?”

James blinked, caught off guard. “What do you mean?”

Amina sighed, her gaze drifting down to the floor for a brief moment. “You know, because of our backgrounds, our ethnicity. The way people look at us, like we don’t belong.”

For a moment, James didn’t respond. He just stood there, his brow furrowing as he seemed to process her words. “I guess… I don’t really think about it,” he said slowly. “I mean, I’ve never felt that kind of thing with you.”

Amina nodded, a faint smile tugging at the corners of her mouth. “I know. And I appreciate that. But I think sometimes people don’t realize that their biases are there, even when they don’t mean to show them. It’s like… it’s in the air, you know? Like it’s part of the culture.”

James opened his mouth to speak, then paused, clearly searching for the right words. “I see what you mean. It’s like… unconscious bias, right?”

“Exactly,” Amina said, grateful that he understood. “But the thing is, it’s not just something that happens in a few isolated moments. It’s everywhere. And it builds up. It’s exhausting.”

They both stood there for a moment in silence, the noise of the café around them fading into the background.

“Is there anything we can do about it?” James asked, his voice quiet but sincere.

Amina took a deep breath, feeling the weight of her question. “I think… we have to start by talking about it. By recognizing it. Not just when it’s obvious, but when it’s subtle. People don’t even realize the impact of the little things, like the tone of voice, the assumptions, or the way they treat people who don’t look like them.”

James nodded slowly, his expression thoughtful. “Yeah. I’ve seen it before. People say things without thinking. I guess I never really understood how it affected you.”

Amina smiled at him, feeling a sense of relief wash over her. “And that’s the thing. It’s not about blaming anyone. It’s about recognizing the patterns and taking responsibility for our own actions.”

“Well,” James said with a small laugh, “I’m glad you brought it up. I’m definitely going to be more mindful from now on.”

Amina’s heart felt lighter as she finished her coffee. The conversation with James had been a small victory. It hadn’t solved everything, but it had opened the door for something bigger—awareness, understanding, and most importantly, change. The truth was, bias was everywhere, embedded in the fabric of society. But that didn’t mean it had to stay there. It didn’t mean people had to accept it. The price of bias was too high, and confronting it wasn’t just necessary—it was essential.

As Amina left the café, she noticed the woman behind the counter again. This time, instead of the usual awkwardness, there was a small but genuine smile. Amina smiled back, a flicker of hope lighting up her chest. Maybe, just maybe, things were starting to change.

The conversation with James was just the beginning. Over the weeks that followed, Amina made a conscious effort to engage with the people around her, to talk about race and bias in a way that was both direct and compassionate. She found that many people didn’t even realize they were carrying biases, that they had been taught to see the world in a certain way, to judge others based on superficial traits. But when given the chance to reflect, many of them were willing to listen, to change.

It wasn’t easy, and not every conversation went as smoothly as the one with James. There were moments when people became defensive, when they resisted the idea that their actions or words could be harmful. But Amina had learned not to shy away from those moments. It wasn’t about being perfect—it was about progress. And little by little, she could see the world around her shifting.

One afternoon, while attending a company-wide meeting, Amina found herself seated next to her manager, Sarah. Sarah was a well-meaning woman, someone who always tried to be inclusive but often missed the mark. Amina had noticed this over the years, the way Sarah would make offhand comments about people from different cultures, unaware of how hurtful they could be.

As the meeting progressed, Amina found herself growing more and more uncomfortable with the way Sarah was speaking about a colleague who had just returned from a business trip abroad. The comments were innocuous enough on the surface, but Amina could hear the underlying tone of judgment. She could feel the bias creeping through the words.

After the meeting, Amina pulled Sarah aside, her heart racing. “Sarah, can I talk to you for a minute?”

“Of course!” Sarah said, smiling brightly, though Amina could tell she wasn’t expecting anything serious.

“I just wanted to mention something from the meeting earlier,” Amina began. “You made a comment about… about Arjun, the guy who just came back from India. And I think you may have unintentionally made him feel uncomfortable.”

Sarah’s face shifted, her smile faltering. “What do you mean?”

“I know you didn’t mean any harm, but sometimes the way we talk about people from different backgrounds can come across as judgmental, even if we don’t mean it that way. It’s something I’ve noticed a lot, and I just wanted to share it with you because I think we can all do better.”

Sarah looked taken aback for a moment, her eyes narrowing slightly as she processed Amina’s words. “I… I didn’t realize. I just thought I was being friendly.”

Amina smiled gently, sensing Sarah’s defensiveness but also her willingness to learn. “It’s okay. It happens to all of us, but it’s important that we stay aware of it. Bias is something we all carry, whether we realize it or not. But it’s up to us to actively try to confront it.”

Sarah nodded slowly, her expression thoughtful. “You’re right. I’ll be more mindful of what I say from now on.”

Amina felt a small sense of triumph. She knew the conversation wouldn’t erase all the biases in the workplace overnight, but it was a start. And sometimes, that was all it took. One conversation at a time. One person willing to listen. One person willing to change.

In the months that followed, Amina continued to have these difficult but necessary conversations. She spoke to friends, family, colleagues—anyone who would listen. Slowly but surely, the ripple effect began. The more people talked about bias and racism, the more they realized how deeply ingrained it was in the fabric of society. The more they acknowledged it, the more they could begin to dismantle it.

It wasn’t about erasing differences—it was about accepting them. It wasn’t about pretending racism didn’t exist—it was about confronting it head-on. It was about creating a world where everyone, regardless of their background, could walk into a room and feel truly seen and heard.

Amina knew it wouldn’t be easy, and she knew that progress would be slow. But she also knew that the price of bias was too high to ignore, and that the only way to confront it was through courage, empathy, and action. The battle against everyday racism would be a long one, but it was a fight worth fighting—one conversation, one person, and one small change at a time.
