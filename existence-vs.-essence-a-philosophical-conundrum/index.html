Existence vs. Essence: A Philosophical Conundrum


In the heart of a bustling city, under the shade of towering skyscrapers, nestled between old coffee shops and modern tech hubs, was a modest bookshop that seemed to exist outside of time. It was a place where the clamor of the outside world faded, and the only sound was the soft rustle of pages turning. The bookshop, though small, had the power to transport anyone who entered into a realm where ideas came alive.

In the back of this shop, nestled in a corner filled with old leather-bound volumes and dusty tomes, sat a man who had become a fixture in the shop. His name was Elias. He wasn’t particularly remarkable in appearance: a slight figure, with dark, contemplative eyes, and a beard that had started to turn grey with the passing years. Yet, Elias was far from ordinary. He was, in fact, a seeker—a philosopher of sorts, driven by the age-old question that had perplexed humanity for centuries: “What comes first—existence, or essence?”

It was in this very bookshop that Elias had spent most of his life contemplating the great mysteries of the universe. He wasn’t just interested in abstract concepts; he yearned to understand how these concepts played out in the very fabric of life. He believed that somewhere between existence and essence lay the truth that could unlock the meaning of life itself.

One cold evening, as the sun dipped below the horizon, casting a golden glow over the dusty shelves of the bookshop, a visitor walked in. She was a young woman, perhaps in her early twenties, with an air of quiet curiosity that seemed to draw people in. Her name was Aria. She had wandered into the bookshop after hearing of it from a friend, though she wasn’t quite sure what she was looking for.

Elias, who had been leafing through an old volume of metaphysical treatises, looked up and saw her standing at the entrance, gazing at the spines of the books. He had seen many people come and go over the years, but something about her presence stirred something in him. He stood up slowly and approached her.

“May I help you find something?” he asked, his voice gentle, yet filled with an unmistakable depth.

Aria turned, surprised at the interruption, but then smiled. “I’m not sure. I’ve heard this place is different from the usual bookstores. I’m looking for something… thought-provoking.”

Elias nodded, a faint smile appearing on his lips. “You’ve come to the right place. But I warn you, the books here might not provide the answers you seek. They might just lead you deeper into the questions.”

Aria’s curiosity deepened. “That sounds intriguing. What kinds of questions?”

Elias paused, as though measuring his words. “The questions that have haunted thinkers for millennia. For instance, do we exist first, and then define who we are? Or do we have an essence that defines our existence?”

Aria’s brow furrowed in thought. “I’ve never really thought about it that way. Isn’t the question of ‘who we are’ more important than ‘whether we exist’?”

Elias chuckled softly, gesturing for her to follow him to a table at the back of the shop. He pulled out a few books, each more worn than the last, each filled with the thoughts of philosophers who had wrestled with the very question Aria had just posed.

“Let me show you something,” he said, opening one of the books to a passage by Jean-Paul Sartre. “Sartre argued that existence precedes essence. We come into the world first, as empty beings, and only then do we define who we are. The essence—our identity—comes later. But what if it’s the other way around? What if our essence determines our existence?”

Aria sat down, intrigued. She had never really considered such an idea. She had always believed in the traditional narrative that existence was the first step. After all, she existed in the world, didn’t she? But as Elias continued to speak, she began to realize how deeply rooted her beliefs were in the concept of essence—that sense of inherent meaning that seemed to be preordained, as if it was a script that one followed without question.

“The essence of a person, according to Sartre, is not given. It is something we must create through our choices and actions. But imagine a world where essence comes before existence,” Elias continued, his voice steady and philosophical. “Where our identity is something intrinsic to our being, something that defines our actions, our thoughts, even before we step into the world.”

Aria leaned forward, her interest piqued. “So, you’re saying we could be born with a predefined essence? That we are born with an inherent purpose or meaning?”

Elias nodded slowly, his gaze intense. “Yes, exactly. In such a world, our existence is shaped by a higher essence—whether it be a divine force, fate, or some metaphysical design. We wouldn’t be wandering through life trying to define ourselves; instead, we would be fulfilling a role that is already set for us.”

Aria sat back in her chair, her mind racing. “But how do we reconcile this with free will? If our essence is predetermined, do we lose the ability to choose? Are we just puppets of fate?”

Elias smiled, a knowing look in his eyes. “That is the crux of the conundrum, isn’t it? If essence precedes existence, then yes, it might seem as though our choices are limited. But if existence precedes essence, then we are the authors of our own story, with no inherent meaning except that which we create. It’s a delicate balance—one that has sparked centuries of debate.”

Aria stood up and began pacing the small room. The idea that essence might come before existence was difficult to grasp. It clashed with everything she had been taught about personal freedom and self-determination. If she was born with a purpose, where did that leave her ability to choose? Could she truly be free if everything about her was already decided?

Elias watched her thoughtfully. “What you are grappling with is not just a philosophical question—it’s a question about your very being. The tension between existence and essence is something that everyone must face at some point in their lives. We are constantly caught between the forces that shape us and the choices we make.”

Aria stopped pacing and turned to face him. “But what if we are both? What if we exist first, but our essence is shaped by the choices we make?”

Elias’s eyes twinkled, and he nodded in approval. “Ah, that’s the beauty of philosophy. It doesn’t give us definitive answers, but it allows us to explore the space between. Perhaps we are both—the product of our existence and the creators of our essence.”

Aria smiled, feeling a sense of peace wash over her. “Maybe that’s the point—maybe the journey of finding who we are is as important as the destination.”

Elias’s smile deepened. “Exactly. And in that journey, we learn not only about the world around us but about the very essence of what it means to be human.”

As the evening wore on, Aria sat with Elias, discussing the nature of existence and essence until the last rays of sunlight had vanished. She left the bookshop that night with a new understanding, not just of the world, but of herself. She had begun to see the tension between existence and essence not as a dilemma to be solved, but as a journey to be embraced—a journey where both existence and essence intertwined, shaping and reshaping each other with every choice she made.

Elias, too, felt something shift within him. He had spent countless years pondering the great philosophical questions, but it was moments like these, when someone new arrived with fresh eyes, that reminded him why he continued his search. The journey, after all, was never about finding the answer. It was about asking the right questions and being open to the possibilities they presented.

And in that quiet, timeless bookshop, under the weight of centuries of philosophical thought, Elias and Aria continued their dialogue, forever caught in the dance between existence and essence—two forces that, perhaps, were never truly meant to be separated.

As Aria walked out into the night, the city lights twinkling above her, she felt a sense of peace. The world was no longer a place where she needed all the answers. It was a place of discovery—of constant questioning and self-reinvention. The balance between existence and essence was not a paradox to be solved. It was a truth to be lived. And that, she realized, was enough.
