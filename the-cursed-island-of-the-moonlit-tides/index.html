The Cursed Island of the Moonlit Tides


Mist undulated like restless spirits above the bronzed swells of the Eastern Sea, where the island—its name spoken in fearful hush—lay veiled beneath the shimmer of a swollen moon. No map plotted its precise location, though every seafarer in Port Argonne shared tales that the ocean itself rebelled against approaching. Ships foundered, compasses spiraled in delirium, and the tides, drawn by uncanny force, gnawed vessels down to driftwood. Yet on the eve of the Crab’s Return, four unlikely souls—each chasing a different hope—sought the haunted shores of Aldervale, known to most as the Cursed Island of the Moonlit Tides.

I. In the Wake of Broken Promises

The sloop’s hull creaked as Reddick peered over the rail, the salt wind coiling his unkempt hair. Beneath him, aged timbers flexed: his life’s savings, spent for passage on this last, desperate errand. The helmswoman, Morae, face tattooed with salt-laced legends, spat in disgust.

“We shouldn’t be here,” she muttered, tightening her grip on the wheel. “Only fools or the damned chase ghosts to Aldervale.”

Reddick didn’t answer. He’d heard the stories: moon-touched beasts prowling the thickets, drowned mariners wailing their secrets to the tides. But he remembered also a girl with eyes like storm-tossed dusk, her hand torn from his by the currents, swallowed by these very waters cycles ago. If there was even a sliver of truth to the rumors—if lost souls truly wandered the island—then perhaps his sister’s fate could be undone.

II. The Blighted Shore

They made landfall beneath a crescent moon, the surf whispering in riddles. The sands sparkled as if strewn with powdered bone. Reddick, Morae, and their companions—a jocular apothecary, Solon, hungry for rare herbs, and the mercenary Odel, lured by legend of a sunken trove—dragged the sloop far up the beach. The silence pressed in.

Every footstep displaced a subtle glow; phosphorescent motes leapt beneath their boots. “The earth is sick here,” Solon murmured, scooping up a handful of sand and letting it slip through his fingers. “Or perhaps bewitched.”

Odel laughed—a low, cynical chortle. “Gold doesn’t care about a little curse.”

Morae scowled. “Everything has its price.” She pointed inland, to where silvered boughs sprawled, gnarled and metallic, shivering in the brackish mist. “If we’re to find what we came for, we follow the river. The moon leads the way. That’s what they say.”

And ahead, the lunatic gleam of the tidal river beckoned.

III. Silverwood and Shadow

Under the haunted moonlight, the island revealed itself in bleak gradients. Every tree bore leaves like sheets of hammered glass, their edges trembling with an inaudible melody. Reddick felt the forest’s pulse; the roots gripped the soil in patterns too purposeful for comfort. Morae led, machete at the ready, while the others followed in uneasy silence.

Every branch dripped with dew that mirrored the stars, and every gust brought a fragrance unfamiliar—a heady mix of salt, decay, and something almost metallic.

They walked for hours or years—time slipped strange on Aldervale—until the trees thinned and gave way to an ancient village, half-drowned and cracked by salt. The architecture was otherworldly: stone arches twisted skyward, pierced by moonbeams as though pleading for rescue.

Odel, ever the pragmatist, wasted no time. “Check the dwellings. Dead people can’t take their gold with them.” His words drew a tremor from the ruins, and frogs fell silent.

Solon scoured the huts, gathering rare moss and blossoms, marveling at flora grown where sunlight never reached. “Remarkable,” he whispered. “This place exists like a phantom limb—partly here, partly vanished.”

Reddick alone kept his attention fixed on the well in the village’s heart. He remembered overheard whispers in Port Argonne: that those who drank of the moonlit water never left unchanged.

IV. Whispers Beneath the Tide

By midnight, Odel had unearthed a handful of trinkets: rings tarnished beyond value, a cracked navigational sphere, a child’s doll whose glass eyes glittered with residual moonlight. Frustration simmered. “All the stories promised more.”

But Reddick couldn’t share in the disappointment. He hovered above the old well, peering into the obsidian water. The reflection twisted—two faces floated up: his own, and his sister’s, sunken and pale. He recoiled, heart hammering.

“You see them too?” Morae’s voice trickled like water. She stood behind him, gaze drawn to the well. “The island shows you what you desire—then feasts on it.”

Far off, something keened. The trees trembled, raining diamond-bright tears.

Odel’s nerves snapped. “Enough of this sorcery!” He stormed toward the shore, every footstep kindling argent fire across the ruined stones.

Solon grasped Reddick’s arm. “We should leave. The tide’s changing.”

But Reddick felt the call grow louder. “My sister… she’s here. I know it.”

Morae shook her head. “That’s what the island wants you to think. Nothing returns from the Moonlit Tides unscathed.”

V. The Heart of the Curse

Dawn nibbled the horizon—pale and indistinct. Yet, driven by longing, Reddick followed the river upcurrent, deeper into thickets that pulsed with spectral life. Morae accompanied him, blade glinting, while Solon, burdened with botanical samples, trailed nervously. Odel’s absence weighed heavy.

They navigated the forest’s snarled arteries until they stumbled upon a meadow awash with ghostly blooms. At its center rose a circle of standing stones, each scored with glyphs that shimmered like trapped moonlight. Here the world felt paused, as if the island held its breath.

In the ring’s center, a young girl knelt, her back toward them, hair matted with seaweed. Her form flickered—solid one moment, translucently skeletal the next.

Reddick’s heart leapt. “Mysa?”

She turned. Her eyes, bottomless black, stared through him.

“Brother,” she rasped, echoing his longing. “You returned.”

He rushed forward, arms outstretched, but Morae hauled him back. “Don’t. The curse masquerades as those you love.”

From Mysa’s shadow, distortions uncurled: drowned sailors, mothers searching for lost children, lovers reaching with tangled fingers. Their voices rose, a liturgy of hunger and regret.

“You promised you would come back for me,” Mysa intoned. “Stay here. Become as I am.”

The stones pulsed with a sickly blue light. The tidal river, unseen but everywhere, dragged at the edges of reality, threatening to pull them all beneath.

VI. The Choice and the Tides

Morae squared her shoulders, voice steady. “It’s all illusion, Reddick. She’s gone, and the island feeds on pain.”

Reddick wept. Yet, in his sorrow, he sensed a pattern. The glyphs on the stones shifted, resolving into something familiar: the cycle of the tides, the phases of the moon, the ceaseless churn of loss and return.

He stepped back, refusing the apparition’s embrace. “I can’t stay. I have to let you go—even if the memory is all I keep.”

Mysa’s form collapsed into foam, voice twisting with the wind. “You’ll never find peace—none of you will.”

Sunrise melted the curse’s glamour. The spectral mourners dissolved into the mist, crying promises that wrung the soul.

VII. Return to the Sea

The three survivors staggered free of the standing stones, breathing salt-laden air as if for the first time. The meadow faded to withered grass; the silverwood groves shed their crystalline sheen. On the return trek, the village was emptier, the shadows receding.

On the beach, they found Odel sprawled in the sand, gaze fixed on the sun, hands clutching his meager findings. He muttered feverishly, haunted by visions only he could see.

Morae and Solon managed to drag him to the sloop. Every motion felt weighted, as though gravity pressed doubly upon them.

They cast off on a rising tide, the island’s silhouette shrinking in the dawn’s blush. Seabirds circled overhead, emboldened by a new day.

Reddick sat in the stern, staring back at the receding loom of Aldervale. Beneath his grief, there was an ember of grace: he had faced the lure of the past and chosen to bear its scars without surrender. The island would wait for others, as it always had, its hunger undiminished.

But for those who escaped the Cursed Island of the Moonlit Tides, the legend became a different sort of truth—a story of sorrow, endurance, and the wisdom of letting go.

Far below, subtle currents shifted. In the drowned ruins beneath Aldervale, moonlight touched ancient bones. The ocean sighed, patient and unbroken, whispering the promise of new visitors.

And the story remained, ready to claim whomever next sought the island at the edge of the moonlit tides.
