Beyond the Veil: Short Paranormal Fiction to Haunt Your Nights


It was a cold autumn evening when Marian stepped off the train at Ashford Station. The wind cut through her coat, tugging at the ends of her scarf, and the faint smell of damp earth lingered in the air. The train, a relic of the past, had arrived late, as usual, and the hour was growing late. The station was nearly empty except for the occasional figure hurrying to their destination. The flickering streetlamps painted the platform in dim orange light, casting long shadows over the cracked cobblestones.

Ashford was a small town nestled in the rolling hills of the countryside, known for its ancient history, quiet streets, and, oddly enough, its reputation for strange occurrences. The locals had always been tight-lipped about the town’s supernatural history. Stories of restless spirits and strange happenings were whispered at the pub, but never openly discussed in polite company. Marian had heard the rumors during her years of studying folklore at university, and now, as she stepped off the train, she couldn’t shake the feeling that there was something more to these tales than idle gossip.

Marian had come to Ashford for a simple reason: to research a mysterious, long-forgotten manor located on the outskirts of town. The Graystone Manor had stood abandoned for nearly a century, and despite being a popular subject of local legends, no one had ever ventured inside. Her fascination with haunted places, particularly those shrouded in mystery, had led her to this remote village. She was determined to uncover the secrets hidden within Graystone’s crumbling walls.

As Marian walked toward the town’s only inn, the weight of the evening air seemed to press against her chest. Her breath misted in front of her, and the long path to the inn seemed to stretch endlessly. The small, ancient building loomed ahead, its crooked windows and worn stone facade giving off an eerie charm.

Inside, the innkeeper, a tall woman with silver hair and an unsettlingly calm demeanor, greeted Marian without much fanfare. “You must be the researcher,” she said, her voice almost a whisper. “Your room is ready. You’ll find it quiet, as always.”

Marian gave a polite smile, trying to ignore the creeping feeling that there was something off about the woman. After a brief exchange, she was led up a narrow staircase to her room.

The room was sparse, with a single bed, a worn armchair by the window, and a small desk cluttered with faded papers and dusty books. As Marian set down her bag and looked out of the window, she could see the faint outline of the Graystone Manor in the distance, half-hidden behind a thicket of twisted trees. The night had fallen fully now, and the manor’s silhouette seemed to watch her, waiting.

The next morning, Marian made her way to the manor. The air was thick with fog, the sun barely a smudge in the sky. The path leading up to Graystone was lined with ancient oak trees, their branches like gnarled hands reaching out to grasp at anyone who dared to venture near. The manor itself, though a stately building in its prime, had succumbed to neglect. Ivy crawled up the sides of the crumbling stone, and the windows were either shattered or completely covered with grime.

Marian’s heart quickened as she approached the large iron gates that stood ajar, creaking slightly in the breeze. She stepped through, feeling a strange shiver run down her spine. As she made her way toward the front door, the ground seemed to shift beneath her feet, and she stumbled slightly, her hand brushing against the cold stone of the building. The door was unlocked, and with a push, it swung open.

Inside, the air was thick with dust, and the silence was deafening. The grand foyer was in a state of decay, with peeling wallpaper, cracked marble floors, and cobwebs hanging from the high ceilings. The furniture, once elegant, was now nothing more than rotting remnants. As Marian stepped further inside, the sound of her footsteps echoed through the empty halls, as if the house itself were listening.

She was drawn toward a large staircase that spiraled upward, its banister twisted and splintered. At the top of the stairs was a long corridor, leading to what appeared to be the heart of the manor. Marian’s pulse quickened with a mixture of fear and excitement. She had read the stories—the rumors of a ghostly presence that lingered in the house, of strange noises and inexplicable happenings. But she was a researcher, a skeptic at heart, and she refused to believe in mere superstition.

As she walked down the hallway, a cold wind swept through the manor, making the hairs on her neck stand on end. The air grew heavier, and the walls seemed to close in around her. She reached a door at the end of the corridor, its frame carved with intricate patterns, but the door was ajar. The faintest sound, like a whisper, drifted through the crack, drawing her closer.

Marian pushed the door open and stepped inside. The room was large, its walls lined with dark, heavy curtains that blocked out the little light that had filtered into the house. At the center of the room was a large, antique mirror, its frame covered in dust and cobwebs. The glass, however, seemed strangely clear, almost too clear. It reflected the room with an unnatural precision, as if it were not a reflection at all, but a doorway into another world.

For a moment, Marian stared at her own reflection, her breath catching in her throat. Then, slowly, she stepped closer, her hand reaching out toward the mirror. As her fingers brushed against the cool surface, she felt a jolt of electricity race through her body, and the world around her seemed to ripple. The air grew colder, the room darker, and the whispering sound grew louder, now a chorus of voices, speaking in a language she could not understand.

Suddenly, the reflection in the mirror shifted. It was no longer her own face that stared back at her, but a pale, twisted figure with hollow eyes and an empty smile. Marian recoiled in terror, stumbling backward, but her feet tangled in the carpet, and she fell to the floor.

When she looked up, the figure in the mirror was gone, replaced once more by her own reflection. Her heart raced in her chest, and she scrambled to her feet, her mind spinning. She couldn’t explain what had just happened—what she had seen, or whether it had even been real. But there was no denying the terror that gripped her.

Shaken, Marian turned to leave the room, but as she reached the door, she heard a soft laugh—a low, haunting sound that seemed to echo from deep within the walls. The hairs on the back of her neck stood on end as she hurried down the hallway, back toward the stairs. Every step felt heavier, as if the house itself were trying to hold her in place.

Just as she reached the bottom of the stairs, the door slammed shut behind her, and the temperature in the room plummeted. She could see her breath misting in the air. The walls seemed to breathe, the floor groaning beneath her feet as if the house were alive.

Marian rushed for the front door, but it wouldn’t open. The handle turned in her hand, but the door remained stubbornly shut. She pounded on the wood, shouting for help, but no sound left her lips. Panic surged through her as the walls began to close in around her.

And then, through the haze of fear and confusion, she heard it. A voice, soft and distant, but unmistakably clear: “You should not have come.”

The next morning, the innkeeper found Marian standing outside the manor, staring at the building with wide, unblinking eyes. Her clothes were torn, and her face was pale, as if the very life had been drained from her.

The innkeeper did not ask questions. She simply took Marian back to the inn, where she was given a quiet room to rest. No one in Ashford spoke of what had happened, and Marian did not speak of it either. She knew better than to share what she had witnessed, what she had felt, and what had followed her from the manor.

But as she lay in the inn’s bed that night, staring at the ceiling, the whispers returned—soft and insistent, like a forgotten memory trying to resurface. And the cold, ghostly presence lingered just beyond the veil, waiting for the next unwitting soul to step through.
