The Ghosts of the Taj Mahal: A Love Story


Night pressed heavily against the white marble. By day, the Taj Mahal dazzled beneath the Indian sun—its domes and minarets piercing the sky, drawing endless rivers of visitors. But at night, as the moon carved shadows across its pearly expanse, the Taj Mahal belonged to the dreams—and the dead.

Prisha Singh didn’t believe in ghosts. She was a historian, trained to trust buried dates and cracked pottery shards, not whispers and unsubstantiated memories. Yet she found herself standing at the gate at midnight, her footsteps echoing off the ancient stones, drawn by curiosity and an inexplicable tug at her heart.

The air was thick with jasmine. Shadows from the garden’s cypress trees sprawled over the pale walkways, and the Yamuna river glimmered like molten silver. Prisha’s hands trembled, whether from the cool of the evening or the excitement of stepping into forbidden hours, she could not say.

She was not alone. The night guard, Ravi, raised his lantern, his eyes heavy-lidded. He recognized her from her daytime visits—her insatiable questions about the Taj Mahal’s history, her gentle tracing of the inlaid gemstones with gloved fingers.

“Are you sure you want to do this, miss?” he murmured, voice raspy with sleep and superstition.

“I have permission,” she replied, flashing her folded letter, but Ravi’s concern lingered.

“They talk at night,” he said, nodding at the tomb. “Sometimes I hear voices. Not mine, not anyone living. Not safe.”

Prisha smiled, disarming and insistent. “I’ll be fine. If I vanish, mention me at the next Archaeological Society meeting.”

Ravi grunted and allowed her inside. The echo of her footfalls mingled with the breathless hush. The Taj Mahal slept as a mausoleum should, but tonight, cracks appeared in the fabric between memory and reality.

She reached the crypt. Here lay Mumtaz Mahal, beloved wife of Shah Jahan, empress of hearts, mother of fourteen, dead at thirty-nine; beside her, in decadent symmetry, Shah Jahan himself, imprisoned in sorrow even before death sealed the stone over his broken body. Above their true resting place, the empty cenotaphs gleamed beneath a shaft of moonlight.

Prisha lingered by the marble screen, filigreed like frozen lace. She whispered, “Are you here?”

The hush deepened. Suddenly, a breath tickled her ear, and when she turned, she was not alone. A translucent figure shimmered only feet away—a woman in the flowing dress of a Mughal noble, veiled, her eyes brimming with an ancient sadness.

A canary note of fear fluttered in Prisha’s ribcage, yet she held her ground.

“You can see me?” The voice was lilting, oddly reassuring. “How long has it been since someone saw me?”

Prisha’s mouth was dry. “You are…?”

“Mumtaz Mahal,” the spirit replied, bowing her head. “But you may call me Arjumand, as he once did.”

The historian’s skepticism rebelled. Yet the sorrow in the apparition’s form, the ache in those familiar brown eyes, cut deeper than disbelief.

“I came… I had to see. Is it true?” Prisha asked, gesturing at the marble mausoleum meant to enshrine a world of love.

Mumtaz drifted forward, her translucent hand passing through the inlaid gemstones. “This? Yes, it is a palace. And yes, it is a grave.” She sighed, her sadness mingling with the midnight haze. “You come for the story. But stories are more than carved marble.”

Prisha gathered her nerve. “Do you… remain here? Even now?”

Mumtaz’s smile was wistful. “Love is not built of stone, child, nor does death end longing.” She gestured, and the air shuddered. The shadows grew deeper, sharper, and then the mausoleum changed.

The marble became a bustling court. Musicians played, silk rustled, and laughter trickled through the air. Prisha saw herself transported to Shah Jahan’s time—the emperor young, regal, his eyes always seeking the woman at his side, Mumtaz, alive and radiant.

Their voices, their laughter, their long walks beside the river—Prisha felt what recorded history never told: the small, sacred softness of shared glances, the solace in clasped hands, the quiet devotion in daily life. She watched as grief folded over joy, saw the emperor’s world crumble when Mumtaz died giving birth to their fourteenth child. She witnessed years pass, grief carving canyons in Shah Jahan’s spirit, driving him to immortalize his beloved in monument and myth.

“Why do you stay?” Prisha asked quietly, as the vision faded, and the marble tomb reasserted itself, gray and cold.

Mumtaz’s gaze lingered on the empty cenotaphs. “Because love’s story isn’t finished. I wait for him. For us.”

A shadow detached itself from the far colonnade, coalescing into a regal man: a prowling panther’s grace, silver beard, eyes lost to timeless longing—Shah Jahan himself.

Prisha’s breath hitched.

He regarded Mumtaz with an ache that made the years between their deaths seem like grains of sand.

“Arjumand…” he whispered.

“Jahan.”

They embraced—no longer ghostly wraiths, but something humid with presence and sorrow.

Prisha watched, feeling the tide of their love, the confusion of immortality. Time passed differently here, nights stretching like centuries, memories echoing off the marble.

“I built you the greatest tomb the world has known,” Shah Jahan said, his voice like the river after rain. “But I would trade all the marble to trace the curve of your cheek just once more.”

Mumtaz smiled, touching his face, her fingers mere breath. “Love is not measured in stone, my emperor, but in remembrance.”

Prisha felt tears on her own cheek. For years, she had chased the legend, dissected documents, cataloged artifacts. Yet the scale of their love eluded ink and paper, lived here instead, where anguish and hope converged with the moonlight.

As dawn paled the sky, the spirits faded, drawn away by the coming crowds. Prisha stood alone, the mausoleum hushed once more. Ravi met her at the gate, his eyes widening.

“They said… they said a woman was singing inside last night.”

Prisha only smiled. “Not just one.”

Her heart beat to an old, sad, beautiful rhythm as she walked away. The Taj Mahal glimmered behind her—a ghost-haunted palace, a sepulcher of longing, a monument to what time cannot conquer.

And within its shining walls, love itself lingered: unfinished, eternal, whispering through the corridors, singing for those who dared listen.
