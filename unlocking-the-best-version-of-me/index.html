Unlocking the Best Version of Me


I never believed mirrors were honest. They cut straight to the surface—hair out of place, a new pimple in bloom, eyes that seemed a little too sleepy—and missed everything that mattered. I stood in the bathroom that Wednesday morning, steel-tipped sunlight turning toothpaste flecks into sparkling specks. My shirt clung at the shoulders. There was no hiding the tug of dread beneath my ribs.

Another day, another trudge into Hillman & Sons, which sounded impressive till you saw the navy carpets scuffed by cheap, rolling office chairs and smelled reheated soup wafting from janitor’s closet break rooms. I made cold calls and shifted numbers on digital spreadsheets that, frankly, could have run themselves without my help. I moved at half-volume and quarter-energy. I hesitated before answering the microwave, as if being the first to pop my head up would make me the next target for Jenkins’s daily disappointment diatribe.

But that Tuesday—one day before—things shifted. I was walking home through the park and passed a man crouched beside a chalk mural, a riot of unfiltered color across the sidewalk. He squinted up, pink dust smeared on his cheekbone. “Hey,” he called, “d’you ever draw on the ground?”

I shook my head, maybe smiled. “Not since elementary school.”

“You look like you need it.” He shrugged and jutted a fistful of sunset hues at me. I kept walking, but a strange itch lingered under my skin. All night, the image of side-walk rainbows and that creative abandon haunted me.

On my walk to work the next morning, I found myself pausing by a different mural. Overnight rain had smudged the colors. Pink stains mixed with gray, like a birthday cake dropped in a puddle. My shoulders sagged, but the memory of the invitation shook me: “You look like you need it.”

I took a deep breath and sat on a nearby bench. Out tumbled things I hadn’t let myself acknowledge: I was tired, not just from work but from playing small. Navigating the world as if it might bite made me shrink inch by subtle inch. What if the man with the chalk was right? What if I needed more than routine? Maybe what I craved was a chance to unlock the best possible version of myself.

Chapter 1: Permission to Imagine

I started small. On the walk home that Wednesday, I stopped when I reached the chalk mural on Oak Street. The artist was gone. I found a stub of blue in the grass and kneeled, sketching a single cloud beside the fading sun. It looked lopsided and lumpy—indisputable proof to my inner critic that my art skills peaked in third grade. Still, something about leaving an honest, imperfect mark—a flash of me, unfiltered—lifted my spirits long after my work faded in the next rainstorm.

Inside my apartment, I stared at my bookshelf. Lately, I’d fallen into a rut of scrolling social media till my thumb felt bruised, watching others blossom while shrinking into digitally curated corners. I picked up an old sketchbook, brushed dust off the cover, and whispered into the silence: “What if I let myself be seen?”

I started a project: a single page every day, not for anyone but me. Hoping nobody would peer over my shoulder, I doodled spaghetti monsters and made bold lists of what I really wanted. Not what my parents expected, not what my bosses assumed—just what set my own pulse skipping. I reread an ancient journal entry from college that said, “One day, I’ll do something daring, just to see what I’m made of.” The ridiculous ambition still burned; it was just quieter now, a cinder under the logs.

Chapter 2: The Stumble and the Spark

Hillman & Sons never expected luminescence from me. I learned to finish my work two hours early and fill the empty time with my burgeoning project. On coffee breaks, rather than absorbing Jenkins’s droning complaints, I caught myself sketching web diagrams of “ways to surprise myself,” “habits that drain me,” “skills I wish I’d tried.”

Self-improvement, when you strip away Instagram-friendly platitudes, is deeply unglamorous. You fall on your face and want desperately to backpedal. For every night I journaled or chipped away at a new routine, there were mornings I’d snooze my alarm three times before hauling my bones out of bed.

But odd things started happening. My cubicle neighbor Toby noticed me drawing funny cat heroes on my sticky notes. “That’s actually pretty great,” he grinned one Thursday. “We should have a cartoon contest.” Next thing I knew, half the floor was passing doodles with punchlines about Jenkins’s infamous tie collection (all paisley, most likely haunted).

Friday, our boss found one and laughed—a full, unfiltered bray. For one suspended moment, the office felt lighter. Toby whispered, “What’d I tell you? You got a knack.”

I’d never seen glimpses of my ‘best self’ in meeting minutes or hollow praise, but there it was, shining through a silly sketch: a moment of connection, a step away from invisible.

Chapter 3: Surrender to Curiosity

The next stretch was turbulence and wonder, equal halves. I bought a set of watercolor pencils and signed up for a community art class, limbs trembling with embarrassment. Nerves buzzed as I entered Room B14, the sharp smell of turpentine riding on the air. Everyone else looked more professional than I’d ever felt; I almost turned around on the spot.

“A new face,” the instructor said, cutting through the noise like a lighthouse. “You here to paint, or just to ghost us halfway through?” Laughter broke the tension; I exhaled. For the first time in years, I didn’t worry about being the least skilled. My hands shook as I drew my first line—crooked, confident despite the imperfections.

Classmates shared tips unprompted. “If you blot the brush on a napkin, it won’t bleed.” “Don’t fuss so much about getting it right—it’s supposed to be fun first.” Such generosity felt strange, porous, contagious. Week by week, I learned to surrender to curiosity rather than aiming for mastery. Some days yielded muddy brown messes, others offered glimpses of beauty.

Art was never just about color—it was about rediscovering the ability to surprise myself. At work, I started asking Toby and the others about their secret ambitions. Melanie admitted she wanted to run a half-marathon. Jamal confessed he wished he’d learned French. We cheered each new attempt, shared setbacks, and celebrated inch-by-inch growth.

One day, Jenkins passed by my desk and glanced at my sketchbook. I braced for sarcasm. Instead, he asked, “Could you show me how you shade clouds?” The question stunned me. His tie clashed with his shirt, but for five minutes, the power dynamic dissolved; we were just two awkward learners fumbling towards something new.

Chapter 4: The Unlocking

Spring opened with green shoots and softened air. My art sprawled beyond sketchbooks; I started sharing photos of my work online under a pseudonym. Strangers commented with kindness, and someone commissioned a pet portrait. I biked to the library and lost myself in books on creativity, neuroscience, and the odd poetry of everyday resilience. I journaled every win, no matter how small: “Drew in the park while kids watched—didn’t panic!” “Tried a recipe I’d botched before; actually edible.”

Saturdays stopped being bleak stretches of recovery. Instead, I’d invite friends from class over to try new crafts, paint mugs, or watch documentaries about wild, obsessive creators who failed as often as they rose. When nerves crept in or old doubts nagged, I’d revisit pages in my sketchbook marked with mantras: “No masterpieces before breakfast.” “Done is better than perfect.”

Something was changing, subtly and irreversibly. Where I’d formerly paddled in circles of sameness, now every week brought a new skill, a new mistake, and a new badge of hard-won confidence.

Chapter 5: The Reckoning

Of course, growth attracts reckoning. Summer’s first thunderstorm hit on a Monday—so heavy it rattled the break room window. Jenkins announced restructuring. My stomach dropped as emails flew, panic seeping through cubicles as pink slips were delivered. For two hours, my future hung in the balance. When the storm cleared, I was still standing, but Toby was let go.

Guilt, grief, and survivor’s relief crashed in. My old shell would have retreated into compliant silence, eyes on the clock. Instead, over microwaved noodles, I found myself listening to Toby’s fears about freelancing and admitting my own uncertainty.

“We can’t stay stuck,” he said. The words were as much a promise as a declaration. I offered to help him build a website, using new skills gleaned from late-night explorations online. We brainstormed his new business—cartoons, design, resume help for other survivors of the layoff. Helping him lit my own path through the fog. I realized I could craft something that outlasted any single employer’s whim.

For the first time, the prospect of change—of choosing, rather than simply enduring—felt like a gateway and not a threat.

Chapter 6: Emergence

My world, which once felt shrunken by routine, expanded in concentric circles. With Toby, I organized “Creativity Afternoons” at the park. People showed up: colleagues past and present, strangers drawn by word of mouth, parents with children who wanted to scribble monsters onto the pavement. I gave away chalk like it was treasure and marveled at how something as simple as collective mess-making turned afternoons golden.

I still had bad days—sleepless nights, assignments fumbled, periods of self-doubt. Unlocking the best version of myself wasn’t some grand transformation, like a butterfly bursting forth. It was more marathon than sprint, a thousand slow iterations stitched together: one act of courage, one honest conversation, one messy drawing at a time.

As summer wore on, my art sold online, small but steady. I started a blog chronicling my experiments with creativity and shared resources for others who wanted to dig out of their own ruts. The comments section bloomed with fellow travelers, each on their own clumsy, dazzling journey.

I cartwheeled, metaphorically, through local galleries and open mics. Unlikely friendships rooted and grew. Jenkins, now less a caricature and more a human being with flaws and foibles, attended one of our chalk events. He asked me about painting clouds again.

Epilogue: The Ongoing Journey

I don’t pretend to have become perfect. The best version of me is a moving target, a point on the horizon forever dancing just further than my outstretched hand. Sometimes, in moments of grit or grace, I draw a little closer.

But I have learned to crave the process over the outcome: to show up, to try, to fail without vanishing, to draw lopsided clouds and not mind if others watch. The best version of me is the one who remains curious, imperfect, brave enough to start again each day. Unlocking it—the self I’d hidden under layers of habit and fear—required nothing more than permission: to imagine, to connect, and to create, one small gesture at a time.

If mirrors can’t show you what matters, maybe chalk can. Or laughter. Or dappled sunlight on a park bench, when you finally let yourself take up space.
