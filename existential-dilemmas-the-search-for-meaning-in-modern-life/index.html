Existential Dilemmas: The Search for Meaning in Modern Life


Elliot woke up on a Wednesday, his alarm humming softly with an urgency it didn’t seem to deserve. The room was gray, the light filtering through mismatched curtains dull as dust. He rolled onto his back, staring at the faint water stain spreading like a comet across the ceiling. Another day, he thought, another patchwork attempt at purpose.

At thirty-four, he’d moved beyond what most people considered a “crisis.” But the existential ache—that gnawing void—had evolved into something subtler, a quiet hum he couldn’t escape. He worked. He dated. He created. And none of it felt enough. It wasn’t about money or fame—he had a decent job at a digital marketing firm and maintained an Instagram account where he posted his amateur photography. People “liked” him in the online sense, and some even liked him in real life. Yet, each interaction seemed a bit rehearsed, siphoned of real depth. He longed for something untouchable, unknowable, as if it were the echo of a song barely remembered.

When Elliot stepped onto the subway that morning, he felt it stronger than ever. The dizzying rush of clattering wheels beneath his feet somehow amplified the chasm—the gap between the world’s relentless movement and his internal stillness. Faces brushed past, collars stiff, eyes narrowed against time. Strangers stood shoulder to shoulder, pretending not to see one another, each locked into their own respective narratives. He found himself wondering, what do they believe in? What whispers guide their actions when no one else is looking?

The train screeched to a halt. Elliot glanced at his phone, deciding impulsively to get off two stops early. Being late to work didn’t matter. Not today. He mumbled to himself, “What’s one more detour?”

The park was quieter than expected for mid-morning, the kind of quiet that made unnoticed things stand out: the whisper of trees in the wind, the rhythmic sway of jogging feet, the distant cry of a child whose balloon had slipped away. Elliot sat on a damp bench, staring at his shoes. He thought of Kafka and Camus, of absurdity and meaninglessness, of a planet spinning relentlessly without an agreed-upon why. His thoughts piled atop themselves like a hurriedly constructed house, shaky and incomplete.

“You look like you just lost a bet,” someone said.

Elliot blinked, startled, and turned toward the voice. A man in his forties, tan coat hanging loosely, stood nearby holding a battered thermos. His face was angular but kind, his smile small but confident.

“Excuse me?” Elliot replied.

“You’re dressed for work, but sitting in a park, brooding like Hamlet. Seems like a bad trade,” the man added.

Elliot flushed but couldn’t help smiling. “What makes you think I’m brooding?”

“Oh, I’ve been there," the man said, sitting without invitation on the other end of the bench. “Drawn to existential questions, huh?”

“What makes you think that?”

The stranger chuckled. “Because I can spot it—happens to the best of us. You ever heard of ’the ache of reason’?”

Elliot narrowed his eyes, shaking his head.

“It’s this idea that once you understand too much—once you really, truly see how interconnected everything is—you can’t just un-see it. You float, looking for anchors. But here’s the thing about anchors: you choose them.”

“…What if you make the wrong choice?” Elliot asked, unable to stop himself.

The man tilted his head slightly. “Who’s to say there’s a right one? Look, come walk with me. We can talk or not talk—either way, maybe it’ll help shake a thought loose.”

They walked in silence for thirty minutes, just far enough for Manhattan’s noises to fade into something softer. The stranger carried himself absentmindedly, sipping occasionally from his thermos, eyes darting to the cracks in the pavement or the graffiti on light poles. Elliot kept waiting for answers—the secrets this stranger seemed to hold—but none came. Instead, the man stopped suddenly near the bridge’s entrance, where the East River rippled below, its surface glinting in sunlight.

“This world,” the man said, gesturing broadly, “is terrifying and beautiful in equal measure. You can stare at it, pick it apart, and try to explain it all day. But none of that will ever make you feel connected to it.”

Elliot folded his arms. “So what do I do? Pretend that the questions don’t matter?”

“No, no pretending. You’ll only end up lying to yourself and watching it all unravel sooner or later,” the man answered. “You accept that the search itself is the meaning—having the courage to ask the questions is the purpose, Elliot.”

“How do you—? Wait, did I even tell you my name?”

The man chuckled. “You didn’t, but it’s written on your coffee mug tucked in your bag. You see, awareness isn’t always existential agony—it can actually be playful, curious.”

Elliot frowned, then laughed nervously, unsure whether the man’s humor was endearing or unsettling. There was a strange ease to their conversation, yet somehow it had left him more uncertain, as though the fragile walls encasing his doubts had only become more transparent.

Back at work, Elliot stared at the graphs and pie charts on his screen while snippets of the stranger’s words drifted back to him. “Awareness isn’t always agony,” he thought. Could life be about dwelling in the questions without demanding ultimate answers? Could meaning be less about solving the puzzle and more about appreciating the pieces fitted imperfectly together?

That evening, Elliot walked past his usual stop and found a jazz lounge tucked between two narrow side streets. He stepped inside. The band played soft and soulful, their melodies winding through the air like smoke. He felt an odd freedom there, surrounded by people who reveled in rhythm without asking where it came from. Elliot ordered a drink he’d never tried before, letting his mind wander.

Perhaps meaning wasn’t held in ceremonies or ideologies or goals. Maybe it was in small, quiet moments like this, moments where nothing had to make sense, where even absurdity felt alive and necessary.

The hum began to soften.
