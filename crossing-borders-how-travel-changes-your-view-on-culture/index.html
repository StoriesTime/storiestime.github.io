Crossing Borders: How Travel Changes Your View on Culture


The moment Maria’s plane touched down in a foreign country, she knew her life was about to change. She had spent years studying anthropology, immersing herself in the theories and writings of others, but it was different to read about a culture than to experience it firsthand. She had traveled before, but this journey felt more significant, as it marked the beginning of her true exploration of the world outside her familiar surroundings.

Maria’s destination was a small village nestled in the mountains of a country she had only learned about in textbooks. The people there spoke a language she could hardly pronounce, and the customs were so different from her own that she had no idea where to begin. She had always been fascinated by the notion of “crossing borders” — the idea that traveling to a new place not only involved crossing physical borders but also mental and emotional ones. Now, she was ready to challenge everything she thought she knew about culture.

The First Encounter: A Lesson in Hospitality

Upon arrival, Maria was greeted by a woman named Amina, who, though initially reserved, welcomed her with a warm smile. Maria tried her best to communicate in the language, fumbling over her words as she introduced herself. Amina laughed softly, a sound that put Maria at ease. She gestured for Maria to follow her, and they walked through narrow cobblestone streets lined with quaint houses that looked as though they had been standing for centuries.

As they entered Amina’s home, Maria was struck by how different everything was. The living room, furnished with colorful rugs and cushions, was dimly lit by soft, flickering lamps. Amina prepared a meal of stews and flatbreads, offering Maria a seat at the low wooden table. The food was unfamiliar but delicious, rich with spices that Maria had never tasted before. As they ate together, Amina’s family slowly trickled in, each one offering a smile and a greeting in a language Maria couldn’t understand.

Yet, despite the language barrier, something profound happened during that meal. There was a warmth and openness in the way Amina’s family interacted with each other. They weren’t just sharing food; they were sharing an experience — an exchange that transcended words. In that moment, Maria understood a concept she had read about but never fully grasped: culture wasn’t just about the things people did. It was about the emotions and values that underpinned those actions.

Navigating a New Culture: The Struggle for Understanding

Over the next few days, Maria tried to immerse herself in the local way of life. She walked through the village, visited markets, and tried to learn as much as she could from the people around her. At first, it was overwhelming. The customs were unlike anything she had experienced before. There were gestures she had to learn, foods she had never imagined, and social structures that seemed so different from her own.

One of the most striking differences Maria noticed was the way time was perceived. In her own country, everything ran on a strict schedule — appointments, meetings, even friendships were governed by clocks. But here, in this small village, time was fluid. People came and went without a sense of urgency, and the concept of punctuality seemed almost irrelevant. At first, Maria found it frustrating. She had planned to visit certain places at specific times, but every time she tried to set a schedule, something unexpected would happen. Someone would invite her for tea, or a conversation would stretch into hours. There was no rush, no need to be anywhere else.

This shift in perspective forced Maria to confront her own assumptions. She realized how much of her daily life was dictated by the clock, and how much she had come to value efficiency over connection. The villagers, however, seemed to embrace the present moment, savoring life’s small pleasures without worrying about what was coming next. Slowly, Maria started to relax, letting go of her need to control every aspect of her day. She allowed herself to be part of the ebb and flow of the community, and it felt liberating.

Understanding the Unspoken: Nonverbal Communication

One of the most eye-opening lessons Maria learned during her travels was the power of nonverbal communication. In her own culture, words were everything. People often spoke in long sentences, explaining their thoughts and intentions in detail. But in this new culture, Maria found that much of the communication took place without words.

During a visit to a local market, Maria stood beside an elderly woman who was selling handmade pottery. As she observed the woman’s interactions with customers, she realized that there were no elaborate greetings or pleasantries. Instead, the woman would offer a nod and a smile, gesturing toward the pottery with a slight tilt of her head. When a customer reached for an item, the woman would raise an eyebrow, as if silently asking, “Do you want it?” If the customer agreed, she would place the item in their hands without saying a word. It was a subtle but powerful way of communicating — a quiet acknowledgment of mutual understanding.

This interaction made Maria think about how much of communication in her own life relied on verbal exchanges. She had been taught to value speaking and writing, but here, she saw how much could be communicated through silence, gesture, and expression. She began to observe how the villagers interacted with each other, noticing how their faces would light up with a smile, how their hands would gently touch in greeting, and how their eyes would convey emotions that words often failed to express. This was a new form of communication, one that Maria had never truly considered before.

Bridging the Gap: Finding Common Ground

As Maria spent more time in the village, she began to develop deep connections with the people around her. Despite the differences in language, customs, and values, there was a shared humanity that transcended cultural boundaries. She came to understand that culture was not a barrier, but a bridge — a way of connecting people to one another, even across vast distances.

One evening, as Maria sat on the porch of Amina’s house, watching the sun set behind the mountains, she realized that travel had changed her in ways she hadn’t anticipated. It had expanded her view of the world, allowing her to see beyond the limitations of her own culture. She had come to understand that culture was not something to be studied from a distance, but something to be experienced, lived, and shared.

Maria had learned that every culture had its own unique rhythm, its own way of seeing the world, and that these differences were not something to be feared, but embraced. By crossing borders — both physical and mental — she had come to appreciate the richness of diversity and the importance of understanding and respecting other ways of life. Her view of the world had shifted, and she knew that the lessons she had learned in this small village would stay with her forever.

The Return: A Changed Perspective

When Maria finally returned home, she brought with her not just memories of a foreign land, but a new perspective on life. She found herself more patient, more open, and more willing to embrace the unknown. The lessons she had learned during her travels had changed the way she interacted with people, how she viewed time, and how she understood the concept of culture.

Her journey had not just been about visiting a new place; it had been about discovering herself. By crossing borders, Maria had been forced to confront her own biases and assumptions, and in doing so, she had gained a deeper understanding of the world around her. She had learned that culture was not a monolithic concept, but a complex, evolving tapestry of experiences that shaped the way people lived and interacted with the world.

In the years that followed, Maria continued to travel, each journey expanding her view of the world and deepening her understanding of the cultures she encountered. And though her travels took her to many different places, the lesson that she carried with her from that small village in the mountains remained the same: that travel, at its heart, was about more than just seeing new places. It was about crossing borders — not just physical ones, but the invisible barriers that separate us from one another. And in crossing those borders, we discover a deeper connection to the world, and to each other.
