Beyond the Surface: Unmasking Racial Prejudice


It was early on a Wednesday morning when Lara stepped off the bus in front of the community center. The sun hung low in the sky, casting a golden hue across the sidewalk as people hurriedly walked to work or to drop their kids off at school. Lara adjusted the strap of her messenger bag and took a deep breath, readying herself for what was to come.

Her heart beat a little faster, not from nervousness but from determination. The community center was hosting a series of workshops aimed at addressing racial prejudice. It had been a long journey for Lara to get here. She wasn’t sure if it was fate or her own inner conviction that had led her down this path, but she knew one thing for certain: something had to change.

As a young African American woman, Lara had often felt the sting of prejudice. It wasn’t just the overt, angry racial slurs that some might expect. It wasn’t even always the stereotypical actions of people who acted out of ignorance or malice. More often than not, it was the quiet microaggressions, the casual dismissal of her abilities, or the constant feeling that she had to work twice as hard to be seen and respected.

But today, she was no longer content to let those moments pass by without acknowledgment. She had witnessed too many times how subtle racial bias could weave its way into conversations, decisions, and even friendships. Today was about unmasking it — peeling back the surface that concealed the true nature of prejudice.

She pushed open the door of the center, her mind already racing through her notes. The room smelled faintly of coffee and old wood, the walls adorned with posters promoting community engagement. Several chairs had been arranged in a circle. A group of diverse individuals, ranging from teenagers to retirees, were already seated and chatting softly.

Lara took a seat near the front, her eyes scanning the room. She was surprised to see some familiar faces, including Jessica, a white woman she’d met at a local book club. They exchanged a warm smile, and Jessica waved her over.

“Good to see you, Lara,” Jessica greeted her, her tone casual, but there was a hint of nervousness in her voice.

“Likewise, Jessica,” Lara replied, though she couldn’t help but notice the way Jessica’s eyes flickered around the room. It wasn’t judgmental; it was more like she was trying to gauge whether or not she was in the right place.

The workshop facilitator, Dr. Marcus Reynolds, a middle-aged Black man with a calm yet commanding presence, stood up at the front of the room. His voice cut through the murmurs like a soft wind, bringing the room to a hush.

“Welcome, everyone,” Dr. Reynolds began, his gaze steady but warm. “Today, we’ll be diving into a topic that many of us would prefer to avoid: racial prejudice. This isn’t about blame or guilt. It’s about understanding how deeply ingrained bias can shape our world, often without us even realizing it.”

Lara listened intently, her hands clasped tightly in her lap. She had attended similar workshops before, but this one felt different. It felt like it was going to be more than just a surface-level conversation about racial issues. Dr. Reynolds wasn’t interested in simply naming the problem; he was determined to confront it head-on.

“We all come from different backgrounds, and that’s what makes us unique,” Dr. Reynolds continued. “But the reality is that we all carry biases, often unconscious ones. These biases affect how we perceive and interact with people who look different from us, whether we want to admit it or not. Today, we’re going to explore that — beyond the surface.”

The group settled into a more focused silence as Dr. Reynolds outlined the goals of the session. They would start with a few activities designed to illustrate how prejudice could manifest in the smallest of actions. Lara felt a sense of anticipation. She had come here to learn, but more than that, she had come to challenge herself, to uncover her own biases and to help others do the same.

The first activity was an icebreaker. Dr. Reynolds passed out sheets of paper with a series of statements on them, asking each person to mark whether they agreed, disagreed, or were unsure about each statement. The statements ranged from simple topics like “I believe people should be treated equally, regardless of race” to more complex ones such as “Black people are more likely to be treated unfairly by law enforcement” or “It’s easier for white people to succeed in the workplace than it is for people of color.”

Lara’s hand hesitated over some of the statements. She was tempted to mark “strongly agree” for some, but she also realized how much of her perception had been shaped by her experiences. She wasn’t sure if her own views were entirely objective or if they had been skewed by the biases she had internalized over the years.

As the activity progressed, Lara noticed that some of the responses were more revealing than others. Jessica, sitting across from her, had marked “strongly agree” to the statement about Black people being unfairly treated by law enforcement, but when it came to workplace discrimination, she had marked “disagree.”

Lara couldn’t help but raise an eyebrow. Jessica had worked in a corporate environment for years, and while they hadn’t discussed race much, Lara knew that many of her colleagues, especially those of color, often spoke about feeling overlooked for promotions or subjected to unfair expectations.

After the icebreaker, Dr. Reynolds led the group into a larger discussion about race, identity, and privilege. He asked each participant to reflect on their own experiences with prejudice and to share a moment when they had felt marginalized or witnessed racial injustice.

Lara listened as others took turns speaking. A young Latino man shared how he had been racially profiled at a grocery store. An older Black woman spoke about how she had been passed over for a job despite being more qualified than her white counterpart. Even Jessica shared a story about a time she had witnessed a colleague of color being unfairly criticized, though she admitted she hadn’t spoken up at the time.

When it was Lara’s turn, she felt a lump form in her throat. She had so many stories, so many moments of quiet hurt. But she knew it was important to be honest. She took a deep breath and spoke.

“I’ve often felt that I had to prove myself more than my white counterparts,” she began, her voice steady but tinged with emotion. “I’ve had people question my abilities, even when I knew I was just as capable. It’s not always overt. It’s the little things — the way people avoid eye contact when I speak, or how they act surprised when I bring up an idea at a meeting. Sometimes it feels like I’m invisible until I do something extraordinary, and even then, it’s as though I have to work harder to make my voice heard.”

The room was silent as Lara spoke, and when she finished, Dr. Reynolds nodded thoughtfully. “That, Lara, is what we call the invisible load of bias. It’s the burden of constantly having to prove your worth, despite being just as qualified as anyone else. It’s exhausting.”

Lara nodded, grateful for his understanding. It wasn’t just her story, though. It was the story of so many others who had to navigate a world that wasn’t built with them in mind. But today, in this room, Lara felt a sense of solidarity. These conversations, uncomfortable as they were, needed to happen.

Dr. Reynolds concluded the workshop by saying, “Unmasking racial prejudice isn’t just about identifying obvious acts of discrimination. It’s about acknowledging the subtle, often unconscious biases that shape how we treat one another. And it’s about challenging ourselves to do better.”

As the session ended, Lara stood up, feeling a weight lift off her shoulders. She wasn’t naive enough to think that one workshop would change everything. But she knew it was a start — a small step toward a larger movement.

As she walked out of the community center, Jessica caught up with her. “Lara,” she began, her voice quieter now, “I’ve never really thought about things from that perspective. I mean, I’ve seen the injustices, but I didn’t realize how deep it goes. I think I’ve been…complicit in ignoring it. I want to do better.”

Lara smiled, a small but genuine smile. “It’s a process. We all have a part to play in it.”

They walked out together, the sun now fully risen above them, casting its light on the path ahead.

The journey to confronting racial prejudice doesn’t end with a single conversation, a single workshop, or a single moment of realization. It’s ongoing. It’s messy. But it’s necessary. Only by going beyond the surface can we begin to unmask the hidden biases that influence our actions, our perceptions, and the world around us. And when we do, we take the first step toward a more just and equitable future for all.
