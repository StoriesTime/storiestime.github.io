Inside the Mind: Short Stories Exploring Psychological Disorders


Sophie stood in the hallway, her fingers brushing the edges of the yellowed wallpaper, feeling the crinkled texture under her skin. It was the wallpaper that had been there for as long as she could remember, but she didn’t recognize the hallway. The doors were closed, all except one. The door at the very end of the corridor was slightly ajar, a sliver of dim light spilling out from the cracks. The faint whisper of a voice echoed from within, but Sophie couldn’t make out the words.

She swallowed, her chest tightening. The hallway seemed endless, but at the same time, it felt suffocatingly small. She needed to move, to open the door and step into the room, but her legs felt like lead. The echo of the voice grew louder, but still muffled, as if it were coming from the farthest reaches of her mind.

As Sophie tried to steady herself, the memories began to blur. The hallway, the door—everything—began to disintegrate into the fog of her thoughts, until only the echo remained, louder now, but more indistinct.

Anxiety.

The word flashed through her mind, and for a brief second, Sophie knew what was happening. But only for a moment. Then, as quickly as it had come, the word disappeared, replaced by an overwhelming emptiness. She couldn’t think. She couldn’t remember why she was here. Why had she come? Was she supposed to be somewhere else?

The door creaked again, its movement slow, deliberate. Sophie’s heartbeat accelerated. There was no time to think, no time to process. She pushed forward, each step like wading through thick water, the air heavy with something unnameable. Finally, Sophie reached the door. She hesitated, her hand hovering over the knob.

The voice inside grew louder.

“Sophie… Sophie…”

It sounded like her mother, but the voice was all wrong—distorted, fragmented. She couldn’t tell if it was real. But she was certain that the voice had been calling her for ages. It felt urgent. It felt… familiar.

Her breath came in shallow bursts. A part of her knew she needed to turn away, to leave this place and find something else. But she couldn’t. She grasped the doorknob and turned it.

Part 2: A Room of Constant Repetition

Inside the room, everything was white—too white. The walls were stark, featureless, except for one thing: a clock on the far wall. Sophie’s eyes were drawn to it immediately. The ticking was incessant, but it wasn’t the sound that bothered her. It was the fact that the hands of the clock weren’t moving. They were frozen in place, suspended in time, as if the world had decided that time had no meaning anymore.

Her heart pounded as the silence closed in. She tried to look away, but the clock kept pulling her attention, like a magnet.

Tick. Tick. Tick. Tick.

Each tick echoed, but there was no progress. Time was stuck.

“Mom?” Sophie called, her voice trembling.

No answer.

Sophie stepped further into the room, her feet feeling strangely heavy. The floor felt like it was made of soft cotton, each step causing a strange resistance as if the world were unwilling to let her move forward. Her chest felt tight, her breathing labored.

Suddenly, the clock’s ticking stopped. In the silence that followed, Sophie thought she heard something—faint but unmistakable. A voice, again. This time, it sounded clearer. “Don’t forget…”

Sophie’s heart skipped a beat. Forget? Forget what? What was she supposed to remember? Was there something important, something she had missed? Her mind raced, but every thought was fleeting, a blur of disconnected fragments.

Memory loss.

The words surfaced in her mind again, but like before, they slipped away almost immediately. Sophie’s legs wobbled beneath her, and she grabbed the edge of the nearest table to steady herself. She could feel a panic rising in her throat, thick and suffocating. But the more she tried to focus, the more the room seemed to shift around her. The walls distorted, stretching and contracting like a living, breathing organism.

“Sophie…” the voice came again. This time, it was not her mother. It was darker, more distant. “You’re losing yourself.”

Sophie froze. She didn’t understand. Her eyes darted around the room, seeking something—anything—that would make sense of it all. But the walls kept breathing. The clock remained frozen. And that voice… it kept calling her name.

“Who are you?” she whispered, her voice barely a breath.

The figure that stepped out of the shadows didn’t answer. It was a vague shape at first, but it slowly became clearer, more defined. It was her, or at least it looked like her. The figure stood still, its eyes hollow and empty, staring into Sophie’s very soul.

“Is this real?” Sophie asked, a sense of dread coiling in her stomach.

The figure nodded slowly, its movements deliberate and almost mechanical. “This is you, Sophie. You’re in your mind. And I am the you that you’re trying to forget.”

Sophie’s heart pounded in her chest. A wave of nausea swept over her, but she couldn’t look away from the figure. It was as if every instinct in her body was screaming at her to run, but her feet were glued to the ground.

“What do you mean?” Sophie managed to choke out.

The figure’s lips curled into a smile—an expression that was all wrong. “You’ve been running from the truth for so long. But you can’t hide forever.”

Sophie’s eyes filled with tears. “I don’t understand. I don’t remember.”

The figure stepped forward, closer now, until it was right in front of her. Its hollow eyes bored into Sophie’s, and for the first time, Sophie felt something close to a sense of clarity. It was fleeting, but it was enough.

“You don’t have to remember,” the figure whispered. “You only need to face what’s already here.”

The words felt like a slap. Sophie’s knees buckled, and she collapsed to the floor, her body wracked with uncontrollable sobs. The figure remained standing, watching, waiting.

Part 3: The Fragility of the Mind

Sophie didn’t know how long she stayed there, kneeling in the white room. The clock still didn’t move, and the figure remained silent, watching. The pain inside her chest felt like a gaping wound, a raw, bleeding wound that would never heal. Her mind was a labyrinth, a tangled mess of emotions and memories that she couldn’t untangle.

And yet, in that moment, she realized that the answer was always within her reach. The pain, the fear—it had all been there, buried beneath the surface of her mind. She had been running, not from the truth, but from herself. She had been denying what she knew deep down: she wasn’t afraid of forgetting. She was afraid of remembering.

Sophie reached up, her fingers trembling as they brushed against the walls. They were warm now, no longer cold and sterile. The breathing had slowed, and the ticking of the clock had resumed, though it was much quieter this time. The figure had disappeared, leaving behind only the quiet hum of time moving forward.

Sophie stood up, her body feeling heavier than it had before. But for the first time in what felt like forever, she could breathe. The weight in her chest had lightened, and the fog in her mind had begun to clear. It wasn’t gone—nothing was ever truly gone. But she had found something she had lost long ago: acceptance.

She turned toward the door and stepped back into the hallway. The lights flickered for a moment, then steadied. Sophie didn’t know where the hallway would lead her next. But for the first time, she felt ready to move forward. To face whatever came next.

Her journey was far from over. But she would no longer be running.
