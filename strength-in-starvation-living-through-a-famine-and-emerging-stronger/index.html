Strength in Starvation: Living Through a Famine and Emerging Stronger


The sky over Moya cracked with the faded light of dawn, an unsubstantial sheen that painted the mud hut walls a sickly gray. Mere color had drained from the world as if in solidarity with the barrenness that now gripped the land. Nothing green remained. The fields—once alive with trembling millet and corn—now lay bare and grooved only by the wind, occasionally scarred with the fingerprints of desperate hands searching for roots too bitter for even goats to chew.

Naima woke on her mat to the hollow hush that had weighed on the village for months. Hunger was part of her, tangled into her muscles and pressed against her bones. It was there as she pushed herself up, as she checked the pot beside her bed for the impossible—a forgotten scrap of last season’s cassava. There was nothing, only the dry patter of mice along the thatch overhead. Outside, her youngest brother whined in his sleep, a thin shiver echoing through his ribs.

She called his name quietly, “Madu?” afraid to wake her mother, too frail herself to sleep deeply, too weak to console waking children. Madu’s eyelids flickered and then settled back into the tangle of his hunger-haunted dreams.

Naima walked outside, blinking at the breeze, her feet numb ripples against the cooling ground. A thin line of villagers gathered around what used to be the schoolhouse. Its chalkboard, long unmarked, stood witness to a different era, inscribed with the memory of daily lessons: date, weather, crops. Today, people waited instead for the monthly ration truck, each carrying sacks, cans, or in Naima’s case, just her hands—her most reliable tool.

She joined the end of the line, surrounded by whispers and the low drone of resignation. Hunger, she had learned, had a way of hollowing out not just bodies but language, too. Chatter had retreated to grunts and downward glances. She offered the old woman ahead of her a cracked smile and received nothing but a nod in return.

The truck always seemed smaller than it should, even on the rare months when it did not arrive empty. Today it scuttled in, a metal beetle, children’s faces pressed against its tired blue panels. Men began to unload the meager tins: a few kilograms of grain, oil, a packet of dried fish with the taste of forgotten rivers. The schoolmaster called out the usual warnings—“One per household!”—his voice snagging on old dignity.

Naima received her ration—two handfuls of dusty millet and a spoonful of oil in a plastic bottle—practically weightless in her arms. She thanked the schoolmaster out of habit and turned away, her mind whirring with calculations best left unvoiced. Each step back toward her home pressed reality into sharper relief: there would not be enough. Not this time. Not unless she found something else.

She paused at her door, listening for Madu’s breathing and her mother’s ceaseless cough. The hut trembled with every exhale, the fabric of life here stretched thin and tight, ready to snap at the faintest provocation. Her home was not unique. The famine drew invisible boundaries here—between the hungry and the starving, the desperate and those past hope.

That afternoon, she left Madu with their mother and walked out of the village. South of the fields, past the thornbushes and the brittle river bed, the land grew up into the splintered foothills that everyone except starving girls avoided. A childhood of caution clung to Naima’s senses—warnings of snakes, of supernatural dangers, of the kinds of men who hid where crops wouldn’t grow, who had less to lose than even she did now.

Her first hour was fruitless; she sent stones clattering into the dry river and came away with bleeding hands. But she persisted, fingers sifting the earth, eyes sharp for anything edible. Hunger pressed against her—a companion, a spur, a cloud that never left. At the base of a skeletal tree, she found what she had hoped for: a knotted pocket of wild yams, white flesh caught beneath the soil like a secret. She tore at them with her nails and teeth, tasting bitter, fibrous hope.

Back at the hut, Madu was waiting at the doorway, lips dusted with thirst, but eyes keen. Naima brandished the yams—her trophy—and watched his face light up, a flicker of power in a world that insisted she was powerless. Her mother, seeing what she carried, tried to rise but fell back, winded. Naima knelt beside her, smoothing the exhaustion from her brow.

They feasted quietly as night fell, if “feast” could ever define a meal taken between three counted breaths per sticky mouthful. The yams filled their bellies with a raw defiance. Hunger, for a moment, lost its voice.

Days bled into weeks: the rhythm changed, never easy, never whole, but inhabited by new routines. Naima learned where the northern hills yielded crawfish after a rare rain, and where the few trees spun their last tough fruit. Some days, success; others, only the taste of defeat and stinging nettles. She taught Madu what to look for—a certain sheen of green beneath dust, a sign of water under flat stones. Her mother was not growing stronger, but her slow decline was matched now by small surges in Madu’s cheeks and chatter.

When the government truck returned months later, its cargo larger, fatter with foreign charity, it mattered—but less than it might have. The community gathered again to receive their share, and Naima felt the odd, upright ache of pride. She had not simply survived. She had learned to fight, to barter, to dig deeper into the earth and her own limits. She recognized now in the faces of other girls—the hollowed cheeks, the thin defiance—a reflection of herself, but also something more: the glory of persistence, the ugly grace of endurance.

Between those endless hungry days and the echo of relief, Naima learned lessons she could not verbalize, not even to herself. Endurance did not mean the absence of pain, but rising inside it, moving forward with the awareness that nothing was promised except the chance to try again. She found in silence not emptiness, but the attention to see what lay hidden: a sprouting vine, a pulse of water, a glint of kindness in a neighbor’s eyes.

As the village slowly revived—gardens replanted, children running with the awkwardness of hope renewed—Naima carried with her the invisible heft of what she’d endured. People murmured admiration when she brought home more than her share, but it wasn’t bragging she felt. It was the certainty that she could survive more than she once imagined. Madu, now old enough to fetch his own yams, copied her walk, her cautious optimism. She let him, knowing some kinds of strength are contagious.

Years later, when her own children asked about the terrible hunger, Naima did not dwell on misery or preach about gratitude. Instead, she told them of discovering that survival was not a matter of waiting on charity, but of seeking, learning, fighting—with your body, your mind, and your heart. She spoke of evenings where, with empty pots and empty bellies, the family met the darkness together, and by morning, had reason to hope again.

The famine faded, but its lessons remained. Naima rebuilt, not because she forgot hunger, but because she remembered how she had persevered. The fields she replanted grew thick and golden, and each harvest, she offered a silent thanks to the barren hills, the bitter yams, and the strength she’d unearthed in the dust. For in the grip of starvation, she had discovered not just the will to endure, but the power to rise—stronger, wiser, and fiercely alive.
