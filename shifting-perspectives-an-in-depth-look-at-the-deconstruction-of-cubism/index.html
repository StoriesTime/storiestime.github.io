Shifting Perspectives: An In-depth Look at the Deconstruction of Cubism


Cubism, a revolutionary art movement that shook the foundations of visual art in the early 20th century, was built on the idea of exploring and expressing reality through abstract, fragmented perspectives. Through the works of Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque, viewers were presented with a new way of perceiving and understanding their surroundings. However, like all things, cubism, too, underwent a deconstruction, leading art enthusiasts on a journey of shifting perspectives.

The Genesis of Cubism

It was at a dusty, sunlit Parisian cafe in 1907 where Picasso and Braque first conceived the seeds of cubism. They exchanged ideas inspired by African and Iberian sculpture they had seen in local museums. The pair were captivated not only by the shards of fragmented reality in the art but also by the questions raised about visual perception, perspective, and the role of the artist.

In their subsequent cubist paintings, they constructed a new visual lexicon with shattered, fractured planes and non-uniform angles, provoking a radical reimagining of the viewing experience. Through interlocking geometric shapes, they teased out new dimensions of meaning and invited viewers into a world where reality was not a static tableau but a mosaic waiting to be assembled.

From Analytic to Synthetic: The Cubist Spectrum

Cubism was not a stagnant movement, but rather, it evolved and expanded in its expression, articulating itself in two primary styles: Analytical and Synthetic Cubism.

Analytical Cubism, prevalent from 1909 to 1912, focused on breaking down objects into their constituent parts. The fragments were meticulously arranged and depicted in multiple angles, much like a cinematic technique of moving from foreground to background. This analytical approach allowed artists like Picasso and Braque to create a profound sense of depth in their works, seamlessly challenging conventional two-dimensional perspectives.

However, as the Analytical period drew to a close, the cubists began to synthesize their shattered planes into more unified compositions. Synthetic Cubism, emerging around 1912, prioritized overlapping planes, fabrics, and textures, opening doors to a more abstract representation and a multilayered narrative - like a palimpsest waiting to be deciphered. The Synthetic Cubist works buzzed with a coherent vitality, evoking the roles of an orchestra, where each element played in harmony, but also contrasted with a unique melody.

Cubism: A Catalyst for Deconstruction

Ultimately, as new genres and movements replaced cubism, it was inevitable that the foundations of this revolutionary art form would be deconstructed.

In the 1920s, Bauhaus artists and constructivists transcended the abstract of cubism and applied it to functional design, while Surrealists rebuilt its dream-like scenarios. Deconstruction happened not as a refusal of cubism’s principles but as a natural progression in the ever-evolving landscape of world art.

In abstract expressionism, artists like Jackson Pollock turned away from the geometric structures of cubism toward the pure act of painting. Yet, cubism’s impact remained, as his dripping, splattering and pushing mediums echoed cubism’s fragmentation of space.

As the cubist mount continued to shift, it influenced the social realism and electrifying color fields of the postmodern era, adding further validation to the premise that cubism was not an island unto itself but rather an immense bridge to multiple other universes.

Conclusion: A Continuing Legacy

The deconstruction of cubism opened doors for infinite possibilities and creatively challenged traditional visual principles, shifting perspectives in art, architecture, design, and beyond.

Cubists like Picasso, Braque, Fernand Léger, and Juan Gris made a profound mark on the world by displaying the countless facets of reality they chose not to fully piece back together. Their work not only influenced the art world but also trickled down to the broader sphere of aesthetics and design.

In the end, cubism remains a testament to the human spirit’s ability to perceive layers of reality, continuously reshape perspectives, and always explore what lies beyond the immediate view. As we shift our perspectives, always onward, we embrace the journey towards a rich and layered understanding of our ever-evolving world.
