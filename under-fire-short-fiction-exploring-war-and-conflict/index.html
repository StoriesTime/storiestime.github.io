Under Fire: Short Fiction Exploring War and Conflict


The rain had been falling in sheets for hours, hammering down on the dense, muddy earth that had long since lost any semblance of solid ground. The sound was deafening, a relentless patter that drowned out everything except the occasional crack of gunfire in the distance. It was the kind of night where even the moon seemed afraid to show its face, hiding behind dark clouds that twisted above the battlefield like an ominous veil.

Corporal Sam Carter crouched low, his chest heaving in short bursts as he breathed in the damp air. His hands gripped his rifle tightly, the cold metal a reminder of the task ahead. His unit had been tasked with holding a small village on the outskirts of the frontline—a place that was supposed to be of little strategic value. But in this war, even the smallest towns became crucial battlefields.

He glanced around at his comrades, their faces grim in the dim light, illuminated only by the flashes of artillery fire in the distance. They had been here for three days, holed up in a makeshift defensive position built out of sandbags and shattered wood, surrounded on all sides by the enemy. The situation was dire, and they all knew it. Reinforcements were days away, and in the meantime, they were left to hold the line with nothing more than sheer will and dwindling supplies.

Sam wiped the rain from his face and checked his watch. It was 0200 hours, and they had just received a fresh wave of intel. Enemy forces were preparing for a major push, and this time, the attack wouldn’t be a simple bombardment. It would be a ground assault—one that would likely overwhelm them if they weren’t ready.

“Get the men in position,” Sam ordered in a low voice to Sergeant Turner, who had been with him since the beginning. Turner nodded, his face a portrait of exhaustion, but the fire in his eyes never seemed to fade.

The tension was thick, like the air just before a storm. Sam could feel it in his bones. They were outnumbered and outgunned, but there was one thing that kept him going: the thought of what would happen if they failed. If this village fell, it wouldn’t just be a loss of land—it would open the path for a full-scale invasion that could wipe out entire regions.

The enemy knew that too. That’s why they were coming.

Private Leo Miller was a young soldier, barely eighteen, but he’d seen enough death and destruction to last a lifetime. His uniform, once pristine, was now caked in mud, and his face had a permanent look of weariness, as if he hadn’t slept in days. His hands trembled slightly as he adjusted his helmet, a futile gesture in a war where nothing—least of all his appearance—could make a difference anymore.

Leo had joined the army to escape the poverty of his small hometown, but now, under the constant threat of fire, he wondered if it had been worth it. Every battle he fought, every life he took, seemed to weigh more heavily on his soul. He’d been trained to survive, but survival felt like a hollow victory in a war that no one seemed to understand.

“Leo, get your head in the game,” Sam’s voice cut through his thoughts. Leo nodded quickly, snapping back to the reality of the moment. The enemy was coming, and there was no room for hesitation.

They had spent hours digging in, fortifying their position, but it was clear now that no amount of sandbags or barbed wire would protect them from the inevitable. The enemy was coming—fast and hard.

“Sir,” Turner spoke up, his voice grave. “We’ve got scouts on the east ridge. They’ll be on us in twenty minutes, maybe less.”

Sam exhaled sharply. He had known this day would come, but that didn’t make it any easier. “We hold here. No retreat.”

Turner didn’t argue. He knew better. Sam’s orders were always followed without question, not out of blind loyalty, but because he earned it. He led from the front, never asking anything from his men that he wasn’t willing to do himself.

The minutes stretched on like hours, and every sound seemed to grow louder—footsteps in the mud, the distant chatter of enemy rifles, the pounding of artillery far off. Leo’s heart pounded in his chest, and the world seemed to slow down around him. He gripped his rifle tighter, forcing himself to focus.

“Get ready,” Sam barked, his voice cutting through the tension. “They’ll hit us hard. Stay low, stay tight, and for God’s sake, watch your ammo.”

Leo’s throat tightened. He wasn’t sure he could hold it together much longer. The weight of the rifle, the uncertainty of survival, the fear of what would happen next—it was all too much. But he couldn’t stop now. He had to keep moving, keep fighting. He thought of his family back home, of the life he had left behind. The thought kept him going.

The first wave hit them just as Sam had predicted—fast and brutal. The enemy soldiers came from all sides, moving like shadows through the rain, their dark figures silhouetted against the flashes of gunfire. The world erupted in chaos.

“Fire!” Sam shouted, and the air was filled with the deafening roar of gunshots.

Leo fired his rifle, the recoil jarring his shoulder, but he didn’t pause. He fired again, and again. He didn’t think about the enemy anymore—he didn’t think about the faces he couldn’t see or the lives he was taking. He just shot, moving his body on autopilot as if his actions were controlled by some force outside himself.

The sound of grenades exploding nearby shook the ground beneath them, and Sam’s voice called out over the noise. “Get to the north wall, now!”

They had to move, had to reposition, but the enemy was already pushing forward, forcing them back. Leo’s legs were like lead, his body aching from the strain. He stumbled but kept moving, following the others, trying not to think about the men who had fallen along the way.

By the time dawn broke, the village was nearly lost. The landscape was scarred with smoke and fire, the remnants of buildings that had once been homes now reduced to ruins. The enemy had breached the outer defenses, and it was clear that they wouldn’t stop until they had taken everything.

Sam stood at the edge of the village, his rifle in hand, his face covered in dirt and blood. He was exhausted, his body sore from hours of fighting, but his mind was clear. The mission wasn’t over. Not yet.

“Turner, Miller,” he said quietly. “We need to fall back. We’ll make a stand at the old church.”

Turner nodded, his face pale but resolute. Leo didn’t speak—he didn’t have the strength to. His mind was numb, and his body felt like it belonged to someone else.

They fought their way to the church, the sound of gunfire echoing in the narrow streets. Sam knew it was only a matter of time before they were overrun, but he wasn’t about to give up without a fight. They would make the enemy pay for every inch of ground they took.

As they reached the church, Sam looked back one last time at the battlefield. The village was falling, but they still had one final chance to hold their ground. It was a long shot, but they were soldiers, and giving up wasn’t in their blood.

The doors of the church groaned as they pushed them open, and Sam led his men inside, barricading the entrance. They would wait for the inevitable assault, hoping against hope that reinforcements would arrive in time.

But as the hours ticked by, the sound of enemy movement grew closer. Sam stood, his back pressed against the cold stone walls, waiting for the moment when they would have to make their final stand.

The final wave came at dusk. The enemy surged forward with everything they had left, determined to break the last line of defense. Sam and his men fought with everything they had, their bodies on the verge of collapse, their minds running on fumes.

The battle raged for hours, the church shaking with the force of artillery strikes, the air thick with smoke and the stench of blood. Leo could barely keep his eyes open, his body screaming for rest, but he kept firing. He had no choice. He couldn’t stop now.

Finally, as the sun began to set, the enemy began to retreat. The battle was over.

Sam stood amidst the wreckage, breathing heavily, his face grim but determined. They had held the line, but at a heavy cost. The village was lost, and many of their comrades had fallen, but they had survived.

For now.

As the rain began to fall once more, Sam looked out across the battlefield, knowing that the war was far from over. But for the first time in days, he allowed himself a moment to breathe. They had made it through the fire. And maybe, just maybe, there was hope for tomorrow.
