The Color of Truth: A Short Story on Racial Injustice


The sun hung low, casting long shadows across the streets of Willow Creek. It was the kind of small town where everyone knew everyone else’s name, and news traveled faster than the breeze on a hot day. Yet, despite the familiarity and close-knit community, Willow Creek was a place divided—not by geography, but by an invisible line drawn between the haves and the have-nots, between those with power and those without.

And at the heart of this division was the color of a man’s skin.

Jared Thompson had spent the first 20 years of his life in Willow Creek, and he knew the town like the back of his hand. He could tell you where the best fishing spots were, which diner served the best apple pie, and how to avoid the potholes on Maple Street. But none of that mattered now. He had left, made something of himself, and returned not out of choice, but because of his father’s death.

The old man had passed away in the night, his heart giving out after years of struggling to breathe under the weight of his own anger. Anger at the system. Anger at the world that had kept him in his place for so long. Jared had been estranged from him for years, their relationship frayed by old wounds and unresolved arguments. But now, standing at the doorstep of his childhood home, Jared couldn’t help but feel a mix of resentment and regret.

“Jared, I… I’m sorry I didn’t make it to the funeral,” said a voice from behind him.

Jared turned to find his cousin, Maya, standing there. She was just a few years younger than him, but the years had been kinder to her. She was still the vibrant, determined woman he remembered, though there was a sadness in her eyes now, one that hadn’t been there before.

“It’s not your fault,” he replied quietly, his gaze fixed on the weathered front door. “I didn’t expect anyone to show up.”

Maya sighed, walking up to stand beside him. “I know how hard it was for you. But you know he didn’t want to die alone.”

Jared nodded but said nothing. There were too many things left unsaid between him and his father. Too many years of silence, too many missed opportunities. The words didn’t seem to matter anymore. His father was gone, and with him went the chance to make things right.

“Are you staying long?” Maya asked after a moment.

Jared shook his head. “Just long enough to take care of things. I’ll be heading back to the city in a few days.”

Maya didn’t press further. She knew how much Jared hated this place—the judgment, the whispers, the unspoken rules that governed everything. But she also knew that Willow Creek was a part of him, whether he wanted to admit it or not.

“Okay,” she said softly, her voice laced with a quiet concern. “If you need anything, you know where to find me.”

Jared didn’t respond. Instead, he turned and walked into the house, leaving Maya standing on the porch, watching as the man she once knew as her cousin disappeared into the darkness of the past.

The funeral had been small. A few old friends of his father’s, some distant relatives, and a handful of townsfolk who had come out of a sense of obligation. Jared had never been comfortable in the presence of grief, especially not his own. It wasn’t until the service was over and the guests began to disperse that he noticed a familiar face in the crowd—Officer Carter.

Carter had been Willow Creek’s sheriff for as long as Jared could remember, a constant figure in the town’s life. He was a man of few words, but when he spoke, people listened. Jared had always respected him, even as a child, though he had never quite understood why the sheriff had always seemed to hold a certain distance from his family.

“Jared,” Carter called out as he approached him. “I didn’t expect to see you back here.”

Jared gave a small, weary smile. “I didn’t expect to be here either, but life doesn’t always give you choices.”

Carter nodded, his expression unreadable. “I know. Your father was a proud man. He wasn’t one to accept help from anyone, even when he needed it most.”

Jared looked away, trying to find the right words. “I wish I could have done more. I wish I could have—”

“Don’t,” Carter interrupted gently. “You did what you could. But there’s something I need to ask you. Something that’s been weighing on my mind.”

Jared looked up, surprised by the seriousness in the sheriff’s tone. “What’s that?”

“It’s about your father,” Carter said slowly, as if weighing each word carefully. “I’ve always known him to be… difficult. But lately, I’ve been wondering if there was more to the story than we all realized. Something deeper.”

Jared frowned, confused. “What are you talking about?”

Carter hesitated before speaking again. “There’s been talk around town… whispers, mostly. People think your father was involved in something more than just his usual grumbling and complaining. Something illegal.”

Jared’s heart skipped a beat. “What kind of illegal activity?”

“I can’t say for sure,” Carter replied, “but I’ve been looking into it. Your father may have had some connections, some dealings that went beyond the law. And I’m worried that it might have something to do with your family.”

Jared stood frozen, his mind racing. His father, a man who had spent most of his life railing against the system, a man who had spent years fighting for fairness, for justice… Could he really have been involved in something so dark?

“I don’t believe it,” Jared said firmly, though his voice betrayed a hint of doubt.

“I didn’t want to believe it either,” Carter admitted, “but the more I dig, the more it seems like there’s something there. I just wanted you to know, in case… in case you needed to be careful.”

Jared stood still for a long moment, processing what Carter had said. Could the man who had raised him, the man who had taught him right from wrong, really have been hiding a secret life? The more he thought about it, the more the pieces began to fall into place. The late-night phone calls, the long drives to the city, the unexplained meetings.

“I’ll look into it,” Jared said at last, his voice hardening with resolve. “But I need you to stay out of it. If my father was involved in something, I’ll deal with it.”

Carter nodded, though the concern in his eyes didn’t fade. “I’ll respect your wishes. But just remember, the truth isn’t always what it seems. And sometimes, it’s buried so deep that you can’t see it until it’s too late.”

The days that followed were a blur of paperwork, phone calls, and forgotten memories. Jared spent hours going through his father’s things, sifting through old receipts, letters, and photographs. He uncovered bits and pieces of his father’s life, things he had never known about before—things he wasn’t sure he wanted to know.

Then, one evening, he found it.

A small envelope tucked away in the back of a drawer. Inside was a single photograph, faded with age. It was a picture of his father standing with a group of men—some familiar, others not—standing in front of a building Jared didn’t recognize. A building with the letters “A.C.T.” painted across the top.

Jared’s hands trembled as he stared at the photo. He had no idea what “A.C.T.” stood for, but he knew it was something important. Something that had been hidden from him, from the rest of the world. Something that might explain everything.

Determined to find out more, Jared began to dig deeper. He contacted old friends of his father’s, visited the library, and even went so far as to speak with the people in town who had known him best. Slowly, the truth began to surface.

A.C.T. stood for the “Alliance for Civil Truth,” a small, underground organization that had fought for racial equality and justice in the 1960s and 1970s. It was a group that had pushed for change, but in doing so, had attracted the attention of powerful people who didn’t want that change to happen. Jared’s father had been a part of that group, fighting for what he believed was right, even if it meant putting himself and his family at risk.

And as Jared pieced together the story, he realized something else—that the fight for truth and justice never ended. It simply evolved, taking on new faces and new challenges, but always driven by the same desire: to right the wrongs of the world.

The truth, Jared learned, was not always black and white. It wasn’t always easy to understand, and it often required a great deal of courage to confront. But once he understood that, once he accepted that the truth was something he had to fight for, no matter what it cost him, he found a new sense of purpose.

As the days went on, Jared stayed in Willow Creek, not to mourn the loss of his father, but to honor his legacy. The legacy of a man who had fought for truth, for justice, for equality, even when it seemed like the world was against him. And in doing so, Jared found his own place in the fight.

In the end, the color of truth was not defined by the skin we were born into, but by the actions we take, the choices we make, and the courage to stand up when it matters most.

And that, Jared realized, was something worth fighting for.

End.
