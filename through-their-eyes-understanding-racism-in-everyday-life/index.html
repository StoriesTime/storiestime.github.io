Through Their Eyes: Understanding Racism in Everyday Life


Lena had always considered herself a thoughtful person. She prided herself on being kind, fair, and aware of the issues that shaped the world around her. As a young professional in her mid-thirties, Lena had worked hard to build a reputation as a reliable and compassionate individual in her community. She lived in a diverse neighborhood, often spending weekends with friends at local markets or in the park, talking about everything from books to politics. She felt that she was open-minded, never hesitant to listen to different perspectives. But one morning, something happened that made her question everything she thought she knew about herself and the world.

It was a warm, sunny Tuesday when Lena ran into an old friend, Kira, at the coffee shop. Kira had grown up in the same town as Lena, but their paths had diverged after high school. Kira had left to attend a prestigious university in another city, while Lena had stayed behind, working at the same company she’d joined straight out of college. They hadn’t spoken much in recent years, so Lena was surprised when Kira approached her with a big smile.

“Lena! It’s so good to see you!” Kira exclaimed, giving her a warm hug. “How have you been?”

Lena smiled back, glad to reconnect. They sat down at a corner table and began chatting about old times. As they spoke, Kira shared her experiences living in a city that was far more racially diverse than the one they had grown up in.

“I’ve learned a lot,” Kira said thoughtfully. “It’s made me more aware of things I didn’t notice before. Things that we just… took for granted.”

Lena nodded. “Like what?” she asked, genuinely curious.

Kira hesitated for a moment, her eyes distant. “Well, racism for one. But it’s not always what you might think. It’s subtle, insidious. It shows up in the small moments. I started noticing things I didn’t see before, things that I didn’t experience growing up in our hometown.”

Lena’s brow furrowed. She had always prided herself on being open-minded. She had never considered herself prejudiced or discriminatory. In fact, she had worked with colleagues from different ethnic backgrounds for years, always promoting equality and fairness in her professional life.

“I’m not sure I understand what you mean,” Lena said slowly, sensing a shift in the conversation.

Kira took a deep breath. “It’s hard to explain, but think about it this way: every day, people like me—people of color—have to navigate the world with a different set of rules. We walk into stores, we walk down the street, and we’re often judged by the way we look. People don’t always realize it, but the way they treat us is shaped by their perceptions of race.”

Lena shifted uncomfortably in her seat. She had heard about racism in the news and from friends, but Kira’s words felt personal, more intimate. Lena had never experienced it herself, so it was difficult to understand.

“I don’t see color,” Lena said, almost reflexively. “I treat everyone the same.”

Kira looked at her, a faint smile tugging at the corners of her lips. “I used to think that way too. It sounds nice, doesn’t it? But not seeing color means you don’t see the challenges that people of color face every single day. You don’t recognize the biases that influence the way people act toward you and the way you act toward others.”

Lena felt a lump form in her throat. She had never thought about it that way. She had always prided herself on being the type of person who treated everyone equally, but what did that really mean in a world that was far from equal?

“Can you give me an example?” Lena asked, her voice quieter now.

Kira nodded, her eyes earnest. “Sure. Last year, I was shopping at a department store. I was just browsing, trying on clothes, and I noticed this woman following me around. She didn’t do it to anyone else—just me. She didn’t say anything, but I knew. She was watching me, assuming I was going to steal something.”

Lena’s eyes widened. “Did you say something to her?”

Kira shook her head. “No, I didn’t. I just left. But that feeling, Lena, that feeling of being watched, of being assumed guilty before I even did anything, is something that happens all the time. It’s not just one bad experience. It’s hundreds of little moments where I’m treated differently because of the color of my skin.”

Lena felt a sting in her chest. She had never considered how deeply these experiences could affect someone. She thought of the times she had walked into a store, confidently browsing through the aisles without a second thought, never once imagining that someone might be watching her, questioning her motives. But Kira had experienced that feeling many times over, and it wasn’t an isolated incident.

“I didn’t know,” Lena murmured. “I didn’t know it was like that.”

Kira looked at her with a mixture of sadness and understanding. “That’s the thing, Lena. Most people don’t. It’s easy to ignore when you’re not the one facing it. But racism isn’t just about hate or overt discrimination. It’s the everyday things, the assumptions people make, the way they look at you, the things they say without thinking. It’s in the way you’re treated when you’re applying for jobs, the way you’re greeted at a restaurant, the way people move out of the way when you’re walking down the street. It’s all of those small moments that add up to a bigger picture.”

Lena sat silently for a moment, processing Kira’s words. It was one thing to read about racism in books or hear about it from the news, but hearing Kira’s story, her personal experience, made it real. She could no longer pretend that racism was something distant, something that happened to other people in faraway places.

“I never thought about it like that,” Lena finally said, her voice tinged with guilt. “I guess I’ve always just… assumed that if I treated everyone the same, then racism wouldn’t exist. But now I see that it’s more complicated than that.”

Kira smiled gently. “It is complicated. But acknowledging it is the first step. We all have biases, Lena. Even if we don’t want to admit it. But recognizing them, being aware of them, and trying to change the way we think and act—that’s how we can make a difference. It’s not about being perfect. It’s about being willing to learn and grow.”

Lena nodded slowly, her mind swirling with new thoughts. She had always considered herself an ally, but now she understood that being an ally meant more than just saying the right things. It meant listening, understanding, and taking action, even in the small moments that might seem insignificant to others.

Over the following weeks, Lena found herself reflecting on Kira’s words. She became more aware of the subtle ways that racism showed up in her everyday life. At the grocery store, she noticed the way the cashier greeted her with a friendly smile, but barely acknowledged the man of color standing behind her in line. At work, she began to notice how often her ideas were dismissed in meetings, only to be later validated when a male colleague said the same thing. She started paying attention to the language people used, the jokes they told, and the assumptions they made about people based on their appearance.

The more Lena observed, the more she realized how deeply ingrained these biases were in the world around her. It wasn’t enough to simply not be racist; she needed to actively challenge the prejudices that existed in her own mind and in the society she lived in.

One afternoon, Lena invited Kira over for coffee. She wanted to talk more, to ask questions and gain a deeper understanding of the complexities of racism. As they sat together, Lena shared her thoughts and feelings about what she had learned over the past few weeks.

“I’ve been thinking a lot about what you said,” Lena began, her voice thoughtful. “I see it now. It’s not just about individual acts of racism; it’s the systems and structures that uphold inequality. The way society is built makes it harder for some people to succeed, to feel safe, to be treated with dignity.”

Kira nodded. “Exactly. Racism isn’t just about individuals being mean or hateful. It’s about how the world is structured in ways that give some people power and opportunities, while denying those same things to others.”

Lena leaned forward, her mind racing with new ideas. “I want to do more. I want to be a part of changing things, but I don’t know where to start.”

Kira smiled warmly. “The first step is what you’re doing right now—listening. But then, you have to take that awareness and use it to make a difference. It’s about having tough conversations, calling out discrimination when you see it, supporting policies that promote equality, and advocating for those who are marginalized. Every small action counts.”

Lena took a deep breath, feeling both overwhelmed and inspired. She knew that understanding racism in everyday life wasn’t something that would happen overnight. It would take time, effort, and a willingness to confront her own biases. But she was ready. She was ready to see the world through different eyes.

The journey of understanding racism in everyday life is not easy. It requires self-reflection, empathy, and the courage to challenge long-held beliefs. But it’s a journey that can lead to a more just and equitable world, where all people are treated with dignity and respect, regardless of their race.
