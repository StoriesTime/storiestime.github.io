The Ghost Who Haunts the Old Lighthouse


At the very tip of Breakwater Point, where the rocks shatter the fury of northern waves, the old Stonebriar Lighthouse still stands sentry against the horizon. Locals say its keeper never left, even after the lantern was extinguished for the last time. They say that on fog-heavy nights, when the moon balances on the lip of the world and the gulls nest in silence, the keeper’s lamp still burns in the tower’s glass eye, and a shadow paces slowly, ever vigilant for ships that no longer sail.

For the villages scattered along the windswept coast, the lighthouse is both a warning and a memory. Children dare each other to sprint up the cracked steps; fishermen mark limits in the sand, pretending not to watch the swing of phantom light across distant waves. In all seasons, the stones whisper tales—of storms, shipwrecks, and the lonely ghost of Keeper Whitlaw: the man who would not abandon his post, in death or in life.

Chapter 1: Stone and Salt

Mara Delaney had grown up with her face pressed to the window during autumn storms, watching the old beacon dissolve in rain. Now, a decade gone from her childhood in Gullhaven, she returned by bus and foot to the rooms over her Aunt Edie’s bakery, seeking respite from the fracture of a life that seemed to split new fault lines every month. The bakery was as she remembered—sea-glass jars of barley sweets, golden loaves cooling behind misted glass, cinnamon rolled thick as winter scarves.

But it was the lighthouse that drew her. Each evening, after closing, Mara would walk the bramble path past the jetty and gaze up at the charcoal silhouette against the faded swirl of the sky. The stones were weather-smooth, each blackened by brine and lichen, and the door—half-hung, warped—yawned its greeting to those bold or foolish enough to enter.

On her second week home, Mara found herself overcome by the same half-terror and half-yearning that had haunted her at eleven: she wanted to see the ghost. Or, if there was no ghost, to know the reason the lighthouse remained such an ache in every story, every night’s tale at the Anchor’s Rest.

Chapter 2: Keeper’s Light

Inside, the lighthouse smelled older than the sea. Driftwood and old oil, salt and rust, and something underneath that hinted at paper and old wool, like someone’s abandoned jacket left to dissolve on a chair. Mara traced the spiral stair—iron, corroded at the edges—lifting her hands from the rail wherever the sea had gnawed through the metal.

At the top, the lantern room was hushed. Its glass was streaked with caked salt from years without cleaning, yet the view reached, undeterred, across the entire sweep of bay and headland. Mara’s fingers found shapes in dust on the brass casings where the great lamp rested, dark since 1951.

It was then, among the ghosts of silence, that she heard it: the slow, deliberate tread of boots, echoing up from below. Her first thought was an intruder; her second, a kind of dread anticipation.

“Who’s there?” she called, though her voice barely cleared her throat.

A reply came, quiet and slightly hoarse, as if coughing up the dust of decades.

“Old habits die hard, Miss. Not even the dead can break ‘em.”

A figure stepped from the stairwell—a man, tall, gaunt, a shadow outlined as much by memory as by the pale blue light leaking in from evening. He wore the uniform Mara remembered from the painting in the bakery’s back room: navy coat, brass buttons, a cap perched at a tilt.

“You’re—”

“Whitlaw,” the ghost said, with a faint, tired smile.

His eyes were grey, not unkind, though they flickered with the trembling unease of one long-exiled from company.

Chapter 3: Storm Lantern

A hundred questions crowded Mara’s tongue, but the ghost simply sat on the low bench beside the lamp, gazing out to sea. Not for the first time, her rational mind rebelled—there was surely some trick of the light, or a stranger who’d taken refuge in tales that kept most at arm’s length. But beneath it, the marrow-deep certainty that she was neither dreaming nor deceived.

“You know people come here to see you,” Mara said finally, sitting opposite on the bare floorboards.

“I know.” His smile was rueful. “Why shouldn’t they? Ghosts—expectations—what else do we have left to haunt, if not those?”

They fell silent. Below, through storm-hardened glass, fog curled around the rocks and the last saffron glow receded into the west.

Whitlaw did not ask who Mara was, why she’d returned. He understood, she thought, the ways brokenness leads one back along old paths, seeking answers in the unsolvable.

“There was a shipwreck, wasn’t there? Right before… before you—”

He nodded. “1951. The Gannet. Caught out in a nor’easter. Lost twin masts within sight of the lantern. I sounded the horn, flagged the signal—all the codes drilled through thirty years—but my light failed. Flooded the housing. Couldn’t save ‘em.”

The admission stilled the air. Mara remembered the village stories—of a keeper so shamed by his imagined failure that his soul could not leave.

“It wasn’t your fault,” she said softly, feeling the inadequacy of words.

“It’s a funny thing,” Whitlaw answered. “A man can spend a life doing his duty, shaping routine out of the chaos, and still, the sea chooses what it will take.”

Chapter 4: Night Watch

Mara began to visit often. Sometimes she brought tea in a thermos, black and hot, pouring two cups though only one ever cooled. She read aloud from the yellowed logbooks stacked behind the bakery’s cooker, or simply sat while Whitlaw tended his imagined rounds—dusting glass, correcting an oil painting of passing ships in the air.

If there was a lesson in their ritual, it was not one Mara dared name. The past, she found, is never truly exorcised. Instead, it accompanies each of us, a quiet footfall, a shadow in the stair. Whitlaw monitored his lighthouse, not out of punishment, but habit, as if his commitment could balance one day’s catastrophe with a thousand nights of vigilance.

As summer aged into fall, Mara’s wounds closed slowly. The lighthouse, with its stubborn defiance against the elements, became talisman and confessional. She learned the whistle of distant buoys, the sharp chill of salt mist against skin, the layered hush of the world just before dawn.

Sometimes, Whitlaw told stories. He spoke of his wife, who brought him knitted socks and letters wrapped in oilcloth; of dogs who chased his shadow up and down the spiral steps; of sailors greeted by his lantern, their cheers lost to the wind. He spoke of the ‘Gannet’ most of all, not as a tragedy, but as a night that marked him, tethered him to this echo of existence.

Chapter 5: Lantern’s End

The season’s change culminated in a storm unlike any Mara had endured. The wind howled through loose panes, and every window in Gullhaven rattled with the heartbeat of the sea’s fury.

That night, Mara found Whitlaw at his post, lantern lit—a globe of impossible, spectral fire that shimmered, casting its reach far past the cove. From the rocks below came voices, real and spectral, layered and mutable as the wind. Mara stood beside him, her hand steady on the old brass railing. For a moment, the boundaries between living and dead blurred; Mara saw, not the forlorn keeper, but a man steadfast, refusing despair, gripping duty as if it were the last rope in a sinking world.

When dawn broke, the light faded. Whitlaw slumped, exhaustion limning the hard edges of his form.

“You’ve kept watch long enough,” Mara said.

He looked at her—not quite hopeful, but quiet, grateful. “Perhaps. But one always wonders, if we leave our posts, who fills the gap?”

“There’s always someone,” Mara replied. “And maybe it’s time you rest.”

For the first time, he removed his cap, running spectral fingers through hair that shimmered, faded. “Would you—” His words trailed off. “If I go, promise me: remember the lighthouse. Let it stand for hope, not just loss.”

Mara nodded. She meant it.

The light blinked off. In the hush of the lantern room, only Mara remained.

Chapter 6: Keeper’s Legacy

Newsworkers and amateur historians came, as always, searching stories. Some claimed the lighthouse was less haunted, that the chill wind eased its bite. Mara let them judge. For her, she felt Whitlaw’s vigil echo only in memory: a phantom comfort, a relief from grief she would never quite lay to rest.

Years passed. Mara became keeper in her own way—organizing a small fund for repairs, guiding children through the cramped tower, retelling Whitlaw’s tale with neither embellishment nor excuse. She did not chase ghosts, nor fear them; she learned, as Keeper Whitlaw had, that devotion shapes meaning as surely as the sea shapes stone.

At night, when the storms rolled in, if Mara felt alone in the old tower, she would stand where Whitlaw had stood, looking out across the water. No ghostly lantern burned, but now and then, a far-off light blinked through the fog; a ship, making its slow way home.

And on those nights, Mara would smile. The past had not left her, nor she it. Together, they watched the world spin forward, ever vigilant, ever enduring, beneath the silent, ancient warning of the old lighthouse.

Stonebriar Lighthouse endured, its legacy a tapestry of hope stitched over the bones of sorrow. Mara’s story, and Whitlaw’s, lingered in every crash of wave and hiss of kelp on stone, written not in marble, but in the stories passed by wind and water. In each retelling, the ghost kept his watch—but so too did those who remembered.
