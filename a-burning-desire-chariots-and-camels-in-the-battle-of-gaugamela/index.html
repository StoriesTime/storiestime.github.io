A Burning Desire: Chariots and Camels in the Battle of Gaugamela


In the year 331 BC, on the infamous plains of Gaugamela, a burning desire drove an ambitious young charioteer named Arimanes to stake his destiny in the greatest battle of his time. The armies of Alexander the Great and King Darios of Persia were about to clash for the ultimate prize of undefeated supremacy.

Arimanes stood impatiently on the dusty ground, gazing at his well-groomed fleet of horses. The golden sun added a radiant touch to their otherwise robust, dark coats. With each horse boasting their own individual personality, Arimanes saw them not just as tools, but real extensions of himself. He glanced at his plated armor, attached with a velvet lining. He’d always been good at reading the room, but today, Arimanes felt he was about to garner the greatest read of his life. Gaugamela was his moment, his chance to rise above the Milk Meadow farmers and fishermen, the scouts, frontline, and reserves. This fight was going to be won on the chasm between prediction and innovation.

The battle began with Alexander the Great launching his cavalry charge. The ruckus grew louder as dust-clouds filled the air. The clanging of weapons, the whinnying of galloping horses, and the distant roar of Eastern warriors echoed throughout the battlefield. Slinging his reins with masterful finesse, Arimanes analyzed his adversary, a formidable Persian camel-riding warrior named Zargamus. As warriors clashed and horses reared, spear tips glinting in the sun, Arimanes would outflank Zargamus, driving his adversaries’ spirits to an even lower ebb.

To many, the Greek doctors and courtiers would recommend scaring the enemy, and so, Arimanes laid out his cunning plan. Initially, he set aside his bow and cloak, keeping only his sword and helmet. By swiftly manipulating his chariot’s reins, he’d lured Zargamus into a tumultuous clash. As the great Persian soldier approached the young Greek skilfully, he easily saw through their decoys, knowing they sought psychological leverage. To the naked eye, the Greek soldiers appeared vulnerable and feeble, despite their well-armed and cunning nature.

But Arimanes wasn’t like his comrades; he had consumed provisions of courage before the battle, fine wines emboldened with wild herbs. As Zargamus approached closer, he was encompassed by waves of fear embodied in the advancing mixed forces of Greek warriors. Something peculiar happened then; Zargamus’ camel sensed Arimanes’ burning desire and halted. Perhaps it sensed the craftsman-like patience Arimanes had applied to train his horses, empathy for the noble creature ran through his veins.

Seizing the opportunity, Arimanes galloped at Zargamus with lightning speed. But just then, a bolt of legalism in Arimanes’ mind took its course; he refrained from fatal assault, choosing instead to make Zargamus flee.

“The world will witness a battle of unprecedented scale,” Arimanes said, “You forget to preserve your troops’ lives even as they are spent in the most effective kind of killing.”

As the dust settled, the outcome of the Battle of Gaugamela lay in the individualistic accounts of soldiers like Arimanes and Zargamus. These personal decisions would determine the fate of empires. Great battles were not just won at the edge of sword and shield, but also within the heart, on the precipice of decision. Their parable would echo through history, serving as a monument to the strengths of the human spirit and the power of choice in the face of destiny. One’s burning desires may drive them into the heat of battle, but the wisdom within us all may be the key to not only victory but also lasting peace.
