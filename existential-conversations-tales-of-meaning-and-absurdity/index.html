Existential Conversations: Tales of Meaning and Absurdity


The sun was dipping low, casting long shadows over the cracked pavement of a quiet city park bench. Two strangers sat facing one another, though neither had intended to engage when they first arrived. The park at twilight was neither busy nor empty — a strange liminal space, much like the moments between sleep and waking, where the ordinary bends subtly into the extraordinary.

“You ever wonder why we’re here?” the younger of the two asked, his voice soft but unwavering, as if the question was both a burden and a secret dare.

The older man looked up slowly from his worn leather journal. He was gray-haired, but with a kind of brightness to his eyes that suggested long, restless nights of pondering. “What do you mean, ‘why we’re here’? In this park, or just… here, in life?”

The young man smiled wryly. “Both, I think. It’s strange, isn’t it? How we spend so much of our time chasing one thing or another, and yet, in the end, it feels like we’re grasping at shadows. We pin our hopes, our meaning, to things that seem solid but maybe are just… illusions.”

The older man nodded, tapping his pen thoughtfully against his journal. “You’re touching on something humans have wrestled with for millennia — the search for meaning. Some find it in faith, others in art, some in science. But the absurdity is that there’s no universal answer. You can stare into the void long enough, and the void responds with silence… or sometimes with laughter.”

“Laughter?” the young man echoed, intrigued.

“Yes,” the elder smiled. “The absurd isn’t just despair or nihilism. It’s the moment you recognize the vast randomness of existence, yet decide to laugh at it, dance with it, create meaning anyway.”

The young man stared at the fading light. “It’s comforting, in a way. To think that meaning isn’t something handed down or found, but something we wrestle out of the chaos. It makes life feel… freer, somehow.”

“Exactly. Think of it this way: life might be a story without an intended author, a narrative constantly rewritten, even page by page. That can be terrifying or liberating, depending on how you choose to see it. I find it’s a bit of both.”

They fell silent, two minds turning tricky kaleidoscopes of thought in the fading day.

It was the second conversation they would share that week, in the same spot, after a chance introduction.

“Yesterday, I told you that life is a story without an author," the older man began, “But perhaps the story is also absurd because we expect it to be coherent, meaningful in a grand sense. What if the story is designed to be chaotic?”

The young man laughed, an unforced sound. “Then we’re fools for trying to predict the ending, aren’t we?”

“Maybe,” the elder agreed. “Or maybe that’s the fun of it — the unknown, the surprises. The absurdity gives us the freedom to invent meaning in ways that defy logic.”

The younger man leaned forward. “But isn’t that exhausting? Creating meaning every day, when everything could just as easily mean nothing?”

“Sometimes it is.” The elder’s voice dropped to a softer tone, “But it’s also an act of rebellion. Against emptiness, against despair. The human spirit is absurdly stubborn — it refuses to give up on hope even when the evidence is neutral or bleak.”

The young man was quiet for a moment, pulled into the gravity of the words. “You once told me you saw laughter in the absurd. But what about sorrow? Doesn’t the same absurdity bring pain?”

The older man’s eyes reflected a deeper understanding. “Absolutely. Sorrow is a profound part of our experience. To confront the fragility of existence, to face loss and the inexplicable randomness of suffering — that’s as existential as it gets. Yet even in sorrow, absurdity invites us to find new ways to live, new ways to feel meaning despite it.”

The park’s lanterns flickered on, casting pools of amber light that kissed their faces gently. They had become odd companions, bound not by friendship in the conventional sense, but by these shared moments of questioning.

The young man pulled the notebook the elder had lent him, flipping to a page scribbled with a quote.

“The struggle itself toward the heights is enough to fill a man’s heart. One must imagine Sisyphus happy.”

“Camus?” the young man said, recognizing the philosophical anchor in the obscure scribble.

“Right. The myth of Sisyphus, eternally condemned to push a boulder uphill only to watch it roll back down, is an apt metaphor for our human condition. The meaning isn’t in completing the task — there is none — but in the perseverance, the rebellion, the choice to keep pushing.”

The young man closed the notebook and looked around at the growing night. “So, in a way, our conversations are a bit like that, aren’t they? We push ideas up the hill, knowing they might tumble, but we keep going because that’s what it means to be alive.”

The elder smiled. “Exactly. To have these talks is to engage with the absurd, to create significance out of uncertainty. It is a small but vital act of meaning-making, no matter how temporary or fragile.”

Days turned into weeks. Their discussions unfolded like the pages of an unending book — at once filled with hope, mystery, humor, and sorrow. They debated whether freedom was an illusion or a gift, argued over whether life’s absurdity was tragedy or comedy, and shared stories about human kindness and cruelty that seemed to dance between meaning and madness.

One evening, as a soft drizzle began to fall, the young man looked up at the gray sky.

“Do you think we’re just characters in someone else’s story? Or is it truly chaos out here?”

The older man chuckled. “Both and neither. Maybe the question itself is absurd. Perhaps the beauty lies in holding the paradox — that we are both tiny and vast, chaotic and ordered, meaningful and meaningless — all at once.”

The young man nodded, watching the raindrops bead on the leaves.

“In that paradox, maybe we find life’s deepest truth: that meaning is less about finding an answer and more about the courage to keep asking the question.”

They sat quietly, wrapped in the strange companionship birthed from existential wonder, beneath a sky that held no certainties but endless possibilities.

Their conversations didn’t solve the mystery, but they didn’t need to. The tales of meaning and absurdity they shared were a testament to the human heart’s refusal to be silent before the vastness. In questioning, laughing, grieving, and dreaming together, they found—in the midst of chaos—a fragile, radiant spark of what it means to be alive.

And that was enough.
