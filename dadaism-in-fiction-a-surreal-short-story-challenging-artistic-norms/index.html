Dadaism in Fiction: A Surreal Short Story Challenging Artistic Norms


In a city stitched from threads of forgotten dreams and fragmented realities, there lived a clockmaker named Marcel who loved to dismantle time rather than construct it. His workshop was a cavernous space where ordinary rules bent and snapped like twigs beneath a playful child’s feet. Clocks without hands ticked in synchronization with shadows; gears spun silently beneath pools of molten candle wax; and the air was thick with the scent of lemon and rust.

Marcel was no ordinary artisan—he was a Dadaist in the most fervent sense, a rebel against the artistic and societal norms that tried to cage expression into neat boxes. Dadaism, with its anarchic spirit, its rejection of logic and conformity, was not just a movement to him but a living, breathing code of existence. His clockwork creations were more than timekeepers; they were manifestos against the rigidity of reason, invitations to chaos wrapped in cogs and springs.

One evening, as twilight melted into a canvas of swirling purples and oranges, Marcel stumbled upon a curious object nestled under a loose floorboard—a bright yellow lemon, but unlike any he had seen. This lemon was not simply a fruit; it shimmered with an iridescent glow, its skin cracked to reveal a labyrinth of miniature gears and tiny mirror fragments reflecting impossible angles of light.

He plucked the lemon from its secret cradle, and immediately the workshop seemed to breathe with newfound life, the clocks around him pulsing in erratic rhythms. Marcel felt time shudder and leap, as if the lemon was a clock of another dimension, a gateway to the surreal landscapes where Dadaism’s spirit danced unbound.

Driven by a manic curiosity, Marcel placed the lemon inside his largest clock—an ornate, baroque contraption adorned with teeth and feathers, a mechanical beast named L’Étranger Temporel, The Temporal Stranger. As the lemon’s gears meshed with the clock’s innards, a cascade of stuttering light burst forth. The walls melted into a collage of fragmented narratives, scenes colliding and converging in a kaleidoscopic frenzy.

Suddenly, Marcel found himself no longer a clockmaker in a workshop but a character within a surreal, shifting story—a narrative that refused to obey logic or convention. Here, dialogues happened in the mind, scenes looped without cause, and characters appeared and vanished like smoke rings. Time unfurled backward and sideways, and nothing had to mean anything or make sense.

In this fictional Dada realm, artistic norms were shredded. A talking pear with a necktie offered cryptic riddles about the absurdity of existence. A paintbrush floated by, dripping colors that morphed surfaces into new dimensions. A chorus of typewriters recited nonsense poems, their keys clacking in staccato bursts that shaped the fabric of reality itself.

Marcel wandered through streets paved with shattered mirrors, where reflections told stories that contradicted reality with gleeful chaos. A clocktower tipped over to reveal a library inside, filled not with books but with jars containing bottled emotions—a swirl of laughter, a pinch of melancholy, a drop of wild abandon.

He met characters who defied labels: a girl who spoke in glances, a man whose footsteps composed symphonies, and an old woman who painted with shadows. They challenged him, provoked him, and invited him to see beyond the constraints of artistic tradition. Each encounter peeled back the veneer of classic storytelling, exposing the raw, untamed heart of creation as a playful rebellion.

As Marcel journeyed deeper into the Dadaist fiction, he realized the story was not just about breaking the rules but about dissolving the very idea of rules themselves. The narrative dissolved into fragments of nonsense and lucid moments of startling clarity, reflecting the duality of Dada—a dance between order and chaos, meaning and absurdity.

But then the lemon clock began to wind down, the iridescent glow dimming as the gears slowed. Marcel felt himself being pulled back, the surreal world folding like origami into the back chamber of his mind. When he opened his eyes, he was back in the workshop, the lemon resting quietly on his workbench, its glow now soft, like a lingering secret.

Yet the experience had transformed him. Marcel’s clocks from that day forward defied function even more boldly—some ticked backwards, others sang lullabies, and a few whispered fragments of poems in the dead of night. His art ignited conversations about the nature of time, narrative, and reality itself, inspiring those who dared to question the very foundations of creative expression.

Marcel understood that Dadaism in fiction was not just a movement frozen in history but a living, breathing invitation to unshackle the mind, celebrate absurdity, and reimagine what art could be. It was a reminder that sometimes the most profound truths reside in nonsense, and that the heart of creation beats strongest when rebellion, chaos, and imagination run wild together.

And the clockmaker’s lemon? It remained a mystery—a surreal talisman reminding all who saw it that time, stories, and meaning were fluid, malleable, and deliciously unpredictable, much like the art of Dada itself.

Through Marcel’s surreal odyssey, we glimpse how fiction mirrors Dadaism’s core tenets: a disquieting refusal to conform, a playful dismantling of logic, and a fierce championing of the absurd. In this poetic disruption, readers and creators alike find freedom—not just from convention, but from the very constraints that bind the imagination, heralding a new era where art is an echo of wonder and rebellion rather than a mere reflection of reality.
