Inseparable: Fictional Stories About Friendship and Loyalty


It was the summer of their senior year, and the air was thick with anticipation. Though they were on the cusp of adulthood, neither Eliza nor Natalie could quite grasp the gravity of the changes ahead. They had spent the last twelve years together—through childhood games, teenage angst, and high school drama. They had been inseparable since the first day they met in kindergarten, when Eliza, shy and reserved, was comforted by Natalie’s loud, infectious laughter as she teased the teacher for giving them extra homework.

Their bond was forged through a shared understanding of one another, an unspoken promise that they would always be there, no matter the storm or sunshine that life brought their way.

It was on a cool September afternoon, just as the trees outside began to change color, that Eliza and Natalie found themselves sitting on their favorite bench overlooking the old stone bridge by the river. The town had always been quiet, small, and idyllic—nothing out of the ordinary ever seemed to happen. But there they were, on the brink of something far greater than they had ever expected. The thought of leaving for college in a few months hung over them like a cloud neither was ready to face.

“You know,” Natalie said, tossing a stone into the river, “I’m not sure I want to go to college.”

Eliza, who had always been more of a thinker than a speaker, raised an eyebrow and turned to look at her. “What do you mean?”

“I mean… all this. I’m going to miss it. You. I don’t know if I’m ready for… change. You know?” Natalie sighed, her eyes distant as she gazed at the ripples in the water.

Eliza smiled softly. “It’s not like we’re never going to see each other again. We’ll make it work.”

But even as she said the words, she realized how hollow they felt. She was no more prepared for the impending separation than Natalie was. The thought of not seeing her every day, of not having her beside her through whatever life threw at them next, was unthinkable. But she couldn’t admit that—not to herself, and certainly not to Natalie.

The weeks passed, and the tension between the two grew palpable. They spent more time together than ever before, grasping at the fleeting moments they had left. Late nights in Eliza’s room, talking about everything and nothing. Hikes in the woods behind their school, where they would sit on a fallen log and talk about their hopes, their fears, and their dreams for the future.

One evening, as they were walking home from the library, Natalie stopped in front of the familiar old oak tree they had climbed as kids. She looked at Eliza with a seriousness that felt too much like finality.

“Eliza… what if we don’t make it?” Natalie’s voice was barely above a whisper.

Eliza’s stomach twisted. She knew what Natalie meant. What if they went their separate ways, lost touch, let time and distance erode what they had? They had always been together—inseparable, unshakable—but now they were standing at the threshold of an uncertain future.

“We will make it. We always have,” Eliza replied, her voice steady even though doubt threatened to creep in.

But even as she said those words, she couldn’t help but wonder what would happen when they were no longer side by side. What if the connection they had was only possible because they had been in the same place, at the same time, for so long?

The day of graduation arrived, and the atmosphere was heavy with both celebration and sorrow. The finality of the day made it feel like they were all being thrust into an unknown future, unsure of what would happen next. Eliza and Natalie stood side by side in their cap and gown, exchanging smiles that hid the fear beneath.

“I’m going to miss this so much,” Natalie said, her voice thick with emotion as they watched their classmates laugh and cheer in the distance.

“We’ll be fine,” Eliza replied, squeezing Natalie’s hand. “We’ll figure it out.”

But as the ceremony progressed and the speeches droned on, Eliza felt her resolve beginning to crack. She knew that they were both trying to convince each other that everything would be okay, but neither of them truly believed it.

After the ceremony, when the crowds had thinned and the sun had begun to set, Eliza and Natalie made their way back to the old bridge, the place where they had spent so many hours talking about their futures, their fears, and their dreams.

They sat down on the edge of the bridge, legs dangling over the side as they watched the sunset.

“I know we said we’d make it work, but… what if it’s not enough?” Natalie asked quietly.

Eliza was silent for a long moment, considering the question. She had always been the one to provide answers, but this time, she didn’t know what to say. She had always believed in their friendship, in the strength of their bond, but what if that wasn’t enough to overcome the distance, the new lives they would lead?

“I don’t know,” Eliza finally admitted. “But I do know this—we’ll always be a part of each other. And no matter what happens, I’ll never forget what we had.”

Natalie’s eyes filled with tears, and for a moment, neither of them spoke. It was as if the world around them had slowed down, and for just a brief, fleeting moment, they were back in that space where everything was simple and they were just two girls with a lifelong friendship.

The years that followed were a blur of new experiences and challenges. Eliza went to college on the East Coast, while Natalie stayed closer to home, working at a local bookstore. They kept in touch through phone calls, texts, and occasional visits, but it wasn’t the same. The distance between them seemed to grow with every passing day, and the fear they had both secretly held onto was becoming a reality.

At times, Eliza would hear about something Natalie had done—some new adventure she had embarked on—and a pang of jealousy would rise in her chest. She hadn’t been there to witness it, to share in the experience. And at other times, Natalie would send her a message, and Eliza would feel the weight of the words—like they were both trying to recapture something that couldn’t be recaptured.

One day, nearly three years after graduation, Eliza found herself standing at a train station, staring at her phone. She had received a message from Natalie: “I’m in town. Want to meet?”

Eliza’s heart skipped a beat. She hadn’t seen Natalie in months. Life had gotten in the way, as it often did. But this was different. This was a chance to reconnect, to reclaim what they had once shared.

The train station was crowded, filled with people rushing to and from their destinations. But when Eliza saw Natalie standing near the entrance, waiting for her, it was as if the world had narrowed down to just the two of them. Their eyes met, and for a moment, everything else faded away.

“You came,” Natalie said with a smile, her eyes sparkling with the same mischief that had always been there.

“Of course I did,” Eliza replied, her voice thick with emotion. “I’ve missed you.”

They embraced, and for the first time in years, Eliza felt a sense of peace settle within her. The distance, the time apart—it had all seemed so daunting, but now it felt like nothing. Because no matter where life took them, no matter how far apart they were, they were always going to be connected in a way that couldn’t be broken.

They sat at a nearby café, talking like no time had passed at all, laughing at the inside jokes that only they understood. The years of separation had not diminished their bond. It had only deepened it, and now they realized that even if they were far apart, they would always be there for each other—inseparable, as they had always been.

And as the sun set that evening, casting a warm golden light over the city, Eliza knew that no matter what the future held, her friendship with Natalie was something that would last a lifetime. Because loyalty, once forged, was never truly broken.
