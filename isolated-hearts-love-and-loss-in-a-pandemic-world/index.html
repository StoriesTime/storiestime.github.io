Isolated Hearts: Love and Loss in a Pandemic World


The city skyline loomed in frozen blues and silvers beneath the midwinter moon. Far below, Eduardo leaned over his apartment balcony, craning towards the distance where empty buses rattled past shuttered bakeries and neon-lit pharmacies. The streets—once pulsing with sound—were now a film reel slowed and dulled, stripped of laughter and the timeworn hum of late-night diners. It was week ten of the pandemic lockdown, and the world had given up its pulse for the hiss of sanitizer and the thump of headlines.

Eduardo’s days marched in strict formation: up at eight, coffee, scrolling through the news—another hospital overloaded, another restriction extended. At noon he stood at the balcony, watching the sky for signs no meteorologist could provide. By three, he worked in a cramped corner he renamed “the office,” though it was just a desk between two tired houseplants. Video meetings pinged, faces pixelated and strained, everyone playing their part: I’m fine. I’m busy. I’m coping.

Of all the rhythms, the only one that mattered happened at 7 p.m., when pots and pans clanged for the essential workers. It was then that Eduardo first noticed her.

She lived in the building across the narrow alley, her apartment window a soft lantern near his floor. Every evening she emerged onto her own small balcony, hands beating a rhythm on a battered cooking pot. He could just make out the text on her purple sweatshirt: “Coney Island Mermaid Parade, 2017.” Her hair—an untamed mess of auburn coils—caught the fading light as fiercely as her laughter caught his heart.

Eduardo started timing his daily walk to coincide with her applause. He dared a wave. The next night, emboldened by the anonymity of isolation, she waved back. She mimed a dance move—sprinkler arms, a shimmy—and he grinned for the first time in weeks. “I’m Eduardo,” he called out, voice muffled by the air.

She cupped her hands and shouted, “Maya! Hi!” Just like that, a lifeline unfurled between balconies.

Time slipped forward not in hours, but in the shape of daily rituals. Maya taught Eduardo how to make bread, writing instructions on a cardboard sign: STIR UNTIL IT STICKS TO YOUR FINGERS!. He held up his failures, and once, to her shrieking delight, almost set off the smoke alarm. They traded playlists, holding phones high over the divide: she sent him folk songs, he gave her Brazilian bossa nova. He confessed he had never danced in public; she promised him lessons—someday, face-to-face.

Maya’s laughter was an anchor, but so was the truth each evening, when the city’s applause died down and sirens swept the avenue. He wanted—needed—a human touch, but no doors opened, no arms reached out, only windows squinting at sickness and at hope. Their conversations prowled around the wound: how long would this last? Were their parents safe? The city’s hospitals sprawled only a few blocks away, fronted by ambulances and clusters of nurses in blue gowns.

Loss seeped through unseen cracks. Eduardo’s grandmother, back in Brazil, fell ill. He called every morning, voice thick with longing for the herbal soup she made whenever he had a cold. “Don’t cry, meu amor. We are strong,” she said, a whisper across continents. When she faded, the burden pressed into his chest, and Maya spent an evening sitting quietly on her balcony, holding up a string of tiny paper stars. She read him a poem about absence and longing—and when he wept, she simply listened.

The days blurred at the edges, identities folding around their digital selves. Eduardo and Maya played chess through an app, each move sent along with a joke or calling card: “Your bishop looks shifty” or “Checkmate by optimism!” They debated films, disagreed about pineapple on pizza, created book clubs that dissolved into all-night chats.

Spring crept back, weak and hesitant. “If you could be anywhere right now?” Maya asked as blossoms ventured along their fire escapes.

“Watching samba in Rio. Dancing with you,” Eduardo replied, tracing the city’s skyline with his finger.

She sent a photo of her parents’ back garden in Queens: tulips, a swing, a tricycle rusted to elegance. “Someday, you’ll teach me the samba there,” she promised, “and we’ll never go inside until it rains.”

**The city waited, breathless and masked. New protocols swept in—testing lines curled around the block, vaccine hopes flickered in the news. Maya’s mother caught the virus, and for a week each night fell silent; Eduardo watched her silhouette, phone pressed to her ear, shoulders hunched beneath the old parade sweatshirt. He left small packages in the foyer for her: a loaf of bread, a sprig of basil, a note: To better days.

One chilly Tuesday, she stood on her balcony, hair tied with a red bandana. “My mom’s stable. She’ll be okay.”

Relief hit him like sunlight. On impulse, Eduardo grabbed a blank page, scribbled a confession, and folded it into a paper airplane. He sent it sailing across the alley—an awkward, wobbly arc that somehow made it to her balcony. She plucked it from the air, opened it. His heart hammered. Her grin—wild and beautiful—gave him the answer.

They were in love, and it was terrifying.

Summer unspooled, uncertain and strange. The city’s parks reawakened, smiling (through masks) at cautious picnics. Eduardo and Maya paced city blocks at dusk, walking separately on opposite sidewalks, hands pressed to hearts. Texts flew: “Turn around,” she’d write, and he’d catch her blowing a theatrical kiss from beneath a lamppost.

One weekend, Maya appeared at his door with groceries, masked, gloves askew. “I’m negative. Want company?”

They sat six feet apart amid Eduardo’s succulents, windows streaming sunlight. They drank coffee—real, not virtual—together, laughter scattering the silence, and they began to build something fragile but true.

It wasn’t perfect. Anxiety prickled with every cough, every bit of news. There were arguments—about risk, about fear, about family borders—and days when the exhaustion of waiting threatened to suction the joy from the room.

But there was also forgiveness. At night before sleep, Eduardo played Maya a soft guitar melody over the phone. She read him short stories about healing, about resilience, about the small, stubborn hope that slipped through even the cruelest winters.

Autumn fell in gold, and things began to shift. The world learned new vocabulary: “immunity,” “mandates,” “pivot.” Most of all, people learned to trust again, though clumsily. Eduardo accepted a job offer at a nonprofit. Maya started a new ceramics class—outside, masked, but surrounded by people once more.

When the first frost hit, Eduardo cooked feijoada and invited Maya as his only guest. They ate by candlelight, trading dreams for the next year—travel, visits to family, a tangled afternoon in Coney Island. They had lost so much, but they could rebuild, brick by patient brick.

In the months that followed, doorways became thresholds not of fear, but of promise. Eduardo’s grandmother’s recipes lived on, Maya’s mother recovered, and neighbors returned tentative smiles. The city awakened—older, wiser, scars visible but adorned with the bright graffiti of hope.

Isolated hearts, drawn across concrete canyons, had found each other. Eduardo and Maya stood together atop their building’s roof, wind tangling their hands. Far below, the city pulsed. Love—silent as ever, unseen but unstoppable—beat beneath every mask, every grin, every shy glance sketched onto a face at a window.

Their isolation was, forever after, only a beginning.
