The Edge of the World: Exploring Forbidden Realms


Roath’s maps ended abruptly at the jagged edge of the parchment, where the last strokes of ink trailed into the void. For years, cartographers agreed that the Edge of the World was real—and final. Beyond it lay chaos, an uncharted abyss where stars dissolved and oceans turned to vapor. Legends warned of monstrous guardians, shimmering walls of energy, and the relentless whispering of unknown forces. No one who had crossed the threshold ever returned, which only heightened the allure of the forbidden frontier.

Roath sat in the flickering glow of his workshop, staring at the map like it was an unsolvable riddle. His hands, worn rough by years of navigation, traced the indistinct line where land surrendered to the unknown. As a seasoned explorer, he had charted forests that devoured compasses, scaled mountains blackened by ancient fires, and sailed seas where the winds played cruel tricks on the gullible. But this quest—this obsession with the forbidden realms—had consumed him long before he understood its cost.

The town elders warned him against this folly. He recalled their voices, thick with caution and tinged with fear. “The Edge is a curse,” they said. “Those whom curiosity leads there find nothing but ruin. We don’t remember their names, yet we mourn their arrogance.” Roath had smirked at those warnings then, his confidence barely touched by doubt. But now, as the day of departure approached, he understood that those men knew more than he gave them credit for.

Before dawn painted the horizon in muted grays, Roath slipped away from his home. He carried only what was necessary—a weathered satchel filled with supplies, his trusty compass, and a journal where he recorded every fragment of his journeys. Alongside him was Kalwen, an unflappable guide who believed that death was inevitable and that longing for safety was a waste of good adventure. Together, they maneuvered through thick mists that clung to the rolling hills surrounding their town.

At midday, Roath and Kalwen stood at the precipice of Elrys Ridge, peering into the distance where myth became indistinct reality. Somewhere out there, beyond the tangible terrain, waited the Edge. It didn’t look extraordinary from here—a faint shimmer distorted the edges of the landscape, like sunlight bending against a ripple. Yet Roath felt an unrelenting pull, an invisible energy woven into the air. Kalwen adjusted the straps of her leather pack and grunted. “Feels like something’s watching us already,” she muttered. Roath said nothing—his focus was fixed entirely on what lay ahead.

Neither could have predicted how quickly their surroundings would transform.

The passage to the Edge was fraught with subtle treachery. The landscape grew strange with every mile they crossed; trees twisted unnaturally, their branches stretching toward the sky like clawing hands. Rivers didn’t flow—they remained perfectly still, their surfaces impossibly reflective. Even the air roared with quiet, carrying the deafening sound of absence. Kalwen remarked that it felt as though the world itself held its breath, waiting.

And then came the storm.

Thunder cracked, not from the sky but from deep within the earth itself, reverberating around them as though the realm rebelled against their presence. Roath’s compass spun wildly, its needle unable to find true north. The rain was thick and heavy, but each droplet shimmered faintly like liquid starlight, coating their surroundings in an eerie glow. Despite Kalwen’s protests to find shelter, Roath pressed on, driven by whatever gravitational pull this forbidden realm exerted over him. The Edge was close now—he could feel it in his bones.

When the storm finally subsided, it revealed something impossible. A city of crystal towers rose on the far horizon, untouched by the chaos around it. The towers pierced the sky, glittering under the light of countless shifting colors, as if refracting dimensions rather than simple sunlight. Roath and Kalwen exchanged a look of disbelief. There were no cities marked on the maps here. This was a place that wasn’t supposed to exist.

Approaching the city unraveled their understanding of reality.

What they had assumed to be towers were not structures but living entities, pulsating softly with light and sound every few moments. Whenever the wind rippled through them, the air hummed with music-like frequencies that neither Roath nor Kalwen had ever heard. The ground beneath their feet shifted constantly, forming patterns—a living mosaic—but seemed stable enough to hold their weight. The veil of shimmering energy that surrounded this realm pulsed faintly with each step they took.

The denizens of the city were not immediately visible, yet their presence was undeniable. Roath had the unmistakable sensation of being observed, weighted by eyes he could not see. Kalwen shifted uncomfortably, muttering about figures that seemed to flicker in and out of existence at the corner of her vision. They pressed forward cautiously, stopping before an archway formed by intertwining beams of translucent light.

Roath reached a hand to touch the arch, and the world gasped.

The landscape shifted instantaneously. They were no longer surrounded by pulsing towers and living ground. Instead, the space seemed infinite—a realm of stars and swirling voids stretching outward forever. Both the earth and horizon were gone entirely, as though something had stolen the foundational rules of existence and replaced them with primordial chaos. Roath stepped forward, the sensation akin to walking underwater despite the absence of liquid.

And then he saw them.

The Guardians of the Edge were unlike anything Roath had imagined. Their forms were fluid, constantly shifting, made of something that resembled both shadow and fire. Great wings sprouted from bodies of luminescent fog, and their mouths whispered secrets in languages older than the earth itself. There were three of them, each equally enormous and surreal, stationed around a spiraling vortex that Roath immediately recognized as the boundary itself—a passage into realms far beyond comprehension.

The air buzzed with questions none could answer. Roath stared into the vortex, and for the first time in his life, doubt consumed him. To cross the Edge was to surrender everything—to leave behind history, identity, and the comforting boundaries of mortal understanding. There would be discovery, yes—but would he recognize himself once the truth revealed itself?

Kalwen took a step back. “This is wrong,” she muttered. “This is—something we were never meant to see.” Her voice cracked slightly, hints of the first real fear Roath had seen from her in years. But he stayed silent, his gaze locked on the swirling vortex. The Guardians basked around it, indifferent to the intruders. They had no need to act, for the most powerful force in the realm was choice.

And choice, Roath knew, was heavier than any weight in the world.

Minutes or hours passed—or perhaps neither. Time had lost meaning in this forbidden space. Kalwen stared helplessly at Roath, unsure. Finally, she broke the silence. “If you go,” came her voice, shaking slightly, “you’ll do it alone. I’m not crossing that—I’m not leaving everything behind.”

Roath gave her a ghost of a smile, but no words followed.

The Edge was cold. Crossing it was nothing like Roath had imagined—it wasn’t the rush of discovery, nor the explosion of clarity. It was infinitely slower, stranger, lonelier. As he stepped forward, the world began to dissolve—not in violence, but in quiet entropy. Kalwen’s distant figure was the last thing to vanish, and then the colors swallowed him whole. Somewhere, at the edge of existence itself, a truth waited to be understood.

As to whether Roath found it?

Some questions never escape the void.
