Under the Surface: Exploring Prejudice Through Short Fiction


The rain had been relentless all day, washing the streets of Maplewood in a steady drizzle that blurred the edges of the world. In the small town’s worn café, where the paint peeled from the window frames and the scent of coffee lingered like a stubborn ghost, two figures sat opposite each other, separated by unspoken walls.

Clara, a woman in her early thirties, stirred her tea absently, eyes fixed on the rain tracing streaks down the glass. Across from her, Marcus leaned back with a slow, deliberate calm but his fingers twitched with barely contained tension.

They had grown up in this town, beneath identical skies yet viewing the world through markedly different lenses shaped by their life stories. Their shared history was a thread tangled in time—a testament to both connection and division.

The First Ripple

Years ago, when they were teenagers, Clara recalled the day with painful clarity.

It was the spring fair, and the air was thick with laughter and the sugary smell of cotton candy. Marcus had been dared to dive into the lake’s murky surface, a challenge he met with an easy grin. But when he emerged, the crowd had murmured something that Clara could not then comprehend—a whispered slur, a word that stuck to the air like smoke.

“Why do they look at you like that?” she asked him later, confusion creasing her youthful brow.

Marcus shrugged, but the weight behind his eyes spoke volumes. “Because I’m different. Because they don’t understand.”

Back then, Clara had bristled. To her, Marcus had always seemed confident, even fearless. Now she realized how much he bore silently—prejudice woven into the fabric of everyday moments.

Layers of Invisible Ink

Time did not erode the invisible lines drawn by bias; instead, it deepened them. Adults in Maplewood rarely spoke openly about differences beyond polite nods or forced smiles. There were subtle exclusions—irrelevant invitations to events, sidelong glances when opinions clashed.

Clara worked at the local library, where she noticed the unspoken patterns as much as the worn pages of old books. Families who wouldn’t sit beside each other, neighbors who exchanged greetings too stiff for warmth, and children who mimicked adult prejudices without question.

Marcus taught at the high school, where he watched the cycle continue—students dividing themselves into cliques based on surface distinctions, sometimes unaware or unwilling to see beyond.

One day, Clara approached Marcus after school, a book in her hand:

“Everyone has an ocean inside, waiting for us to dive deep, beyond what the surface shows.”

“Maybe that’s where we need to start,” Clara said softly, sliding the book across the table.

Marcus glanced at the cover, then met her gaze. “Because prejudice is just the shallowest splash, isn’t it? The real stuff is hidden beneath the waves.”

Breaking the Surface

Their conversations became a quiet rebellion—every discussion a small battle against the expectations tethered to their identities. They talked about history, culture, and what it meant to belong.

Marcus recounted stories of his grandfather, a man who fought not only societal barriers but his inner doubts. Clara shared her struggles with her family’s narrow views, the inherited biases she had learned to question.

One evening, they organized a community event—Voices Beneath the Surface. It was an invitation to share stories that hadn’t been told, to challenge assumptions through narrative and empathy.

The turnout surprised them. People of all ages and backgrounds gathered in the town hall, hesitating at first, then opening up in ways that chipped away at the walls built over generations.

A young girl spoke about her best friend, whose skin was a different shade, and how their laughter was the same underneath. An elderly man confessed his regrets for past judgments, wishing for a chance to rewrite history with kindness.

Clara and Marcus listened, heartened by the stories that unfolded. It was messy, sometimes uncomfortable, but profoundly necessary.

Underneath It All

Prejudice is a tide that ebbs and flows, but beneath it lies the possibility of connection—shared hopes, fears, and dreams. The surface scratches the faint outline, but true understanding demands looking deeper.

As the rain finally ceased, the café’s window no longer blurred the world. The droplets clung to the glass, shimmering like a mosaic of tiny, fragile mirrors reflecting not just light, but the potential for change.

Clara and Marcus stepped outside together, wiping water from their faces. The streets gleamed wet but clean, and for once, the division felt like a distant echo rather than a looming presence.

“Maybe one day, nobody will see color or background first,” Clara said, a tentative hope in her voice.

Marcus smiled, eyes bright with quiet certainty. “Maybe, but until then, we keep diving beneath the surface.”

And with that, they walked forward—no longer just neighbors sharing a town, but allies diving deep, exposing the undercurrents of prejudice to shine a light and, at last, begin to heal.

Reflection on Prejudice Through Fiction

Short stories like this one illuminate the nuanced layers of prejudice that permeate even the quietest corners of life. By exploring the internal struggles and external confrontations faced by people like Clara and Marcus, we can see that the journey toward understanding is neither simple nor immediate.

It requires patience, vulnerability, and the courage to peel back the surface and confront uncomfortable truths. When communities acknowledge these stories, they foster empathy, opening pathways to dismantle old biases and build bridges that transcend difference.

In fiction, prejudice is not just a villain to defeat but a puzzle to solve, layered with history and individual experience. Through such narratives, readers can gain not only insight but inspiration to challenge their own assumptions and engage with the world more compassionately.

Under the surface, there is always more—waiting to be discovered, understood, and transformed.
