Viral Pressure: The Race for Online Approval


Lena sat at her desk, the soft hum of her laptop the only sound in the room. Her fingers hovered above the keyboard, the cursor blinking on the screen in a rhythmic pattern. She scrolled through her social media feeds, half-heartedly liking posts and watching videos. Despite the endless flow of content, she felt an emptiness in her chest. There was something more she needed to prove, something elusive that had taken over her thoughts in the past few weeks.

The idea came to her late one night as she scrolled through yet another viral video. It was of a young influencer—perhaps no older than eighteen—standing in front of a stunning backdrop, speaking confidently to the camera. Within hours, the video had racked up millions of views, and the comments section was filled with praise, admiration, and envy. It wasn’t just the content itself that had gone viral, but the validation, the attention, the rush of being seen by so many.

“Why can’t I have that?” Lena whispered to herself.

Her mind raced with possibilities. She had been creating content for years now—memes, beauty tutorials, vlogs—but nothing ever truly took off. Sure, there had been a few posts here and there that garnered attention, but it was always fleeting. She had never been able to break through the noise of the endless digital crowd. The competition was fierce, and the pressure to stay relevant felt like an unspoken weight on her shoulders.

That night, Lena made a decision: she would join the race for online approval.

The following morning, Lena began brainstorming. She knew she had to stand out, but what could she offer that others hadn’t already done? What would make her different?

She spent hours researching the trends—the latest viral challenges, the hottest memes, the influencers who were making waves on every platform. Lena noted down every popular hashtag, every catchphrase, and every type of content that seemed to be gaining traction. But nothing felt like her. Nothing felt authentic.

She closed her eyes, took a deep breath, and reminded herself of the advice she’d heard countless times before: “Be yourself.”

“Be yourself,” she muttered, her fingers tapping nervously on the desk.

Then, it clicked. Maybe it wasn’t about trying to fit in with the crowd. Maybe it was about amplifying what made her unique. Lena wasn’t a polished influencer with a perfectly curated aesthetic. She didn’t have the flawless skin or the perfectly staged backgrounds that others had. But she had a sense of humor. She had a personality that was unapologetically hers. Maybe that was enough.

A few days later, Lena posted her first video. It was simple, raw, and honest—no fancy editing, no filters. She sat in front of her camera and shared a story about the time she had tried to bake cookies from scratch and ended up setting off the smoke alarm. She told the story with humor, emphasizing how even the simplest tasks could turn into disasters. It was relatable. It was imperfect. And it was real.

The video received a modest number of views at first—nothing viral, but certainly more than her typical posts. The comments came pouring in: “You’re so real,” “I love how you don’t pretend to be perfect,” and “This is exactly what I needed to see today.”

Lena felt a small spark of hope. Maybe, just maybe, she had found her niche. She could be the girl who wasn’t afraid to mess up, the girl who was willing to laugh at herself. But even as the likes and comments rolled in, she couldn’t shake the feeling that something was missing. The validation was good, but it wasn’t the validation she craved—the kind of validation that could send her video into the stratosphere of viral fame.

She wanted more.

The next video was a calculated risk. Lena had noticed the success of “reaction videos”—people watching something and filming their unfiltered responses. They were a dime a dozen, but some had managed to become viral sensations by choosing unexpected content to react to. So, Lena decided to react to something that no one would expect: an old, obscure infomercial for a product she’d never heard of.

The video was quirky and fun. Lena exaggerated her reactions, laughed loudly, and made sharp-witted comments about the ridiculousness of the product. She had fun with it. The video quickly gained traction, and before she knew it, it was everywhere.

Her notifications blew up. A popular influencer shared the video, and Lena’s follower count skyrocketed. More likes, more shares, more comments. She finally felt the rush—the excitement of seeing her face everywhere, of feeling validated by the digital world. People were tagging their friends, sharing it on other platforms, and using her catchphrases in their own posts.

For a brief moment, Lena felt on top of the world. She was finally part of the digital elite, the influencers who shaped trends and determined what went viral. But as the hours passed, a new feeling began to creep in. It wasn’t just excitement anymore—it was pressure.

The pressure mounted quickly. With every new video, Lena felt an overwhelming need to do better than the last. The competition wasn’t just about having fun or expressing herself anymore—it was about staying on top, keeping the views coming, and maintaining the momentum. The comments were no longer just compliments; they were expectations. “You’ve got to do something even crazier next time!” one person wrote. “When’s the next reaction video coming? I need more!” another commented.

Lena started losing sleep, her mind racing with ideas for the next big thing. She poured over her analytics, obsessing over every detail—watch time, engagement rate, follower growth. If she wasn’t hitting the numbers, it meant failure. The thrill of validation began to feel more like a prison than a reward.

She began experimenting with increasingly outlandish content. Staged pranks, over-the-top challenges, and scripted drama became her new reality. But each video left her feeling emptier than the last. The content wasn’t hers anymore; it was what people expected of her. She was no longer just Lena—she was a brand, a persona designed to capture attention.

One night, Lena woke up in the middle of a panic attack. Her phone buzzed incessantly next to her bed, an endless stream of notifications demanding her attention. She had been awake for hours, her mind swirling with numbers and demands from followers. But it was the overwhelming sense of emptiness that shook her the most. This race for online approval had drained her, and she was no longer sure who she was beneath the persona she had created.

The pressure of constantly chasing the next viral moment had begun to take a toll on her mental health. She realized that the pursuit of fame, validation, and approval had shifted her focus away from what she truly cared about. She was no longer creating content because it was fun or meaningful to her—it was now about staying relevant, keeping up with the ever-growing demands of her audience.

She needed a break.

Lena took a step back. She paused posting for a week, giving herself the space to breathe. She reflected on why she started creating content in the first place—to connect with others, to share her life, her experiences, and her sense of humor. She realized that somewhere along the way, she had lost sight of that purpose.

When Lena returned, her approach was different. She began posting with a renewed focus on authenticity, sharing stories and content that truly reflected who she was. She no longer felt the need to chase viral success, and in doing so, the pressure eased. The likes and comments didn’t matter as much anymore. What mattered was the joy of creating for the sake of creating—without worrying about approval or validation.

It was a slow process, but over time, Lena found a balance. Her following grew, not because she was trying to be someone she wasn’t, but because people could see the real her—the imperfect, unfiltered version of herself that wasn’t chasing after viral moments. She learned that online approval could be fleeting, but staying true to herself would always hold more value in the long run.

And for the first time in a long time, Lena was at peace.
