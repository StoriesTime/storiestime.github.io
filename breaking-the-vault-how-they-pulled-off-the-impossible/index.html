It was the night of September 2, 1971, and the world was abuzz with news of the perfect crime. The Bank of Bangladesh, a seemingly impenetrable fortress in the heart of Dhaka, had been robbed of $113 million, making it the largest heist in history. But what the world didn't know was the incredible audacity and cunning that went into making it happen. The Perfect Plan The mastermind behind the heist was Keshav Giri, a 30-year-old smuggler with a reputation for getting what he wanted. Giri had been hired by a group of wealthy Bengalis, dissatisfied with the state of corruption in their government, to pull off the impossible. They wanted to showcase the deep-seated divisions between the haves and have-nots in their country. Giri and his team of six engineers, led by expert safe-cracker Palem Chakraborty, set out to craft the ultimate plan. They began by scouting the location, gathering intel on the bank's security systems and policymakers' routines. They discovered that the bank's vault was locked behind a series of mechanical doors, protected by armor-plated plates and fiercest alarm systems.
