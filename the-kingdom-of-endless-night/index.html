The Kingdom of Endless Night


The first thing a traveler notices about the border of the kingdom is the absence of twilight. North, south, and west: buttery dusklight lingers. Cross the threshold into the east, and the world is replaced by obsidian gloom, as seamless as a locked chest. When Lucia crossed the fallen pillar that marked the end of the Umberlands, she felt the dark close over her senses like a velvet glove, muting echoes from the countryside behind. It was said that even the land itself refused to dream within the borders of this kingdom, but Lucia, who had come so far for answers, pressed forward.

She carried with her a lantern—a glass-walled orb, hung on a willow-whittled staff. The light it cast was valiant, but so thin, it might have been spun from spider silk. Still, Lucia’s hope lay ahead: along a forgotten road flanked by crumbled statues, in the heart of a realm abandoned by sun and mercy for a century and a day.

Shadows with Names

Lore in the neighboring lands called it the Kingdom of Endless Night, haunted by a curse that devoured both rulers and commoners, warping their dreams into shadows with names. No one who entered emerged unchanged. Most never emerged at all. Lucia had listened to these tales as a child, shivering beneath patchwork quilts while storms tap-tapped on the roof. They spoke of a vanished queen, of forbidden pacts, of gardens where roots grew upward and moonblooms whispered in a tongue none remembered. And woven into those tales: the possibility of a cure, a lantern that would grow brighter with each truth unearthed.

Lucia was no stranger to darkness. Loss wrapped her tightly as winter wool—her brother stolen away by an ailment doctors failed to tame, her father lost to grief, and her mother drifting beyond reach on rivers of her own forgotten memories. All that remained was a hope some called foolish, others courageous: that the answer to her family’s unraveling slept somewhere inside the unyielding gloom beyond the fallen pillar.

Her footsteps crunched glassy-black gravel that glimmered when brushed by her lantern’s frail illumination. Every so often, she glimpsed movement: the ripple of shadow on shadow, shapeless forms pacing boundaries just beyond her reach, quietly regarding her.

A voice rose from the path’s side, at first barely discernible above the silence. “Lightbearer.” Startled, Lucia froze, holding her lantern higher.

A figure: tall, robed in things that suggested neither silk nor chainmail, with a cloak that seemed stitched from the finest dusk. Beneath the cowl, ancient eyes barely gleamed in purple depths.

“Have you come seeking what you have lost?” the figure said.

Lucia hesitated, then nodded. She tried to steady her voice. “I seek what was stolen, and perhaps what was never mine at all.”

The figure’s mouth, when it appeared, seemed painted with moon-ash. “All who enter seek, but few understand what is truly lost. Tell me, why should the night give back what it keeps?”

“Because I ask. Because I’m willing to pay the price,” Lucia replied, surprised by the strength in her words.

The figure nodded approvingly. “Then, lightbearer, remember only this: in Endless Night, every answer births a shadow. Take care that you do not lose yourself in their ranks.” And with a rustle like falling leaves, the guide—or perhaps only a memory in the shape of one—slipped away between the fractured stones.

Castles Made from Memory

Without another word, Lucia pressed onward. Her thoughts tangled as the path climbed, each sense hush-softened by the smothering dark. If despair was a presence, she met it again and again—her own doubts lingering in every whispered wind, every echo.

Eventually, a golden glimmer broke the monotony. She approached a gate formed not from metal or wood, but from thousands of interlocked mirrored shards. Reality doubled and tripled in their glint: Lucia, a hundred times, fretful and determined.

Beyond the gate stood the remnants of a castle, its towers reaching upward as if to prod a forgotten moon. The architecture seemed not built, but conjured, twisting in ways that rebuffed logic—stairs ascending into blank spaces, doors opening onto nothingness, windows blocking out even the faintest suggestion of starlight.

It was here, the stories said, that the kingdom had made its fatal wager. Once, Queen Amalthea had ruled these halls. Famished by sorrow—her own daughter cursed to perpetual slumber—she had resorted to unsanctioned magics, bartering years of daylight for a single wish: that love would trump loss, and light pierce even the blackest despair.

The bargain went awry. Dawn retreated, and the kingdom was lost to the ages, its memory warped into myth. Only those daring or desperate enough set foot in the ruins now.

Inside, Lucia found herself moving by instinct. Her lantern pulsed in time with her heartbeat. Portraits lined the main hall; their faces dissolved when approached, reforming into something unrecognizable when seen from afar. Dust the color of old secrets carpeted the floor.

A voice, crisp as water on glass: “What is it that you seek?”

Startled, Lucia turned to find a child on the dais—barefoot, garbed in a shift of midnight satin. Eyes wide, she cradled a moth the size of a sparrow.

“My brother,” Lucia managed. “And my own memory of peace. Selfish, perhaps.”

The girl smiled—a fierce, knowing expression far older than her features. “The night holds many kinds of memory. Some we inherit, some we make, some we forget on purpose.”

Lucia stepped forward, compelled more by honesty than the promise of safety. “Can you lead me to him? Or to the truths this kingdom keeps?”

With a gesture, the girl beckoned Lucia to a spiral staircase—one that bent downward, into a chill more biting than before. Lucia followed, the lantern wavering.

Trials in the Underhall

They descended into a labyrinth hewn from onyx, lit only by the hesitant radiance of Lucia’s lantern. Hallways forked at odd angles, giving rise to the fear she might walk in circles forever. Statues lined every alcove, too lifelike to be comforting, each one caught mid-expression: surprise, sorrow, rage.

“The shapes you see,” the girl whispered, “are memories fossilized by grief. The night collects them, but it will also release what is freely given.”

They stopped before a statue—a boy about Lucia’s age, holding a broken music box. He wore a crown askew on his head and a look of tremendous loss. Lucia wept, suddenly realizing the boy’s likeness was too true, his stance too reminiscent of her brother just before the fever took him.

“He tried to sing away the dark,” the guide said quietly. “But he forgot the melody when he crossed the border.”

Lucia reached out. Her fingers met cold stone, but she closed her eyes and let memory take precedence: evenings by the hearth, soft songs traded like secrets. The lantern’s glow strengthened, threads of gold brightening as she remembered each lyric, every promise whispered between siblings.

A gust of wind, impossibly warm, seethed from the statue. The stone cracked, and for a heartbeat, Lucia felt her brother’s hand in hers, a curve of laughter whispered. Then he was gone—the statue, only flakes on the floor.

The girl turned to her, approval in her gaze. “To release someone is not to forget them, but to untangle memory from longing. The night hungers for stories unresolved. But you give it what it cannot resist: closure.”

They traveled on, through rooms alive with dreams and regrets. Lucia faced her father’s silhouette slumped over a chessboard, her mother’s voice trapped in an echoing kitchen, her own reflection arguing with itself in a hall of endless mirrors. Each time, she pressed forward, refusing to turn away, even as the lantern flickered wildly.

At last, deep beneath the castle’s foundations, Lucia stood before a well whose water gleamed darkly. The girl hovered at its edge. “This is the spring from which the curse flows. It cycles sorrow into midnight and hope into riddles.”

Lucia fell to her knees. “What can break the cycle?”

The girl’s reply echoed with the certainty of tides: “A gift without expectation. An act that needs no witness. The curse feeds on bargains and bargains alone.”

Dawns in Waiting

Lucia thought of her journey—of the danger, the exhaustion, the bite of endless yearning. She drew from her satchel the music box her brother had loved, careful not to cling to hope for revival. With trembling fingers, she wound it, let it sing its fractured melody, and when the tune faded, she dropped the box into the well. No wish flared in her mind. No repayment demanded.

A hush blanketed the chamber. The shadows thickened—then, in the heart of the well, a single star ignited—tiny, unsure. Above, the ground shuddered. Lucia watched, awestruck, as the lantern in her hand burst into flames not of fire, but of honest dawn: a bead of daybreak catching in the darkness, spreading like ripples on spring water.

The girl by her side was changing too. No longer merely spectral, her outline refined until she appeared ageless. “Thank you,” she breathed, voice lighter than silence. “You remembered that some gifts are for the giver as much as for the given.”

Racing upward, Lucia found herself ascending through the castle—not climbing, but rising, as if pulled by invisible hands. She emerged on the castle’s highest tower as fractured moonlight began to tumble through cracks in the clouds above. For the first time since entering, the black retreated, replaced by cobalt blue, the blush of coming day.

The land below responded—plants stirring, rivers gleaming. The memory of the curse faltered, its spell broken by the simple act of letting go.

Lucia clutched her lantern—a relic no longer, but a piece of the morning. She closed her eyes and felt grief soften around her, not vanquished, but shared with a place that understood.

The Return

Crossing the border once more, Lucia heard birdsong. The countryside lay transformed—not in its structure, but in its lightness, as though it drew breath after a long-held suspense. Behind her, the ruined pillar stood unchanged—a lonely marker. Yet the path ahead now shimmered with possibility.

The stories would speak of her in time—a lightbearer who entered the kingdom and returned not just with answers, but with dawn. Lucia did not linger for legends. Her heart felt lighter knowing that in the realm where darkness once ruled, hope had found a foothold. So she carried that dawn forward, a lantern ever-bright, into all the places that needed it most.

And so the Kingdom of Endless Night became the land of long shadows and longer mornings. It slept still, but now it dreamed.

End.
