Unspoken Walls: A Chronicle of Everyday Racism


The walls of the old library had witnessed far more than the human eye often cared to see. Each stone, each chiseled crack, housed stories long silenced, conversations abruptly ended, and moments hidden behind the veil of an uncomfortable stillness. The library itself was situated in the heart of a bustling metropolitan city, a place where the past clashed with present ideals, yet echoes of yesterday’s prejudices still lingered, invisible but potent.

It was a cold afternoon in late October when Lila stepped into the building. Her boots left faint imprints of autumn’s residue, streaks of damp leaves and mud that caught the librarian’s notice almost immediately.

“Do be careful not to ruin the carpets,” Mr. Graham snapped from behind the counter, though his words were softened by a sheepish smile. But this wasn’t the first time Lila felt this kind of scrutiny from him. His graying hair and pressed suit seemed to underscore an old-world demeanor that had yet to fit itself into the tapestry of the city’s progress. It wasn’t what he said aloud that irked her—it was what he didn’t say, but implied nonetheless.

Lila knew this feeling well, like a scent you could never quite describe but recognized all the same. She’d grown up with such strained silences around her. Polite smiles, words laced with hesitant tones, and the occasional too-long glance had been part of her daily existence. Here, even amidst the towering shelves filled with literary treasures, the air was no different.

Still, she had a job to do. Pulling her scarf tighter around her neck, she shook off the damp air and made her way toward the archive room. She had volunteered to catalog the library’s special collections as part of her graduate research project. It was an opportunity she couldn’t pass up; this collection housed narratives from underrepresented communities, stories that had been denied platforms for far too long. If anything, she wanted to be part of amplifying those voices.

The first few hours passed quietly, broken only by the sound of her pencil scratching against paper and the occasional creak of the building settling into its ancient frame. Amid the work, Lila found herself drawn to a small, leather-bound sketchbook stored in a plain brown box. The inventory list had no description attached to it, just the initials R.S.

Curious, she flipped it open and was immediately struck by the detailed pencil sketches within. They depicted scenes of everyday life—a barbershop, a park bench, a bustling market. Yet each image contained a subtle tension. In one sketch, two children played hopscotch while their mothers stood a few feet apart, one smiling cautiously, the other looking off into the distance. In another, a man sat on a subway, his expression thoughtful as the passenger beside him gripped her bag a little tighter.

The accompanying notes, scrawled neatly in the margins, were equally poignant.

“She forced a smile when we made eye contact but clutched her daughter’s hand a little tighter.”

“He said, ‘You speak so well,’ as if it were a compliment.”

“The laughter stopped when I walked into the room, but the silent glances began right after.”

Lila felt a lump form in her throat as she turned the pages. These were not grand narratives of historical significance or dramatic moments of violent hatred; they were snapshots of quiet marginalization, tiny fractures in the veneer of civility. She couldn’t help but recognize the experiences; they mirrored so much of her own.

The sound of someone clearing their throat startled her. She looked up to see a young janitor, a boy no older than 18, standing shyly by the doorway. His dark brown skin contrasted sharply against the white walls behind him, and his uniform hung loosely on his thin frame. He tilted his head toward the sketchbook in her hands.

“That one’s my favorite,” he said softly, before stepping inside and beginning to sweep the floor.

“You’ve seen this before?” Lila asked, holding up the book.

The boy nodded. “Yeah. I mean, not in a long time, but yeah. My grandfather used to talk about those drawings. He knew the artist—Raymond Saunders. They grew up in the same neighborhood. He used to say those pictures were some of the realest things he ever saw, you know? Not exaggerated, just real. Stuff people don’t like to talk about but feel every day.”

Lila studied the boy for a moment, then flipped through the pages again. It struck her that this artist, whoever he was, had captured what she’d always struggled to articulate. How could you explain the exhaustion of walking on eggshells, the endless mental dialogue about how to respond to a subtle dig, or the way your shoulders stiffened when the vibe in a room shifted ever so slightly?

“Does the library have anything else by him?” she asked, hoping to uncover more of the artist’s work.

The boy shrugged. “Not that I know of. I think most of his stuff got lost, or maybe nobody cared enough to keep it. My grandfather said he stopped drawing after a while. Said it got too heavy for him, carrying those stories,” he paused, leaning on his broom. “But it’s a shame, you know? People need to see stuff like that. Makes you think.”

The boy’s words lingered as Lila continued her work. Over the next several weeks, she found herself coming back to that sketchbook, unable to shake the intimacy of the images and the words attached to them. It was as though Saunders had unlocked a door within her, revealing a reservoir of unspoken wounds she didn’t even know she carried. She began to look at her own life through the lens of those drawings.

There was the time her professor insisted she serve as the “diverse” voice in a classroom discussion, though she’d never volunteered to do so. There was the neighbor who offered her unsolicited advice about blending in, lightly suggesting toned-down hairstyles and “neutral” shades of clothing. There was the way clerks eyed her differently when she walked into high-end stores, their polite masks slipping just enough for her to notice.

Each of these moments seemed small on their own, but together they formed an invisible cage, one constructed of a thousand microaggressions and unspoken expectations.

On the last day of her volunteer work at the library, Lila returned the sketchbook to its box and sealed it with a reverence she hadn’t anticipated feeling. As she carried it back to the inventory closet, she felt the weight of responsibility settle on her shoulders. Raymond Saunders may have stopped drawing, but his work had ignited something in her. She couldn’t let his voice—or the voices of countless others like him—be forgotten.

Before leaving, she approached Mr. Graham at the front counter.

“Did you ever look through the special collections?” she asked. Her tone was measured, but there was an edge to her question.

Mr. Graham adjusted his glasses, perplexed. “Not particularly, no. Why do you ask?”

“Because there’s a sketchbook in there—drawings by an artist named Raymond Saunders. They’re unlike anything I’ve ever seen. You might want to take a look sometime,” she replied. Without waiting for his response, she smiled, hoisted her bag over her shoulder, and walked out into the crisp autumn air.

As Lila made her way home, she thought about the conversations still waiting to be had, the walls still standing between people and their shared humanity. She couldn’t predict where her journey would take her, but she knew one thing for certain: it was time to start speaking the unsaid truths.

It was time to dismantle the unspoken walls.
