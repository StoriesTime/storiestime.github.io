Healing from Trauma: A Psychological Perspective


Lena sat at the edge of the couch, her fingers tightly gripping the fabric. The air in the room was thick with the weight of unsaid words, and for the umpteenth time, she found herself staring at the soft beige carpet instead of looking at Dr. Mitchell, the therapist who had been guiding her through this difficult journey for the past six months. The ticking of the clock on the wall was the only sound that filled the room.

“How are you feeling today?” Dr. Mitchell asked, his voice gentle yet firm, a contrast to the turmoil that churned inside her.

Lena took a deep breath, her chest rising and falling with the effort. It had been a long, exhausting road. She had seen it all—counseling sessions, books on self-help, and well-meaning advice from friends. But nothing had worked. Nothing, until now. The trauma from her childhood had seemed to define her, painting every aspect of her life in colors of fear, guilt, and hopelessness. It was as though the past was an anchor that kept dragging her down, no matter how hard she tried to swim to the surface.

“It’s been better,” Lena whispered, her voice shaky, uncertain. “But I still feel stuck. Like I’m not really… here. I feel like I’m just going through the motions.”

Dr. Mitchell nodded, his eyes studying her face with a mixture of empathy and understanding. “That’s completely understandable, Lena. Healing from trauma isn’t linear. It’s not about a sudden shift, but more about small, meaningful changes over time. We have to break down those old patterns and allow new ones to take shape.”

Lena thought about that. It felt true. After all, she had spent most of her life carrying the weight of past events—events she could barely remember in their entirety but that still haunted her dreams. As a child, she had learned early on that the world wasn’t a safe place. Her father had been harsh, emotionally distant, and often quick to anger. Her mother had been loving but absent, unable to provide the protection Lena so desperately needed.

But those memories, those experiences, had shaped her. They had colored the way she viewed herself, the world, and her ability to trust. As she grew older, she became more self-conscious, more distant from those around her. She avoided relationships, fearing intimacy. She built walls, convinced that she would never be good enough, that she didn’t deserve love or peace.

“The trauma, Lena, it didn’t just happen in a moment. It’s been compounded over time,” Dr. Mitchell continued, breaking her train of thought. “What you’re experiencing now is a reflection of the defense mechanisms you developed as a way to survive. The fear, the anxiety, the numbness—they’re all part of your brain’s attempt to protect you from further harm.”

Lena nodded slowly, as though processing each word. She had learned a great deal about trauma in the past few months, but it still felt like she was wading through murky waters. “But how do I stop feeling like this?” she asked, her voice cracking slightly. “How do I stop living in the shadow of something that happened so long ago?”

Dr. Mitchell leaned forward slightly, his tone soft but clear. “The first step is understanding that healing doesn’t mean forgetting. It means transforming your relationship with the past. Instead of seeing the trauma as something that defines you, you begin to see it as something that shaped you, yes, but doesn’t control you anymore.”

Lena looked down at her hands. The thought of transforming her relationship with her past seemed both daunting and freeing. But how could she possibly start?

“One of the most powerful tools we have in trauma recovery is self-compassion,” Dr. Mitchell said, sensing the struggle in her expression. “It’s about treating yourself with the same kindness, patience, and understanding that you would offer a loved one who is suffering. You have to stop blaming yourself for things that were out of your control. You were a child, and you did what you could to survive.”

Lena felt a pang in her chest. Blame. It had been a constant companion. She blamed herself for the fights between her parents. She blamed herself for not being strong enough to stand up to them. She blamed herself for not being able to fix everything. It was a burden she had carried for so long, and the thought of letting it go felt almost impossible.

“But how do I forgive myself for everything?” she asked, her voice barely above a whisper. “How do I let go of all the anger and regret?”

Dr. Mitchell’s eyes softened with empathy. “Forgiveness doesn’t mean condoning what happened or forgetting the pain. It means releasing the hold that anger and regret have over you. It’s about giving yourself permission to heal, to feel without judgment. The anger you feel—it’s valid. But holding on to it forever only keeps you tied to the past.”

Lena closed her eyes for a moment, taking in the weight of his words. Letting go. Forgiving herself. It seemed so foreign, so out of reach. And yet, for the first time, there was a flicker of hope, a small ember of possibility.

“Start small,” Dr. Mitchell continued, as if reading her mind. “It’s not about trying to forgive yourself all at once. It’s about taking one small step at a time. Maybe today, you allow yourself to feel whatever emotions arise without judgment. Maybe tomorrow, you can tell yourself that you’re doing your best, and that’s enough.”

Lena let out a shaky breath, feeling both exhausted and strangely relieved. One step at a time. She could do that.

The next few weeks were challenging. Lena had moments where she felt as though the weight of her past was too much to bear. But there were also moments where she could feel something shifting. It wasn’t immediate or dramatic, but there was a quiet, subtle change. She began to allow herself to feel her emotions, to sit with them instead of pushing them down. She started journaling, writing down the pain, the sadness, the fear—and then gently letting it go.

One evening, she sat by the window in her apartment, watching the sunset. The colors in the sky were soft and vibrant, and for the first time in a long while, Lena felt a sense of calm. She didn’t feel healed, not completely, but she felt a glimmer of something new. Something that wasn’t defined by her past.

The next day, during her session with Dr. Mitchell, she smiled faintly as she recounted the moment.

“I think I felt peace for the first time,” she said softly. “I didn’t expect it, but it was there, like a little crack in the wall I’ve built around myself.”

Dr. Mitchell smiled warmly. “That’s progress, Lena. Real progress. And that peace, however small it may seem now, will grow. It’s a sign that you’re reconnecting with yourself in a way that’s healthy and healing.”

Lena nodded, her heart full of a quiet hope. The road ahead was still long, but she was no longer walking it alone. She had the tools, the support, and most importantly, the strength to keep moving forward.

Healing wasn’t about erasing the past. It was about learning to live with it—understanding it, transforming it, and eventually using it as a source of strength rather than a chain that held her back. Slowly but surely, Lena was learning to embrace the person she was becoming: someone who had experienced pain but was learning to love herself, flaws and all.

And that, in itself, was a kind of healing.
