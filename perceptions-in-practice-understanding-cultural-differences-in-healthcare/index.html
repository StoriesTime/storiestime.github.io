Perceptions in Practice: Understanding Cultural Differences in Healthcare


The sun had dipped below the horizon and twilight bathed the small village in an ethereal glow. The village was tucked away in a remote mountainous region in Asia, inhabited by a tight-knit community who valued their age-old traditions. Healthcare in this village had always been based on ancient remedies and local wisdom passed down through generations. However, World Health Organization’s (WHO) efforts to provide modern healthcare to such remote regions was now bringing about a significant cultural shift in the treatment practices.

The center of attention on this chilly evening was a small gathering of villagers, led by the village chief. This gathering served dual purposes - to deliberate on an unforeseen illness outbreak and to discuss new healthcare practices brought upon by the outsiders. They were awaiting the arrival of an illness intervention expert from WHO.

It was twilight, birds had stopped singing and only a wind-generated noise could be heard when the expert finally arrived. Dr. Yasmin introduced herself in local language, “Namaste, mai Dr. Yasmin huṁ.” The air of respect around her suggested that her expertise was highly sought after.

The village chief cleared his throat and said, “Doctor, you are welcome to share what modern medicine has to teach us. We, however, request you to respect our beliefs and practices. We trust them; we take them as truths.” Dr. Yasmin nodded in agreement, appreciating the village chief’s dignified request.

Dr. Yasmin began her intervention by describing how germs caused diseases and how vaccination can help prevent them. The villagers listened intently, hands on their hearts and bafflement beaming from their eyes. Dr. Yasmin then demonstrated ways to powerfully reduce the risk of infection, explaining about sanitation and hygiene practices.

A large woman, named Niru, interrupted after Dr. Yasmin’s presentation, “We appreciate your words, Doctor. They are enlightening, but… we have been using home-brewed alcohol to sanitize. And we have been doing fine until now.” The room echoed with a hush, showcasing respect for Niru’s courage to share her doubts.

Dr. Yasmin smiled warmly, acknowledging Niru’s apprehension. “Indeed, you have been doing well. However, using alcohol isn’t enough. You also need to ensure proper waste disposal, efficient handwashing techniques, and disease-specific health advisories depending on the outbreak,” Dr. Yasmin added.

The village chief intervened, “You see, Niru, our ways have worked fine, but we need to embrace this new reality. Our old ways can’t combat new threats.” A murmur of agreement washed over the crowd.

As the discussion continued, the sun rose once again in the eastern sky, rejuvenating the rural horizon. It was apparent that Dr. Yasmin’s words were challenging perceived wisdom. The villagers appreciated their immemorial traditions, but they also gradually realized the necessity of adapting to the new healthcare practices.

In the next days, they created a healthcare management committee comprising village elder and health professionals from both traditional and modern streams. Each day, the duo had a different experience, dealing with traditional healing, pharmaceutical medicine, mental health, and hygiene practices. And this synthesis of ancient wisdom and modern medicine aimed at painting a holistic picture of healthcare within the community’s cultural setting.

The first formal meeting between the healthcare committee and Dr. Yasmin began with the chief stating, “Doctor, you have opened our eyes to a new reality. While we treasure our traditional practices, we need to adapt to the changing times. Disease-causing factors are drastically different now, and we need modern approaches to combat them.” Dr. Yasmin agreed, emphasizing the strategic necessity for the community to adapt and integrate.

Together, the committee and Dr. Yasmin devised a collective health strategy. By the end of the third month, the village’s cleanliness level matched that of metropolitan cities, and villagers had developed a new viewpoint toward healthcare. This transformation not only guaranteed healthy lives for the villagers, but it also cherished the community’s diverse cultural heritage.

Notably, they didn’t abandon their traditions. Rather, the villagers found new ways to incorporate and integrate traditional medicine with standard healthcare procedures. This amalgamation gave birth to a healthier, more aware village. The once-lowkey village now served as a model to the world - keeping the essence of culture alive while adapting to global changes. It was a testament to the wisdom of the people and their willingness to evolve, learning from both past knowledge and present understanding.
