Unseen in the Feed: A Tale of Virtual Isolation


Garrick scrolled through his social media feed with the precision of muscle memory, the soothing rhythm a nightly ritual after work. Posts blurred past his eyes: colorful brunches, gym selfies, vacation snapshots from white sand beaches. But something about tonight was different. Despite the countless vibrant images, Garrick felt remarkably empty, like he was siphoning bits of his life into a digital void.

He paused at a photo posted by his best friend, Lenora, who had recently gone mountain climbing in the Rockies. The caption read, “The world feels infinite up here, but all that matters is the connection.” A dozen mutual friends had commented below, congratulating her or reminiscing about similar adventures. Garrick’s thumb hovered over the “like” button but then retreated. Connection. The word sank into his thoughts like moss clinging to an abandoned stairwell.

It had been months since Lenora had texted him directly, longer still since anyone had asked how he was doing offline. He grimaced, realizing he was merely a passive observer of her success. She’d amassed followers like athletes collect medals, but Garrick—without audacity or a picturesque life to flaunt—had faded into the background.

He glanced at his own profile. The last image he’d posted was a blurry photo of his dog attempting to climb his couch, two months ago. It had garnered a meager eight likes. Beneath it lay a littered timeline of links to news articles, random musings nobody replied to, and an occasional snapshot of his nondescript little apartment. Without realizing it, Garrick had become unseen—not just by users scrolling past, but by the platform itself.

He leaned back in his chair and wondered: Does anyone notice me anymore?

The next morning, Garrick awoke to the electronic chime of his alarm clock. Between brushing his teeth and pouring coffee into his thermos, the realization lingered that he hadn’t received a single direct message in weeks. His phone remained silent unless buzzing with some faceless corporate promotion.

The subtle sting of insignificance grew stronger on his commute. Sitting in the crowded subway, he stared down at his phone screen, hungry for validation yet unwilling to post something just for attention. The news apps didn’t offer much, just a bland headline about stock market turbulence and the launch of yet another streaming platform. Nothing that warranted commentary. Garrick flipped absently to his social media feed without thinking.

As expected, everything shone with polished perfection. Mostly curated moments and victories flattened into a scrolling tableau of comparisons. It wasn’t jealousy that stirred inside him—it was something murkier. Why did connection feel so elusive when it was written across every post?

The algorithm, it seemed, had cloaked him in invisibility, relegating his existence to the margins. His thoughts teetered uneasily between resignation and rebellion. If being unseen online meant he didn’t matter, did it imply his offline existence carried the same insignificance?

Returning home that evening, Garrick resolved to conduct a strange little experiment. He had no grand plan, only festering curiosity. By analyzing his feed, he realized three things: people garnered likes by flaunting achievements, staging authenticity, or shocking others with provocative statements. Garrick decided to attempt all three in a layered gamble with the algorithm’s favor.

For his first post, Garrick uploaded a carefully edited photo of his bookshelf featuring rare tomes, captioning it: Finally completed organizing after years of collecting treasures. What book most changed your life? It was innocent enough, designed to invite engagement without seeming desperate.

The likes trickled in sporadically—mostly from acquaintances he barely spoke to—but the activity wasn’t enough to make waves. Next came a plunge into authenticity. Garrick snapped a photo of his mundane dinner—a simple bowl of stir-fried vegetables—and added the text: Eating alone tonight. Sometimes solitude feels like friendship with yourself. A few users commented, sharing their own thoughts about solitude, but the engagement remained tepid.

It wasn’t until Garrick composed his third post—a pointed critique of growing disconnection in digital spaces—that things took a dramatic turn. “Are we losing touch with reality by curating lives that prioritize validation over authenticity?” he asked, along with a photo of himself staring into his computer screen, eyes heavy with truth.

Something clicked. Within hours, comments erupted beneath his post. Some hailed him as insightful, others accused him of cynicism. The polarized feedback somehow tugged viewers toward him. Garrick watched his notifications grow, equal parts fascinated and conflicted.

But the experiment did little to fill the void. The comments and likes fluttered like moths around his screen, ephemeral and devoid of weight. Even when people mentioned his name, tagging him in follow-up discussions, Garrick felt unnamed—a digitally reconstructed silhouette of himself.

Weeks passed, and he continued to play with the formula. His life offline stagnated as Garrick began crafting increasingly nuanced posts designed to trigger emotional responses. Strange, then, how none of them restored his sense of connection. Even after his follower count doubled, his phone was still silent at night. He finally understood what it meant to feel crowded and utterly alone.

One night, as Garrick stared at the glowing rectangle in his hand, something in him shifted. If digital spaces offered only surface interaction, what did real connection look like? He closed his phone for the first time in hours and stepped into the night with no destination in mind. The air smelled faintly of rain, and distant streetlights blurred against the darkness.

The world hummed gently, indifferent to his existence. Yet somehow, that felt more honest—more present—than crowds applauding his digital persona. Garrick wandered for hours, his thoughts finally shedding their desperate need for affirmation. Loneliness bent and scattered into something simpler: quiet solitude.

Days faded into weeks, and Garrick slowly untangled himself from the grind of performative internet life. He didn’t abandon technology entirely, but neither did he bend to its demands. Sometimes, his accounts grew dusty without updates. Curiously, when he returned, he’d find that fewer people thought to notice. The fleeting space he’d claimed on others’ feeds—like water parting around a pebble—closed behind him.

He didn’t mind. Garrick began investing energy into offline connections that lingered in spaces beyond scrolling timelines. It took effort—more than he anticipated—but the outcomes felt genuine enough to quiet his restless heart. There were no flashing notifications or brightly colored metrics to measure worth, just warm smiles and shared moments. As Garrick learned, value wasn’t found in the algorithm’s favor; it was found in slowing down enough to truly be seen.

In the end, Garrick wasn’t unseen at all—not in ways that mattered.
