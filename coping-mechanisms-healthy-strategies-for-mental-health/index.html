Coping Mechanisms: Healthy Strategies for Mental Health


Ellie had always loved the scent of lilacs. There was something about their soft, floral aroma that reminded her of simpler times—childhood summers spent at her grandmother’s house, her chubby fingers reaching for the blooms in the garden. Now, as an adult, she found herself returning to that memory whenever the weight of life threatened to crush her under its invisible thumb.

The small apartment Ellie lived in bore little resemblance to the sprawling old home from her youth. Its walls closed in at night, especially when her anxiety whispered sharp nothings into her ears. The job she had once been thrilled to land now felt like a cage, her desk piled high with reports she dared not leave unfinished. Deadlines loomed like thunderclouds, the storm growing heavier with each passing hour.

It had started subtly enough. A missed night of sleep here and there. A forgotten lunch at work. But by the time the panic attacks came—sudden, breath-stealing things that left her clutching her chest—Ellie knew she couldn’t ignore it anymore. She needed help. What she didn’t realize was that the help she sought wouldn’t just be a solution to her spiraling; it would change her life.

The first time Ellie sat in Dr. Avery’s office, she felt silly. The walls were painted a soothing shade of green, and the couch she perched on was deceptively soft, but that did little to restrain the anxiety gnawing at her insides. She fidgeted with the hem of her sweater, unsure where to begin.

“You’re not here to impress me, Ellie,” Dr. Avery said gently. “You don’t have to get this perfect. Let’s just start with what’s brought you here today.”

Ellie wanted to laugh. WHAT hadn’t brought her here? The sleepless nights, the unbearable heaviness of an unspoken sadness that coated her world gray, the fear that if she didn’t fix herself, she’d crack open and everything would spill out, unfixable.

“I just… I feel like I can’t breathe sometimes,” she said finally. “It’s like there’s this weight on my chest, and my heart races and—” She stopped, her words caught in her throat. “I just don’t know how to be okay anymore.”

Dr. Avery nodded, her calm eyes meeting Ellie’s. “Feeling that way is exhausting, isn’t it?” Her voice was like a balm, soothing but not dismissive. “Have you ever considered learning strategies to help you cope with these feelings when they come up?”

Ellie shook her head.

“Let’s explore some things together,” Dr. Avery said. “But first, let me say this: you are not broken. What you’re experiencing is something many people face, and with the right tools, you can manage it. You don’t have to fight this alone.”

Over the weeks that followed, Ellie worked diligently with Dr. Avery. At first, it felt mechanical—learning breathing exercises, scheduling short breaks into her workday, and practicing something called mindfulness. Ellie wasn’t sure any of it would help. But slowly, she began to notice things she hadn’t seen before.

One morning, walking to work, she paused under a flowering tree and marveled at how blue the sky looked against its pink blossoms. It was a small thing, she thought, but a month earlier, she wouldn’t have noticed at all.

Dr. Avery called it an “intentional pause.” Ellie called it a miracle.

Still, it wasn’t always easy. There were days when the panic returned, days when Ellie slipped back into old habits of working through lunch and ignoring her body’s cries for rest. But now, Ellie was armed with tools. She would close her office door, sit with her feet firmly planted on the floor, and breathe in for four counts, hold for four counts, and exhale for six. She visualized her grandmother’s lilac bushes, picturing the soft shapes of the blooms and how they swayed in summer breezes. It didn’t always solve everything, but it reminded her she had the power to guide herself back to calm.

The turning point came unexpectedly.

It was a Friday evening, and Ellie was convinced she’d ruin the weekend if she didn’t finish a report before leaving work. Tension radiated through her shoulders as her fingers danced frantically on the keyboard. The office was nearly empty, and the fluorescent light above her desk hummed faintly.

Somewhere in the back of her mind, she knew she was spiraling. She knew she should step away. But the thoughts kept coming—What if you miss the deadline? What if they think you’re incapable? What if—

And then, her phone buzzed. It was a reminder she had set earlier with Dr. Avery’s help. The notification read, Take a break. You deserve to breathe.

Ellie stared at the screen for a moment before her breath hitched—half a laugh, half a sob. It was such a simple thing. “You deserve to breathe.” She hadn’t realized how often she had denied herself even that.

She closed her laptop, shut off the light, and walked outside. The cool evening air carried her tears away as she made her way to the small park down the street. The sun had dipped below the horizon, painting the sky in fiery hues of orange and pink, and Ellie let it wash over her.

In the months that followed, Ellie continued to grow. She wasn’t fixed, but she was healing. She took up journaling, finding that writing down her thoughts helped untangle the messy web of worries she often carried. On weekends, she tried mindful walking—no phone, no distractions, just the crunch of gravel under her boots and the rhythm of the world around her. And in moments of panic, she returned to her breath, grounding herself in the beauty of the present.

It wasn’t just the tools themselves that helped Ellie; it was the shift in mindset. She began to think of caring for her mental health the way one might care for a garden. Some days needed more work than others, but every small act of care mattered. Drinking water, laughing with friends, learning to say “no” without guilt—these were seeds she planted for herself.

One spring afternoon, Ellie found herself at a nursery. It had been years since she’d bought plants, but something about the season called to her. As she walked between rows of bright blooms, a familiar scent stopped her in her tracks.

Lilacs.

Reaching out, she touched their petal-soft tips. Their scent brought a dozen memories rushing back, but for the first time, Ellie didn’t cry when she remembered her grandmother’s garden. Instead, she smiled.

At home, she placed the lilac pot on her windowsill, where it could catch the sunlight. That evening, as the golden light refracted through the glass, Ellie sat beside it, grateful not just for the plant, but for the journey that had brought her back to herself.

In the field of her mind, Ellie realized, there would always be weeds. But through patience and care, she also knew something else: flowers could grow there, too.
