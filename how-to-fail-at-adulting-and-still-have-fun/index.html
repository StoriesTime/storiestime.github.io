How to Fail at Adulting and Still Have Fun


It started, like most things, with a small, somewhat inconspicuous decision that snowballed into an avalanche of questionable choices. I had always been one to avoid the heavy responsibilities of adulthood. But somehow, I had reached the age where people expect you to have your life together. To “adult.” To pay bills, manage time, hold a full-time job, cook meals, and handle life’s various curveballs. Instead, I was an expert at finding creative ways to dodge all of those things. And yet, I wasn’t miserable. In fact, I had more fun than most people I knew.

It wasn’t a matter of wanting to be irresponsible. It was more about simply refusing to take life too seriously, even when the world around me seemed obsessed with the idea of growing up. Sure, I lived in a cluttered apartment with a fridge full of forgotten takeout, and my laundry was constantly in some state of being “almost done.” But somehow, I made it work. And somehow, I had more fun than anyone I knew who was fully entrenched in the so-called “adult” grind.

Chapter 1: The Myth of “Adulting”

I still remember my first real job interview after college. The excitement was palpable—mostly because I had no idea what I was doing. I had spent my last semester juggling a part-time job, studying for exams that I was barely passing, and planning what to wear to the interview (which, as it turned out, was the hardest part). I showed up in a slightly too-big blazer and sat across from a hiring manager who was likely wondering how I had made it this far without completely flunking out of life.

“Tell me about a time you handled a difficult situation at work,” she asked, her eyes narrowing.

I blinked. My mind went blank.

“Well, there was this one time when I spilled coffee all over my boss’s desk,” I said, thinking quickly. “It was a total disaster. But I cleaned it up, apologized, and made sure to buy him a new coffee mug after. I think he appreciated the effort.”

There was an awkward silence before the woman smiled, clearly unsure whether to be horrified or impressed.

In the end, I got the job. Mostly because I was charming, I think. Or maybe because the hiring manager had also once spilled coffee on someone else’s desk and had somehow survived it. But that moment marked the beginning of my career in the world of “adulting,” and I quickly realized it wasn’t as glamorous as everyone made it out to be.

Chapter 2: The Art of Dodging Responsibilities

I can’t say I ever embraced the idea of a rigid routine. The 9-to-5 grind? An endless cycle of waking up, commuting, working, and then trying to figure out what to do with the precious few hours of freedom in the evening. Most people would call it “adult life.” I, on the other hand, saw it as a necessary evil, something to be avoided when possible.

I had learned the fine art of procrastination. Not in a harmful way, but in a way that allowed me to enjoy life in a more carefree manner. My bills rarely got paid on time, but somehow, the late fees didn’t stack up as much as I had anticipated. I learned that the world didn’t immediately implode if you missed a deadline—sometimes, people even forgave you.

For example, I had a month-long battle with my landlord over the rent. I had “forgotten” to pay it for three weeks, and I lived in constant fear of eviction. But every time I was called out, I promised I would take care of it soon. The landlord, an older gentleman who also seemed to be a little tired of the adult world, finally just shrugged and said, “Next time, don’t make me chase you.” I promised I wouldn’t, but I’m sure he didn’t believe me.

Despite all my misadventures with responsibilities, I always made sure to have fun. I took spontaneous trips to the beach, even when I knew I had work the next day. I hosted dinner parties where no one was expected to bring anything, and the theme was always “easy and lazy.” If there was a chance to relax, I seized it with open arms.

Chapter 3: Embracing the Chaos

One of the perks of not fully committing to the whole “adult” thing was that I didn’t have to constantly worry about making everything perfect. And that made life far more enjoyable.

There was that time I decided to bake a cake for a friend’s birthday. The internet said it was easy, and I convinced myself that my lack of baking skills wouldn’t matter. I bought the ingredients, spent an hour watching YouTube tutorials, and then—after an intense mix of overconfidence and impatience—ended up with a cake that resembled a sad, collapsed pancake more than anything else.

But I wasn’t deterred. I covered it with frosting, added some sprinkles, and plopped a candle on top. When my friend blew out the candle, everyone cheered. It didn’t matter that the cake looked like something from a horror movie. The fact was, I was having a blast. And my friends were too.

I didn’t strive for perfection; I strove for fun. I embraced the chaos, the messiness of life. My apartment was always cluttered, but there was never a shortage of laughter or impromptu dance parties in the living room. I could barely keep my plants alive, but at least I tried. Every failed project was just another story to tell, and I loved telling those stories.

Chapter 4: The Joy of Unfinished Tasks

In the world of adulting, people love to cross things off to-do lists. And while I certainly made to-do lists (I was very good at making lists), I was even better at leaving them unfinished. But here’s the thing: I didn’t let that get to me. I didn’t let the fact that I hadn’t cleaned the bathroom in weeks or that my taxes were consistently filed late stop me from enjoying my life.

Take my “unpaid” gym membership. I signed up for a year-long subscription, fully intending to go. But months went by, and I never set foot in the gym. Yet every month, I paid the fee without complaint. Some might have seen it as a waste of money, but I preferred to think of it as a subscription to the idea of fitness. It was the thought that counted, right?

And so, I continued with my life. I never felt overwhelmed by the things I didn’t do, because I focused on the things I did do. Like attending a friend’s spontaneous barbecue, or binge-watching an entire series of documentaries on obscure topics, or sitting on the couch and playing video games until I ran out of snacks.

Adulting didn’t require checking off a never-ending list of tasks. It was about finding joy in the small moments. It was about knowing that even if I didn’t have it all together, I was still having a good time.

Chapter 5: The Perks of Ignoring Expectations

As I drifted further away from the typical “adult” lifestyle, I found that people started to admire my carefree attitude. I didn’t have it all figured out, but I was comfortable in my own skin. I may not have been keeping up with the latest trends or going to the gym every day, but I knew how to enjoy life.

And in a world full of constant pressure to perform, that was something rare. People didn’t ask how I managed to make a meal out of a jar of peanut butter and some stale crackers; they were more interested in how I was always so relaxed. They wanted to know how I managed to be happy despite not ticking off all the expected boxes of adulthood.

My secret? I had learned that it was okay to fail. In fact, failing was just part of the adventure. There was no rulebook for living; there was only what made you feel good. And what made me feel good was enjoying the little moments, embracing imperfection, and never letting life become too serious.

I wasn’t “adulting” the way society expected, but I was living my version of it. And honestly, I wouldn’t trade it for anything.
