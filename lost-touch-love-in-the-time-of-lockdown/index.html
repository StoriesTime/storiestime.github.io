Lost Touch: Love in the Time of Lockdown


The streets were quiet, hollow—like the world had inhaled and forgotten to exhale. Emma stared out the small kitchen window of her London flat, tracing invisible patterns on the cold glass with her fingertips. It had been almost eight weeks since the lockdown began. Eight weeks since the world shifted from bustling streets and crowded cafés into a fragmented existence of video calls, delivery vans, and masked exchanges at supermarket checkouts.

Her phone buzzed just as the kettle finished boiling. Steam rose, curling in gentle wisps, as Emma slid her thumb across the screen, unlocking the notification. It was him. Ethan.

“Morning. Cool gray again, or does London surprise me with an actual sunbeam?”

She smiled, her fingers hesitating for just a moment before typing back a reply.

“Gray, always gray. But rumors of optimism swirl in the air. You?”

Ethan was nearly 5,000 miles away, quarantined in his tiny Brooklyn apartment, but his words had a way of making Emma feel as though he was sitting right there in her kitchen, filling the room with his easy laughter and quick wit. What had started as a casual exchange on a travel forum months before the pandemic now felt like the cornerstone of her days.

As the kettle whistled sharply, Emma reached for the tea bags, the familiar smell of Earl Grey grounding her amidst the chaos. It was strange how a single message from Ethan could make the loneliness lift, even if only for a fleeting moment.

Their connection had started with simple conversations about life and travel—wanderlust-fueled dreams of places neither of them could visit now. Ethan was a native New Yorker, intent on exploring every nook and cranny of his city before venturing elsewhere. Emma had been planning her third trip to Southeast Asia before everything had come to a grinding halt. Lockdown had turned their flirtation into something else, something deeper. It wasn’t just banter anymore—it was survival, emotional sustenance in a world that felt suddenly small.

They spoke of future plans as though they were characters in an unfinished play. Galway, Bali, Kyoto—each destination a promise inked into words exchanged at midnight. Over time, these dreams became less about escapism and more about helping each other imagine a future. At first, Emma wondered whether it was all fleeting, a temporary distraction in the face of uncertainty. But Ethan didn’t fade away. If anything, he seemed to shine brighter against the dim backdrop of isolation.

She’d never felt so close to someone who was so far.

The days stretched on like elastic, sometimes snapping back in brittle frustration. There were moments when Ethan sounded distant, drowning under the oppressive quiet of his fourth-floor apartment. Other times, he would come alive, describing the chaos of the city beyond his window, even as it lay muted: delivery bikes weaving through empty avenues, sunset hues bouncing off skyscrapers that seldom saw light in busier days.

Emma couldn’t help but envision him pacing, coffee in hand, planting dreams of someday beneath the ceiling of his cramped space. She found herself waiting eagerly for his messages, rereading old ones as though they held some potent secret. Was this love? Or just the phantom limb of human connection, desperate to find someone to hold onto?

She hated asking herself that question. Love had always been tactile to her—kisses at crosswalks, warm arms in bed after a long day, shared laughter floating between glasses of wine. But now everything was distilled into words, through screens and cables. Could you build something real out of such fragile threads?

One day, Ethan suggested something bold.

“Emma… let’s actually see each other. Virtually, I mean. I can’t keep imagining your face without knowing it.”

Emma hesitated. She wasn’t exactly camera-shy, but their back-and-forth had been so strangely sacred, untouched by the superficiality of seeing one another up close. What if the magic unraveled when the pixels tried to piece them together?

Still, curiosity won. They set up a time, 8 p.m. London, 3 p.m. New York. When the screen flickered to life, Emma found herself holding her breath. Ethan appeared, sharper in detail than her mind had ever painted him. His dark curls were slightly disheveled, and his glasses sat a little crooked on his nose. He smiled, a kind of shy, lopsided grin that melted her as instantly as any handwritten message had before.

“You look…” Ethan started, then broke into a quiet laugh. “I was going to say radiant but realized that’s a bit dramatic for a pandemic first date.”

Emma couldn’t stop laughing. “Radiant? Really? You’re not too bad yourself. Though you’re missing the classically New York backdrop I imagined—do I spot laundry hanging in the background?”

“Very authentic Brooklyn chic,” he joked, gesturing to the cluttered chaos behind him. “But hey, you have teapots, so I feel like this unofficially qualifies as a British rom-com.”

They talked for hours that night, and though the technology wasn’t perfect—the occasional glitches, the brief interruptions—they felt undeniably closer.

Weeks passed, and the routine of their virtual dates became second nature. They cooked dinner over video calls, sending each other recipes and laughing at their respective missteps in the kitchen. They watched movies together, pressing play at the same time and texting through scenes they loved or hated. Each shared experience chipped away at the confines of isolation.

Emma often marveled at how something so intangible could make her feel grounded. Ethan was the only one who didn’t make her spiral into existential thoughts during these endless days indoors. Their friendship—or love, she dared whisper silently in her head—had become the anchor in all this uncertainty. She couldn’t imagine getting through lockdown without him.

And yet, there were hard moments too. Ethan would apologize when his texts came late, when he couldn’t summon the energy to talk, when the walls of his tiny apartment seemed to close in. Emma understood—those moments hit her as well, waves of sadness so sweeping that even a good book or comforting tea couldn’t stem the tide. But knowing Ethan was there made the sadness sting less.

One night, after the world outside had grown completely still, Ethan said something that pulled Emma out of herself.

“Do you believe this ends?”

Emma didn’t respond right away. Instead, she looked at the ceiling as if some answer was etched into its aged plaster. She could hear the quiet tremor in his voice, the raw vulnerability of confessing doubt.

“I don’t know. I don’t think anyone does. But I know this—when it does, when the doors open back up and the streets hum again, I want to find you.”

There it was. The truth at last. A sudden silence filled the space between them, a silence bursting with possibility.

“You think this city-boy could survive a rain-soaked London summer?” Ethan teased, but his words were threaded with something deeper, something more certain.

Emma smiled. “I think I’d take my chances on you.”

The days began to blur, but one thing never did—their shared promise of someday. It didn’t matter when the world reopened. What mattered was that they had found each other in the darkest time, proving that even amid isolation, connection could break through the noise.
