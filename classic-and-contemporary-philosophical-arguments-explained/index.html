Classic and Contemporary Philosophical Arguments Explained


Under the golden light filtering through the stained glass window of an ancient library, two friends sat engrossed in conversation. Elara, a bright-eyed philosophy student, and Theo, a seasoned professor known for his lively discussions, embarked on a quest to unravel some of the most significant philosophical arguments, bridging classical and contemporary thought.

The Dance of Being and Nothingness: Ontological Musings

Their dialogue began with existence itself. “Tell me, Theo,” Elara started, “what makes the ontological argument such a cornerstone in philosophy?”

Theo smiled, tracing a finger along a dusty page of Anselm’s Proslogion. “Anselm’s ontological argument is a bold attempt to prove God’s existence through pure reason alone. It suggests that the very concept of a greatest being — one that than which none greater can be conceived — implies existence. If such a being didn’t exist, it wouldn’t be the greatest, because existence is a perfection.”

Elara nodded. “So, it claims that if God exists in our understanding, He must exist in reality too; otherwise, we would conceive of something greater — a God that exists.”

“But,” Theo said thoughtfully, “philosophers like Kant criticized this argument, arguing that existence is not a predicate or a perfection that can be simply attached to a concept. It doesn’t enhance the idea of a thing; it merely posits that the thing is present in reality.”

Elara’s eyes sparkled with curiosity. “That’s where the contemporary perspectives come in, right? How do modern philosophers approach this?”

“They often incorporate modal logic,” Theo explained, “like Alvin Plantinga using possible worlds to argue that if God’s existence is possible, then God must exist in some possible world, and thus in every possible world, including ours.”

This leap from medieval scholasticism to modal metaphysics showed Elara how classical ideas found fresh life in contemporary frameworks.

Morality’s Compass: From Divine Command to Ethical Naturalism

Their conversation pivoted to ethics. “Is morality inherently tied to divine authority, or can it stand alone?” Elara asked.

Theo chuckled. “The Divine Command Theory proposes that moral rightness stems from God’s commands. But consider Euthyphro’s question from Plato’s dialogues — is something good because God commands it, or does God command it because it is good? This challenges theologians and ethicists alike.”

Elara recalled how that question shook her own views. “If morality depends solely on divine will, it could seem arbitrary. But if morality is independent, then divine command is unnecessary.”

“Exactly,” Theo said. “Modern philosophers often lean toward ethical naturalism or moral realism, suggesting that moral truths exist objectively and can be discovered through reason or human experience.”

Elara leaned forward. “So, for example, utilitarianism, as a consequentialist approach, judges actions by their outcomes — the greatest happiness for the greatest number — rather than divine edicts?”

“Spot on,” said Theo. “John Stuart Mill took Jeremy Bentham’s ideas and integrated a more qualitative aspect to happiness. Contemporary debates have expanded to discuss preference utilitarianism, rule utilitarianism, and more nuanced consequences.”

“But there are also critiques,” Elara said, “like the deontological ethics of Kant, which focuses on duty and intention rather than outcomes.”

“Right,” Theo replied. “Kant’s categorical imperative demands that one act only according to maxims that can be universally applied. It respects human autonomy and dignity, contrasting with the sometimes cold calculations of utilitarianism.”

Elara marveled at the rich tapestry of ethical reasoning, appreciating the dynamic tension between competing frameworks.

The Problem of Knowledge: Skepticism Meets Epistemology

“So, how do we really know what we think we know?” Elara asked. “Skepticism seems to challenge all of our claims.”

Theo leaned back. “Skepticism, from ancient Pyrrho to the more methodical Descartes, pushes us to doubt. Descartes, for example, famously doubted everything to find an indubitable foundation — Cogito, ergo sum — I think, therefore I am.”

Elara smiled at the familiarity. “But even that only guarantees self-awareness, not external reality.”

“True,” Theo acknowledged. “Contemporary epistemology addresses skepticism by examining the nature, scope, and limits of knowledge. Reliabilism, for instance, argues that beliefs formed through reliable processes count as knowledge.”

Elara probed further, “What about the Gettier problem? That seems to show that justified true belief might not be enough for knowledge.”

“Yes,” Theo said, enthusiastic, “that was a significant wake-up call. It compelled philosophers to refine the definition of knowledge and consider factors like luck or epistemic luck.”

The pair explored various theories like internalism vs. externalism, foundationalism vs. coherentism, and virtue epistemology, illustrating a vibrant field adjusting to new challenges.

Free Will and Determinism: The Puzzle of Human Agency

Their discourse turned to one of philosophy’s perennial puzzles: free will. “Are we truly free, or are our choices determined by prior causes?” Elara inquired.

Theo nodded. “The deterministic worldview, rooted in classical physics, suggests every event is causally necessitated. This challenges the idea of libertarian free will — the notion that humans can make genuinely free choices not determined by past events.”

Elara reflected, “But compatibilists argue that free will is compatible with determinism if freedom is understood as acting according to one’s desires without external constraint.”

“Exactly,” said Theo. “Philosophers like Daniel Dennett defend compatibilism, suggesting that free will is about the capacity to reflect, deliberate, and act upon reasons.”

Elara mused, “Neuroscientific experiments seem to show decisions are made unconsciously before we are aware of them. Doesn’t this undermine free will?”

“That continues to be hotly debated,” Theo said. “Some argue that such findings mean our conscious experience of choice is illusory, while others interpret it differently, emphasizing the complexity of brain processes and conscious agency.”

The conversation highlighted how new scientific insights continually provoke philosophical reexamination.

Consciousness and the Mind-Body Problem: Bridging the Divide

Finally, Elara sought to understand a puzzle that transcended centuries: “How do physical processes in the brain give rise to subjective experience?”

Theo sighed, as if addressing an eternal mystery. “The mind-body problem asks how mental states relate to physical processes. Dualists like Descartes separated mind and body into distinct substances, while physicalists argue that mental states are brain states.”

Elara considered this. “But simple identity theories seem incomplete because subjective ‘qualia’ — the ‘what-it’s-like’ aspect — resist reduction to physical explanations.”

“The hard problem of consciousness,” Theo affirmed. “Contemporary philosophers like David Chalmers have emphasized this gap. Some propose panpsychism, suggesting consciousness may be a fundamental feature of the universe. Others explore emergentism or sophisticated physicalist accounts.”

Elara found herself immersed in debates that blended metaphysics, neuroscience, and philosophy of mind, recognizing the profound challenges that remain.

Epilogue: The Ever-Expanding Horizon

As the evening deepened and the library’s shadows lengthened, Elara and Theo closed their books. Their journey through classic and contemporary philosophical arguments revealed an ongoing conversation across time — a conversation about existence, morality, knowledge, freedom, and consciousness.

Elara smiled, feeling both grounded in ancient wisdom and exhilarated by the evolving landscape of modern thought. Philosophy, she realized, was less about reaching definitive answers and more about cultivating a curious, critical, and open mind — forever weaving threads of thought into the ever-expanding tapestry of human understanding.

If you’re fascinated by the deep questions of philosophy and how timeless arguments continue to shape contemporary discourse, the journey of exploration is endless—each question inviting new perspectives and insights. Whether pondering the nature of being, the foundations of morality, or the mysteries of consciousness, philosophy remains a vibrant conversation across the ages.
