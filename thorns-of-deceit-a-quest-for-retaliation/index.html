Thorns of Deceit: A Quest for Retaliation


Beneath the muted rays of a waning sun, a dense thicket of brambles stretched across the forgotten valleys of Kaelith Moor. The air carried the sharp, acrid scent of dry leaves and the ominous whispers of an impending storm. No one wandered into the moor without reason; it was a place shrouded in dark tales of betrayal, curses, and the sharp sting of failure. But tonight, a cloaked figure strode purposefully through its labyrinth, armed with both resolve and regret.

Andrin Kael locked his gaze ahead, ignoring the gnarled vines clutching at his boots as if the earth itself sought to restrain him. His path was faint, marked by faint etchings on jagged rocks—symbols his mother once taught him to decipher as a child. Those were better times: seasons of warmth, hearth tales, and trust that felt indestructible. But the present was grim, colored by betrayal and a single, rusted dagger that had found its way into his father’s back.

“Fiora,” Andrin murmured her name like a curse. It was she who had betrayed them—his childhood friend, his father’s closest confidante, and the one no one dared suspect. She had swayed like a serpent in their lives, her honeyed words and saccharine promises masking the venom beneath. Until, one bloody Harvest Moon, the truth had spilled forth like an unending tide, drowning his family in sorrow. And when she fled, her escape marked with the murder of Andrin’s father, she retreated into Kaelith Moor. For two years, she had evaded justice, her name spoken only in hushed tones, like a shadow no one could banish.

No longer.

“She won’t escape me this time,” Andrin vowed under his breath.

The moor grew darker with every step, its twisted vines turning sharper, denser, as though feeding off the bitterness that Andrin carried in his heart. With his free hand, he clutched the hilt of his sword—a family heirloom passed through generations. Though many called the blade elegant, to Andrin, it felt heavy, oppressive, a reminder of burdens he could neither shirk nor share.

Ahead, a subtle flicker caught his attention. Firelight. He allowed himself a grim smile. She was close now.

When Andrin reached the clearing, he crouched behind the massive roots of an ancient tree. Seven figures clustered around the fire—a mix of ruffians, mercenaries, and thieves he recognized from wanted posters in the towns he’d passed. Yet his focus remained fixed on the woman seated at the center of the circle. Her wild black curls shimmered in the firelight, lending her a ghostlike elegance. She held herself with the same unshakable confidence he remembered from their shared youth, but her face was harder now, her eyes carrying the weariness of a hunted beast.

Fiora.

Andrin’s grip on his sword tightened, the adrenaline surging through him interwoven with the pain of betrayal. He forced himself to remain still and observe. She commanded their respect, if not their loyalty. Each man hung on her words, nodding to her every gesture as though swayed by her charisma. He could almost hear the lies brushing past her lips, the same lies she’d used to twist his father’s mind—to destroy what should have been unbreakable.

“She’s planning something,” Andrin realized, narrowing his eyes. He needed answers as much as he craved vengeance.

But action required patience.

The night deepened, and the fire began to die. Fiora dismissed her group with a casual flick of her wrist, retreating to a small tent at the far edge of the clearing. Her men dispersed throughout the camp, taking up guard positions or collapsing to rest. Andrin waited for the shadows to swallow the camp whole before making his move.

He approached silently, crouching low to avoid detection, his heart pounding with every step. When he reached her tent, he pushed the flap aside, slipping inside like a whisper. Fiora was bent over a table, studying a worn map illuminated by a single candle. The moment she looked up, her expression shifted: shock, anger, and finally, recognition.

“You,” she breathed.

The sound of her voice sent a shiver through him—not of fear, but of something far more sinister.

“You didn’t think I’d find you,” Andrin said, his voice low.

The dagger she reached for was faster than her words, but Andrin was faster still. His blade met hers in midair, sparks flying as steel clashed. The sound was deafening in the small tent, as if the walls themselves held their breath.

“Two years,” Andrin hissed through gritted teeth. “Two years of running. Did you think I’d let you live after what you did?”

Fiora’s lips curled into a smirk, though her eyes betrayed a hint of fear. “And what exactly did I do? Enlighten me.”

“You murdered my father. You shattered my family. All for what? Gold? Power?”

She threw her weight into her blade, forcing him back a step. “You still see the world in black and white, Andrin. You think your father was some saint? You think your family mattered?”

Her words ignited something primal within him. He swung his sword with renewed fury, overcoming her defenses and pinning her against the table. The tip of his blade hovered near her throat, and for the first time, she flinched.

“End it,” she challenged, her voice quieter now, almost tender. “Or are you still the scared little boy I left behind?”

His hands trembled. Here she was, the source of his nightmares, the architect of his pain—defeated, vulnerable, at his mercy. And yet, he hesitated. Was this justice? Or merely vengeance disguised as something noble?

“Tell me why,” he demanded, his voice cracking like the storm outside. “Why did you do it?”

Fiora’s expression softened. For a moment, she looked almost human. “Because your father betrayed me first,” she whispered. “He promised me the world and then sold me out to save his own skin. Everything I did, I did to survive.”

Andrin’s grip faltered. Was she lying again? He searched her face for answers, but the storm of emotions within him clouded any clarity. He wanted to believe her. He wanted the truth to be as simple as drawing this blade across her throat and ending it. But deep down, it never was.

“Your father wasn’t a hero, Andrin,” she continued. “Neither am I. And neither are you. Justice is an illusion—one you’ll never satisfy.”

The anger drained out of him, replaced by something hollow. When he lowered his blade, she exhaled a shaky breath.

By morning, Fiora’s camp was deserted. Her men and their leader vanished like phantoms into the mist. Andrin wandered back through Kaelith Moor, the thorns scraping at his skin feeling more suffocating than ever.

The truth—or lies—she had uttered haunted him like the chain he couldn’t break. Justice wasn’t what he’d thought it would be. Retaliation had left him emptier than before.

As the moor swallowed him whole, Andrin couldn’t help but wonder: Was this what his father felt?
