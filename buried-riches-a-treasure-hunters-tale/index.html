Buried Riches: A Treasure Hunter's Tale


A chorus of cicadas filtered through the jungle canopy as David Wyatt brushed yet another bead of sweat from his brow. The air tasted of wet earth—loamy, jungle-rich, punctured by sweet rot and the spicy promise of something long hidden. He shoved aside a clutch of ferns and checked the battered journal in his pack.

He’d walked five hours since dawn. Five hours that had been punctuated by scrambling up mossy slopes, cursing under his breath at hidden roots, and listening for the snapping twigs and soft grunts that meant something bigger than a monkey stalked the shadowed depths. Still, nothing would slow him. Not hunger, not the humidity, and certainly not the lingering aches from a recent run-in with dubious customs officials in Panama City. He was too close.

The trail—what there was of it—vanished into tangled groundcover. This far out past the last remote village in the Darién, even the hardiest contraband runners feared to tread. As his uncle had always said: “Gold doesn’t lie above ground, David. If it were easy to find, someone else would be rich, not you.”

David would never admit just how often he still heard his uncle’s voice in that rough Tennessee drawl, especially now, in the half-world between ruin and revelation.

The journal, its leather soft and battered at the edges, was scrawled in three languages and half a dozen codes. It had once belonged to Captain Ramón Ortega—Spanish privateer, sometimes-pirate, and, if rumor was gospel, the last man alive to know the final resting place of the Santa Florencia’s lost fortune. Ortega had been dead for over three centuries, yet today he was David’s sole companion.

A clearing opened and David stopped, pulse thudding in his ears. The small, crumbling stone wall in the underbrush matched the sketch in Ortega’s journal; not impressive, but in these parts, history clung to the land by brushstrokes. He squatted next to a carved stone, fingers tracing a sun symbol. Twelve rays. Ortega’s riddles had spoken of twelve suns hiding in the dark heart of the earth.

He sucked in a breath and knelt, consulting the journal. The notes described a “serpent’s path, where the sun falls twice.” According to the worn compass in David’s pack, he was east of the stone wall. He scanned the surroundings—there, half-hidden, a line of stones wound away through strangler vines. Beyond the last, a depression where the jungle soil slumped into a sort of shallow pit.

He set to work, scraping away dirt and roots with hands and his trusty trowel. Minutes became hours; the sweat rolling down his back grew cold and sticky as clouds closed overhead. At last, the trowel scraped something harder than packed earth—a corner of rotting wood.

Not a chest, but planks—old but sturdy. His heart flipped. He cleared more, revealing an ancient trapdoor reinforced with corroded iron bands. He grinned, grim and triumphant.

The lock gave after a few careful taps with his chisel. He eased open the hatch. Stale, trapped air exhaled. Below, rough-hewn steps descended into darkness, limned by wheeling motes of light from above.

He paused, testing each tread, heart thumping, as he moved below ground. Five, ten, twelve steps. The path opened into a stone chamber damp with centuries of decay. His flashlight beam caught the glint of glass, the flash of pottery. Jars, all intact. But it was the raised dais in the center that drew his gaze.

On the dais sat a chest. Heavy, carved, banded with iron and sealed with wax melted away to mere crumbs. He reached for the lid, breathless, and with a push, it creaked open.

Inside, layers of coins, dull but unmistakable even beneath threads of mold—doubloons, weathered and bright. Atop the heap rested a pendant shaped like the rising sun, gold radiating twelve fine rays. He remembered the journal’s words: “Only one who brings the sun to the surface will claim her glory.” Gold and gemstone light danced in the dark, proving that sometimes, legends paid in more than dust.

But David lingered before filling his pack. He reached beneath the top layers—a habit born from caution as much as greed—and found a bundle wrapped in brittle oilskin. Inside: a packet of letters, sealed and sealed again.

Curiosity trumped caution. He opened the topmost letter. Written in a careful, spidery Spanish hand, it detailed murder and betrayal—Ortega had not buried this fortune willingly, but in fear. He’d been hunted by his own quartermaster after they’d plundered the Santa Florencia. The money was blood money, cache and curse intertwined.

Outside, thunder rumbled. Jungle shadows pressed close. David weighed the coins in his palm, heavy and almost warm, then considered the bundle of letters. Every hunter dreamed of riches, but the stories behind the gold… they were bargains struck in a different currency.

As the sky cracked open and rain battered the canopy above, David sat within his stone refuge, a modern-day explorer savoring more than gold—the quiet ache of old bones, the hunger for stories that treasure always brings.

The trek home should have left him empty, muscles burned raw and thoughts spent. But as he retraced his path, pack heavier than ever, the journal’s final entry echoed in his mind. “He who seeks gold finds secrets. He who keeps them may one day wish for only the freedom of the wind.”

It was an old truth, woven into every hunter’s tale. Gold glitters, but it’s the story, and its secrets, that stays buried deepest.

That night, David spilled the coins onto a cracked hotel nightstand, but the letters—those he slipped carefully into a new journal, one with plenty of blank pages left. Some treasures, he realized, were meant to be unearthed twice.
