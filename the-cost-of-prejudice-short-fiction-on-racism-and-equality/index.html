The Cost of Prejudice: Short Fiction on Racism and Equality


The old town of Parkerville was nestled between rolling green hills and a river that wound its way like a silvery serpent through the landscape. It was a place where everyone knew each other—or thought they did—and where tradition held the community together like the roots of an ancient tree.

Eleanor Thompson had lived in Parkerville all her life. At fifty-two, she was known as the town’s florist, her shop, The Bloom Room, a fragrant patch of color on Main Street. Her hands were always stained with soil, and her voice carried the kind of warmth that made even strangers feel like long-lost friends. But beneath the surface of that gentle demeanor, Eleanor carried a silent ache, one she’d guarded closely for years.

It began twenty years earlier, the summer when Jaden Carter moved to Parkerville.

Jaden was unlike anyone the town had seen. A Black man with deep, thoughtful eyes and a wide smile that carried the weight of distant struggles, he had come as a schoolteacher. His arrival stirred whispers and sideways glances. Some welcomed him cautiously, others outright rejected the change his presence implied.

Eleanor remembered the conversations clearly.

“It’s just not right,” Mr. Jenkins, a shopkeeper, had grumbled one morning. “This town doesn’t need outsiders changing its ways.”

Mrs. Perry, the librarian, said more softly, “But children should learn from someone with different experiences. It might broaden their horizons.”

That summer, Eleanor found herself tangled in her own contradictions. She admired Jaden’s courage and kindness but felt trapped within her community’s unforgiving expectations. At church, where she sang in the choir, the topic of Mr. Carter came up often. Prejudices, veiled in concern and fear rather than overt malice, spilled from polite lips like slow poison.

One day, a fight broke out at the park. Jaden had taken a group of students to the town baseball field for an informal practice. A few local boys, incited by their parents’ whispers, confronted him, shouting slurs. It escalated quickly, and Jaden was blasted by hateful words before the police were called. The incident became the talk of the town, dividing families and friendships.

Eleanor remembered standing at her shop’s doorway, watching the chaos unfold with tears streaking her dusty cheeks. She wanted to speak out, to defend Jaden, but fear held her tongue. Deep down, she knew her silence cost more than she ever imagined.

Years passed, and the town gradually changed. Newcomers arrived, diversity slowly seeped into Parkerville’s fabric. Yet, the old wounds remained hidden beneath polite smiles. Jaden left after five years, moving to a city where his efforts were met with encouragement rather than suspicion.

Eleanor remained, but the flower shop became her sanctuary not only for blooms but for breaking the silence she once held inside.

One autumn afternoon, a young woman named Maya walked in with hesitant steps. She was mixed-race, timid, and carried a worn-out notebook clutched tightly in her hands. Maya had heard stories about the town’s past, its struggles with acceptance, and she was searching for a place to belong.

Eleanor welcomed her with a sincere smile, offering a bouquet of sunflowers. They talked, and Maya shared her fears about being different in a world still wrestling with racism.

“I want to teach kids here, just like Jaden did,” Maya said softly. “It feels like a mountain I have to climb alone.”

Eleanor’s heart ached with recognition. She promised to help Maya build support within the community.

Together, they organized a series of workshops at the local community center, focusing on stories—real lives shattered and healed by prejudice and equality. Elders shared their memories, younger generations spoke of hope and fear.

The town’s people slowly began to confront uncomfortable truths. Eleanor herself led a discussion where she confessed her past silence and its cost: friendships lost, a community divided, and a personal life shadowed by regret.

Her openness ignited a ripple effect, encouraging others to voice their experiences and hopes for unity.

The turning point came during the annual Harvest Festival. Traditionally, it was a celebration rooted in the town’s old customs, but this year, it became a symbol of transformation.

Maya and Eleanor collaborated on an exhibit titled Reflections of Us, showcasing photographs, letters, and artifacts narrating Parkerville’s journey from prejudice toward equality. The display was moving, confronting, and ultimately unifying.

When the mayor, a man who had once whispered doubts about inclusivity, stepped in front of the crowd to speak, the air shifted.

“Our town has a history we cannot erase,” he said, “but it is our future—one of fairness and respect—that we must choose. Change isn’t easy, but it is necessary.”

Tears glistened in many eyes that day—the cost of prejudice became undeniable, but so did the power of empathy and courage.

Eleanor looked out over the crowd, her heart full. She thought of Jaden, of Maya, and of all the unseen battles fought in quiet corners. The cost of prejudice had been steep, marked by pain and lost opportunities, but the cost of silence, she realized, was the heaviest of all.

Parkerville was still a work in progress, just like its people. But now, the roots of that ancient tree were deepening in strength through shared understanding and unwavering commitment to equality.

And in the heart of town, The Bloom Room blossomed brighter than ever—an emblem not just of flowers, but of hope and transformation.
