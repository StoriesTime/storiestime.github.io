The Winds of Change: A Story from the Cultural Revolution


Li Lifen first noticed the chill in the wind the morning the village loudspeakers crackled to life, their mechanical voices slicing through dawn mist. The year was 1966, and all around Tongshan, spring stuttered between hesitant sunlight and the remnants of a long, brutal winter. Lifen paused with her hand on the kettle, watching vapor curl from the spout, listening.

“Citizens,” the loudspeaker blared, “Enter a new era. Sweep away the Four Olds—old customs, culture, habits, and ideas! Chairman Mao calls upon the masses!”

Her mother, peeling radishes at the narrow table, grunted. “Again,” she muttered, fingers moving faster, “another campaign, another round of shouting.”

Lifen smoothed her dark hair, bit back a retort. She was sixteen, had just been voted Class Propaganda Officer, a badge she wore pinned to her jacket as much for pride as for protection. She didn’t love shouting slogans. But she’d learned that not shouting was the surest way to draw trouble; her uncle had been denounced the previous autumn, and his presence lingered only as a ghost behind closed doors.

After breakfast, Lifen pulled on a battered red scarf and hurried into the street. Her classmate, Zhang Wei—tall, with angular elbows and boyish bravado—waited by the public well. He brandished a haste-scrawled poster.

“Ready?” he asked, eyes glinting.

Lifen nodded. “Where to first?”

The campaign against the Four Olds seemed at first an extended game. They pasted slogans over the faded lettering of the village’s ancestral gate: “Down with the Old World!” They took hammers to ancestral tablets, the brittle wood splintering under blows. There was laughter—nervous and uncertain, yes—but laughter nonetheless, especially when old Mrs. Guo tried to chase Red Guards out of her home with a broom. Someone snapped a photograph. It would be tacked to the schoolhouse wall for all to see.

Over the weeks, the game darkened. There were meetings—struggle sessions—where anyone suspected of old ideas was dragged before the villagers. Lifen’s teacher, Mr. Yu, who once recited Tang poetry with a voice trembling like bamboo leaves, stood accused for hoarding foreign books. She watched as students taunted him, forced him to wear a dunce’s cap, paraded him through the village square. Wei shouted loudest; Lifen sometimes joined in, sometimes bit her tongue until it bled.

At night, her mother would sit by the window, needle flashing. “Don’t draw attention,” she whispered. “Go along, but don’t stand out. Remember Uncle.”

Lifen understood. But what did one do when the very air buzzed with accusation, when neighbors were transformed into strangers by a single word?

Before the Revolution, Tongshan had been a place of slow, predictable rhythms. Now, everything was movement, suspicion, heat. Lifen grew used to the uniforms, the constant marches, the smell of sweat and paraffin and overripe gossip fermenting in humid courtyards.

She’d once liked Mr. Yu’s stories: of moonlit lakes, silent scholars. Now, she dared not meet his eyes, not when the whole village gathered to hurl insults. Still, she noticed how his hands trembled, how a single tear cut a clean path down his sooty face.

One evening, after a particularly fevered meeting, Lifen found herself alone in the shadow of the schoolhouse. Someone was crying in the alley.

It was Mr. Yu, holding the tattered remains of a calligraphy scroll. Seeing Lifen, he started, but she didn’t move.

“You were always a clever girl,” he said softly. “Do you understand what’s happening?”

She shook her head. “No, Teacher.”

“You don’t have to say anything,” he said. “But remember beauty. It is worth more than slogans.” He pressed the shreds of paper into her hands—a final lesson, silent and desperate.

She slipped them into her pocket, shivering. The winds were changing, and she could not see where they led.

The next autumn, a new set of Red Guards arrived from the city, crisp uniforms gleaming, and with them a searing sense of purpose. They went house to house searching for relics: foreign radios, Buddhist figurines, old wedding dresses, imperial-era books. Some villagers protested, most kept their eyes on their shoes.

Wei, emboldened, led the search of Lifen’s home. She heard him roar, saw his foot break open a chest that had belonged to her grandmother. He found only cabbage pickles, yet his triumph made her flinch.

“You must prove your loyalty, Lifen,” he hissed later, when the others were gone. “Help us root out the poison. Only then will we build a new China.”

His words rang empty, yet they echoed in every meeting, every classroom, even her dreams. At night, she clutched Mr. Yu’s scraps, hiding them beneath her pillow, as if their faint brushstrokes could anchor her in a world unraveled.

Winter brought hunger. The fields yielded little after months of neglect. The commune’s rations shrank; tempers frayed. Slogans failed to fill empty stomachs. One night, Lifen’s little brother fell ill. Her mother spent hours coaxing soup from withered greens, coaxing hope from gathering despair.

Yet outside, the campaign gained momentum. There were rumors: families vanished, teachers sent away for “re-education,” old men dying with their names erased. Lifen learned not to ask questions. She learned to nod at the right moments, write slogans in careful red ink, and keep secrets pressed between mattress and wall.

Sometimes, she caught sight of herself in the cracked mirror, a girl with hungry eyes and calluses on her palms. What would become of her, when the winds changed again?

The spring of 1968 arrived with the first shipment of sent-down youth from the city, a wave of students in ill-fitting tunics, bearing dreams and fears and a loneliness as sharp as the mountain wind. Lifen was paired with Chen Min, a girl from Shanghai with nimble fingers and a voice like a mockingbird.

At night, Min whispered stories: jazz on the phonograph, bright lights on Nanjing Road, her father’s sudden disappearance and her mother’s silence.

“Do you think it will ever end?” Min asked one moonless night, as the frogs croaked in the flooded paddies.

“What?” Lifen replied.

“This—struggle, accusation, suspicion. The world upside down.”

Lifen hesitated. She ran her thumb over the calligraphy fragments, hidden in her blouse. “I don’t know. Maybe not. Maybe we just find a way through.”

Min sighed. “My mother says there’s always another campaign.”

Lifen stared into the darkness, the scent of earth and promise heavy in the air. She wondered who she would be, at the end of it all. If there was an end.

The years blurred: one endless cycle of marches, denunciations, half-truths, and uneasy silences. Lifen grew from a girl into a woman, her hair streaked with gray by the time the movements lost their heat. Wei disappeared one winter, packed onto a truck and sent north. No word was ever received.

News drifted in from Beijing, soft as falling petals: the Chairman was gone. The old ways—cautious, scarred—crept back into the world. The school reopened, led by a hunched, cautious principal who spoke little of politics. The children no longer chanted in unison before faded portraits. Some teachers returned, their backs bent, voices hushed.

One rainy morning, Lifen found herself alone in the school library, sorting a pile of battered textbooks. Tucked between aging pages she discovered a book of poems, its margins scribbled with Mr. Yu’s careful hand.

She read aloud, voice trembling, and the words seemed to shiver through the empty classroom: a hymn to willow trees, lost rivers, the courage of remembering.

Lifen closed the book, pressing her palms flat on the cover, feeling a corner of herself—damaged, but not broken—unfurl like green shoots after a hard winter.

The winds of change had torn Tongshan apart, scattered its pieces to the corners of history. Yet here, in the silence of the schoolhouse, surrounded by the ghosts of vanished voices, Lifen understood what Mr. Yu meant: truth lived not in slogans, but in quiet acts of remembering, in the stubborn endurance of beauty.

She stood by the window, watching sunlight filter through broken panes, as if the future could be coaxed—gently—into bloom.
