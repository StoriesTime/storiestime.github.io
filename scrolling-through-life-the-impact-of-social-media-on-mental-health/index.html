Scrolling Through Life: The Impact of Social Media on Mental Health


The soft glow of the phone screen illuminated Jamie’s face as she sat on the edge of her bed, scrolling through her social media feed. It had become a routine. The habitual swipe of her thumb, the flicker of posts, the quick glances at her notifications—it was all so automatic now. She didn’t even need to think about it anymore. But as the hours slipped by, the joy and connection she once felt from her online interactions had begun to fade. Something had shifted, and she couldn’t quite put her finger on it.

It started innocuously. A friend from high school posted a vacation photo on a beach in Bali. Another shared a seemingly perfect family dinner. Then, there was the influencer with her radiant smile, perfect skin, and an array of skincare products she endorsed. At first, Jamie simply liked the posts, commenting occasionally with words like “You go, girl!” or “That looks amazing!” But as time went on, she couldn’t help but compare herself to these curated lives. Why didn’t her vacation look like theirs? How come her skin didn’t glow the way it seemed to in every influencer’s post? Why was her life not as exciting as everyone else’s?

Soon, her scrolling became a constant race to measure up. She didn’t just mindlessly scroll anymore; she was seeking validation. She was chasing after the sense of belonging, the feeling of approval that each like and comment gave her. But even then, it never felt enough. The more she scrolled, the more her sense of self seemed to shrink.

It wasn’t long before Jamie found herself constantly checking her phone. Whether it was first thing in the morning or in the middle of the night, her fingers would instinctively reach for it. Notifications, likes, messages—they were all there, waiting for her. Yet, no matter how much attention she gave them, they never seemed to bring satisfaction. There was always something else to see, someone else to compare herself to. The digital world was vast, and its grip on her grew tighter every day.

The effects of social media on mental health weren’t new to Jamie, but they had begun to affect her in ways she didn’t anticipate. As a teenager, she had heard about how social media could create unrealistic beauty standards, but she didn’t think it would impact her in the same way. She used to think those studies about anxiety and depression were extreme, but now, she felt the weight of it. Each photo of a smiling face on vacation or an influencer living her best life felt like a constant reminder of what she was missing.

Her friends, too, seemed more distant, even though they were closer than ever—virtually speaking. They liked each other’s photos, posted group pictures from hangouts, and shared memes. But in person, conversations had become shallow. What was the point of talking when everything was already shared online? It felt like everyone was busy building their “brand” on social media, focusing on curating an image instead of making genuine connections. Sometimes, when she sat in a room full of people, everyone was glued to their phones, caught up in a different world than the one right in front of them.

The realization came one evening as Jamie scrolled through her feed during another lonely night. She had just seen a post from her best friend, Ellie, who had recently moved to another city for work. Ellie was glowing in the picture, smiling next to a new group of friends, all gathered at some trendy restaurant. Jamie smiled weakly at the photo but quickly felt a pang of jealousy. It wasn’t just that Ellie seemed to be thriving in her new environment; it was the fact that Ellie seemed to have it all figured out, while Jamie felt like she was sinking deeper into a hole she couldn’t escape.

Jamie didn’t even bother to message Ellie that night. The thought of typing a casual, “How are you?” felt hollow. Ellie’s life appeared to be perfect; what could she possibly share? The self-doubt was overwhelming. She started to wonder whether anyone was truly happy in real life or if they were just pretending.

That’s when Jamie realized something that shook her: social media wasn’t real. It was a distortion of reality, a warped mirror that people held up to their lives. The likes, the comments, the constant pursuit of digital affirmation—it was all a facade. She had been buying into it for so long, thinking that everyone else was living their best life while she was stuck in hers. But the truth was, everyone was trapped in this endless cycle of comparison, feeding off of one another’s curated images.

The next day, Jamie took a deep breath and made a decision. She needed to break the cycle. Social media was no longer something that added value to her life; it had become a source of stress, anxiety, and insecurity. She wasn’t the first to feel this way. Studies had shown that excessive social media use often led to feelings of isolation and inadequacy. The constant bombardment of idealized images could lead to mental health struggles like anxiety and depression. It didn’t help that social media platforms were designed to keep users engaged for as long as possible, creating a dopamine loop that left them wanting more.

Jamie’s first step was to reduce the time she spent on social media. She deleted the apps from her phone for a week. The withdrawal was difficult, especially during moments of boredom. She reached for her phone instinctively, but the apps weren’t there. It felt strange at first. She didn’t have the constant stream of images and updates to fill the space. But over time, something shifted.

Without the pressure of keeping up with everyone’s posts, Jamie began to rediscover the world around her. She took long walks outside, appreciating the simple pleasures of nature that she had once overlooked. She spent more time reading books, engaging in conversations with friends and family without the constant distraction of her phone buzzing in her pocket. For the first time in months, she felt present in her own life. She wasn’t looking at someone else’s vacation or comparing her body to an influencer’s. She was living her own life, imperfect as it was, and that was enough.

However, Jamie knew that she couldn’t just erase social media entirely from her life. It had its positives: staying in touch with old friends, learning new things, and keeping up with current events. But it had to be done mindfully, with boundaries in place. She reinstalled the apps but set limits. She began to unfollow accounts that didn’t add value to her life and followed accounts that focused on realness, authenticity, and self-care. Slowly, she began to create a feed that felt supportive rather than draining.

It wasn’t an instant fix. There were days when Jamie still found herself mindlessly scrolling, feeling the temptation to compare herself to others. But she had learned to recognize the signs and pull herself back before it got overwhelming. She knew that her worth was not determined by how many likes or followers she had. It was determined by how she felt about herself and how she lived her life—away from the screen.

In the months that followed, Jamie began to feel lighter. The constant weight of comparison had lifted, and she no longer sought validation through social media. Her self-esteem wasn’t tied to the number of likes or comments she received. Instead, it came from within, from the relationships she nurtured, the passions she pursued, and the small victories she achieved every day. Social media no longer ruled her life—it was just one small part of it.

And as she closed her phone for the night, Jamie smiled. The glow from the screen no longer had the power to cast shadows over her mind. The journey wasn’t over, but she had learned to navigate it with a little more awareness, a little more peace.

She had scrolled through life. Now, she was finally living it.
